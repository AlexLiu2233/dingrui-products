## Big Idea (PC11, 2018)

> **Quadratic relationships are prevalent in the world around us.**

Sample inquiry questions (from the curriculum's elaboration block) tied
to this Big Idea:

- What are some examples of quadratic relationships in the world around
  us, and what are the similarities and differences between these?
- Why are quadratic relationships so prevalent in the world around us?
- How does the predictable pattern of linear functions extend to
  quadratic functions?
- Why is the shape of a quadratic function called a parabola?
- How can we decide which form of a quadratic function to use for a
  given problem?
- What effect does each term of a quadratic function have on its
  graph?

The companion algebra Big Idea also supports quadratics:

> **The meanings of, and connections between, operations extend to
> powers, radicals, and polynomials.**

Sample inquiry questions tied to algebra Big Idea (relevant to
quadratics):

- What are the similarities and differences between quadratic functions
  and linear functions? How are they connected?
- What do we notice about the rate of change in a quadratic function?
- How do the strategies for solving linear equations extend to solving
  quadratic, radical, or rational equations?

## Content — Quadratic functions and equations

Verbatim from the PC11 Content list (Learning Standards) and
elaborations:

> Students are expected to know the following:
>
> - real number system
> - powers with rational exponents
> - radical operations and equations
> - polynomial factoring
> - rational expressions and equations
> - **quadratic functions and equations**
> - **linear and quadratic inequalities**
> - trigonometry: non-right triangles and angles in standard position
> - financial literacy: compound interest, investments, loans

The elaboration block under each Content bullet gives the granular
sub-topics.

### Content elaboration — polynomial factoring (supports quadratics)

> - greatest common factor of a polynomial
> - trinomials of the form $ax^2 + bx + c$ where $a = 1$
> - difference of squares of the form $a^2 - b^2$
> - may extend to $a^2 - 2ab + b^2$ (perfect square trinomials),
>   $a x^2 + bx + c$ where $a \ne 1$

### Content elaboration — quadratic functions and equations

> - **identifying characteristics of graphs** (including domain and
>   range, intercepts, vertex, symmetry), **multiple forms**, function
>   notation, **extrema**
> - **exploring transformations**
> - **solving equations** (e.g., factoring, quadratic formula,
>   completing the square, graphing, square root method)
> - **connecting equation-solving strategies**
> - **connecting equations with functions**
> - **solving problems in context**

### Content elaboration — linear and quadratic inequalities

> - single variable (e.g., $ax^2 + bx + c \le 0$)
> - domain and range restrictions from problems in situational contexts
> - **sign analysis**: identifying intervals where a function is
>   positive, negative, or zero
> - symbolic notation for inequality statements, including interval
>   notation

## Curricular Competency citations (PC11)

Quadratics-relevant competencies the curriculum expects students to
*do*:

- **Reasoning and modelling** — "Model with mathematics in situational
  contexts" — supports the quadratic-modeling worked-example pattern
  (projectile motion, profit/revenue parabolas).
- **Understanding and solving** — "Apply flexible and strategic
  approaches to solve problems" — supports the
  factor-vs-formula-vs-completing-the-square decision tree.
- **Communicating and representing** — "Explain and justify
  mathematical ideas and decisions in many ways" — supports the
  inequality-sign-analysis justification step.
- **Connecting and reflecting** — "examine the structure of and
  connections between mathematical ideas (e.g., trinomial factoring,
  roots of quadratic equations)" — the curriculum explicitly names
  trinomial factoring and quadratic roots in this connection-step
  competency.
