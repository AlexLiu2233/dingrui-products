# British Columbia — Biology Extract (Life Sciences 11 + Anatomy and Physiology 12 + Science 10 foundations)

**Source:** BC's redesigned curriculum (Building Student Success),
Science. This extract covers the two senior biology courses —
**Life Sciences 11** and **Anatomy and Physiology 12** — plus the
biology Big Ideas and Content from **Science 10** (the foundation course).

**Fetched via** the official BC curriculum site
<https://curriculum.gov.bc.ca/curriculum/science>:
- <https://curriculum.gov.bc.ca/curriculum/science/11/life-sciences>
- <https://curriculum.gov.bc.ca/curriculum/science/12/anatomy-and-physiology>
- <https://curriculum.gov.bc.ca/curriculum/science/10/core>

The "elaborations" PDFs (e.g.
`…/files/curriculum/science/en_science_11_life-sciences_elab.pdf`,
`…/en_science_12_anatomy-and-physiology_elab.pdf`) add example
elaborations to each Content bullet; they were **not** fully transcribed
here — see GAPS.

**Structure of a BC course.** Each BC course has three Learning-Standard
components: **Big Ideas** (the enduring understandings), **Curricular
Competencies** (the science-skills "doing" standards — common across all
science courses and grouped under Questioning and predicting / Planning
and conducting / Processing and analyzing data and information /
Evaluating / Applying and innovating / Communicating), and **Content**
(the "Students are expected to know the following" topic bullets). BC has
**no per-statement alphanumeric code** like NGSS/AB/ON — citations are by
course name + Big Idea / Content bullet. The Content bullets below are
**verbatim**; Curricular Competencies are summarised (they are identical
in form across science courses).

## Life Sciences 11

**Big Ideas (verbatim):**
> 1. "Life is a result of interactions at the molecular and cellular
>    levels."
> 2. "Evolution occurs at the population level."
> 3. "Organisms are grouped based on common characteristics."

**Curricular Competencies (summary).** Questioning and predicting;
Planning and conducting (planning investigations, assessing risks,
addressing ethical and environmental concerns); Processing and analyzing
data and information (analyzing patterns/trends, constructing and
interpreting graphs and models); Evaluating (evaluating methods and
experimental conditions, inconsistencies); Applying and innovating;
Communicating. Common to all senior science courses — not transcribed in
full.

**Content (verbatim — "Students are expected to know the following"):**
> - levels of organization: molecular, cellular, tissue, organ, organ
>   system, organism, population, community, ecosystem
> - cell structure and function: prokaryotic and eukaryotic; unicellular
>   and multicellular; cell specialization
> - sexual and asexual reproduction: mitosis, meiosis, budding,
>   conjugation, binary fission
> - energy transformations in cells: cellular respiration: glucose broken
>   down in the presence of water yields energy (ATP) and carbon dioxide;
>   photosynthesis: consumes carbon dioxide and water, produces oxygen and
>   sugars
> - viruses: at the boundary of living and non-living; lytic and lysogenic
>   cycles; viral disease: immunity, vaccines, herd immunity, reducing the
>   spread of viral diseases (e.g., H1N1, avian flu, HIV, Ebola, STIs)
> - First Peoples understandings of interrelationships between organisms:
>   plants as indicators of timing for corresponding events, decaying
>   animals as plant nutrients
> - microevolution: change within a species that occurs over time in a
>   population: adaptation to changing environments; changes in DNA:
>   mutations, population genetics; natural selection: mechanisms of
>   gradual change
> - macroevolution: major evolutionary changes over long periods of time;
>   origin of new species: speciation: neo-Darwinism (gradualism);
>   punctuated equilibrium; genetic drift; sexual selection; adaptive
>   radiation; processes of macroevolution: divergent; convergent;
>   co-evolution; evidence for macroevolution: embryology; mitochondrial
>   DNA; molecular evolution; fossil record
> - artificial selection and genetic modifications: gene therapy, GMOs,
>   ethical considerations
> - single-celled and multi-celled organisms: prokaryotic and eukaryotic;
>   aerobic and anaerobic; sexual and asexual reproduction
> - trends in complexity among various life forms: symmetry, coelom,
>   tissue development, transport, gas exchange, cephalization,
>   reproduction, vascularization, alternation of generations, seed
>   production
> - evidence for phylogenetic relationships: DNA, biochemistry, anatomy,
>   embryology, fossil evidence, biogeography
> - taxonomic principles for classifying organisms: taxa: kingdom, phylum,
>   class, order, family, genus, species; phylogenetic tree (cladogram);
>   dichotomous key; binomial nomenclature
> - First Peoples knowledge on classification: classification of animals
>   based on use (e.g., traditional clothing, food, hunting seasons);
>   classification of BC plants based on use (e.g., food, medicine)
> - similarities and differences between domains and kingdoms: unifying
>   criteria for classification; hierarchical nature of diversity; changing
>   models based on emerging knowledge

*Maps to:* Unit 1 (cell structure & function; levels of organization),
Unit 3 (energy transformations — note: same conceptual level as NGSS, no
biochemical-pathway detail), Unit 4 (mitosis/meiosis under reproduction),
Unit 7 (micro- and macroevolution — much richer than NGSS, includes
punctuated equilibrium, genetic drift, sexual selection, adaptive
radiation), Unit 8 (taxonomy, phylogeny, dichotomous keys, domains/
kingdoms — BC **has** explicit taxonomy content, unlike NGSS).

## Anatomy and Physiology 12  *(harder Grade-12 stream — honors-flag)*

**Big Ideas (verbatim):**
> 1. "Homeostasis is maintained through physiological processes."
> 2. "Gene expression, through protein synthesis, is an interaction
>    between genes and the environment."
> 3. "Organ systems have complex interrelationships to maintain
>    homeostasis."

**Curricular Competencies (summary).** Same six science-skill groupings
as Life Sciences 11 (Questioning and predicting; Planning and conducting;
Processing and analyzing; Evaluating; Applying and innovating;
Communicating, including constructing evidence-based arguments and
suggesting a course of action for a purpose and audience). Not transcribed
in full.

**Content (verbatim — "Students are expected to know the following"):**
> - Biological molecules: water, acids, bases, buffers; dehydration and
>   synthesis reactions; organic molecules: carbohydrates, lipids,
>   proteins, nucleic acids, ATP
> - Metabolism and enzymes: anabolism and catabolism; ATP production and
>   utilization; models and regulation of enzymatic reactions (e.g.,
>   lock-and-key model); substrate, coenzyme, activation energy; regulation
>   of enzyme activity (e.g., allosteric inhibition)
> - Feedback loops and regulation of the body's internal environment:
>   negative feedback loops (maintaining normal body temperature, normal
>   CO2 and glucose levels in blood); positive feedback loops (temperature
>   regulation, CO2 levels in blood, oxytocin in childbirth, blood
>   clotting)
> - Transport across a cell membrane: structure of the plasma membrane;
>   selective permeability; diffusion, osmosis, facilitated transport,
>   active transport, endocytosis, exocytosis
> - DNA: the cell's genetic information; replication
> - Gene expression and protein synthesis
> - Proteins and their relationship to the structure and function of all
>   cells: structural proteins, hormones, enzymes
> - Genomics and biotechnology: Human Genome Project, 1000 Genomes
>   Project, 1000 Plant Genomes Project, personal genomics; cloning,
>   recombinant DNA, GMOs, transgenic organisms, genetic modification, gene
>   therapy
> - Micro to macro organization: molecules, organelles, cells, tissues,
>   organs, organ systems, organisms
> - Organ systems: nervous, endocrine, digestive, cardiovascular,
>   lymphatic/immune, respiratory, urinary, reproductive; structure and
>   function; structural and functional interdependence; maintenance of
>   homeostasis
> - Lifestyle differences and their effects on human health: dietary
>   plans, exercise, sleep, smoking, salt intake, alcohol consumption,
>   drugs, vaccinations, contraception, fertility drugs
> - Holistic approach to health: health care that integrates mind, body,
>   and spirit with community
> - Disease as an imbalance in homeostasis: may occur when one or more body
>   systems fail to maintain homeostasis (e.g., ulcers, hypertension,
>   lactose intolerance, diabetes, HIV-AIDS)

*Maps to:* Unit 2 (Biochemistry — biological molecules, metabolism &
enzymes), Unit 6 (Molecular Genetics — DNA replication, gene expression,
protein synthesis), Unit 10 (Human Anatomy & Physiology — all eight organ
systems; this is the deepest A&P treatment of the four curricula), Unit 11
(Homeostasis — negative/positive feedback loops, disease as homeostatic
imbalance). BC concentrates the human-physiology / homeostasis / molecular
load into this **Grade-12** course.

## Science 10 (foundations) — biology Big Ideas + Content

**Big Ideas (verbatim — all four; the biology-relevant one is #1):**
> 1. "DNA is the basis for the diversity of living things."
> 2. "Energy change is required as atoms rearrange in chemical processes."
> 3. "Energy is conserved, and its transformation can affect living things
>    and the environment."
> 4. "The formation of the universe can be explained by the big bang
>    theory."

**Content (verbatim — biology / life-science bullets):**
> - DNA structure and function: genes and chromosomes; gene expression;
>   interactions of genes and the environment
> - patterns of inheritance: Mendelian genetics, Punnett squares, complete
>   dominance, co-dominance, incomplete dominance, sex-linked inheritance,
>   human genetics
> - mechanisms for the diversity of life: mutation (positive, negative,
>   and neutral impacts; mutagens and carcinogens; its impact on
>   evolution); natural selection (adaptive radiation, selection pressure,
>   adaptations, extinctions); artificial selection (in agriculture;
>   breeding — plant and animal)
> - applied genetics: genomics, GMOs, gene therapy, cloning, stem cells,
>   reproductive technology, species, population and ecosystems, forensics,
>   genetic engineering
> - ethical considerations: the health, environmental, social, and
>   political implications of modern genetics

*Maps to:* Unit 5 (Mendelian Genetics — BC introduces Punnett squares and
inheritance patterns as early as **Science 10**, the foundation course),
Unit 6 (Molecular Genetics — DNA, genes, gene expression), and Unit 7
(Evolution — natural/artificial selection, mutation's impact on
evolution). BC front-loads genetics and selection into the Grade-10
foundation, then deepens at Grade 11 (Life Sciences 11) and Grade 12
(Anatomy and Physiology 12).

## Unit-mapping summary (British Columbia)

| HS Biology Unit | Science 10 | Life Sciences 11 | Anatomy & Physiology 12 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 Cell Structure and Function | — | cell structure & function; levels of organization | micro-to-macro organization |
| 2 Biochemistry | — | — | Biological molecules; Metabolism and enzymes |
| 3 Cellular Energetics | — | energy transformations in cells (respiration, photosynthesis) | metabolism / ATP |
| 4 Cell Division and the Cell Cycle | — | mitosis, meiosis (under reproduction) | — |
| 5 Mendelian Genetics and Heredity | patterns of inheritance (Punnett squares) | — | — |
| 6 Molecular Genetics | DNA structure & function; gene expression | (microevolution: changes in DNA) | DNA replication; gene expression and protein synthesis |
| 7 Evolution and Natural Selection | mechanisms for diversity (natural/artificial selection) | microevolution + macroevolution | — |
| 8 Biodiversity and Classification | — | taxonomic principles; phylogeny; domains/kingdoms | — |
| 9 Ecology and Ecosystems | (applied genetics: population & ecosystems) | levels of organization (community, ecosystem) | — |
| 10 Human Anatomy and Physiology | — | trends in complexity (gas exchange, transport) | Organ systems (8 systems) — deepest A&P treatment |
| 11 Homeostasis | — | — | Feedback loops; Disease as imbalance in homeostasis |
| 12 Population Biology | — | microevolution: population genetics | — |

*Placement notes:* BC introduces **Mendelian genetics + Punnett squares
in Science 10** (earlier than NGSS/AB, comparable to ON SBI3U). BC
**Life Sciences 11** carries the richest **evolution** treatment of the
four curricula (explicit micro- vs. macroevolution, punctuated
equilibrium, genetic drift, adaptive radiation) and the only explicit
**taxonomy/classification** content alongside Ontario. BC concentrates
**human physiology, homeostasis, and molecular genetics** into **Anatomy
and Physiology 12** — a dedicated Grade-12 course with no parallel in the
other three curricula (NGSS has no human-systems course; AB spreads it
across Biology 20/30; ON puts it in SBI3U/SBI4U). Flag A&P 12 with an
`honors-flag` as the harder Grade-12 stream.
