INTRODUCTION

                       The Alberta 10Ł12 Mathematics Program of
                       Studies with Achievement Indicators has been
                       derived from The Common Curriculum
                       Framework for Grades 10Ł12 Mathematics:
                       Western and Northern Canadian Protocol, January
                       2008 (the Common Curriculum Framework). The
                       program of studies incorporates the conceptual
                       framework for Grades 10Ł12 Mathematics and
                       most of the general outcomes and specific
                       outcomes that were established in the Common
                       Curriculum Framework. (Note: Some of the
                       outcomes for Mathematics 20-2 and 30-2 in this
                       program of studies are different from the outcomes
                       for Foundations of Mathematics in the Common
                       Curriculum Framework.)

BACKGROUND

                       The Common Curriculum Framework was
                       developed by seven ministries of education
                       (Alberta, British Columbia, Manitoba,
                       Northwest Territories, Nunavut, Saskatchewan and
                       Yukon Territory) in collaboration with teachers,
                       administrators, parents, business representatives,
                       post-secondary educators and others. The
                       framework identifies beliefs about mathematics,
                       general and specific student outcomes, and
                       achievement indicators agreed upon by the seven
                       jurisdictions.

Teachers need     First Nations, Mķtis and Inuit students in northern
to understand     and western Canada come from diverse geographic
the diversity     areas with varied cultural and linguistic
of students'      backgrounds. Students attend schools in a variety
cultures          of settings, including urban, rural and isolated
and experiences.  communities. Teachers need to understand the
                  diversity of students' cultures and experiences.

                  First Nations, Mķtis and Inuit students often have a
                  holistic view of the environment--they look for
                  connections in learning and learn best when
                  mathematics is contextualized. They may come
                  from cultures where learning takes place through
                  active participation. Traditionally, little emphasis
                  was placed upon the written word, so oral
                  communication and practical applications and
                  experiences are important to student learning and
                  understanding. By understanding and responding
                  to nonverbal cues, teachers can optimize student
                  learning and mathematical understanding.

                  A variety of teaching and assessment strategies
                  help build upon the diverse knowledge, cultures,
                  communication styles, skills, attitudes, experiences
                  and learning styles of students.

                  Research indicates that when strategies go beyond
                  the incidental inclusion of topics and objects
                  unique to a culture or region, greater levels of
                  understanding can be achieved (Banks and Banks,
                  1993).

INSTRUCTIONAL FOCUS

                          Each course sequence in The Alberta 10Ł12
                          Mathematics Program of Studies with Achievement
                          Indicators is arranged by topics. Students should be
                          engaged in making connections among concepts both
                          within and across topics to make mathematical
                          learning experiences meaningful.

                          Teachers should consider the following points when
                          planning for instruction and assessment.
                          Ę The mathematical processes that are identified

                             with the outcome are intended to help teachers
                             select effective pedagogical approaches for the
                             teaching and learning of the outcome.
                          Ę All seven mathematical processes must be
                             integrated throughout teaching and learning
                             approaches, and should support the intent of the
                             outcomes.
                          Ę Wherever possible, meaningful contexts should be
                             used in examples, problems and projects.
                          Ę Instruction should flow from simple to complex
                             and from concrete to abstract.
                          Ę The assessment plan for the course should be a
                             balance of assessment for learning, assessment as
                             learning and assessment of learning.

                          The focus of student learning should be on
                          developing a conceptual and procedural
                          understanding of mathematics. Students' conceptual
                          understanding and procedural understanding must be
                          directly related.
