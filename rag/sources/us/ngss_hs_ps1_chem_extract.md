# NGSS HS-PS1 — Chemistry Extract (Matter and Its Interactions)

**Source:** Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS), High School —
Physical Science, topic group **HS-PS1 "Matter and Its Interactions"**.
The eight performance expectations HS-PS1-1 … HS-PS1-8 are the chemistry
backbone of the NGSS high-school physical-science strand.

**Fetched via** the per-PE pages at <https://www.nextgenscience.org/>:
- DCI arrangement page:
  <https://www.nextgenscience.org/dci-arrangement/hs-ps1-matter-and-its-interactions>
- Per-PE pages `…/pe/hs-ps1-{n}-matter-and-its-interactions` for n = 1…7.
- HS-PS1-8 verbatim text is shared with the physics extract
  (`rag/sources/us/ngss_hs_ps_extract.md`); reproduced here for the
  chemistry-scope nuclear unit.

> **IMPORTANT — the US science standard is NGSS, NOT Common Core.**
> Common Core State Standards cover Mathematics and English Language Arts
> only; Common Core has **no chemistry strand**. Do not label any of this
> content "Common Core." The citable unit for a US chemistry guide is the
> NGSS performance expectation (e.g. `HS-PS1-2`), since NGSS is the
> national framework most states adopt or adapt.

**Scope & citation note.** NGSS PEs are *coarse* three-dimensional
performance expectations, not a topic-by-topic content outline. Several
of the 14 chemistry units therefore share a PE, and some units
(Nomenclature & Formulae, the standalone Mole/Stoichiometry skill set,
much of States-of-Matter/Gas-Laws) have **no dedicated HS-PS1 PE** —
NGSS folds them into broader expectations or treats them as foundational
skills supporting the PEs. That coarseness is expected and is documented
per-unit below. Each PE statement, Clarification Statement, and
Assessment Boundary is quoted **verbatim** from the NGSS site.

## Unit 1 — Atomic Structure & the Quantum Model  ·  Unit 2 — The Periodic Table & Periodic Trends

### HS-PS1-1
> "Use the periodic table as a model to predict the relative properties
> of elements based on the patterns of electrons in the outermost energy
> level of atoms. [Clarification Statement: Examples of properties that
> could be predicted from patterns could include reactivity of metals,
> types of bonds formed, numbers of bonds formed, and reactions with
> oxygen.] [Assessment Boundary: Assessment is limited to main group
> elements. Assessment does not include quantitative understanding of
> ionization energy beyond relative trends.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 1 (electron arrangement in energy levels / outermost
shell) and Unit 2 (the periodic table as a predictive model; periodic
trends in reactivity and bonding). This is the primary anchor for both
the atomic-model and periodicity units. NGSS keeps ionization energy
**qualitative** (relative trends only) — contrast the quantitative
treatments in ON SCH3U B2/B3 and BC Chemistry 11.

## Unit 2 — The Periodic Table & Periodic Trends  ·  Unit 3 — Chemical Bonding  ·  Unit 6 — Chemical Reactions & Equations

### HS-PS1-2
> "Construct and revise an explanation for the outcome of a simple
> chemical reaction based on the outermost electron states of atoms,
> trends in the periodic table, and knowledge of the patterns of chemical
> properties. [Clarification Statement: Examples of chemical reactions
> could include the reaction of sodium and chlorine, of carbon and
> oxygen, or of carbon and hydrogen.] [Assessment Boundary: Assessment is
> limited to chemical reactions involving main group elements and
> combustion reactions.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 2 (using periodic trends to explain outcomes), Unit 3
(bonding driven by outermost electron states), and Unit 6 (predicting
the outcome of a chemical reaction). NGSS limits the scope to main-group
elements and combustion reactions.

---

## Unit 3 — Chemical Bonding  ·  Unit 7 — States of Matter & Gas Laws  ·  Unit 8 — Solutions & Solubility

### HS-PS1-3
> "Plan and conduct an investigation to gather evidence to compare the
> structure of substances at the bulk scale to infer the strength of
> electrical forces between particles. [Clarification Statement: Emphasis
> is on understanding the strengths of forces between particles, not on
> naming specific intermolecular forces (such as dipole-dipole). Examples
> of particles could include ions, atoms, molecules, and networked
> materials (such as graphite). Examples of bulk properties of substances
> could include the melting point and boiling point, vapor pressure, and
> surface tension.] [Assessment Boundary: Assessment does not include
> Raoult's law calculations of vapor pressure.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 3 (electrical forces between particles → bonding /
intermolecular forces and the property consequences), Unit 7 (states of
matter as a consequence of particle forces; melting/boiling points), and
Unit 8 (solution behaviour). NGSS deliberately keeps this at the level of
**force strength → bulk property**, not the naming of specific IMFs —
contrast ON SCH4U C3.4 / AB 20-A2.6k, which name London, dipole-dipole,
and hydrogen bonding explicitly.

## Unit 10 — Thermochemistry & Energy

### HS-PS1-4
> "Develop a model to illustrate that the release or absorption of energy
> from a chemical reaction system depends upon the changes in total bond
> energy. [Clarification Statement: Emphasis is on the idea that a
> chemical reaction is a system that affects the energy change. Examples
> of models could include molecular-level drawings and diagrams of
> reactions, graphs showing the relative energies of reactants and
> products, and representations showing energy is conserved.] [Assessment
> Boundary: Assessment does not include calculating the total bond energy
> changes during a chemical reaction from the bond energies of reactants
> and products.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 10 (endothermic/exothermic reactions, bond energy,
energy diagrams). NGSS keeps this **qualitative** — no bond-energy or
enthalpy calculation. Contrast ON SCH4U D2 (Q = mcΔT, Hess's law,
standard enthalpies of formation) and AB Chemistry 30 Unit A
(`30-A1.5k`–`30-A1.8k`, calorimetry, Hess's law), which are
quantitative. Tag the quantitative content honors-flag for an NGSS-only
student.

## Unit 11 — Reaction Rates (Kinetics)

### HS-PS1-5
> "Apply scientific principles and evidence to provide an explanation
> about the effects of changing the temperature or concentration of the
> reacting particles on the rate at which a reaction occurs.
> [Clarification Statement: Emphasis is on student reasoning that focuses
> on the number and energy of collisions between molecules.] [Assessment
> Boundary: Assessment is limited to simple reactions in which there are
> only two reactants; evidence from temperature, concentration, and rate
> data; and qualitative relationships between rate and temperature.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 11 (collision theory; effect of temperature and
concentration on rate). NGSS keeps the rate–temperature relationship
**qualitative**; no rate-law or Arrhenius treatment.

---

## Unit 12 — Chemical Equilibrium

### HS-PS1-6
> "Refine the design of a chemical system by specifying a change in
> conditions that would produce increased amounts of products at
> equilibrium. [Clarification Statement: Emphasis is on the application of
> Le Chatelier's Principle and on refining designs of chemical reaction
> systems, including descriptions of the connection between changes made
> at the macroscopic level and what happens at the molecular level.
> Examples of designs could include different ways to increase product
> formation including adding reactants or removing products.] [Assessment
> Boundary: Assessment is limited to specifying the change in only one
> variable at a time. Assessment does not include calculating equilibrium
> constants and concentrations.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 12 (Le Chatelier's principle, shifting equilibrium).
NGSS is **qualitative** (Le Chatelier shift-direction only, one variable
at a time) and explicitly excludes equilibrium-constant calculation.
Contrast ON SCH4U E2/E3 (Keq, Ksp, Ka, Kb, ICE-table calculations), BC
Chemistry 12 (Keq, Ksp, quantitative relationships), and AB Chemistry 30
Unit D GO2 (`30-D2.2k`, `30-D2.3k`), all of which are quantitative.
Strong honors-flag divergence — flag with a `syllabus-note`.

## Unit 5 — The Mole & Stoichiometry  ·  Unit 6 — Chemical Reactions & Equations

### HS-PS1-7
> "Use mathematical representations to support the claim that atoms, and
> therefore mass, are conserved during a chemical reaction. [Clarification
> Statement: Emphasis is on using mathematical ideas to communicate the
> proportional relationships between masses of atoms in the reactants and
> the products, and the translation of these relationships to the
> macroscopic scale using the mole as the conversion from the atomic to
> the macroscopic scale. Emphasis is on assessing students' use of
> mathematical thinking and not on memorization and rote application of
> problem-solving techniques.] [Assessment Boundary: Assessment does not
> include complex chemical reactions.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 5 (the mole as the atomic-to-macroscopic conversion;
mass conservation underpinning stoichiometry) and Unit 6 (balancing /
conservation of mass). This is the closest NGSS comes to a stoichiometry
PE — note it frames the mole conceptually and excludes "complex chemical
reactions," so multi-step limiting-reagent / percent-yield problems (ON
SCH3U D2, AB 20-D2) sit beyond the NGSS assessment boundary.

## Unit 1 — Atomic Structure (nuclear)  ·  (feeds Unit 10 energy framing)

### HS-PS1-8
> "Develop models to illustrate the changes in the composition of the
> nucleus of the atom and the energy released during the processes of
> fission, fusion, and radioactive decay. [Clarification Statement:
> Emphasis is on simple qualitative models, such as pictures or diagrams,
> and on the scale of energy released in nuclear processes relative to
> other kinds of transformations.] [Assessment Boundary: Assessment does
> not include quantitative calculation of energy released. Assessment is
> limited to alpha, beta, and gamma radioactive decays.]"

*Maps to:* Unit 1 (nuclear composition, isotopes/radioisotopes, fission,
fusion, radioactive decay) within the atomic-structure guide. NGSS keeps
this **qualitative** (alpha/beta/gamma only; no energy calculation). This
PE is shared with the physics extract's Modern/Nuclear unit.

## Units with NO dedicated HS-PS1 performance expectation

NGSS coarseness means several of the 14 chemistry units have **no direct
PE** and are framed as foundational skills that support the PEs above:

- **Unit 4 — Nomenclature & Chemical Formulae.** No PE. IUPAC naming /
  formula-writing is a foundational skill underlying HS-PS1-2 and
  HS-PS1-7. The provincial curricula make this explicit (ON SCH3U B2.7,
  AB 20-A1/A2 naming outcomes, BC Chemistry 11 "organic compounds:
  names, structures"); NGSS does not. A US-only student meets naming
  implicitly through reaction work.
- **Unit 7 — States of Matter & the Gas Laws.** No gas-law PE. NGSS
  touches particle forces / states under HS-PS1-3, and energy of state
  change under HS-PS3 (see the physics extract), but has **no Boyle's /
  Charles's / ideal-gas-law PE**. Contrast ON SCH3U strand F (full gas
  laws, ideal gas law, Dalton's law) and AB Chemistry 20 Unit B. Strong
  divergence — flag with a `syllabus-note`.
- **Unit 9 — Acids, Bases & pH.** No dedicated acid–base PE. NGSS
  treats acid–base reactions only obliquely through HS-PS1-2
  (reaction outcomes) and HS-PS1-6 (equilibrium). pH, titration, and
  acid/base strength are explicit only in the provincial curricula (ON
  SCH3U E2/E3 + SCH4U E3, BC Chemistry 11/12, AB Chemistry 20 Unit C +
  Chemistry 30 Unit D). Major divergence — `syllabus-note` + honors-flag
  for the quantitative pH/Ka work.
- **Unit 13 — Redox & Electrochemistry.** No dedicated PE. NGSS treats
  electron transfer only within the bonding/energy framing (HS-PS1-2,
  HS-PS3-5 electric-field energy in the physics extract). Galvanic /
  electrolytic cells, cell potentials, and Faraday's law are explicit
  only provincially (ON SCH4U F, BC Chemistry 12, AB Chemistry 30
  Unit B). Major divergence — `syllabus-note` + honors-flag.
- **Unit 14 — Introduction to Organic Chemistry.** No dedicated PE.
  Carbon chemistry appears only through HS-PS1-2 (C+H, C+O reactions).
  Functional groups, IUPAC organic naming, and reaction types are
  explicit only provincially (ON SCH4U B, BC Chemistry 11 "organic
  compounds / applications of organic chemistry," AB Chemistry 30
  Unit C). Major divergence — `syllabus-note`.

## Cross-cutting note — three dimensions

Every NGSS PE integrates a **Science and Engineering Practice (SEP)**, a
**Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI)**, and a **Crosscutting Concept (CCC)**.
The HS-PS1 DCIs are **PS1.A Structure and Properties of Matter**,
**PS1.B Chemical Reactions**, and **PS1.C Nuclear Processes**. The
granular DCI "Foundation Box" sub-bullets were **not** pulled verbatim
into this extract — see GAPS.
