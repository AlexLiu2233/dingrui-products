Topic 5.1 · FUN-1

Using the Mean Value Theorem

The Mean Value Theorem (MVT) is one of calculus's most powerful existence theorems. It bridges the gap between average behavior and instantaneous behavior, guaranteeing that somewhere on an interval, a function's instantaneous rate of change matches its average rate of change.

Mean Value Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, then there exists at least one $c$ in $(a, b)$ such that:

$$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

In other words, the theorem guarantees the existence of a point where the tangent line is parallel to the secant line connecting $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$.

Checking Hypotheses

Before applying the MVT, you must verify both conditions: continuity on the closed interval $[a, b]$ and differentiability on the open interval $(a, b)$. If either condition fails, the conclusion is not guaranteed. On the AP Exam, always state explicitly that these conditions are met before drawing a conclusion.

Exam Tip — Justification Language

A strong MVT justification names the function, confirms continuity and differentiability, computes the average rate of change, and then concludes that there exists a value $c$ with $f'(c)$ equal to that average. Use the theorem's name by name.

Learning ObjectiveEssential Knowledge
FUN-1.B Justify conclusions about functions by applying the Mean Value Theorem over an interval. If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then MVT guarantees a point in the open interval where the instantaneous rate of change equals the average rate of change over the interval.
Worked Example
Applying the Mean Value Theorem on $f(x)=x^2-4x+1$ over $[1,5]$
Step 1 Since $f$ is a polynomial, it is continuous on $[1,5]$ and differentiable on $(1,5)$.
Step 2 Compute the average rate of change: $$\frac{f(5)-f(1)}{5-1}=\frac{6-(-2)}{4}=2.$$
Step 3 Differentiate first: $$f'(x)=2x-4.$$ Now set the derivative equal to the average rate of change: $$2c-4=2 \Rightarrow c=3.$$
Conclusion By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists a value $c=3$ in $(1,5)$ such that the tangent slope equals the secant slope.

Topic 5.2 · FUN-1

Extreme Value Theorem, Global vs. Local Extrema, and Critical Points

The Extreme Value Theorem (EVT) provides another existence guarantee: a continuous function on a closed interval must attain both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum. This simple-sounding result is the foundation for all optimization analysis.

Extreme Value Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then $f$ attains at least one minimum value and at least one maximum value on $[a, b]$.

Critical Points

A critical point of $f$ is a point $x = c$ in the domain of $f$ where $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist. Critical points are the only candidates for local extrema—but not every critical point produces one. A critical point where the derivative changes sign yields a local extremum; one where it does not (such as an inflection point with a horizontal tangent) does not.

Common Pitfall

Not every critical point is an extremum. For example, $f(x) = x^3$ has $f'(0) = 0$, but $x = 0$ is neither a local max nor a local min. Always apply a test—first or second derivative—before concluding.

FUN-1.C Justify conclusions about functions by applying the Extreme Value Theorem. EVT guarantees a min and max on $[a,b]$ if $f$ is continuous. Critical points occur where $f'=0$ or $f'$ DNE. All local extrema occur at critical points, but not all critical points are local extrema.
Topic 5.3 · FUN-4

Determining Intervals on Which a Function Is Increasing or Decreasing

The first derivative is the primary tool for analyzing where a function rises and falls. The sign of $f'(x)$ on an interval directly tells you the behavior of $f$ on that interval.

Key Principle

If $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x$ in an interval, then $f$ is increasing on that interval. If $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x$ in an interval, then $f$ is decreasing on that interval.

Process

To determine intervals of increase or decrease: find all critical points of $f$ (where $f' = 0$ or $f'$ does not exist), then test the sign of $f'$ in each interval between consecutive critical points. Use a sign chart or number line as a workspace, but remember that on the AP Exam, sign charts alone are not sufficient justification—you must express your reasoning in sentences.

Exam Tip

When asked whether $f$ is increasing or decreasing at a point, frame your answer in terms of the derivative: "$f$ is increasing on $(a, b)$ because $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x$ in $(a, b)$." Never say "it's going up" without referencing the derivative.

Topic 5.4 · FUN-4

Using the First Derivative Test to Determine Relative (Local) Extrema

The First Derivative Test uses sign changes of $f'$ around a critical point to classify it as a local maximum, local minimum, or neither.

First Derivative Test

If $c$ is a critical point of $f$ and $f'$ changes from positive to negative at $c$, then $f$ has a local maximum at $c$. If $f'$ changes from negative to positive at $c$, then $f$ has a local minimum at $c$. If $f'$ does not change sign, then $c$ is neither.

Writing a Complete Justification

A full justification requires three elements: (1) identify $c$ as a critical point, (2) describe the sign change of $f'$ around $c$, and (3) state the conclusion using the name of the test. For instance: "$f'(c) = 0$, and $f'$ changes from positive to negative at $x = c$, so by the First Derivative Test, $f$ has a local maximum at $x = c$."

Skill Focus — 3.D

Apply an appropriate mathematical definition, theorem, or test. When justifying extrema, choose between the First Derivative Test and Second Derivative Test based on what information is available.

Topic 5.5 · FUN-4

Using the Candidates Test to Determine Absolute (Global) Extrema

When a function is continuous on a closed interval $[a, b]$, the Extreme Value Theorem guarantees absolute extrema exist. The Candidates Test is the procedure for finding them.

Candidates Test Procedure

On a closed interval $[a, b]$: (1) Find all critical points of $f$ in $(a, b)$. (2) Evaluate $f$ at each critical point and at both endpoints $a$ and $b$. (3) The largest value is the absolute maximum; the smallest is the absolute minimum.

This method works because absolute extrema on a closed interval can only occur at critical points or endpoints—there are no other possibilities. The test is straightforward but requires careful computation; do not forget to evaluate the function at the endpoints.

Common Pitfall

Students sometimes forget to check the endpoints. On a closed interval, the absolute max or min may occur at an endpoint rather than at a critical point. Always evaluate $f(a)$ and $f(b)$.

Topic 5.6 · FUN-4

Determining Concavity of Functions over Their Domains

Concavity describes the curvature of a graph. While the first derivative tells us direction (increasing/decreasing), the second derivative tells us how the rate of change itself is changing—the shape of the curve.

Concavity and the Second Derivative

If $f''(x) > 0$ on an interval, then $f$ is concave up on that interval, so the graph bends upward like a cup. If $f''(x) < 0$ on an interval, then $f$ is concave down on that interval, so the graph bends downward like a frown.

Equivalently, $f$ is concave up on an interval where $f'$ is increasing, and concave down where $f'$ is decreasing. This connection between $f'$ behavior and $f$ concavity is essential for graph analysis.

Points of Inflection

A point of inflection is a point where the concavity of $f$ changes. At an inflection point, $f''$ must either equal zero or fail to exist—but as with critical points, $f'' = 0$ alone does not guarantee an inflection point. You must confirm that concavity actually changes by checking the sign of $f''$ on either side.

Exam Tip

When justifying concavity, always reference $f''$. Say "$f$ is concave up on $(a, b)$ because $f''(x) > 0$ on $(a, b)$," not just "the graph is concave up." Precision in naming $f$, $f'$, and $f''$ is critical.

Topic 5.7 · FUN-4

Using the Second Derivative Test to Determine Extrema

The Second Derivative Test provides an alternative to the First Derivative Test for classifying critical points. It is often more efficient when the second derivative is easy to evaluate.

Second Derivative Test

If $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) > 0$, then $f$ has a local minimum at $x = c$. If $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) < 0$, then $f$ has a local maximum at $x = c$. If $f''(c) = 0$, the test is inconclusive so you should switch to the First Derivative Test.

One Critical Point on an Interval

When a continuous function has exactly one critical point on an interval and that critical point is a local extremum, it is also the absolute extremum on that interval. This is a powerful shortcut in optimization problems.

When to Use Which Test

Use the First Derivative Test when you already have sign information about $f'$ or when $f''$ is difficult to compute. Use the Second Derivative Test when $f''$ is readily available and you simply need to check its sign at a critical point.

Topic 5.8 · FUN-4

Sketching Graphs of Functions and Their Derivatives

This topic ties together everything you've learned about derivatives and graph behavior. You should be able to move fluidly between the graph of $f$, the graph of $f'$, and the graph of $f''$.

Key Features to Identify

Key features of a function's graph—intervals of increase/decrease, local extrema, concavity, and inflection points—can all be identified from $f'$ and $f''$. Conversely, given a graph of $f$, you should be able to sketch $f'$.

Reading $f$ from $f'$

Where $f' > 0$, $f$ is increasing. Where $f' < 0$, $f$ is decreasing. Where $f'$ changes sign, $f$ has a local extremum. Where $f'$ is increasing, $f$ is concave up. Where $f'$ is decreasing, $f$ is concave down. Where $f'$ has an extremum, $f$ has an inflection point.

Critical Warning

Do not confuse properties of $f'$ with properties of $f$. When looking at the graph of $f'$, a positive $y$-value means $f$ is increasing—not that $f$ is positive. Always be explicit: "Because $f'(x) > 0$…" rather than "because it is positive…"

Topic 5.10 · FUN-4

Introduction to Optimization Problems

Optimization problems ask you to find the maximum or minimum value of a quantity subject to given constraints. These are among the most applied and most tested topics in AP Calculus. The key challenge is translating a word problem into a single-variable function that you can analyze with calculus.

Optimization Strategy

(1) Identify the quantity to be optimized. (2) Write it as a function of one or more variables. (3) Use a constraint equation to reduce to a single variable. (4) Find critical points. (5) Determine which critical point yields the desired extremum. (6) Answer the question that was asked.

Common optimization contexts include maximizing area or volume given a constraint on perimeter or material, minimizing distance or cost, and finding dimensions that optimize a geometric quantity.

Worked Example
Largest area of a rectangle with perimeter $20$
Setup Let the sides be $x$ and $y$. From $2x+2y=20$, solve for one variable: $$y=10-x.$$
Objective Write area as a function of one variable: $$A(x)=x(10-x)=10x-x^2,$$ with domain $0<x<10$.
Differentiate Find the derivative and locate the critical point: $$A'(x)=10-2x,$$ so $$10-2x=0 \Rightarrow x=5.$$
Justify Since $$A''(x)=-2<0,$$ the area function is concave down, so $x=5$ gives a maximum.
Answer The rectangle of greatest area is $5\times5$, so the maximum area is $25$ square units.
Topic 5.11 · FUN-4

Solving Optimization Problems

This topic extends the setup from Topic 5.10 into full solutions with interpretation. On the AP Exam, you are expected not just to find the optimal value, but to explain what it means in context.

Key Principle

Minimum and maximum values of a function take on specific meanings in applied contexts. After computing, always circle back to the original question and state your answer in terms of the scenario described.

Exam Tip — Justification in Optimization

A complete optimization solution must justify why the critical value is indeed a maximum or minimum. Use the First or Second Derivative Test, or the Candidates Test on a closed interval. Simply finding $f'(c) = 0$ is not sufficient—you must confirm the nature of the extremum.

Topic 5.12 · FUN-4

Exploring Behaviors of Implicit Relations

The analytical techniques developed throughout this unit—finding critical points, determining increasing/decreasing behavior, analyzing concavity—extend naturally to implicitly defined functions. The key difference is that derivatives obtained via implicit differentiation are typically expressed in both $x$ and $y$.

Critical Points of Implicit Relations

For an implicitly defined function, a critical point occurs where $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ or $\frac{dy}{dx}$ does not exist. Since $\frac{dy}{dx}$ is usually a fraction involving $x$ and $y$, set the numerator equal to zero (for $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$) or analyze where the denominator equals zero (for $\frac{dy}{dx}$ DNE).

Second Derivatives of Implicit Relations

When computing $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ for an implicit relation, the result typically involves $x$, $y$, and $\frac{dy}{dx}$. You may substitute the expression for $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to simplify. The second derivative can then be used to determine concavity and apply the Second Derivative Test, just as with explicit functions.

Skill Focus — 1.E & 3.E

Apply appropriate mathematical rules or procedures, and provide reasons or rationales for solutions and conclusions. With implicit relations, precision in algebraic manipulation and careful chain rule application are essential.

Review · Strategy

Exam Strategy

1. Precision in Language

Always refer to $f$, $f'$, and $f''$ by name. Never write "it" or "the function" when referencing a derivative. Ambiguity costs points. If the question names the function $g$, use $g$, $g'$, and $g''$ consistently.

2. Start with the Question's Language

If a question asks "Does $g$ have a relative minimum, maximum, or neither at $x = 10$? Justify your answer," begin your response by repeating the framework: "$g$ has a relative minimum at $x = 10$ because…"

3. Justify from Derivatives, Not Graphs

When given a graph of $f'$, make arguments about $f$ based directly on the graph of the derivative. For example: "$f$ is concave up on $a < x < b$ because the graph of $f'$ is increasing on $a < x < b$." This is more reliable than trying to read the graph of $f$ itself.

4. Verify Hypotheses

Before applying any theorem (MVT, EVT), explicitly confirm its hypotheses are satisfied. State continuity and differentiability conditions before drawing conclusions.

5. Optimization Requires Justification

Finding a critical point is not enough. You must justify that it yields a maximum or minimum using a derivative test or the Candidates Test. A complete answer includes the value and its context interpretation.

Review · Pitfalls

Common Mistakes

Confusing $f$ and $f'$

The most prevalent error. Students read a graph of $f'$ and describe features as if they are features of $f$. For example, seeing that $f'$ is positive and concluding "$f$ is positive" instead of "$f$ is increasing."

Using "it" Instead of Naming Functions

Vague language like "it is increasing" can be interpreted as referring to $f$ or $f'$. Always name the function explicitly.

Assuming $f'(c) = 0$ Means Extremum

A critical point is only an extremum if the derivative changes sign (First Derivative Test) or if the second derivative test confirms it. $f'(c) = 0$ alone is insufficient.

Forgetting Endpoints in Candidates Test

On a closed interval, always evaluate $f$ at both endpoints. The absolute extremum may occur at an endpoint, not at an interior critical point.

Describing Instead of Justifying

Saying "the graph goes up" is a description, not a justification. A justification uses calculus reasoning: "$f$ is increasing because $f'(x) > 0$."

Applying MVT Without Checking Hypotheses

You must confirm continuity on $[a,b]$ and differentiability on $(a,b)$ before applying the Mean Value Theorem. Skipping this step means your argument is incomplete.

Review · Quiz

Unit Quiz

Test your understanding. Select the best answer for each question.

1. The Mean Value Theorem guarantees the existence of a value $c$ in $(a,b)$ such that $f'(c)$ equals the average rate of change over $[a,b]$. Which conditions must hold?

Correct! The MVT requires continuity on the closed interval $[a,b]$ and differentiability on the open interval $(a,b)$. Both conditions are necessary.

2. If $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) = 0$, what can you conclude about $f$ at $x = c$?

Correct! When $f''(c) = 0$, the Second Derivative Test gives no information. You must use the First Derivative Test or further analysis to classify the critical point.

3. On the interval $(-2, 5)$, $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x$. Which statement is true?

Correct! $f'(x) < 0$ tells us about the direction of $f$ (decreasing), not its sign or concavity. This is a classic distinction tested on the AP Exam.

4. A continuous function $f$ on $[1, 7]$ has critical points at $x = 3$ and $x = 5$. To find the absolute maximum of $f$, which values must you compare?

Correct! The Candidates Test requires evaluating $f$ at all critical points in the interval AND at both endpoints. The largest value is the absolute maximum.

5. The graph of $f'$ is increasing on $(a, b)$. What does this tell you about $f$?

Correct! If $f'$ is increasing, then $f'' > 0$, which means $f$ is concave up. The fact that $f'$ is increasing says nothing about the sign of $f'$ or $f$ itself.
