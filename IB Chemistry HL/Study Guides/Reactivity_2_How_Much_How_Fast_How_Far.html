How Much, How Fast, How Far?

Three diagnostic questions about any chemical reaction: how much material is produced (stoichiometry & yield), how fast it gets there (kinetics), and how far the reaction proceeds before reaching equilibrium (extent).

SL: 21 hrs · HL: 31 hrs 3 Sub-topics 2.2 & 2.3 have HL extensions
Reactivity 2.1

How Much? The Amount of Chemical Change

Guiding Question How are chemical equations used to calculate reacting ratios? A balanced equation is a recipe in molar units — once you know the ratio, you can find any mass, volume, or concentration from any other.

Balanced Equations & State Symbols

A chemical equation is balanced when every element has equal atom counts on both sides — a statement of conservation of mass. State symbols indicate phase: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous solution. Use them whenever you write an equation.

Example — Combustion of methane
$$\mathrm{CH_4(g) + 2\,O_2(g) \to CO_2(g) + 2\,H_2O(l)}$$
Atom check: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O on each side. ✓

The Mole Ratio

The coefficients of a balanced equation are the mole ratio of reactants and products. From the mole ratio you can compute reacting masses, volumes (for gases or solutions), and concentrations using three standard relationships:

Mole relationships (covered in Structure 1.4 & 1.5)
$$n = \dfrac{m}{M_r} \qquad n = c\,V \qquad n = \dfrac{V_{\text{gas}}}{V_m}$$
$M_r$ is the molar mass (g mol⁻¹). For ideal gases at STP, $V_m = 22.7~\mathrm{dm^3\,mol^{-1}}$. Concentrations in $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$.
Worked Example — Mass from Mole Ratio

What mass of CO₂ is produced when 8.0 g of CH₄ burns completely in oxygen?

Identify
Balanced equation: $\mathrm{CH_4 + 2O_2 \to CO_2 + 2H_2O}$. Mole ratio CH₄ : CO₂ = 1 : 1.
Set Up
$$n(\mathrm{CH_4}) = \dfrac{m}{M_r} = \dfrac{8.0}{16.05} = 0.498~\mathrm{mol}$$
Execute
From the 1:1 ratio: $n(\mathrm{CO_2}) = 0.498~\mathrm{mol}$.
$$m(\mathrm{CO_2}) = n M_r = 0.498 \times 44.01 = 21.9~\mathrm{g}$$
Evaluate
Roughly tripled mass (16 → 44 g mol⁻¹), consistent with the C atom picking up two extra O atoms.

Limiting Reactant & Theoretical Yield

When reactants aren't supplied in exact stoichiometric ratio, one runs out first: the limiting reactant. It caps how much product can form (the theoretical yield); the others are in excess.

How to Identify the Limiting Reactant Convert each reactant's mass (or volume) to moles, then divide each by its coefficient in the balanced equation. The reactant with the smallest ratio is the limiting reactant.

Percentage Yield

The experimental yield is what you actually obtain in a real reaction; it's almost always less than the theoretical yield due to incomplete reaction, side reactions, losses on transfer, and impure reactants.

Percentage Yield
$$\text{\% yield} = \dfrac{\text{experimental yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100\%$$
Always between 0 and 100% (any higher means impure product or weighing error).
Worked Example — Limiting Reactant & % Yield

25.0 g of N₂ is reacted with 6.00 g of H₂ to make NH₃ via the Haber process: $\mathrm{N_2 + 3H_2 \to 2NH_3}$. If 22.5 g of NH₃ is obtained, find (i) the limiting reactant and (ii) the percentage yield.

Step 1 — Moles available
$$n(\mathrm{N_2}) = \dfrac{25.0}{28.02} = 0.892~\mathrm{mol} \qquad n(\mathrm{H_2}) = \dfrac{6.00}{2.02} = 2.97~\mathrm{mol}$$
Step 2 — Limiting reactant
Divide by coefficients: N₂ → 0.892/1 = 0.892; H₂ → 2.97/3 = 0.990. N₂ has the smaller ratio, so N₂ is limiting.
Step 3 — Theoretical yield
$$n(\mathrm{NH_3}) = 2 \times 0.892 = 1.785~\mathrm{mol}$$
$$m_{\text{theor}}(\mathrm{NH_3}) = 1.785 \times 17.04 = 30.4~\mathrm{g}$$
Step 4 — % yield
$$\text{\% yield} = \dfrac{22.5}{30.4} \times 100 = 74.0\%$$

Atom Economy & Green Chemistry

The atom economy measures what fraction of the reactant mass ends up in the desired product (as opposed to wasted by-products). It's a different efficiency metric than % yield: a reaction with a high % yield can still have a low atom economy if it produces a lot of by-product per useful molecule.

Atom Economy (given in the data booklet)
$$\text{atom economy} = \dfrac{M_r(\text{desired product})}{\sum M_r(\text{all products})} \times 100\%$$
Higher = less wasted mass = greener. The ideal addition reaction (one product) has 100% atom economy.
Yield vs Atom Economy Both report "efficiency" but answer different questions. % yield: did the reaction actually run to completion? (kinetic / practical) Atom economy: is the reaction inherently wasteful by stoichiometry? (intrinsic / chemical) Industrial processes aim to maximise both.
Iron(III) oxide is reduced by carbon monoxide: $\mathrm{Fe_2O_3 + 3\,CO \to 2\,Fe + 3\,CO_2}$. If 32.0 g of $\mathrm{Fe_2O_3}$ ($M_r = 159.7$) reacts with excess CO, the maximum mass of iron ($M_r = 55.85$) that can be produced is closest to
$11.2~\mathrm{g}$

Use the 1:2 mole ratio. $n(\mathrm{Fe_2O_3}) = 32.0/159.7 = 0.200$ mol $\Rightarrow$ $n(\mathrm{Fe}) = 0.400$ mol $\Rightarrow$ $m = 22.3$ g. Answer: (C).
Two ways to make ethanol: (i) hydration of ethene $\mathrm{C_2H_4 + H_2O \to C_2H_5OH}$, and (ii) fermentation $\mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6 \to 2\,C_2H_5OH + 2\,CO_2}$. Which statement is correct?

Reactivity 2.2

How Fast? The Rate of Chemical Change

Guiding Question How can the rate of a reaction be controlled? Reaction rate is set by collision frequency, collision geometry, and the fraction of collisions with enough energy to react.

Defining Reaction Rate

The rate of a reaction is the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time. For a generic reaction $aA + bB \to cC + dD$:

Reaction Rate
$$\text{rate} = -\dfrac{1}{a}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -\dfrac{1}{b}\dfrac{d[B]}{dt} = +\dfrac{1}{c}\dfrac{d[C]}{dt} = +\dfrac{1}{d}\dfrac{d[D]}{dt}$$
Negative signs for reactants (their concentration falls); positive for products. Common units: $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}\,s^{-1}}$.

In practice you read the rate off a graph of concentration vs. time: the tangent at any point gives the instantaneous rate at that moment. Concentration is rarely measured directly — typical proxies include gas volume, mass loss (CO₂ released), pH, absorbance, conductivity, or titration of a quenched sample.

Collision Theory

For two species to react, their particles must collide with two prerequisites:

  1. Sufficient kinetic energy — at least the activation energy $E_a$.
  2. Correct orientation — the reactive parts of the molecules must meet (the "steric factor").

The kinetic energy of particles is determined by temperature: in Kelvin, $\langle KE \rangle \propto T$. Doubling the absolute temperature doubles the average kinetic energy — but, far more importantly, it shifts the tail of the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution past $E_a$, which is what really drives rate up.

Factors That Affect Rate

FactorEffect on rateMechanism
Concentration ↑ (or pressure ↑ for gases)↑More particles per volume → more collisions per second
Surface area ↑ (for solids)↑More exposed particles available to collide
Temperature ↑↑↑ (large)More particles exceed $E_a$ (shifts Maxwell–Boltzmann tail)
Catalyst added↑↑Lowers $E_a$ by providing an alternative pathway

Activation Energy & the Maxwell–Boltzmann Distribution

The activation energy $E_a$ is the minimum kinetic energy a colliding pair must have for the collision to be successful. A Maxwell–Boltzmann (MB) distribution plots the number of particles vs. kinetic energy at a given temperature:

Maxwell–Boltzmann at a Glance
  • The area to the right of $E_a$ = the fraction of particles with enough energy to react.
  • Raising $T$ flattens and broadens the distribution, shifting the peak right and dramatically increasing the high-energy tail.
  • A small increase in $T$ produces a large increase in the area beyond $E_a$ — this is why rate is very sensitive to temperature.

Catalysts & Energy Profiles

A catalyst increases the rate of a reaction by providing an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy. The catalyst is regenerated in a later step, so it isn't consumed. Energy profiles for catalysed vs uncatalysed reactions:

Key Points About Catalysts
  • Lower $E_a$ for both forward and reverse reactions equally (no shift in equilibrium — see Reactivity 2.3).
  • Don't change $\Delta H$ — the energy difference between reactants and products is fixed by the chemistry.
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts; they're typically extremely selective.
  • Homogeneous vs heterogeneous mechanisms are not assessed at IB.
Worked Example — Rate from a Concentration-Time Tangent

A reactant's concentration drops from 0.500 to 0.380 mol dm⁻³ over the first 30 s of a reaction. Estimate the initial rate (with respect to this reactant).

Set Up
Average rate over the interval (a coarse proxy for the initial rate):
$$\text{rate} = -\dfrac{\Delta[A]}{\Delta t} = -\dfrac{(0.380 - 0.500)}{30}~\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}\,s^{-1}}$$
Execute
$$\text{rate} = \dfrac{0.120}{30} = 4.0 \times 10^{-3}~\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}\,s^{-1}}$$
Evaluate
This is the average rate over the first 30 s; the true initial rate (the tangent to the curve at $t=0$) is typically a bit higher because rate falls as the reactant depletes.

Reaction Mechanisms & the Rate-Determining Step HL

Most reactions proceed through a series of elementary steps rather than a single concerted event. The overall rate is set by the rate-determining step (RDS) — the slowest step in the mechanism. Reaction intermediates are species formed in one step and consumed in a later step; transition states, by contrast, are unstable maxima on the energy profile and exist only fleetingly.

Intermediate vs Transition State An intermediate appears as a local minimum on the energy profile — it has a finite (if short) lifetime and a real chemical formula. A transition state is at the energy maximum between steps and cannot be isolated; partial bonds are forming or breaking.

A proposed mechanism must be consistent with both (a) the observed rate equation and (b) the stoichiometry of the overall reaction. The RDS is not necessarily the first step.

Molecularity & Rate Equations HL

The molecularity of an elementary step is the number of particles that react in that step:

  • Unimolecular — one particle (e.g. a decomposition).
  • Bimolecular — two particles (the most common elementary step).
  • Termolecular — three particles (rare; statistically unlikely).
General Rate Equation
$$\text{rate} = k\,[A]^m\,[B]^n$$
$m$ is the order with respect to $A$; $n$ is the order with respect to $B$. Orders must be determined experimentally — you cannot read them off the balanced equation.

The overall reaction order is the sum of the individual orders ($m + n$). At IB HL only integer orders (0, 1, 2) will be assessed.

The Rate Constant k HL

The rate constant $k$ is temperature-dependent (Arrhenius — see below) and has units determined by the overall order so that rate always comes out in $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}\,s^{-1}}$:

Worked Example — Rate Equation from Initial-Rates Data

For the reaction $\mathrm{A + 2B \to C}$, initial rates were measured at fixed temperature with different starting concentrations:

Run$[A]_0$ (mol dm⁻³)$[B]_0$ (mol dm⁻³)Initial rate (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹)
10.100.10$2.0 \times 10^{-4}$
20.200.10$4.0 \times 10^{-4}$
30.100.20$8.0 \times 10^{-4}$

Find the order with respect to A, the order with respect to B, the overall order, and $k$ with its units.

Order in A
Compare runs 1 and 2: [B] held constant, [A] doubled, rate doubled. Order in A = 1.
Order in B
Compare runs 1 and 3: [A] held constant, [B] doubled, rate quadrupled ($2^2$). Order in B = 2.
Rate equation & overall order
$$\text{rate} = k[A][B]^2 \qquad \text{overall order} = 1 + 2 = 3$$
Determine $k$
$$k = \dfrac{\text{rate}}{[A][B]^2} = \dfrac{2.0 \times 10^{-4}}{(0.10)(0.10)^2} = 0.20~\mathrm{dm^6\,mol^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$$

The Arrhenius Equation HL

The rate constant's temperature dependence is captured by the Arrhenius equation:

Arrhenius (data booklet)
$$k = A\,e^{-E_a / RT}$$
$A$ is the Arrhenius factor (collision frequency × steric factor); $R = 8.31~\mathrm{J\,K^{-1}\,mol^{-1}}$; $T$ in K. As $T \uparrow$, the exponent gets less negative, so $k$ rises sharply.

The linear form — obtained by taking $\ln$ of both sides — is what you actually use in problem-solving:

Linear Arrhenius Plot
$$\ln k = -\dfrac{E_a}{R}\cdot\dfrac{1}{T} + \ln A$$
Plot $\ln k$ vs $1/T$ to get a straight line with slope $-E_a/R$ and y-intercept $\ln A$.
Worked Example — Arrhenius Plot to Find E_a

A reaction has $k = 2.5 \times 10^{-3}~\mathrm{s^{-1}}$ at 300 K and $k = 4.0 \times 10^{-2}~\mathrm{s^{-1}}$ at 350 K. Estimate $E_a$.

Two-point form
$$\ln\!\left(\dfrac{k_2}{k_1}\right) = -\dfrac{E_a}{R}\left(\dfrac{1}{T_2} - \dfrac{1}{T_1}\right)$$
Plug In
$$\ln\!\left(\dfrac{4.0 \times 10^{-2}}{2.5 \times 10^{-3}}\right) = \ln 16 \approx 2.77$$
$$\dfrac{1}{350} - \dfrac{1}{300} = 2.857\times10^{-3} - 3.333\times10^{-3} = -4.76\times10^{-4}~\mathrm{K^{-1}}$$
Solve for $E_a$
$$E_a = -\dfrac{R \ln(k_2/k_1)}{1/T_2 - 1/T_1} = -\dfrac{(8.31)(2.77)}{-4.76\times10^{-4}} \approx 4.8\times10^{4}~\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}$$
Evaluate
$E_a \approx 48~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ — a typical magnitude for a moderately fast organic reaction.
A reaction's rate doubles when the temperature is raised from 300 K to 310 K. Which best explains this?
The average kinetic energy of particles doubles.
The fraction of particles with energy ≥ $E_a$ increases substantially.
Collision frequency between reactants doubles.
The activation energy decreases.
Correct! Raising $T$ from 300 to 310 K only increases the mean $KE$ by ~3% but shifts the Maxwell–Boltzmann tail beyond $E_a$ enough to roughly double the reactive fraction.
Mean $KE$ goes up by only $\sim T_2/T_1 = 1.03$ (a few %), so (A) and (C) overestimate. $E_a$ is a property of the reaction itself (unless a catalyst is added). The big change is the high-energy tail. Answer: (B).
(HL) The rate law for a reaction is rate $= k[A]^2[B]$. Which statement is correct?
The reaction is third order with respect to A.
Doubling [B] halves the rate.
Doubling [A] quadruples the rate; doubling [B] doubles the rate.
$k$ has units of $\mathrm{s^{-1}}$.

Read the exponents carefully: [A]² means order 2 in A, [B]¹ means order 1 in B. The overall third-order $k$ has units $\mathrm{dm^6\,mol^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$. Answer: (C).
Reactivity 2.3

How Far? The Extent of Chemical Change

Guiding Question How can the extent of a reversible reaction be influenced? The equilibrium constant tells you where the reaction settles; Le Châtelier's principle tells you how disturbances shift that settling point.

Dynamic Equilibrium

A reversible reaction in a closed system reaches dynamic equilibrium when the forward and reverse rates become equal. Macroscopic properties (concentrations, colour, pressure) stop changing, but the forward and reverse processes never stop — they merely cancel.

Hallmarks of a System at Equilibrium
  • Forward rate = reverse rate (continuous interchange).
  • Closed system (no leakage of reactants or products to the surroundings).
  • Constant macroscopic properties (concentration, pressure, colour).
  • Reached from either direction (the same $K$ is obtained starting from reactants or starting from products).

The Equilibrium Constant K

For a homogeneous reaction $aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD$ at a given temperature, the equilibrium law says:

Equilibrium Expression
$$K_c = \dfrac{[C]^c\,[D]^d}{[A]^a\,[B]^b}$$
Only equilibrium concentrations enter; coefficients become exponents. $K$ is temperature-dependent.

For the reverse reaction at the same temperature, $K_\text{rev} = 1/K_\text{fwd}$. If you multiply a reaction's coefficients by $n$, the new equilibrium constant is $K^n$.

What the Magnitude of K Tells You

$K$Extent of reaction at equilibrium
$K \gg 1$Products strongly favoured (reaction essentially "goes to completion").
$K > 1$Products favoured, but reactants still present.
$K = 1$Comparable amounts of reactants and products.
$K < 1$Reactants favoured.
$K \ll 1$Reactants strongly favoured (reaction barely proceeds).

Le Châtelier's Principle

If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, the equilibrium will shift to partially counteract the disturbance. This lets you predict qualitatively how the position changes without doing the full thermodynamics. Important: $K$ itself only changes with temperature; concentration and pressure shifts re-establish the same $K$.

DisturbanceDirection of shiftChanges $K$?
↑ [reactant]→ productsNo
↑ [product]← reactantsNo
↑ pressure (gas)Toward side with fewer moles of gasNo
↑ temperatureIn endothermic directionYes
Catalyst addedNo shiftNo
Why Catalysts Don't Shift Equilibrium A catalyst lowers $E_a$ for the forward and reverse reactions equally — so both rates rise by the same factor and the equilibrium position is unchanged. The system just gets there faster.
Worked Example — Le Châtelier Predictions

For the Haber process: $\mathrm{N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)}$, $\Delta H = -92~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$. Predict the direction of shift and the effect on the yield of NH₃ for each change.

(a) Increase pressure
4 mol of gas on the reactant side, 2 mol on the product side. Shift → toward products. Yield of NH₃ increases.
(b) Increase temperature
Forward reaction is exothermic ($\Delta H < 0$). The system absorbs the added heat by shifting in the endothermic direction → toward reactants. Yield of NH₃ decreases, and $K$ also decreases.
(c) Remove NH₃ as it forms
Lowering [NH₃] forces the system to make more. Shift → products. Total yield over time increases (this is how industrial Haber plants operate — continuous NH₃ removal by condensation).
(d) Add an iron catalyst
No shift in equilibrium position. Yield unchanged — but the time to reach equilibrium is much shorter, which is what makes the process economic.

The Reaction Quotient Q HL

The reaction quotient $Q$ has the same form as the equilibrium expression but uses any instantaneous concentrations — not necessarily equilibrium ones. Comparing $Q$ to $K$ tells you which way the reaction will go to reach equilibrium:

ComparisonStatusReaction will…
$Q < K$Too few productsProceed forward (→ products)
$Q = K$At equilibriumNo net change
$Q > K$Too many productsProceed backward (← reactants)

Quantitative Equilibrium Calculations (ICE) HL

To find equilibrium composition given initial concentrations and $K$, use an ICE table (Initial / Change / Equilibrium). When $K$ is very small, the approximation $[\text{reactant}]_\text{initial} \approx [\text{reactant}]_\text{eqm}$ avoids needing quadratics.

Worked Example — Small-K Equilibrium

For the dissociation $\mathrm{N_2O_4(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NO_2(g)}$, $K_c = 4.6 \times 10^{-3}$ at 298 K. If 0.20 mol of $\mathrm{N_2O_4}$ is placed in a 1.0 dm³ flask, find the equilibrium concentrations.

ICE table
$$ \begin{array}{c|ccc} & [\mathrm{N_2O_4}] & & [\mathrm{NO_2}] \\ \hline \text{I} & 0.20 & & 0 \\ \text{C} & -x & & +2x \\ \text{E} & 0.20 - x & & 2x \end{array} $$
Apply $K_c$
$$K_c = \dfrac{[\mathrm{NO_2}]^2}{[\mathrm{N_2O_4}]} = \dfrac{(2x)^2}{0.20 - x} = 4.6 \times 10^{-3}$$
Small-$K$ approximation
Since $K$ is small, $x \ll 0.20$ and $0.20 - x \approx 0.20$. Then:
$$\dfrac{4x^2}{0.20} = 4.6 \times 10^{-3} \;\Longrightarrow\; x^2 = 2.30 \times 10^{-4}$$
$$x = 0.0152~\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$$
Equilibrium
$$[\mathrm{N_2O_4}] \approx 0.185~\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}, \quad [\mathrm{NO_2}] = 2x = 0.030~\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$$
Sanity check: $x / 0.20 = 7.6\%$, comfortably below the usual 5–10% threshold for the small-$K$ approximation.

Linking K to Gibbs Energy HL

The equilibrium constant and the standard Gibbs energy change are two views of the same thing — both tell you where a reaction settles:

Gibbs Energy ↔ K (data booklet)
$$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -RT \ln K$$
$R = 8.31~\mathrm{J\,K^{-1}\,mol^{-1}}$. Sign of $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ matches $K$: $\Delta G^{\ominus} < 0 \Leftrightarrow K > 1$ (products favoured at equilibrium).
Sign Summary
  • $\Delta G^{\ominus} \ll 0 \Rightarrow K \gg 1$: products strongly favoured.
  • $\Delta G^{\ominus} = 0 \Rightarrow K = 1$: comparable amounts.
  • $\Delta G^{\ominus} \gg 0 \Rightarrow K \ll 1$: reactants strongly favoured.
Cross-link: Reactivity 1.4 derived $\Delta G^{\ominus} = \Delta H^{\ominus} - T\Delta S^{\ominus}$. Putting both equations together lets you predict $K$ at any temperature from tabulated thermochemical data.
Worked Example — Gibbs Energy from K

For the reaction $\mathrm{H_2(g) + I_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2HI(g)}$, $K_c = 50.0$ at 700 K. Calculate $\Delta G^{\ominus}$.

Apply
$$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -RT\ln K = -(8.31)(700)\ln(50.0)$$
Execute
$$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -(8.31)(700)(3.912) = -22{,}763~\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}$$
$$\Delta G^{\ominus} \approx -22.8~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$$
Evaluate
Negative $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ and $K > 1$ agree — products are favoured under standard conditions.
For the equilibrium $\mathrm{2SO_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3(g)}$ with $\Delta H < 0$, which change would increase both the yield of $\mathrm{SO_3}$ and the value of $K$?
Increase the temperature
Add a catalyst
Increase the pressure at constant temperature
Decrease the temperature
Correct! Forward reaction is exothermic, so lowering $T$ shifts toward products AND increases $K$. Pressure shifts the position but leaves $K$ unchanged; catalysts change neither.
Only temperature changes $K$. For an exothermic forward reaction, lowering $T$ shifts toward products and raises $K$. Answer: (D).
(HL) For a reaction with $K = 1$, what is $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ at any temperature?
$\Delta G^{\ominus} > 0$
$\Delta G^{\ominus} = 0$
$\Delta G^{\ominus} < 0$
$\Delta G^{\ominus}$ depends on temperature; cannot be determined

Preparation

Exam Strategy

Paper 1 (MC, no calc, no data booklet)

Reactivity 2 questions here lean on qualitative reasoning: identify the limiting reactant from mole ratios you can do in your head, predict the direction of a Le Châtelier shift, classify orders from a rate–[A] graph, or sketch a Maxwell–Boltzmann shift. Don't burn time on numerical kinetics in Paper 1 — there's no calculator.

Paper 2 (short + long, calculator + data booklet)

This is where the numerical Reactivity 2 work lives. Expect (a) a stoichiometry problem with limiting reactant + % yield (often combined with atom economy), (b) a kinetics question with initial-rates data or an Arrhenius plot, and (c) an equilibrium question — usually $K$ from concentration data, then a Le Châtelier prediction, sometimes plus a $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ calculation at HL.

Paper 3 (data-based, HL)

Reactivity 2 commonly drives Paper-3 data analysis: a clock-reaction or gas-collection experiment, where you plot, identify reaction order, and extract $k$ and $E_a$. Practice reading the slope of $\ln k$ vs $1/T$ and connecting it to $E_a = -R \cdot \text{slope}$.

Data Booklet — Useful Tables Section 1 (Avogadro, $V_m$), Section 2 (constants — $R$, $k_B$), Section 5 ($A_r$ to 2 d.p.), and the formula sheet (atom economy, $\Delta G^{\ominus} = -RT\ln K$, Arrhenius equation and its linear form). Bookmark these before the exam.
Watch Out

Common Mistakes

Forgetting to balance before mole-ratio work The mole ratio comes from the balanced equation. Always verify atom counts before extracting the ratio.
Confusing % yield with atom economy % yield is about practical efficiency (you didn't get back as much as you should have); atom economy is about intrinsic efficiency (the reaction by its stoichiometry produces wasteful by-products). A reaction can have 100% yield and only 30% atom economy.
Reading orders off the balanced equation Orders in the rate equation are only determined experimentally. The exponents in rate $= k[A]^m[B]^n$ are not the stoichiometric coefficients (except for elementary steps).
"Catalysts shift equilibrium" Never. A catalyst speeds up forward and reverse equally — same equilibrium position, same $K$. It only changes how fast you get there.
Treating $K$ as a constant across temperatures $K$ is "constant" in the sense that for a given temperature it has a fixed value — but it does change with $T$. Don't reuse a $K$ from one $T$ at a different $T$.
Forgetting to convert temperature to Kelvin Every kinetics and equilibrium equation that involves $T$ requires absolute temperature. $0~^\circ\mathrm{C} = 273.15~\mathrm{K}$ — not 273.
Skipping units of $k$ Examiners check whether you derived $k$'s units from the overall order. A second-order $k$ in $\mathrm{s^{-1}}$ is wrong even if the numerical value is right.

How does a catalyst increase rate?
Provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy. Doesn't affect $\Delta H$ or the equilibrium position.
(HL) Linear Arrhenius equation?
$\ln k = \ln A - \dfrac{E_a}{R}\cdot\dfrac{1}{T}$ — plot $\ln k$ vs $1/T$ to get slope $= -E_a/R$.
Le Châtelier — what changes $K$?
Only temperature. Pressure and concentration changes shift the position but $K$ stays the same.
$K$ expression for $aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD$?
$K_c = \dfrac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$ — equilibrium concentrations only, coefficients become exponents.
(HL) Q vs K interpretation?
$Q < K$: shift forward. $Q = K$: at equilibrium. $Q > K$: shift backward.
(HL) $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ ↔ $K$?
$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -RT \ln K$. Negative $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ ⇔ $K > 1$ (products favoured).
