Multiple Choice

Q1Unit IEmpirical Formula

40.0% C, 6.7% H, 53.3% O → empirical formula?

Answer: (B)  $\mathrm{CH_2O}$

Divide each percentage by the atomic mass to get a mole ratio (assume 100 g of compound):

$$\mathrm{C}:\dfrac{40.0}{12.0}=3.33 \quad \mathrm{H}:\dfrac{6.7}{1.0}=6.7 \quad \mathrm{O}:\dfrac{53.3}{16.0}=3.33$$

Divide through by the smallest (3.33): $\;\mathrm{C}:1,\;\mathrm{H}:2,\;\mathrm{O}:1\;\Rightarrow\;\mathbf{CH_2O}$.

Trap (C) $\mathrm{C_2H_4O_2}$ is a molecular formula with the same ratio — the question asks for the empirical (simplest) formula.

Q2Unit IGas Laws / Avogadro

Equal masses of H₂, He, CH₄, CO₂ at the same T, p — largest volume?

Answer: (A)  $\mathrm{H_2}$

At the same temperature and pressure, equal volumes contain equal moles (Avogadro), so volume $\propto$ moles. For a fixed mass, $n = m/M$, so the gas with the smallest molar mass has the most moles and the largest volume.

Molar masses: $\mathrm{H_2}=2$, $\mathrm{He}=4$, $\mathrm{CH_4}=16$, $\mathrm{CO_2}=44$. Hydrogen ($M=2$) wins.

Don't be lured by "$\mathrm{CO_2}$ is the biggest molecule" — bigger molar mass means fewer moles per gram, hence the smallest volume.

Q3Unit ILimiting Reagent

2.0 mol N₂ + 3.0 mol H₂; max NH₃?

Answer: (B)  2.0 mol

$\mathrm{N_2 + 3H_2 \to 2NH_3}$. Check which runs out: 2.0 mol $\mathrm{N_2}$ would need $3\times2.0 = 6.0$ mol $\mathrm{H_2}$, but only 3.0 mol is available — so $\mathrm{H_2}$ is limiting.

$$n(\mathrm{NH_3}) = 3.0~\mathrm{mol\,H_2}\times\dfrac{2~\mathrm{mol\,NH_3}}{3~\mathrm{mol\,H_2}} = 2.0~\mathrm{mol}$$

Using $\mathrm{N_2}$ (the excess reagent) gives the wrong answer 4.0 mol — always test the limiting reagent first.

Q4Unit IDilution

25.0 cm³ of 0.80 M diluted to 100.0 cm³ → concentration?

Answer: (A)  $0.20~\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$

Dilution conserves moles: $c_1V_1 = c_2V_2$.

$$c_2 = \dfrac{c_1V_1}{V_2} = \dfrac{(0.80)(25.0)}{100.0} = 0.20~\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$$

A fourfold increase in volume gives a fourfold decrease in concentration.

Q5Unit IIIsotopes

$^{10}\mathrm{B}$ / $^{11}\mathrm{B}$, $A_r = 10.81$. % of $^{11}\mathrm{B}$?

Answer: (C)  81%

Let the fraction of $^{11}\mathrm{B}$ be $x$:

$$10(1-x) + 11x = 10.81 \;\Rightarrow\; 10 + x = 10.81 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 0.81$$

So $^{11}\mathrm{B}$ is 81%. Sanity check: $A_r = 10.81$ sits much closer to 11 than 10, so the heavier isotope must dominate.

Q6Unit IIElectron Configuration

Ground-state configuration of $\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}$?

Answer: (A)  $[\mathrm{Ar}]\,3d^{9}$

Neutral copper is an anomaly: $\mathrm{Cu} = [\mathrm{Ar}]\,3d^{10}4s^{1}$ (a full $3d^{10}$ is extra-stable, so an electron is promoted from $4s$). When forming cations the $4s$ electrons leave first:

$$[\mathrm{Ar}]3d^{10}4s^1 \xrightarrow{-2e^-} [\mathrm{Ar}]3d^9$$

(B) $[\mathrm{Ar}]3d^7 4s^2$ wrongly removes $3d$ before $4s$ — the classic error.

Q7Unit IISuccessive IE

IEs $786, 1580, 3230, 4360, 16\,100$. Group?

Answer: (C)  Group 14

Find the large jump — it marks where you break into the stable inner (noble-gas) shell:

$$786,\;1580,\;3230,\;4360\;\big|\;16\,100\;(\times 3.7)$$

Four electrons come off relatively easily; the 5th jumps nearly fourfold. Four valence electrons ⇒ Group 14 (these are the ionisation energies of silicon).

Q8Unit IIPeriodic Trend

First IE trend across Period 3?

Answer: (B)  generally increases, with dips at Al and S

Rising nuclear charge with little extra shielding pulls electrons in, so IE rises overall — but with two dips:

  • Al $<$ Mg: Al's outer electron is in a higher-energy $3p$ orbital, easier to remove than Mg's stable $3s^2$.
  • S $<$ P: P has a stable half-filled $3p^3$; S ($3p^4$) has one doubly-occupied $3p$ orbital whose electron repulsion eases removal.

(D) names Mg and P, which are the local maxima, not the dips.

Q9Unit IIHLTransition Metals

Why is Zn not a typical transition metal?

Answer: (C)

A transition metal is defined (in the IB sense) as an element that forms at least one stable ion with a partially filled d sub-shell. Zinc's only common ion is $\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}$:

$$\mathrm{Zn}\;[\mathrm{Ar}]3d^{10}4s^2 \xrightarrow{-2e^-} \mathrm{Zn^{2+}}\;[\mathrm{Ar}]3d^{10}$$

$\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}$ has a full $3d^{10}$ sub-shell. With no partially filled d orbitals it cannot show variable oxidation states, and d–d electronic transitions are impossible, so its compounds are colourless. (Scandium is excluded for the opposite reason — $\mathrm{Sc^{3+}}$ is $3d^0$.)

Q10Unit IIIHLVSEPR

T-shaped $\mathrm{ClF_3}$: axial–equatorial $\mathrm{F\!-\!Cl\!-\!F}$ angle?

Answer: (B)  slightly less than $90^\circ$

Cl has 5 electron domains (3 bonding + 2 lone pairs) ⇒ trigonal bipyramidal electron geometry. The lone pairs take the roomy equatorial sites, giving a T-shaped molecule. Lone-pair repulsion is stronger than bond-pair repulsion, so the two equatorial lone pairs squeeze the axial Cl–F bonds inward — the angle drops from the ideal $90^\circ$ to slightly less (observed $\approx 87.5^\circ$).

Q11Unit IIIHLFormal Charge

Formal charge on the central O in $\mathrm{O_3}$?

Answer: (B)  $+1$

In an ozone resonance structure the central O has one single and one double bond (3 bonding pairs) and one lone pair:

$$\text{FC} = 6 - 2 - \tfrac{1}{2}(6) = +1$$

The singly-bonded terminal O is $-1$ and the doubly-bonded terminal O is $0$, summing to a neutral molecule.

Q12Unit IIIIntermolecular Forces

Increasing bp: butane, propanal, propan-1-ol.

Answer: (B)  butane $<$ propanal $<$ propan-1-ol

With near-equal molar masses, only the type of intermolecular force matters:

  • Butane — London dispersion only (weakest), bp $-1\,^\circ$C.
  • Propanal — polar C=O ⇒ dipole–dipole, bp $49\,^\circ$C.
  • Propan-1-ol — O–H ⇒ hydrogen bonding (strongest), bp $97\,^\circ$C.

Order: dispersion $<$ dipole–dipole $<$ hydrogen bonding.

Q13Unit IIIHLHybridization

Which has an sp-hybridized central atom?

Answer: (A)  $\mathrm{CO_2}$

Hybridization is set by the number of electron domains (σ-bonds + lone pairs) on the central atom:

  • $\mathrm{CO_2}$: 2 domains (two $\mathrm{C\!=\!O}$, no lone pairs on C) ⇒ sp, linear. ✓
  • $\mathrm{H_2O}$: 4 domains (2 bonds + 2 lp) ⇒ sp³.
  • $\mathrm{NH_3}$: 4 domains (3 bonds + 1 lp) ⇒ sp³.
  • $\mathrm{CH_4}$: 4 domains ⇒ sp³.

Only $\mathrm{CO_2}$ has two electron domains, hence sp. (A double bond still counts as a single domain.)

Q14Unit IVBond Enthalpy

$\mathrm{H_2 + Cl_2 \to 2HCl}$ from bond enthalpies?

Answer: (A)  $-184~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$

$\Delta H = \sum(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum(\text{bonds made})$:

$$\Delta H = (436 + 242) - 2(431) = 678 - 862 = -184~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$$

Two moles of H–Cl form — forgetting the factor of 2 gives $-247$ (option C).

Q15Unit IVHLEntropy

Most positive $\Delta S^{\circ}$?

Answer: (B)  $\mathrm{CaCO_3(s) \to CaO(s) + CO_2(g)}$

Entropy rises most when moles of gas increase. Only (B) creates gas from a solid ($0\to1$ mol gas) — strongly positive. (A) and (C) lose gas moles; (D) liquid→solid loses disorder. All others are negative.

Q16Unit IVHLGibbs Energy

$\Delta H^{\circ}=+178$ kJ mol⁻¹, $\Delta S^{\circ}=+161$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. Feasible above?

Answer: (C)  $\approx 1110~\mathrm{K}$

Feasibility means $\Delta G^{\circ} = \Delta H^{\circ} - T\Delta S^{\circ}\le 0$; the crossover is at $\Delta G^{\circ}=0$:

$$T = \dfrac{\Delta H^{\circ}}{\Delta S^{\circ}} = \dfrac{178\,000}{161} = 1106~\mathrm{K} \approx 1110~\mathrm{K}$$

Convert $\Delta H$ to joules first. Leaving it in kJ gives the absurd $T = 1.1~\mathrm{K}$.

Q17Unit VMaxwell–Boltzmann

MB curve at 350 K vs 300 K?

Answer: (B)

At higher $T$ the energies spread out: the peak shifts right and down, the curve broadens, but the total area is unchanged (same number of particles). Crucially the area beyond $E_a$ — the reactive fraction — grows substantially, which is why rate rises so steeply with temperature.

Q18Unit VHLUnits of $k$

rate $= k[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]^2$. Units of $k$?

Answer: (C)  $\mathrm{dm^6\,mol^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$

Overall order $= 1 + 2 = 3$. Rearranging for $k$:

$$k = \dfrac{\text{rate}}{[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]^2} = \dfrac{\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}\,s^{-1}}}{(\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}})^3} = \mathrm{dm^6\,mol^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$$

Memorise: 1st order $\mathrm{s^{-1}}$; 2nd order $\mathrm{dm^3\,mol^{-1}\,s^{-1}}$; 3rd order $\mathrm{dm^6\,mol^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$.

Q19Unit VHLArrhenius

$\ln k$ vs $1/T$, negative slope. The slope gives?

Answer: (C)  the activation energy $E_a$

The linear form of the Arrhenius equation is

$$\ln k = -\dfrac{E_a}{R}\cdot\dfrac{1}{T} + \ln A$$

So a plot of $\ln k$ against $1/T$ has gradient $-E_a/R$, giving $E_a = -R\times\text{slope}$. The $y$-intercept gives $\ln A$. ($E_a$ is a kinetic barrier, not the thermodynamic $\Delta H$.)

Q20Unit VCatalysts

Which property is not changed by a catalyst?

Answer: (C)  Enthalpy change $\Delta H$

A catalyst provides an alternative pathway (new mechanism) with lower activation energy, increasing the rate. But it leaves the reactant and product energy levels untouched, so $\Delta H$ — fixed by the difference between them — is unchanged.

(D) is the tempting wrong answer: a catalyst does change the mechanism (that is precisely how it works).

SR 1HARDUnit IBack-Titration · Gas Law

0.500 g impure CaCO₃ + 25.0 cm³ of 0.500 M HCl (excess); back-titrate excess with 0.250 M NaOH (18.0 cm³).

(a) Equations.
$$\mathrm{CaCO_3(s) + 2HCl(aq) \to CaCl_2(aq) + H_2O(l) + CO_2(g)}$$
$$\mathrm{HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) \to NaCl(aq) + H_2O(l)}$$
(b) Moles of acid.
$$n(\mathrm{HCl})_{\text{initial}} = 0.0250~\mathrm{dm^3}\times0.500 = 0.01250~\mathrm{mol}$$
$$n(\mathrm{HCl})_{\text{excess}} = n(\mathrm{NaOH}) = 0.0180\times0.250 = 0.004500~\mathrm{mol}$$
(c) Purity. Acid that reacted with the carbonate:
$$n(\mathrm{HCl})_{\text{reacted}} = 0.01250 - 0.004500 = 0.008000~\mathrm{mol}$$
The 1 : 2 ratio gives $n(\mathrm{CaCO_3}) = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.008000) = 0.004000~\mathrm{mol}$:
$$m(\mathrm{CaCO_3}) = 0.004000\times100.09 = 0.4004~\mathrm{g}$$
$$\text{purity} = \dfrac{0.4004}{0.500}\times100 = \mathbf{80.1\%}$$
(d) Volume of CO₂. $n(\mathrm{CO_2}) = n(\mathrm{CaCO_3}) = 0.004000~\mathrm{mol}$. Using $pV = nRT$ with $p = 100~\mathrm{kPa}$, $R = 8.31$, $T = 298~\mathrm{K}$ (and $1~\mathrm{kPa\,dm^3 = 1~J}$, so $V$ comes out in $\mathrm{dm^3}$):
$$V = \dfrac{nRT}{p} = \dfrac{(0.004000)(8.31)(298)}{100} = 0.0991~\mathrm{dm^3} = \mathbf{99.1~\mathrm{cm^3}}$$

SR 3HARDUnit IIHLAtomic Structure · Periodicity

Atomic structure and Period-3 trends.

(a) Definition + equation. First ionisation energy is the energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms in their ground state (forming gaseous 1+ ions). The 2nd IE of magnesium:
$$\mathrm{Mg^+(g) \to Mg^{2+}(g) + e^-}$$
(b) Chromium configurations.
$$\mathrm{Cr}: 1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^6\,3s^2\,3p^6\,3d^5\,4s^1 \qquad \mathrm{Cr^{3+}}: [\mathrm{Ar}]\,3d^3$$
The atom is an exception because a half-filled $3d^5$ set (one electron in each d orbital) together with a half-filled $4s^1$ is more stable than the expected $3d^4\,4s^2$, so one $4s$ electron moves into $3d$. (For the ion, the $4s$ electron and two $3d$ electrons are removed.)
(c) Trends.
  • Atomic radius decreases across Period 3: nuclear charge increases while electrons enter the same principal shell, so shielding is roughly constant; the greater effective nuclear charge pulls the outer shell in.
  • IE(S) $<$ IE(P): phosphorus has a stable half-filled $3p^3$ configuration. Sulfur's $3p^4$ has two electrons paired in one $3p$ orbital, and the inter-electron repulsion in that orbital makes the fourth $3p$ electron easier to remove — so the IE dips at S.
(d) Period-3 oxides.
$$\mathrm{Na_2O(s) + H_2O(l) \to 2NaOH(aq)} \qquad \mathrm{SO_3(g) + H_2O(l) \to H_2SO_4(aq)}$$
Across the period the oxides change from basic (the metal oxides $\mathrm{Na_2O}$, $\mathrm{MgO}$) through amphoteric ($\mathrm{Al_2O_3}$) to acidic (the non-metal oxides $\mathrm{P_4O_{10}}$, $\mathrm{SO_3}$). The trend follows the metallic → non-metallic character of the element: metals form ionic, basic oxides; non-metals form covalent, acidic oxides.
(e) HL Zinc does not show variable oxidation states or coloured compounds (its $\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}$ is $3d^{10}$). Chromium in $\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}$: $\;2x + 7(-2) = -2 \Rightarrow x = +6$.
SR 4HARDUnit IIIHLBonding · Shape · IMF

Bonding and structure.

(a) SO₂. Lewis structure: S central, one $\mathrm{S\!=\!O}$ double bond and one $\mathrm{S\!-\!O}$ (with the negative end), plus one lone pair on S. Central S has 3 electron domains (2 bonding + 1 lone pair) ⇒ bent / V-shaped, $\mathrm{O\!-\!S\!-\!O}\approx \mathbf{119^\circ}$ (just under 120° because the lone pair repels slightly more). Resonance: the double bond can be drawn to either oxygen, so the true structure is an average of two equivalent contributors — the two S–O bonds are identical, of bond order 1.5, not one single and one double.
(b) Hybridization.
  • $\mathrm{SO_2}$: 3 domains ⇒ sp².
  • $\mathrm{CO_2}$: 2 domains ⇒ sp.
  • $\mathrm{CH_4}$: 4 domains ⇒ sp³.
The number of hybrid orbitals equals the number of electron domains (σ-bonds + lone pairs) on the central atom.
(c) Polarity. $\mathrm{CO_2}$ is linear and symmetric, so its two equal $\mathrm{C\!=\!O}$ bond dipoles point in exactly opposite directions and cancel ⇒ net dipole zero ⇒ non-polar. $\mathrm{SO_2}$ is bent, so its two S–O bond dipoles do not cancel; they add to a net dipole ⇒ polar.
(d) Graphite vs diamond.
  • Graphite: each C is sp² and bonds to 3 others in layers; the 4th (delocalised) electron per atom moves freely along the layers ⇒ conducts electricity. Only weak London forces act between layers, so they slide over one another ⇒ soft / slippery.
  • Diamond: each C is sp³ and forms 4 strong covalent bonds in a rigid 3-D tetrahedral network; there are no delocalised electrons ⇒ does not conduct, and the continuous covalent lattice makes it extremely hard.
(e) Bonding triangle. $\mathrm{MgO}$ (large electronegativity difference, metal + non-metal) → predominantly ionic; $\mathrm{ICl}$ (small difference between two non-metals) → polar covalent; $\mathrm{Cl_2}$ (zero difference) → pure (non-polar) covalent.
SR 5HARDUnit IVHLCalorimetry · Hess · Spontaneity

Neutralisation calorimetry; combustion of methane; spontaneity.

(a) Enthalpy of neutralisation. Total mass of solution $= 100.0 + 100.0 = 200.0~\mathrm{g}$; $\Delta T = 26.8 - 20.0 = 6.8~\mathrm{K}$.
$$q = mc\Delta T = (200.0)(4.18)(6.8) = 5685~\mathrm{J} = 5.685~\mathrm{kJ}$$
Moles of water formed $= n(\mathrm{HCl}) = 0.1000\times1.00 = 0.100~\mathrm{mol}$ (1 : 1, both limiting):
$$\Delta H_{\text{neut}} = -\dfrac{q}{n} = -\dfrac{5.685}{0.100} = \mathbf{-56.8~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}}$$
(Exothermic, so negative — close to the textbook $-57~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$.)
(b) Enthalpy of combustion (Hess via formation enthalpies).
$$\Delta H_c^{\circ} = \sum \Delta H_f^{\circ}(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta H_f^{\circ}(\text{reactants})$$
$$= \big[(-393.5) + 2(-285.8)\big] - \big[(-74.6) + 2(0)\big]$$
$$= (-965.1) - (-74.6) = \mathbf{-890.5~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}}$$
(c) Why the bond-enthalpy value differs.
  • Bond enthalpies are average values taken across many different molecules, not exact for the specific bonds in $\mathrm{CH_4}$, $\mathrm{CO_2}$ and water.
  • Bond-enthalpy calculations assume all species are gaseous; here the water is formed as a liquid, and the extra energy released on condensation is not accounted for — making the formation-enthalpy value more exothermic.
(d) HL Spontaneity. With $\Delta S^{\circ} = -243~\mathrm{J\,K^{-1}\,mol^{-1}} = -0.243~\mathrm{kJ\,K^{-1}\,mol^{-1}}$:
$$\Delta G^{\circ} = \Delta H^{\circ} - T\Delta S^{\circ} = -890.5 - (298)(-0.243) = -890.5 + 72.4 = \mathbf{-818~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}}$$
$\Delta G^{\circ} < 0$, so the reaction is spontaneous at 298 K. $\Delta S^{\circ}$ is negative because the gas moles fall (3 mol gas → 1 mol gas) and two moles of liquid water are formed, both reducing disorder; nevertheless the very exothermic $\Delta H$ dominates, keeping $\Delta G$ negative.
SR 6HARDUnit VHLRate Equation · Mechanism · Arrhenius

$\mathrm{A + B \to products}$; initial-rates data at 300 K.

(a) Orders, rate equation, $k$.
  • Runs 1→2: $[\mathrm{A}]$ doubles, $[\mathrm{B}]$ fixed; rate ×4 $= 2^2$ ⇒ order in A = 2.
  • Runs 1→3: $[\mathrm{B}]$ doubles, $[\mathrm{A}]$ fixed; rate unchanged ⇒ order in B = 0.
Rate equation: $\text{rate} = k[\mathrm{A}]^2$. From run 1:
$$k = \dfrac{\text{rate}}{[\mathrm{A}]^2} = \dfrac{2.0\times10^{-3}}{(0.10)^2} = 0.20~\mathrm{dm^3\,mol^{-1}\,s^{-1}}$$
(b) Mechanism consistency. The slow step (Step 1, $\mathrm{A + A \to C}$) is rate-determining, so the rate law comes from it: $\text{rate} = k[\mathrm{A}]^2$ — exactly the experimental result. B appears only in the fast Step 2, which occurs after the rate-determining step; species that enter only after the RDS do not affect the overall rate, so the reaction is zero-order in B even though B is a reactant.
(c) Activation energy. $k_2/k_1 = 2$, $T_1 = 300$, $T_2 = 310$:
$$\dfrac{1}{T_2} - \dfrac{1}{T_1} = \dfrac{1}{310} - \dfrac{1}{300} = -1.075\times10^{-4}~\mathrm{K^{-1}}$$
$$E_a = -\dfrac{R\,\ln(k_2/k_1)}{1/T_2 - 1/T_1} = -\dfrac{(8.31)(0.693)}{-1.075\times10^{-4}} = 5.36\times10^{4}~\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}} \approx \mathbf{53.6~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}}$$

C1HARDUnit IVHLBorn–Haber

Lattice enthalpy of NaCl from a Born–Haber cycle.

(a) Definition. The lattice enthalpy of NaCl is the enthalpy change when one mole of solid NaCl is separated into its gaseous ions (an endothermic, bond-breaking process):
$$\mathrm{NaCl(s) \to Na^+(g) + Cl^-(g)} \qquad \Delta H^{\circ}_{\text{lat}} > 0$$
(b) Hess cycle.
$$\Delta H_f^{\circ} = \Delta H_{\text{atom}}(\mathrm{Na}) + \mathrm{IE_1}(\mathrm{Na}) + \tfrac{1}{2}E(\mathrm{Cl\!-\!Cl}) + \Delta H_{\text{ea}}(\mathrm{Cl}) - \Delta H_{\text{lat}}$$
$$-411 = 107 + 496 + 121 - 349 - \Delta H_{\text{lat}} = 375 - \Delta H_{\text{lat}}$$
$$\Delta H_{\text{lat}} = 375 + 411 = \mathbf{+786~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}}$$

Take half the Cl–Cl bond enthalpy — only one Cl atom is needed per formula unit.

(c) Comparison. The ionic-model value ($+770$) is very close to the experimental ($+786$), within $\sim 2\%$. The close agreement shows NaCl is almost perfectly ionic, with only a very small degree of covalent character.
(d) MgO ≫ NaCl. $\Delta H_{\text{lat}} \propto \dfrac{|z^+||z^-|}{r^+ + r^-}$.
  • Charges: MgO has $2+$/$2-$ ions (charge product 4) vs $1+$/$1-$ for NaCl (product 1) — roughly a fourfold stronger attraction (the dominant factor).
  • Radii: $\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$ and $\mathrm{O^{2-}}$ are smaller than $\mathrm{Na^+}$/$\mathrm{Cl^-}$, so the ions sit closer, strengthening attraction further.
Larger charges over shorter distances ⇒ much greater lattice enthalpy.
C2STRETCHUnit II + IVHLIE ↔ Formula

Why MgCl₂ forms but not MgCl or MgCl₃.

(a) IE pattern. The 1st and 2nd electrons both come from the outer $3s$ sub-shell; the 2nd is only modestly harder because it leaves a more positive $\mathrm{Mg^+}$ ion (greater attraction), hence roughly double. The 3rd electron must be removed from the filled, low-energy $2p$ inner shell of the $\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$ (neon) core — much closer to the nucleus and far more strongly held — so IE₃ leaps to more than five times IE₂.
(b) Disproportionation of MgCl.
$$\mathrm{2\,MgCl(s) \to Mg(s) + MgCl_2(s)} \qquad \Delta H < 0$$
Although $\mathrm{MgCl}$ has a favourable enthalpy of formation, $\mathrm{MgCl_2}$ is far more stable: its lattice enthalpy is much greater because it contains the doubly-charged $\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$. The extra lattice energy released on forming $\mathrm{MgCl_2}$ more than repays the 2nd ionisation energy, so converting $\mathrm{MgCl}$ into $\mathrm{Mg} + \mathrm{MgCl_2}$ is energetically downhill — $\mathrm{MgCl}$ disproportionates and cannot be isolated.
(c) Why no MgCl₃. Forming $\mathrm{Mg^{3+}}$ requires IE₃ $= +7733~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ on top of IE₁ + IE₂. The only large compensating exothermic term is the lattice enthalpy. Even though $\mathrm{MgCl_3}$ (with $\mathrm{Mg^{3+}}$ and an extra $\mathrm{Cl^-}$) would have a larger lattice enthalpy than $\mathrm{MgCl_2}$, that increase — at most one to two thousand $\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ — comes nowhere near recovering the $7733~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ cost of removing a core electron. So $\Delta H_f(\mathrm{MgCl_3})$ would be strongly positive (endothermic) and it does not form. $\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$ is the highest charge reachable before breaking into the noble-gas core, so $\mathrm{MgCl_2}$ is the optimum.
(d) Principle & aluminium. An ionic compound forms only when the lattice enthalpy released offsets the ionisation energy invested in the cation; ionisation stops at the noble-gas core because removing a core electron costs more than any lattice gain can repay.

For aluminium, IE₁–IE₃ ($577, 1820, 2740$) remove the three $3s^23p^1$ valence electrons at moderate cost, but IE₄ ($11\,600$) breaks into the neon core. So aluminium stops at $\mathrm{Al^{3+}}$ ⇒ the chloride is $\mathrm{AlCl_3}$.

C3HARDUnit VHLKinetics · Mechanism · Arrhenius

$\mathrm{2NO + 2H_2 \to N_2 + 2H_2O}$; initial-rate data at 600 K.

(a) Orders.
  • Runs 1→2: $[\mathrm{NO}]$ doubles, rate ×4 $= 2^2$ ⇒ order in NO = 2.
  • Runs 2→3: $[\mathrm{H_2}]$ doubles, rate ×2 ⇒ order in H₂ = 1.
Overall order $= 2 + 1 = \mathbf{3}$.
(b) Rate equation and $k$. $\text{rate} = k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{H_2}]$. From run 1:
$$k = \dfrac{1.0\times10^{-3}}{(0.10)^2(0.10)} = \dfrac{1.0\times10^{-3}}{1.0\times10^{-3}} = 1.0~\mathrm{dm^6\,mol^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$$
(c) Mechanism check. Step 2 is slow ⇒ rate-determining: $\text{rate} = k_2[\mathrm{N_2O_2}][\mathrm{H_2}]$. The intermediate $\mathrm{N_2O_2}$ is removed using the fast Step-1 equilibrium, $K_1 = \dfrac{[\mathrm{N_2O_2}]}{[\mathrm{NO}]^2}$ so $[\mathrm{N_2O_2}] = K_1[\mathrm{NO}]^2$:
$$\text{rate} = k_2K_1[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{H_2}] = k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{H_2}]$$
This matches the experimental rate equation, so the mechanism is consistent.
  • Rate-determining step: Step 2.
  • Intermediates: $\mathrm{N_2O_2}$ (made in 1, used in 2) and $\mathrm{N_2O}$ (made in 2, used in 3) — neither appears in the overall equation.
(d) Activation energy. $k_2/k_1 = 10$, $T_1 = 600$, $T_2 = 650$:
$$\dfrac{1}{650} - \dfrac{1}{600} = -1.282\times10^{-4}~\mathrm{K^{-1}}$$
$$E_a = -\dfrac{(8.31)(\ln 10)}{-1.282\times10^{-4}} = \dfrac{(8.31)(2.303)}{1.282\times10^{-4}} = 1.49\times10^{5}~\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}} \approx \mathbf{149~\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}}$$
