PART I  ·  SHORT RESPONSESAT-style MCQ + ON/AB short answer · 18 marks

Section A · Short Response

Mix of multiple-choice and short-answer items. For MCQs, circle the letter; show enough work in the margin that a marker could verify. For short-answer items, write probabilities as exact fractions or to four decimal places when irrational. No calculator on Q1–Q3; calculator permitted on Q4–Q5.

Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Sample Spaces · HSS-CP.A.1 [3 marks]

Two fair six-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability that the sum of the two dice equals $8$?

  1. (A) $\dfrac{4}{36} = \dfrac{1}{9}$
  2. (B) $\dfrac{5}{36}$
  3. (C) $\dfrac{6}{36} = \dfrac{1}{6}$
  4. (D) $\dfrac{8}{36} = \dfrac{2}{9}$
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Complement Rule · HSS-CP.A.1 [3 marks]

A fair coin is flipped $5$ times. What is the probability of obtaining at least one tail?

  1. (A) $\dfrac{1}{32}$
  2. (B) $\dfrac{5}{32}$
  3. (C) $\dfrac{1}{2}$
  4. (D) $\dfrac{31}{32}$
Q3MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §3 Independence Test · HSS-CP.A.2 [4 marks]

Two events $A$ and $B$ have $P(A) = 0.6$, $P(B) = 0.5$, and $P(A \cup B) = 0.8$. Which of the following statements is true?

  1. (A) $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive.
  2. (B) $A$ and $B$ are independent because $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$.
  3. (C) $A$ and $B$ are dependent because $P(A \cap B) \ne P(A)P(B)$.
  4. (D) The given information is insufficient to decide.
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON MDM4U-style §2 Conditional Probability · HSS-CP.B.6 [4 marks]

(a) Find $P($ first red and second red $)$. State the conditional probability used. [2]
(b) Find $P($ both same colour $)$. [2]
Q5MEDIUM 🇨🇦 AB AB 30-2-style §6 z-Scores · Math 30-2 Stats GO 1.6 [4 marks]

A standardised test has scores modelled by a normal distribution with mean $\mu = 500$ and standard deviation $\sigma = 80$, i.e. $X \sim N(500, 80^{2})$.

(a) Compute the $z$-score of a raw score of $620$. [2]
(b) Using the $68$-$95$-$99.7$ rule, estimate $P(X > 660)$. [2]

Q6HARDHonors for US A2 / AB 30-2 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Bayes' Rule · HSS-CP.A.3 / .B.6 [8 marks]

A rare allergy affects $2\%$ of a population, so $P(D) = 0.02$. A screening test for the allergy has sensitivity $P(+ \mid D) = 0.95$ and false-positive rate $P(+ \mid D^{c}) = 0.04$.

(a) Identify the prior probability $P(D)$ and the two likelihoods $P(+ \mid D)$ and $P(+ \mid D^{c})$ explicitly. [1]
(b) Use the law of total probability to compute $P(+)$. [2]
(c) Apply Bayes' rule to compute the posterior probability $P(D \mid +)$. Round to four decimal places. [3]
(d) Interpret the result in context. Comment on the base-rate fallacy: why does a positive test on a $95\%$-sensitive screen still yield a posterior under $50\%$? [2]
Q7HARDHonors for US A2 / AB 30-2 🇨🇦 ON ON MDM4U-style §4 Discrete RV: $E(X)$, $\mathrm{Var}(X)$ · HSS-MD.A.2–3 [9 marks]

A discrete random variable $X$ has the probability distribution given below.

$x$$0$$1$$2$$3$$4$
$P(X = x)$$0.10$$0.25$$0.30$$0.25$$0.10$
(a) Verify that the distribution is valid: each $P(X = x) \ge 0$ and $\sum P(X = x) = 1$. [1]
(b) Compute the expected value $E(X) = \sum x P(X = x)$. [2]
(c) Compute $E(X^{2}) = \sum x^{2} P(X = x)$. [2]
(d) Use the computational formula $\mathrm{Var}(X) = E(X^{2}) - [E(X)]^{2}$ to compute the variance and the standard deviation $\sigma_{X} = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)}$. [3]
(e) Interpret $E(X)$ and $\sigma_{X}$ in one sentence each (long-run-average vs. typical-deviation). [1]
Q8MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 AB AP-feeder FRQ §5 Binomial Distribution · HSS-MD.A.3 / Math 30-1 Binomial [9 marks]

A free-throw shooter makes each shot independently with probability $p = 0.8$. Let $X$ count the number of made shots in $n = 10$ attempts, so $X \sim B(10, 0.8)$.

(a) State the binomial PMF $P(X = k) = \dbinom{n}{k} p^{k} (1-p)^{n-k}$ with $n$ and $p$ substituted. [1]
(b) Compute $P(X = 8)$ exactly, then to four decimals. [2]
(c) Compute $P(X \ge 9) = P(X = 9) + P(X = 10)$ to four decimals. [2]
(d) State $E(X) = np$ and $\mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$ for this $X$, and compute the standard deviation $\sigma_{X}$. [2]
(e) State the four binomial conditions (BINS: Binary outcomes, Independent trials, fixed Number of trials, constant Success probability) and verify each holds for this shooter. [2]
Q9MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Two-Way Tables · HSS-CP.B.6 [9 marks]

A school surveys $200$ students about whether they own a bicycle and whether they walk to school. The results are tabulated below.

Walks to schoolDoes not walkTotal
Owns a bike$45$$75$$120$
No bike$15$$65$$80$
Total$60$$140$$200$
(a) Find $P($ owns a bike $)$ and $P($ walks $)$. [2]
(b) Find $P($ owns a bike $\cap$ walks $)$. [1]
(c) Find $P($ walks $\mid$ owns a bike $)$ and $P($ owns a bike $\mid$ walks $)$. Show the row-restriction (or column-restriction) interpretation explicitly. [3]
(d) Are the events "owns a bike" and "walks to school" independent? Use the test $P(A \cap B) \stackrel{?}{=} P(A)P(B)$ and justify your conclusion numerically. [3]

Q10MEDIUM 🇨🇦 AB AB 30-2-style §6 Normal Distribution · Math 30-2 Stats GO 1 [12 marks]

The heights of adult women in a population are modelled by a normal distribution $X \sim N(165, 7^{2})$ cm, so $\mu = 165$ cm and $\sigma = 7$ cm.

(a) State the three intervals on either side of $\mu$ corresponding to the $68$-$95$-$99.7$ rule. [2]
(b) Use the $68$-$95$-$99.7$ rule to estimate $P(158 \le X \le 172)$ and $P(151 \le X \le 179)$. [2]
(c) Compute the $z$-score of a height of $176$ cm and the $z$-score of a height of $150$ cm. [2]
(d) Using the $z$-table, find $P(X > 176)$ and $P(X < 150)$ to four decimals. [3]
(e) Find $P(150 \le X \le 176)$ to four decimals, and interpret the result in context. [3]
Q11MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON MDM4U-style §7 Descriptive Statistics · HSS-ID.A.2–3 [12 marks]

A teacher records the quiz scores (out of $10$) of $11$ students on a recent quiz: $$ 4, \;6, \;7, \;7, \;8, \;8, \;8, \;9, \;9, \;9, \;10. $$

(a) Compute the mean $\bar{x}$, the median, and the mode. [3]
(b) Compute the range, and the quartiles $Q_{1}$ and $Q_{3}$. State the IQR $= Q_{3} - Q_{1}$. [3]
(c) Compute the sample standard deviation $s = \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{n-1} \sum (x_{i} - \bar{x})^{2}}$ to four decimal places. [3]
(d) Use the $1.5 \cdot \mathrm{IQR}$ rule to identify any outliers: a data point $x$ is an outlier if $x < Q_{1} - 1.5 \cdot \mathrm{IQR}$ or $x > Q_{3} + 1.5 \cdot \mathrm{IQR}$. State the fences and list any outliers. [2]
(e) Compare mean and median: explain in one sentence whether the distribution is roughly symmetric, left-skewed, or right-skewed, citing the relative positions of $\bar{x}$ and the median. [1]
Q12HARDHonors for US A2 / AB 30-2 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 AB AP-feeder FRQ §5–§6 Binomial + Normal · HSS-MD.A.3 / HSS-IC.A [13 marks]

A factory produces light bulbs. Historically, $5\%$ of bulbs are defective; assume defects across bulbs are independent. A quality-control inspector samples $n = 20$ bulbs from a production run. Let $X$ count defective bulbs in the sample.

(a) State the distribution of $X$ in the form $X \sim B(n, p)$. [1]
(b) Compute $P(X = 0)$ (no defects) and $P(X = 1)$ to four decimals. [2]
(c) Compute $P(X \ge 2)$ using the complement: $P(X \ge 2) = 1 - P(X = 0) - P(X = 1)$. Give your answer to four decimals. [3]
(d) Compute $E(X) = np$ and $\mathrm{SD}(X) = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$ for this sample. [2]
(e) The inspector now examines a larger run of $n = 400$ bulbs. Let $Y$ be the number of defective bulbs in the larger run, $Y \sim B(400, 0.05)$. State $E(Y)$ and $\mathrm{SD}(Y)$, and use a normal approximation $Y \approx N(\mu_{Y}, \sigma_{Y}^{2})$ together with the $68$-$95$-$99.7$ rule to estimate $P(11 \le Y \le 29)$ (use the rule of thumb that this interval is approximately $\mu_{Y} \pm 2 \sigma_{Y}$). [3]
(f) Comment in one sentence on when the normal approximation $Y \approx N(np, np(1-p))$ is appropriate (cite the standard rule of thumb $np \ge 10$ and $n(1-p) \ge 10$). [2]
