PART I  ·  SHORT RESPONSESAT-style MCQ + ON / AB short answer · 18 marks

Section A · Short Response

Mix of multiple-choice and short-answer items. For MCQs, circle the letter; show enough work in the margin that a marker could verify. State the standard form $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$ or its conic analogue explicitly before reading off centre / radius / foci. No calculator on Q1–Q4; calculator permitted on Q5.

Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Circle Standard Form · HSG-GPE.A.1 [3 marks]

In the $xy$-plane, the equation $(x - 2)^{2} + (y + 5)^{2} = 49$ describes a circle. What are the centre and radius of this circle?

  1. (A) Centre $(-2, 5)$, radius $7$
  2. (B) Centre $(2, -5)$, radius $7$
  3. (C) Centre $(2, -5)$, radius $49$
  4. (D) Centre $(-2, 5)$, radius $49$
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §2 Parabola Focus-Directrix · HSG-GPE.A.2 [3 marks]

A parabola in the $xy$-plane has focus $(0, 3)$ and directrix $y = -3$. Which of the following is its equation?

  1. (A) $y = \dfrac{1}{6}\, x^{2}$
  2. (B) $y = \dfrac{1}{12}\, x^{2}$
  3. (C) $y = 6 x^{2}$
  4. (D) $y = 12 x^{2}$

An ellipse centred at the origin has equation $\dfrac{x^{2}}{25} + \dfrac{y^{2}}{9} = 1$. What is its eccentricity $e$?

  1. (A) $e = \dfrac{3}{5}$
  2. (B) $e = \dfrac{4}{5}$
  3. (C) $e = \dfrac{5}{3}$

The points $A(-1, 2)$ and $B(7, 8)$ are the endpoints of a diameter of a circle in the $xy$-plane.

(a) Determine the centre of the circle (midpoint of $AB$). [1]

Q5MEDIUM 🇨🇦 AB AB Provincial-style §2 Parabola, Vertex Form · AB Math 20-1 RF GO 3 [5 marks]

A parabola has vertex $(2, -3)$ and opens upward with $a = \dfrac{1}{4}$ in the form $y = a(x - h)^{2} + k$. Use the focus-directrix relation $4p = \dfrac{1}{a}$ (with focus $(h, k + p)$ and directrix $y = k - p$) to answer the following.

(a) Write the equation of the parabola in vertex form. [1]

PART II  ·  EXTENDED RESPONSEAP-feeder FRQ + honors · 35 marks

Section B · Extended Response

Show every algebraic step. State the standard form of the conic (circle, ellipse, hyperbola, or parabola) before reading off centre, vertices, foci, or asymptotes. Use $a^{2}$, $b^{2}$, $c^{2}$ consistently and cite the focal relation ($c^{2} = a^{2} - b^{2}$ for ellipses, $c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2}$ for hyperbolas) explicitly. No calculator on Q6–Q9 unless noted.

Q6MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §3 Ellipse Standard Form · HSG-GPE.A.3 (+) [8 marks]

An ellipse centred at the origin has its major axis along the $x$-axis, semi-major axis length $a = 5$, and semi-minor axis length $b = 4$.

(a) Write the equation of the ellipse in standard form $\dfrac{x^{2}}{a^{2}} + \dfrac{y^{2}}{b^{2}} = 1$. [1]
(b) State the coordinates of the four vertices (endpoints of the major and minor axes). [2]
(c) Use the focal relation $c^{2} = a^{2} - b^{2}$ to find $c$, then state the coordinates of the two foci. [3]
(d) Compute the eccentricity $e = c/a$. Briefly classify the ellipse as "nearly circular" or "elongated" based on $e$. [2]
Q7HARDHonors 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §4 Hyperbola, Foci & Asymptotes · HSG-GPE.A.3 (+) [9 marks]

Consider the hyperbola $\dfrac{x^{2}}{9} - \dfrac{y^{2}}{16} = 1$. Note: hyperbolas are not in the standard BC PC 11 / PC 12 syllabus; this is an honors-extension question for students continuing to first-year university analytic geometry.

(a) State $a$, $b$, and the orientation of the transverse axis (horizontal or vertical). [2]
(b) State the coordinates of the two vertices (endpoints of the transverse axis). [1]
(c) Use the hyperbola focal relation $c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2}$ to find $c$, then state the coordinates of the two foci. [3]
(d) State the equations of the two asymptotes (lines $y = \pm (b/a) x$ for a horizontal transverse axis). [2]
(e) Briefly explain in one sentence why the focal-relation sign differs between ellipse ($c^{2} = a^{2} - b^{2}$) and hyperbola ($c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2}$). [1]
Q8HARDHonors 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §5 Conic Discriminant $B^{2} - 4AC$ · HSG-GPE.A.3 (+) [9 marks]

Recall the general second-degree equation in two variables $Ax^{2} + Bxy + Cy^{2} + Dx + Ey + F = 0$. The conic discriminant $\Delta_{\text{conic}} = B^{2} - 4AC$ classifies the (non-degenerate) conic: $< 0 \Rightarrow$ ellipse (or circle if $A = C$ and $B = 0$); $= 0 \Rightarrow$ parabola; $> 0 \Rightarrow$ hyperbola. Hyperbola / ellipse / discriminant classification is not in the standard ON MCR3U / MHF4U syllabus; this is honors-extension content.

(a) Identify $A$, $B$, $C$ and compute $\Delta_{\text{conic}}$ for $4x^{2} + 9y^{2} - 16x + 18y - 11 = 0$. Classify the conic. [3]
(b) Identify $A$, $B$, $C$ and compute $\Delta_{\text{conic}}$ for $x^{2} - y^{2} + 6x - 4y + 4 = 0$. Classify the conic. [2]
(c) Identify $A$, $B$, $C$ and compute $\Delta_{\text{conic}}$ for $y^{2} - 4x - 8y + 20 = 0$. Classify the conic. [2]
(d) For the equation in part (a), explain in one sentence why the test $A = C$ would (or would not) further reduce the ellipse to a circle. [2]
Q9MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §6 Translations of Conics · HSG-GPE.A.1 / A.3 [9 marks]

Consider the equation $x^{2} + y^{2} - 6x + 8y - 11 = 0$.

(a) Complete the square in $x$ and in $y$ separately to convert the equation into the standard form $(x - h)^{2} + (y - k)^{2} = r^{2}$. Show each add-and-subtract step explicitly. [4]
(b) State the centre $(h, k)$ and the radius $r$. [2]
(c) Determine whether the point $(7, -4)$ lies inside, on, or outside the circle. Justify using the standard form. [2]
(d) Briefly describe the geometric transformation that takes the unit circle $x^{2} + y^{2} = 1$ to the circle in part (a). [1]
PART III  ·  MODELING / APPLIEDUniversal · 28 marks

Section C · Modeling and Applications

Name your variables (with units) before writing equations. Place the conic in a convenient coordinate system (state your axis choice), set up the standard form, solve, and conclude with a one-sentence answer in context. Calculator permitted throughout Part III.

Q10MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §7 Parabolic Dish (Reflector) · HSG-GPE.A.2 [9 marks]

A parabolic satellite dish has a circular rim of diameter $1.2$ m and a depth of $0.15$ m at its centre. Place the vertex of the parabola at the origin with the axis of symmetry along the positive $y$-axis, so the parabola has the form $y = a x^{2}$. The dish's receiver is mounted at the focus of the parabola.

(a) State the coordinates of the rim's edge point in this coordinate system (the point on the parabola directly above the rim's edge). [1]
(b) Substitute this point into $y = a x^{2}$ and solve for $a$. [2]
(c) Use $4p = 1/a$ to determine the focal length $p$, in metres (rounded to the nearest centimetre). [3]
(d) State the height (above the vertex of the dish) at which the receiver should be mounted, and explain in one sentence why parallel incoming radio waves are concentrated at this point. [3]
Q11MEDIUMHonors 🇨🇦 AB 🇺🇸 US AB Provincial-style §7 Kepler Orbit / Whisper Gallery · HSG-GPE.A.3 (+) [9 marks]

A whisper gallery is built in the shape of half an ellipse, with its major axis horizontal along the floor. The gallery is $24$ m long (the full length of the major axis) and $7$ m tall at its centre (the semi-minor axis). Two visitors stand at the foci of the ellipse so that any whisper from one focus reflects off the ceiling and arrives clearly at the other. Ellipse foci / Kepler orbits are not in standard AB Math 20-1 / 30-1; this is honors-extension content.

(a) Place the centre of the ellipse at the origin with the major axis along the $x$-axis. State $a$ and $b$ (in metres). [1]
(b) Use the focal relation $c^{2} = a^{2} - b^{2}$ to compute $c$, the distance from the centre to each focus. Round to the nearest tenth of a metre. [3]
(c) State the coordinates of the two foci (where the visitors should stand) and the distance between them. [2]
(d) Compute the eccentricity $e = c/a$ of the elliptical cross-section. State, in one sentence, what range of $e$ values would make the gallery "nearly circular" (poor whisper effect) versus "elongated" (strong whisper effect). [3]
Q12HARDHonors 🇨🇦 BC 🇺🇸 US BC Provincial-style §7 LORAN Hyperbolic Navigation · HSG-GPE.A.3 (+) [10 marks]

In a LORAN (Long-Range Navigation) system, a ship measures the time difference between two synchronized radio pulses from two transmitter stations. The set of points whose difference of distances to the two stations is a constant traces out one branch of a hyperbola. Two LORAN stations are located at $F_{1}(-200, 0)$ and $F_{2}(200, 0)$ (coordinates in kilometres). A ship records that its distance to $F_{1}$ is $240$ km greater than its distance to $F_{2}$. Hyperbolas / LORAN are not in standard BC PC 11 / PC 12; this is honors-extension content.

(a) State the focal distance $c$ (in kilometres) and the constant difference $2a$, then deduce $a$. [2]
(b) Use the hyperbola focal relation $c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2}$ to compute $b^{2}$. [2]
(c) State the equation of the hyperbola (centred at the origin, transverse axis along the $x$-axis) in standard form $\dfrac{x^{2}}{a^{2}} - \dfrac{y^{2}}{b^{2}} = 1$. [1]
(d) Determine on which branch (left, $x \le -a$, or right, $x \ge a$) the ship's location lies, given that the distance to $F_{1}$ exceeds the distance to $F_{2}$. Justify briefly. [2]
(e) If a second pair of LORAN stations independently locates the ship on another hyperbola, explain in one sentence how the ship's exact position is determined. [1]
(f) State the equations of the two asymptotes of the LORAN hyperbola from part (c). Round slopes to three decimal places. [2]
