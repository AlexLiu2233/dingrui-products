PART I  ·  SHORT RESPONSEON MCV4U + US HSN-VM (+) · 18 marks

Section A · Short Response

Mix of multiple-choice and short-answer items. For MCQs, circle the letter; show enough work in the margin that a marker could verify. Write vectors in angle-bracket component form $\langle a, b\rangle$ unless the question states otherwise. State magnitudes to two decimals or exact-radical form as the question requires. No calculator on Q1–Q3; calculator permitted on Q4–Q5.

Q1EASY 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US ON Provincial-style §1 Directed Segments · HSN-VM.A.2 (+) [3 marks]

The vector $\vec{AB}$ has initial point $A(-1, 4)$ and terminal point $B(5, -4)$. Which of the following is the component form of $\vec{AB}$, and what is its magnitude $|\vec{AB}|$?

  1. (A) $\vec{AB} = \langle 6, -8\rangle$, $|\vec{AB}| = 10$
  2. (B) $\vec{AB} = \langle -6, 8\rangle$, $|\vec{AB}| = 10$
  3. (C) $\vec{AB} = \langle 6, -8\rangle$, $|\vec{AB}| = 14$
  4. (D) $\vec{AB} = \langle 4, 0\rangle$, $|\vec{AB}| = 4$
Q2EASY 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Magnitude & Unit Vector · HSN-VM.A.1 (+) [3 marks]

Let $\vec{v} = \langle -3, 4\rangle$. Which of the following gives $|\vec{v}|$ and the unit vector $\hat{v}$ in the same direction as $\vec{v}$?

  1. (A) $|\vec{v}| = 1$, $\hat{v} = \langle -3, 4\rangle$
  2. (B) $|\vec{v}| = 5$, $\hat{v} = \left\langle -\tfrac{3}{5}, \tfrac{4}{5}\right\rangle$
  3. (C) $|\vec{v}| = 5$, $\hat{v} = \left\langle \tfrac{3}{5}, -\tfrac{4}{5}\right\rangle$
  4. (D) $|\vec{v}| = 7$, $\hat{v} = \left\langle -\tfrac{3}{7}, \tfrac{4}{7}\right\rangle$
Q3MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §3 Addition + Scalar Multiplication · HSN-VM.B.4 & B.5 (+) [3 marks]

Let $\vec{u} = \langle 2, -1\rangle$ and $\vec{w} = \langle -4, 3\rangle$. Compute $2\vec{u} - \vec{w}$ in component form.

  1. (A) $\langle 8, -5\rangle$
  2. (B) $\langle 0, -5\rangle$
  3. (C) $\langle 8, 1\rangle$
  4. (D) $\langle -8, 5\rangle$
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §1 & §2 Magnitude / Direction · MCV4U Strand C1 [4 marks]

A wind vector $\vec{w}$ has magnitude $|\vec{w}| = 20$ km/h and points in a direction $35^{\circ}$ north of east. Take east as the positive $x$-axis and north as the positive $y$-axis.

(a) Write $\vec{w}$ in component form $\langle a, b\rangle$, with each component rounded to two decimals (km/h). [2]
(b) Verify your answer by computing $|\vec{w}|$ from your components; confirm it rounds to $20$ km/h. [2]
Q5MEDIUMHonors 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §4 Dot Product / Perpendicularity · MCV4U C3 / HSN-VM (+) [5 marks]

Let $\vec{a} = \langle 3, -2\rangle$ and $\vec{b} = \langle 4, k\rangle$ where $k$ is a real parameter.

(a) Compute $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$ in terms of $k$. [2]
(b) Find the value of $k$ for which $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ are perpendicular. State the perpendicularity test you used. [3]
PART II  ·  EXTENDED RESPONSEAP-feeder FRQ + ON MCV4U C3 · 35 marks

Section B · Extended Response

Show every step of each dot- and cross-product computation. For cross products, write the $3 \times 3$ determinant expansion explicitly and watch the sign of the middle component (Sarrus / cofactor expansion both work). State angles in degrees rounded to one decimal unless the question asks for radians. No calculator on Q6–Q8; calculator permitted on Q9.

Q6MEDIUMHonors 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §4 Dot Product / Angle · MCV4U C3 / HSN-VM (+) [8 marks]

Let $\vec{u} = \langle 2, 3\rangle$ and $\vec{v} = \langle -1, 5\rangle$.

(a) Compute $\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}$ algebraically (componentwise). [2]
(b) Compute $|\vec{u}|$ and $|\vec{v}|$ in exact-radical form. [2]
(c) Use the geometric form $\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}| \cos\theta$ to find the angle $\theta$ between $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$, in degrees, rounded to one decimal. [3]
(d) State whether $\theta$ is acute, right, or obtuse, and link that classification back to the sign of $\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}$. [1]
Q7HARDHonors 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §5 Vectors in 3-D · MCV4U C1-C3 [9 marks]

In $\mathbb{R}^{3}$, let $\vec{p} = \langle 1, -2, 2\rangle$ and $\vec{q} = \langle 3, 0, -4\rangle$.

(a) Compute $|\vec{p}|$ and $|\vec{q}|$. [2]
(b) Find the unit vector $\hat{p}$ in the direction of $\vec{p}$. [2]
(c) Compute $\vec{p} \cdot \vec{q}$. [2]
(d) Find the angle between $\vec{p}$ and $\vec{q}$, in degrees, rounded to one decimal. [3]
Q8HARDHonors 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §6 Cross Product · MCV4U C3 / HSN-VM (+) [8 marks]

In $\mathbb{R}^{3}$, let $\vec{a} = \langle 2, -1, 3\rangle$ and $\vec{b} = \langle 1, 4, -2\rangle$.

(a) Compute $\vec{a} \times \vec{b}$ by writing out the $3 \times 3$ determinant expansion explicitly. Be careful with the sign of the $\hat{j}$-component, the cofactor expansion picks up an extra minus sign. [4]
(b) Verify your answer is perpendicular to both $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ by checking $(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{a} = 0$ and $(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{b} = 0$. [2]
(c) Compute $|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|$, and state its geometric meaning (area of which figure?). [2]
Q9HARDHonors 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §4-§6 Mixed Vector Algebra · MCV4U C3 / HSN-VM (+) [10 marks]

In $\mathbb{R}^{3}$, let $\vec{u} = \langle 1, 2, -1\rangle$ and $\vec{v} = \langle 2, 0, 1\rangle$.

(a) Compute $\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}$ and the angle $\theta$ between $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ (in degrees, to one decimal). [3]
(b) Compute $\vec{u} \times \vec{v}$ via the determinant expansion. [3]
(c) Verify the identity $|\vec{u}|^{2} |\vec{v}|^{2} = (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v})^{2} + |\vec{u} \times \vec{v}|^{2}$ (Lagrange's identity). Show both sides numerically. [3]
(d) Without recomputing, state $\vec{v} \times \vec{u}$, citing the antisymmetry property $\vec{v} \times \vec{u} = -(\vec{u} \times \vec{v})$. [1]
PART III  ·  MODELING / APPLIEDAP Physics-feeder + ON MCV4U C2-C3 · 28 marks

Section C · Modeling and Applications

Name your axes and units before writing equations. Set up the vector model, solve, and conclude with a one-sentence answer in context (with units). Calculator permitted throughout Part III. These three problems are exactly the kinds of force / work / moment computations students will see in AP Physics Unit 1 (kinematics & force vectors) and IB Math HL Topic C3.

Q10MEDIUMHonors 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §7 Force Resolution / Inclined Plane · HSN-VM.A.3 (+) [9 marks]

A $10$ kg crate sits on a frictionless ramp inclined at $25^{\circ}$ above the horizontal. Take gravity $g = 9.8$ m/s$^{2}$ acting straight down with magnitude $W = mg$.

(a) Compute the magnitude of the gravitational force $W = mg$ in newtons. [1]
(b) Resolve $W$ into a component parallel to the ramp surface (call it $W_{\parallel}$, pointing down the slope) and a component perpendicular to the ramp (call it $W_{\perp}$, pressing into the surface). Express both as multiples of $W$ with trig factors, then evaluate to the nearest tenth of a newton. [4]
(c) State the minimum force, parallel to the ramp and directed up the slope, needed to hold the crate stationary. [2]
(d) Sanity-check: compute $W_{\parallel}^{2} + W_{\perp}^{2}$ and confirm it equals $W^{2}$ (Pythagoras on perpendicular components). [2]
Q11MEDIUMHonors 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §7 Work as Dot Product · HSN-VM.A.3 (+) [9 marks]

A worker pulls a sled by a rope angled $30^{\circ}$ above the horizontal. The rope tension has magnitude $|\vec{F}| = 80$ N. The sled is displaced by $\vec{d} = \langle 12, 0\rangle$ (m) along level ground (east).

(a) Write the force vector $\vec{F}$ in component form $\langle F_{x}, F_{y}\rangle$, taking east as $+x$ and up as $+y$. Round each to one decimal place. [3]
(b) Compute the work $W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}$ using the algebraic (componentwise) form. State the units. [3]
(c) Verify your answer using the geometric form $W = |\vec{F}|\,|\vec{d}|\cos\theta$, with $\theta = 30^{\circ}$. The two forms should agree to within rounding. [2]
(d) Explain in one sentence why the vertical component of $\vec{F}$ does no work on the sled in this scenario. [1]
Q12HARDHonors 🇨🇦 ON 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §6 + §7 Torque (Moment) as Cross Product · HSN-VM.A.3 (+) [10 marks]

A wrench is used to tighten a bolt at the origin. The applied force is $\vec{F} = \langle 0, 10, 0\rangle$ N. The point of application (the end of the wrench handle) is at $\vec{r} = \langle 0.30, 0, 0\rangle$ m. Torque about the origin is defined by $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$.

(a) Compute $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$ in component form (N·m). Write out the determinant expansion explicitly. [3]
(b) State $|\vec{\tau}|$ (the magnitude of the torque), and the direction of $\vec{\tau}$ relative to the standard right-hand coordinate axes. [2]
(c) The mechanic now repositions the wrench so that the force is applied at $\vec{r}' = \langle 0.30, 0.10, 0\rangle$ m with the same $\vec{F} = \langle 0, 10, 0\rangle$ N. Re-compute $\vec{\tau}' = \vec{r}' \times \vec{F}$. [3]
(d) Compare $|\vec{\tau}|$ to $|\vec{\tau}'|$. Explain in one sentence why the $y$-component of $\vec{r}'$ does not increase the torque, and connect this to the geometric formula $|\vec{\tau}| = |\vec{r}|\,|\vec{F}|\sin\theta$. [2]
