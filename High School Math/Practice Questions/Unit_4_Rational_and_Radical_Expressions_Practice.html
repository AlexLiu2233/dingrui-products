PART I  ·  SHORT RESPONSESAT-style MCQ + ON/BC short answer · 22 marks

Section A · Short Response

Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Simplify Rationals · HSA-APR.D.6 [3 marks]

Which of the following is the simplified form of $\dfrac{x^{2} - 25}{x^{2} - 4 x - 5}$, together with its restriction set?

  1. (A) $\dfrac{x - 5}{x - 1}$, $x \ne 5$
  2. (B) $\dfrac{x + 5}{x + 1}$, $x \ne -1, 5$
  3. (C) $\dfrac{x + 5}{x - 1}$ (no restrictions)
  4. (D) $\dfrac{x + 5}{x + 1}$, $x \ne -1$
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §5 Radical Operations · HSN-RN.A.2 [3 marks]

Which expression is equivalent to $\sqrt{50} + \sqrt{18} - \sqrt{8}$?

  1. (A) $6 \sqrt{2}$
  2. (B) $\sqrt{60}$
  3. (C) $10 \sqrt{2}$
  4. (D) $2 \sqrt{10}$
Q3EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §4 Horizontal Asymptote · HSF-IF.B.4 [3 marks]

The function $f(x) = \dfrac{4 x^{2} + 3 x - 1}{2 x^{2} - 5}$ has which horizontal asymptote?

  1. (A) $y = 0$
  2. (B) $y = 2$
  3. (C) $y = -1/5$
  4. (D) No horizontal asymptote; a slant asymptote instead
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §2 Add & Subtract Rationals · MHF4U Strand C [6 marks]

Combine each pair of rational expressions into a single rational expression in lowest terms. State all restrictions on the variable.

(a) $\dfrac{3}{x - 2} + \dfrac{5}{x + 4}$. [3]
(b) $\dfrac{2 x}{x^{2} - 9} - \dfrac{1}{x - 3}$ (factor the first denominator before forming the LCD). [3]
Q5MEDIUMHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §7 Rational Exponents · HSN-RN.A.1 / BC PC11 [7 marks]

Throughout this question, assume $x > 0$.

(a) Evaluate $27^{2/3}$ and $32^{-3/5}$ exactly, citing the identity $a^{m/n} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^{m}$. [2]
(b) Rewrite $\dfrac{\sqrt{x} \cdot \sqrt[3]{x^{2}}}{\sqrt[6]{x^{5}}}$ as a single power $x^{r}$ in lowest terms. Show the LCD-of-fractions step. [3]
(c) Cite the Common Core standard HSN-RN.A.1 in one sentence: explain why the definition $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$ is forced by extending the exponent law $(a^{p})^{q} = a^{p q}$ from integer exponents to rational ones. [2]
PART II  ·  EXTENDED RESPONSEAP-feeder FRQ + honors · 36 marks

Section B · Extended Response

Show every algebraic step. State the domain restriction before clearing denominators (rational equations) or squaring (radical equations). For every candidate root, verify against the original equation and explicitly flag any extraneous solutions. No calculator on Q6–Q9.

Q6MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Add & Subtract Rationals · HSA-APR.D.6 [8 marks]

Let $R(x) = \dfrac{x + 1}{x^{2} - 4} + \dfrac{2}{x - 2} - \dfrac{1}{x + 2}$.

(a) Factor every denominator and state the least common denominator (LCD). State the restriction set on $x$. [2]
(b) Combine $R(x)$ into a single rational expression over the LCD. Expand and collect the numerator, then factor it if possible. [4]
(c) Cancel any common factor and state the simplified form of $R(x)$. Reaffirm the full restriction set (including any restriction that becomes invisible after cancellation, as required by HSA-APR.D.6). [2]
Q7MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §3 Rational Equations · HSA-REI.A.2 [9 marks]

Solve each equation. In each part, state restrictions before clearing denominators, list every candidate root, and verify each candidate against the original equation. Explicitly flag any extraneous solutions.

(a) $\dfrac{1}{x - 1} + \dfrac{1}{x + 1} = \dfrac{4}{x^{2} - 1}$. [4]
(b) $\dfrac{x}{x - 3} - \dfrac{2}{x} = \dfrac{6}{x (x - 3)}$. [4]
(c) In one sentence, cite Common Core HSA-REI.A.2 ("solve simple rational and radical equations in one variable, and give examples showing how extraneous solutions may arise") to explain why the verification step in part (a) was necessary even though the algebra was correct. [1]
Q8HARD 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §6 Radical Equations · BC PC11 radical equations [8 marks]

Solve each radical equation by isolating, squaring, and checking against the original. For each part, state the radicand-non-negative condition before squaring, and flag any extraneous root.

(a) $\sqrt{3 x + 1} = x - 1$ (single radical; one squaring). [3]
(b) $\sqrt{x + 4} - \sqrt{x - 3} = 1$ (two radicals; isolate one, square, isolate the other, square again). [4]
(c) State the BC PC11 Big Idea phrasing the inquiry behind this question: "the meanings of, and connections between, operations extend to powers, radicals, and polynomials." Identify the one step in part (b) that introduces the possibility of an extraneous root. [1]
Q9HARDHonors 🇨🇦 BC 🇨🇦 ON AP-feeder FRQ §4 Rational Function · BC PC12 / MHF4U Strand C [11 marks]

Consider the rational function $f(x) = \dfrac{x^{2} - x - 6}{x^{2} - 4 x + 3}$. BC PC12's rational functions Content elaboration names the analysis below verbatim: asymptotes, intercepts, point discontinuities (holes), domain, end-behaviour.

(a) Factor both numerator and denominator. State any common factor. [2]
(b) Identify the point discontinuity (hole) of $f$. Give the coordinates $(a, f^{*}(a))$ where $f^{*}$ is the function after cancellation. [2]
(c) Identify any vertical asymptote(s). Justify by citing the rule "denominator-only zero $\Rightarrow$ VA; common-factor zero $\Rightarrow$ hole." [2]
(d) Determine the horizontal asymptote of $f$ by degree comparison. Then state the $x$-intercept(s) and the $y$-intercept of $f$. [3]
(e) State the domain of $f$ in set-builder notation. List every value of $x$ excluded by the original denominator (the cancelled hole is still excluded, as the BC PC12 "domain" elaboration requires). [2]
PART III  ·  MODELING / APPLIEDUniversal · 32 marks

Section C · Modeling and Applications

Name your variables (with units) before writing equations. State the situational domain. Set up your model, solve, and conclude with a one-sentence answer in context. Calculator permitted throughout Part III.

Q10MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §6 Radical Equation Modeling · HSA-REI.A.2 [10 marks]

The period $T$ (in seconds) of a simple pendulum of length $L$ (in metres) is modeled by $T = 2 \pi \sqrt{L / g}$, where $g = 9.8$ m/s$^{2}$ is the acceleration due to gravity near Earth's surface.

(a) A grandfather clock has a pendulum of length $L = 0.994$ m. Compute its period $T$ to three decimal places. Show the substitution into the radical model. [2]
(b) Solve the model for $L$ in terms of $T$. (Square both sides; isolate $L$.) State the situational domain $L > 0$, $T > 0$. [3]
(c) A playground swing is designed to have period $T = 3.5$ s. Use part (b) to determine the required length $L$, rounded to two decimal places. State units. [3]
(d) Verify your answer to part (c) by substituting back into the original model $T = 2 \pi \sqrt{L / g}$. The verification step is required by Common Core HSA-REI.A.2 for radical equations — cite the standard and explain why the verification is structurally needed (squaring is a non-injective operation). [2]
Q11MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §3 Rational Equation Modeling · MHF4U Strand C [11 marks]

Two students paint a fence. Working alone, Aanya finishes the job in $x$ hours; Ben finishes it in $x + 3$ hours. Together they finish in $2$ hours.

(a) State Aanya's rate of work and Ben's rate of work, each as a fraction of the job per hour, using $x$. [2]
(b) Write a rational equation in $x$ that expresses the combined-rate condition "in $2$ hours together, they complete $1$ full job." State the restriction $x > 0$ and any other restriction implied by the algebra. [2]
(c) Multiply through by the LCD and reduce the equation to a quadratic in $x$. Solve the quadratic exactly using the quadratic formula from Unit 2. [4]
(d) One root is positive, one is negative. Reject the negative root with a one-sentence justification (the situational domain $x > 0$ comes from the modeling context, not just the algebraic restrictions). State Aanya's time and Ben's time, each rounded to one decimal place. [2]
(e) Verify your answer by computing $1/x + 1/(x + 3)$ at the value found in (d) and checking that the sum equals $1/2$ (the combined hourly rate). Round to three decimal places. [1]
Q12HARD 🇨🇦 BC 🇺🇸 US BC Provincial-style §2 + §4 Rational Function App · BC PC12 / HSA-CED.A.1 [11 marks]

A small bottling plant has fixed daily costs of CA$240 and a variable cost of CA$0.60 per bottle. The plant produces $x$ bottles per day ($x > 0$). The average cost per bottle, in dollars, is the rational function $\bar C(x) = \dfrac{240 + 0.60 x}{x}$.

(a) Rewrite $\bar C(x)$ as a sum: $\bar C(x) = \dfrac{240}{x} + 0.60$. Identify which term came from fixed cost and which from variable cost. (This is the §2 "split a single rational into a sum" move in reverse.) [2]
(b) Compute $\bar C(100)$, $\bar C(400)$, and $\bar C(1200)$. Round each to the nearest cent. Briefly interpret why $\bar C$ decreases as $x$ increases. [3]
(c) Determine the horizontal asymptote of $\bar C(x)$ by degree comparison, and interpret its meaning as $x \to \infty$ in context (the asymptotic per-bottle cost a high-volume plant approaches but never reaches). [3]
(d) The plant is profitable if and only if $\bar C(x) \le 0.85$ (the wholesale price per bottle). Set up a rational equation $\bar C(x) = 0.85$, clear the denominator, and solve for the break-even production $x$. [2]
(e) State the situational domain of $\bar C$. Cite the BC PC12 rational functions elaboration (asymptotes, intercepts, point discontinuities, domain, end-behaviour) to justify that $x = 0$ is excluded both algebraically (denominator-only zero $\Rightarrow$ vertical asymptote) and contextually (a plant producing zero bottles has undefined per-bottle cost). [1]
