Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Exp Parent & Asymptote · HSF-IF.C.7e [3 marks]

The graph of $f(x) = 4 \cdot 2^{x}$ is sketched in the $xy$-plane. Which statement correctly describes its $y$-intercept and horizontal asymptote?

  1. (A) $y$-intercept $(0, 1)$; asymptote $y = 0$
  2. (B) $y$-intercept $(0, 4)$; asymptote $y = 0$
  3. (C) $y$-intercept $(0, 4)$; asymptote $y = 4$
  4. (D) $y$-intercept $(0, 2)$; asymptote $x = 0$

  2. (B) $x = 2$

  2. (B) $5^{3} = x$

  4. (D) $x^{3} = 5$
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §4 Laws of Logs · MHF4U Exp & Log [6 marks]

Use the laws of logarithms to evaluate or simplify each expression. State which law(s) you applied at each step.

(a) Evaluate exactly: $\log_{2}(32) - \log_{2}(4)$. [2]
(b) Condense to a single logarithm: $2 \log_{3}(x) + \log_{3}(5) - \log_{3}(y)$. [2]
(c) Expand and simplify: $\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{100 \, x^{3}}{\sqrt{y}}\right)$. [2]
Q5MEDIUM 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §5 Different-Base Exp · BC PC12 exp eqs [7 marks]

Q6MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §4 Change-of-Base · HSF-BF.B.5 (+) [8 marks]

Let $L = \log_{4}(50)$.

(a) Write $L$ in change-of-base form using the natural logarithm, then approximate $L$ to four decimal places. [3]
(b) Without using a calculator, prove the identity $\log_{b}(x^{p}) = p \log_{b}(x)$ for $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$, $x > 0$ by starting from $y = \log_{b}(x^{p}) \iff b^{y} = x^{p}$ and applying laws of exponents. [3]
(c) Use part (b) to evaluate exactly $\log_{4}(50) - \log_{4}(2) - \log_{4}(25)$ without a calculator. [2]
Q7HARD 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §5 Log Equations + Extraneous · MHF4U Exp & Log [9 marks]

Solve each logarithmic equation algebraically. For each candidate solution, verify the argument of every logarithm is strictly positive; discard extraneous solutions and state the final solution set.

(a) $\log_{2}(x) + \log_{2}(x - 2) = 3$. [4]
(b) $\log_{5}(2 x + 3) - \log_{5}(x - 1) = 1$. [3]
(c) State, in one sentence, why checking the domain of each logarithm is required and not optional. [2]
Q8HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §6 Base $e$ & Natural Log · BC PC12 / HSF-LE.A.4 [10 marks]

Let $g(x) = 3 e^{2 x} - 5$.

(a) State the horizontal asymptote of $y = g(x)$ and the $y$-intercept. [2]
(b) Solve $g(x) = 16$ exactly using the natural logarithm, then approximate to three decimal places. [3]
(c) Determine the inverse $g^{-1}(x)$ explicitly in terms of $\ln$. State the domain and range of $g^{-1}$. [3]
(d) Cite the BC PC12 Big Idea on inverses (one sentence) and explain why solving $g(x) = 16$ in (b) is precisely an instance of it. [2]
Q9HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §6 Continuous Compounding · BC PC12 base $e$ / HSF-LE.B.5 [9 marks]

A bank advertises a nominal annual interest rate of $4.5\%$ on a savings account with continuous compounding. The balance after $t$ years on an initial principal $P_{0}$ is modeled by $P(t) = P_{0} \, e^{0.045 \, t}$.

(a) A customer deposits US$$2{,}000$. Compute the balance after $10$ years to the nearest cent. [2]
(b) Solve symbolically for the doubling time $T$ such that $P(T) = 2 P_{0}$. Express $T$ exactly using $\ln$, then approximate to two decimal places. [3]
(c) Interpret the parameters $P_{0}$ and the rate $0.045$ in the context of the situation. Cite HSF-LE.B.5: "interpret the parameters in… an exponential function in terms of a context." [2]
(d) A rival bank offers $4.5\%$ compounded annually instead of continuously. After $10$ years on the same US$$2{,}000$ principal, which account holds more, and by how much (nearest cent)? [2]
PART III  ·  MODELING / APPLIEDUniversal · 32 marks

Section C · Modeling and Applications

Name your variables (with units) before writing equations. State whether the situation is growth or decay and identify the growth/decay factor before substituting. Conclude with a one-sentence answer in context, rounded sensibly. Calculator permitted throughout Part III.

Q10MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §7 Population Growth · HSF-LE.A.1c / HSF-LE.A.2 [10 marks]

A coastal town's population grows by a constant percent rate of $2.4\%$ per year. In the year $2020$ its population was $48{,}000$. Let $P(t)$ denote the population $t$ years after $2020$.

(a) Write the explicit model $P(t) = a \cdot b^{t}$. State the values of $a$ and $b$ and identify which represents the initial value and which the annual growth factor. [3]
(b) Predict the population in $2030$ to the nearest whole person. [2]
(c) Determine, using logarithms, the year in which the population is first projected to exceed $75{,}000$. Round your value of $t$ up to the next whole year. [3]
(d) Cite HSF-LE.A.1c to explain why "$2.4\%$ per year" produces an exponential rather than a linear model. [2]
Q11MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §7 Radioactive Half-Life · MHF4U Exp & Log [11 marks]

Iodine-131 has a half-life of approximately $8.02$ days. A medical sample initially contains $40$ mg of I-131. Let $M(t)$ be the mass remaining (in mg) after $t$ days.

(a) Write $M(t)$ in the half-life form $M(t) = M_{0} \cdot \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{t / h}$. State $M_{0}$ and $h$. [2]
(b) Compute the mass remaining after $30$ days, to two decimal places. [2]
(c) Determine, using logarithms, the time required for the sample to decay to $5$ mg. Round to the nearest tenth of a day. [4]
(d) State the practical (situational) domain of $M(t)$ in interval notation, and identify whether this is exponential growth or decay. [2]
(e) State one assumption baked into the half-life model that is in practice only approximately true. [1]
Q12HARD 🇨🇦 BC 🇺🇸 US BC Provincial-style §7 Compound Interest · BC PC12 / HSF-LE.A.3 [11 marks]

Two investment options are available for an initial principal of CA$$5{,}000$ held for $t$ years.

Option A pays $6\%$ annual interest compounded monthly: $A(t) = 5000 \left(1 + \tfrac{0.06}{12}\right)^{12 t}$.
Option B is a linear-growth bond paying simple interest of CA$$320$ per year: $B(t) = 5000 + 320 \, t$.

(a) Compute $A(5)$ and $B(5)$, each to the nearest cent. Which is larger after $5$ years, and by how much? [3]
(b) Determine the doubling time for Option A: the value of $t$ for which $A(t) = 10{,}000$. Use logarithms and give an exact expression, then round to two decimal places. [3]
(c) Cite HSF-LE.A.3: explain in one or two sentences why $A(t)$ must eventually exceed $B(t)$ regardless of how large the initial gap might be, and identify (approximately, by table or by inspection) the year $t$ in which $A(t)$ first exceeds $B(t)$. [3]
(d) State one situational restriction on $t$ (e.g. non-negativity or a contractual maturity), and one reason a real saver might still prefer Option B in the short term. [2]
