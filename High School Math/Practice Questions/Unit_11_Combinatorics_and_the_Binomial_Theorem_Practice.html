Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §3 Combinations · MDM4U Counting and Probability [6 marks]

A school's debate club has $12$ members. Three of them must be chosen to attend a tournament.

(a) If the three attendees are sent as an undifferentiated team (no roles), how many ways are there to choose them? State whether you used $_{n}P_{r}$ or $\binom{n}{r}$ and justify in one sentence. [3]
(b) If, instead, the three attendees are to be assigned the distinct roles of captain, first speaker, and second speaker, how many ways are there? [2]
(c) Verify numerically that your answer to (b) equals $3!$ times your answer to (a), and explain the factor of $3!$ combinatorially. [1]
Q5MEDIUM 🇨🇦 AB AB Provincial-style §2 Identical-Elements Permutations · AB Math 30-1 SO 2 (indicator 2.6) [7 marks]

Consider the word STATISTICS ($10$ letters: $3$ S, $3$ T, $1$ A, $2$ I, $1$ C).

(a) State the identical-elements formula for the number of distinguishable letter arrangements of a word with repeated letters, in terms of the total letter count $n$ and the multiplicities $k_{1}, k_{2}, \ldots, k_{m}$. [2]
(b) Compute the number of distinguishable letter arrangements of STATISTICS. Give the exact integer. [3]
(c) If the word were instead STATS ($5$ letters: $2$ S, $2$ T, $1$ A), how many distinguishable arrangements would there be? [2]

Q6MEDIUM 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §3 Committee Counts · BC PC 12 Permutations/Combinations [8 marks]

A committee of $5$ is to be chosen from a group of $8$ women and $6$ men.

(a) How many committees are possible with no restriction on composition? [2]
(b) How many committees contain exactly $3$ women and $2$ men? [3]
(c) How many committees contain at least $4$ women? [3]
Q7MEDIUM 🇨🇦 AB AB Provincial-style §5 Binomial Theorem · AB Math 30-1 SO 4 (indicators 4.1, 4.5) [9 marks]

Consider the expansion of $(2x + 3)^{5}$ via the Binomial Theorem.

(a) Write row $5$ of Pascal's triangle. [1]
(b) Write out the general term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^{r}$ for $(2x + 3)^{5}$, with $a = 2x$, $b = 3$, $n = 5$, $r = 0, 1, \ldots, 5$. [2]
(c) Compute the full expansion of $(2x + 3)^{5}$ in expanded standard form, simplifying each coefficient. [5]
(d) State the sum of all coefficients in the expansion, and check it equals $(2(1) + 3)^{5} = 5^{5} = 3125$. [1]
Q8HARD 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §6 Term Extraction (positive powers) · MHF4U Polynomial Functions [10 marks]

Consider the expansion of $(2x - 3)^{12}$.

(a) State the general term $T_{r+1}$ of $(2x - 3)^{12}$ as a function of $r$, where $r \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, 12\}$. Simplify so that the dependence on $x$ is in a single power $x^{12-r}$. [3]
(b) Find the value of $r$ that produces the term containing $x^{7}$. [2]
(c) Compute the coefficient of $x^{7}$ in the expansion of $(2x - 3)^{12}$. Give the exact signed integer. [4]
(d) State the sign of your coefficient and explain in one sentence why this sign is consistent with the pattern $T_{r+1}$ alternates sign as $r$ increases by one. [1]
Q9HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §6 Term Extraction (negative powers) · BC PC 12 / HSA-APR.C.5 (+) [11 marks]

Consider the expansion of $\left(x + \dfrac{2}{x}\right)^{10}$, valid for $x \ne 0$.

(a) State the general term $T_{r+1}$ as a function of $r$, where $r \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, 10\}$. Simplify so that the dependence on $x$ is in a single power $x^{10-2r}$. [3]
(b) Find the value of $r$ that produces the constant term (the term with $x^{0}$). [2]
(c) Compute the constant term. Give the exact integer. [3]
(d) Find the value of $r$ that produces the term containing $x^{4}$, and compute the coefficient of $x^{4}$. [2]
(e) Explain in one sentence why only even powers of $x$ can appear in this expansion (cite the form of the exponent of $x$ in $T_{r+1}$). [1]

Q10MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §1 + §3 Modeling · MDM4U Counting and Probability [10 marks]

A province issues licence plates with $3$ uppercase letters from $\{A, B, \ldots, Z\}$ followed by $3$ digits from $\{0, 1, \ldots, 9\}$. The letters $I, O$ and the digit $0$ are excluded as confusing (so there are effectively $24$ usable letters and $9$ usable digits).

(a) How many distinct plates are possible if repetition is allowed throughout? [2]
(b) How many plates are possible if no letter and no digit may be repeated? [3]
(c) A driver applies for a plate at random from the with-repetition pool in (a). What is the probability that the plate has no repeated letter and no repeated digit? Give an exact ratio and a decimal to three decimal places. [3]
(d) If the province later allows plates of the form two letters, two digits, in any one of the two interleaved orders (i.e. LL-DD or LD-LD), how many plates are possible with the same $24$-letter, $9$-digit alphabets and repetition allowed? Justify your "multiply, not add" reasoning. [2]
Q11HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §7 Subset-Count Identities · HSA-APR.C.5 (+) [10 marks]

Let $n$ be a positive integer. Consider the identity $$ \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} \;=\; 2^{n}. $$

(a) Derive this identity as a corollary of the Binomial Theorem by choosing a specific substitution into $(a + b)^{n} = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^{r}$. State the substitution and show the resulting line of algebra. [3]
(b) Give a combinatorial (double-counting) proof of the same identity. The left side counts what? The right side counts what? Explain in two sentences why both expressions count the same thing. [3]
(c) Verify the identity numerically for $n = 6$ by adding row $6$ of Pascal's triangle ($1, 6, 15, 20, 15, 6, 1$) and confirming that the sum equals $2^{6} = 64$. [2]
(d) Use the analogous substitution $a = 1$, $b = -1$ into the Binomial Theorem to derive the alternating-sum identity $\sum_{r=0}^{n} (-1)^{r} \binom{n}{r} = 0$ (for $n \ge 1$). State the substitution; show the resulting line. [2]
Q12HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 AB AP-feeder FRQ §3 + §5 Capstone · HSS-CP.B.9 (+) / AB Math 30-1 SO 3, 4 [10 marks]

A standard $52$-card deck is shuffled and a $5$-card poker hand is dealt at random (order does not matter).

(a) State the size of the sample space, $\binom{52}{5}$, as a simplified integer. [2]
(b) A "flush" is a $5$-card hand whose cards all share the same suit (there are $4$ suits, each of $13$ cards). Count the number of flushes. [3]
(c) Compute the probability of being dealt a flush, $P(\text{flush})$, as an exact ratio of binomial coefficients and as a decimal to four decimal places. [2]
(d) Independently, consider the expansion of $(x + 1)^{52}$. State the coefficient of $x^{5}$ in this expansion, and explain in one sentence why this number is equal to your answer in (a). Reference the combinatorial interpretation $\binom{n}{r}$ counts $r$-subsets of an $n$-set. [3]
