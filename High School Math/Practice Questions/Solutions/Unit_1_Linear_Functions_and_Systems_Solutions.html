Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Slope · HSF-IF.B.6 [3 marks]

Slope of the line through $(-4, 7)$ and $(2, -5)$.

Answer:  (A)  $m = -2$

(a) Apply slope formula M1·A1·A1

$$ m \;=\; \frac{y_{2} - y_{1}}{x_{2} - x_{1}} \;=\; \frac{-5 - 7}{2 - (-4)} \;=\; \frac{-12}{6} \;=\; -2. $$ So the slope is $-2$, matching option (A).
Why the distractors fail.
(B) $-\tfrac{1}{2}$: the reciprocal, comes from swapping numerator and denominator in the slope formula.
(C) $\tfrac{1}{2}$: reciprocal with the sign also dropped, two errors at once.
(D) $2$: dropped the negative sign from $-12 / 6$.
Pin down "rise over run", direction-locked. The sign of slope is fixed the moment you pick which point is $(x_{1}, y_{1})$, but only if you keep that choice consistent in both numerator and denominator. The common slip is writing $\dfrac{y_{2} - y_{1}}{x_{1} - x_{2}}$, which flips the sign. Either lock the order before you compute, or use the magnitude $\left|\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\right|$ and read the sign off the geometry: as $x$ increases from $-4$ to $2$, $y$ falls from $7$ to $-5$, so $m < 0$.
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §2 Slope-Intercept · HSA-REI.B.3 [3 marks]

Rewrite $4x - 3y = 12$ in slope-intercept form.

Answer:  (B)  $y = \tfrac{4}{3}\,x - 4$

(a) Isolate $y$ M1·A1

Start from $4x - 3y = 12$. Subtract $4x$ from both sides: $$ -3y \;=\; -4x + 12. $$ Divide every term by $-3$, flipping every sign: $$ y \;=\; \tfrac{-4}{-3}\,x + \tfrac{12}{-3} \;=\; \tfrac{4}{3}\,x - 4. $$

(b) Read $(m, b)$ and match A1

Slope $m = \tfrac{4}{3}$, $y$-intercept $b = -4$. Hence option (B).
Why the distractors fail.
(A) $y = -\tfrac{4}{3} x + 4$: sign error on both the slope and the intercept, divided by $+3$ instead of $-3$.
(C) $y = 4x - 12$: "moved" the $-3y$ across the equals sign without dividing the rest of the equation.
(D) $y = \tfrac{3}{4} x - 3$: read off the equation as if it were already $y = mx + b$, treating the $-3$ multiplier as a constant.
Read slope only from $y = mx + b$ form. A line in standard form $Ax + By = C$ has slope $-A/B$ and $y$-intercept $C/B$, not $A$ and $C$. Many students glance at $4x - 3y = 12$ and answer "slope 4". On the SAT the only safe move is to isolate $y$ before reading anything off the equation. The second trap is sign-handling: dividing by a negative coefficient flips every term, both the $x$-coefficient and the constant.
Q3EASY 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §1 Rate of Change · MPM1D Linear Relations [4 marks]

Pool drains linearly: $V(3) = 4200$ L, $V(11) = 2600$ L. (a) Rate. (b) Interpret sign. (c) Restriction on $t$.

Answer:  (a) $\dfrac{\Delta V}{\Delta t} = -200$ L/min  ·  (b) negative $\Rightarrow$ water is leaving  ·  (c) $0 \le t \le 24$ min

(a) Compute the rate, with units M1·A1

Using two data points $(t, V) = (3, 4200)$ and $(11, 2600)$: $$ \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta t} \;=\; \frac{2600 - 4200}{11 - 3} \;=\; \frac{-1600}{8} \;=\; -200 \;\; \text{L/min}. $$

(b) Interpret the sign A1

The negative sign means $V$ is decreasing over time: the pool is losing $200$ litres of water every minute. This is consistent with "the pool is being drained."

(c) Domain restriction A1

The linear model is $V(t) = -200 t + b$. Using $V(3) = 4200$: $b = 4200 + 600 = 4800$, so $V(t) = 4800 - 200 t$. Physically the volume cannot go below zero: $V(t) \ge 0 \;\Leftrightarrow\; t \le 24$. Time also cannot run backwards from the start of the drain: $t \ge 0$. Hence $0 \le t \le 24$ minutes.
"Restriction on $t$" wants the physical boundary, not just $t \ge 0$. Provincial markers reward students who recognise that a linear model of a physical quantity has two domain boundaries: a start time (often $t = 0$) and a depletion time (set the modelled quantity to zero and solve). Writing only "$t \ge 0$" leaves the upper-bound mark on the table, the pool can't keep draining once it is empty at $t = 24$. State both endpoints explicitly with units.
Q4MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §2 Vertical Line · HSF-IF.C.7a [3 marks]

Equation of the line through $(5, -8)$ and $(5, 17)$.

Answer:  (C)  $x = 5$

(a) Recognise the vertical-line pattern M1·A1

Both given points share the same $x$-coordinate, $x = 5$. Attempting the slope formula gives $$ m \;=\; \frac{17 - (-8)}{5 - 5} \;=\; \frac{25}{0}, $$ which is undefined. A line with undefined slope is vertical, and a vertical line consists of every point whose $x$-coordinate equals the common value.

(b) Write the equation A1

Hence $x = 5$, option (C).
Why the distractors fail.
(A) $y = 5$: a horizontal line at height 5, students confuse "constant $x$" with "constant $y$".
(B) $y = -8$: uses only one point and writes a horizontal line through it.
(D) $y = \tfrac{25}{2} x$: reads $\Delta y / \Delta x = 25 / 2$ from the two points, but $\Delta x = 0$, not $2$. This distractor invites the student who tries to "force" the slope formula.
A vertical line is not a function. The single most-tested vertical-line trap on the SAT: students see two points and reach for $y = mx + b$, which fails because $m$ is undefined. The honest move is to inspect the $x$-coordinates first: if they match, the line is $x = (\text{that value})$ and you are done. The same diagnostic works for horizontal lines: matching $y$-coordinates $\Rightarrow$ $y = (\text{that value})$. Train the eye to compare coordinates before computing.
Q5MEDIUM 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §3 Graphing · FMPC10 linear functions [5 marks]

$3x + 4y = 12$. (a) Intercepts. (b) Sketch with intercepts and one lattice point. (c) Slope and sign.

Answer:  (a) $x$-int $(4, 0)$, $y$-int $(0, 3)$  ·  (b) line through $(4,0), (0,3), (-4, 6)$  ·  (c) $m = -\tfrac{3}{4}$, negative

(a) Intercepts A1·A1

$y$-intercept: set $x = 0 \Rightarrow 4y = 12 \Rightarrow y = 3$. So $(0, 3)$.
$x$-intercept: set $y = 0 \Rightarrow 3x = 12 \Rightarrow x = 4$. So $(4, 0)$.

(b) Sketch with an extra lattice point M1·A1

A convenient lattice point: pick $x = -4$, giving $3(-4) + 4y = 12 \Rightarrow 4y = 24 \Rightarrow y = 6$. So $(-4, 6)$ lies on the line. Plot all three points $(4, 0)$, $(0, 3)$, $(-4, 6)$, label them, and draw a straight line through them. The line falls from upper-left to lower-right.

(c) Slope and sign A1

Using the two intercepts: $$ m \;=\; \frac{3 - 0}{0 - 4} \;=\; -\tfrac{3}{4}. $$ Slope is negative. (Cross-check: rearrange $3x + 4y = 12 \Rightarrow y = -\tfrac{3}{4}\,x + 3$, same slope.)
Intercept method beats slope-intercept for sketching. When a line is in standard form $Ax + By = C$, the fastest route to a sketch is the intercept method: set the other variable to zero twice. This gives you two well-defined lattice points (if $A$, $B$, $C$ behave) and a line in three pen strokes. Converting to $y = mx + b$ first is wasted work for a sketch. The third lattice point is a check, if it doesn't fall on the line drawn through the intercepts, your arithmetic is wrong. Provincial markers look for that third point as evidence the student didn't just trace two dots.

Q6MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §4 Parallel & Perpendicular · HSF-LE.A.2 [7 marks]

$\ell_{0}: 2x + 5y = 10$. (a) Slope-intercept form and $m_{0}$. (b) Parallel line through $(-3, 1)$. (c) Perpendicular line through $(4, -2)$.

Answer:  (a) $y = -\tfrac{2}{5}x + 2$, $m_{0} = -\tfrac{2}{5}$  ·  (b) $y = -\tfrac{2}{5}x - \tfrac{1}{5}$  ·  (c) $y = \tfrac{5}{2}x - 12$

(a) Slope-intercept form of $\ell_{0}$ M1·A1

From $2x + 5y = 10$, isolate $y$: $$ 5y \;=\; -2x + 10 \;\Longrightarrow\; y \;=\; -\tfrac{2}{5}\,x + 2. $$ So $m_{0} = -\tfrac{2}{5}$ and the $y$-intercept is $2$.

(b) Parallel through $(-3, 1)$ M1·A1

Parallel $\Rightarrow$ same slope: $m = -\tfrac{2}{5}$. Use point-slope through $(-3, 1)$: $$ y - 1 \;=\; -\tfrac{2}{5}\,(x - (-3)) \;=\; -\tfrac{2}{5}\,x - \tfrac{6}{5}. $$ Hence $y = -\tfrac{2}{5}\,x - \tfrac{6}{5} + 1 = -\tfrac{2}{5}\,x - \tfrac{1}{5}$.

(c) Perpendicular through $(4, -2)$ M1·A1·A1

Perpendicular slope is the negative reciprocal of $m_{0}$: $$ m_{\perp} \;=\; -\frac{1}{m_{0}} \;=\; -\frac{1}{-2/5} \;=\; \tfrac{5}{2}. $$ Point-slope through $(4, -2)$: $$ y - (-2) \;=\; \tfrac{5}{2}\,(x - 4) \;\Longrightarrow\; y + 2 \;=\; \tfrac{5}{2}\,x - 10. $$ So $y = \tfrac{5}{2}\,x - 12$.
Convert before reading; reciprocate and flip sign for perpendiculars. Two locked moves on every parallel/perpendicular question: (1) Reading the slope of $2x + 5y = 10$ as "$2$" is the most-missed-by-one-mark error in HSF-LE.A.2; only $y = mx + b$ form gives the slope directly. (2) "Negative reciprocal" means both sign flips and reciprocal, slope $-\tfrac{2}{5}$ has perpendicular slope $+\tfrac{5}{2}$, not $\tfrac{2}{5}$ and not $-\tfrac{5}{2}$. Getting the sign right separates the A1 from the wrong-line-family error.
Q7MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §4 Equations from a Table · MCR3U A1.2 / A1.7 [8 marks]

Table $(x, y)$: $(-2, 11), (1, 5), (4, -1), (7, -7), (10, -13)$. (a) First differences. (b) $y = mx + b$. (c) $f(20)$. (d) $f^{-1}(x)$.

Answer:  (a) $\Delta y = -6$ constant for $\Delta x = 3$  ·  (b) $y = -2x + 7$  ·  (c) $f(20) = -33$  ·  (d) $f^{-1}(x) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,x + \tfrac{7}{2}$

(a) First differences M1·A1

With $\Delta x = 3$ between consecutive entries, the $y$-differences are $$ 5 - 11 = -6, \quad -1 - 5 = -6, \quad -7 - (-1) = -6, \quad -13 - (-7) = -6. $$ The first differences are constant at $-6$, so the relation is linear.

(b) Equation in $y = mx + b$ form M1·A1·A1

Slope $m = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{-6}{3} = -2$. To find $b$, substitute any point, e.g. $(-2, 11)$: $$ 11 \;=\; -2(-2) + b \;=\; 4 + b \;\Longrightarrow\; b \;=\; 7. $$ Hence $f(x) = -2x + 7$. (Verify against another point: $f(10) = -20 + 7 = -13$. $\checkmark$)

(c) Evaluate $f(20)$ A1

$f(20) = -2(20) + 7 = -40 + 7 = -33$.

(d) Inverse function M1·A1

Start from $y = -2x + 7$. Swap $x \leftrightarrow y$: $\;x = -2y + 7$. Solve for $y$: $$ 2y \;=\; 7 - x \;\Longrightarrow\; y \;=\; \tfrac{7 - x}{2} \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2}\,x + \tfrac{7}{2}. $$ So $f^{-1}(x) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,x + \tfrac{7}{2}$.
Slope of the inverse $=$ reciprocal of the slope, sign-preserved. A line $f$ with slope $m$ has inverse $f^{-1}$ with slope $1/m$ (not $-1/m$). Here $m = -2 \Rightarrow$ inverse slope $-\tfrac{1}{2}$, both negative. The negative-reciprocal rule is for perpendicular lines (Q6), not inverses. A line and its inverse are reflections in $y = x$, so a line of slope $-2$ reflects to a line of slope $-\tfrac{1}{2}$, same sign, reciprocal magnitude. Verify by checking that $f(f^{-1}(x)) = -2 \cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2}x + \tfrac{7}{2}) + 7 = x - 7 + 7 = x$. $\checkmark$
Q8HARD 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §5 Linear Systems · HSA-REI.C.5 / C.6 [9 marks]

System $3x + 2y = 16$, $5x - 4y = 1$. (a) Elimination. (b) Substitution check. (c) Why row operations preserve solutions. (d) Geometric meaning.

Answer:  (a), (b) $(x, y) = \left(3, \tfrac{7}{2}\right)$  ·  (d) two lines intersect at a single point

(a) Elimination M1·A1·A1

Multiply the first equation by $2$ to align the $y$-coefficient: $$ R_{1} \to 2 R_{1}: \quad 6x + 4y \;=\; 32. $$ Add to $R_{2}$ (the $y$-terms cancel): $$ R_{2} + (2R_{1}): \quad (5x - 4y) + (6x + 4y) \;=\; 1 + 32 \;\Longrightarrow\; 11x \;=\; 33 \;\Longrightarrow\; x \;=\; 3. $$ Back-substitute into $3x + 2y = 16$: $9 + 2y = 16 \Rightarrow 2y = 7 \Rightarrow y = \tfrac{7}{2}$. So $(x, y) = (3, \tfrac{7}{2})$.

(b) Substitution cross-check M1·A1·A1

From $3x + 2y = 16$, isolate $y$: $y = \dfrac{16 - 3x}{2}$. Substitute into $5x - 4y = 1$: $$ 5x - 4 \cdot \frac{16 - 3x}{2} \;=\; 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; 5x - 2(16 - 3x) \;=\; 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; 5x - 32 + 6x \;=\; 1. $$ Hence $11x = 33$, $x = 3$, $y = \dfrac{16 - 9}{2} = \tfrac{7}{2}$. Both methods give the same ordered pair $(3, \tfrac{7}{2})$.

(c) Why row operations preserve the solution set M1·R1

An elementary row operation of the form $R_{i} \to R_{i} + k R_{j}$ replaces equation $i$ with the sum of equation $i$ and $k$ times equation $j$. If $(x, y)$ satisfies both original equations, it satisfies the new equation $i$ (a true equality plus $k$ times another true equality is still true). The operation is reversible: applying $R_{i} \to R_{i} - k R_{j}$ to the new system returns the original, so any solution of the new system also solves the original. Hence the two systems have identical solution sets, this is exactly the substance of HSA-REI.C.5.

(d) Geometric meaning A1

The two equations are lines in the $xy$-plane with different slopes ($-\tfrac{3}{2}$ and $\tfrac{5}{4}$), so they are not parallel and meet at exactly one point, namely $(3, \tfrac{7}{2})$. The system is consistent and independent.
Picking the elimination multiplier: look at the LCM of the column you want to kill. The $y$-column has coefficients $+2$ and $-4$; their LCM is $4$. Multiplying $R_{1}$ by $2$ gives $+4$, which cancels $-4$ in one addition, no second multiplication needed, no sign hunt. Less obvious systems benefit from explicitly recording the row operation (e.g., $R_{2} \to R_{2} + 2 R_{1}$) so partial credit survives an arithmetic slip later. AP graders give an M1 for the row operation even when the A1 for the final answer goes missing.
Q9HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC 🇨🇦 ON AP-feeder FRQ §7 Matrix Row-Reduction · HSA-REI.C.8/9 (+) / BC PC12 / ON MCV4U [11 marks]

$3 \times 3$ system: $x + 2y + z = 9$, $2x - y + z = 3$, $x + y - z = 0$. (a) Augmented matrix. (b) Row-reduce. (c) Back-substitute. (d) Verify.

Answer:  (a) $\left[\begin{smallmatrix}1 & 2 & 1 & \big| & 9 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 & \big| & 3 \\ 1 & 1 & -1 & \big| & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$  ·  (c) $(x, y, z) = \left(1, \tfrac{7}{3}, \tfrac{10}{3}\right)$

(a) Augmented matrix A1

$$ [A \,|\, \mathbf{b}] \;=\; \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 1 & 9 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \\ 1 & 1 & -1 & 0 \end{array}\right]. $$

(b) Row-reduce to row-echelon form M1·A1·A1·A1·R1

Step 1. Clear column $1$ below the pivot using $R_{1}$: $$ R_{2} \to R_{2} - 2 R_{1}: \quad (2, -1, 1, 3) - 2(1, 2, 1, 9) \;=\; (0, -5, -1, -15). $$ $$ R_{3} \to R_{3} - R_{1}: \quad (1, 1, -1, 0) - (1, 2, 1, 9) \;=\; (0, -1, -2, -9). $$ State of the matrix: $$ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 1 & 9 \\ 0 & -5 & -1 & -15 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 & -9 \end{array}\right]. $$ Step 2. Clear column $2$ below the pivot in $R_{2}$ (pivot is $-5$). Use $R_{3} \to R_{3} - \tfrac{1}{5} R_{2}$: $$ R_{3} \to R_{3} - \tfrac{1}{5} R_{2}: \quad (0, -1, -2, -9) - \tfrac{1}{5}(0, -5, -1, -15). $$ Component-by-component: $-1 - \tfrac{1}{5}(-5) = -1 + 1 = 0$; $-2 - \tfrac{1}{5}(-1) = -2 + \tfrac{1}{5} = -\tfrac{9}{5}$; $-9 - \tfrac{1}{5}(-15) = -9 + 3 = -6$. So $$ R_{3} \;=\; \bigl(0, 0, -\tfrac{9}{5}, -6\bigr). $$ Step 3. (Optional) scale $R_{3}$ to make the pivot $1$: $R_{3} \to -\tfrac{5}{9} R_{3} = (0, 0, 1, \tfrac{10}{3})$. Row-echelon form: $$ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 1 & 9 \\ 0 & -5 & -1 & -15 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \tfrac{10}{3} \end{array}\right]. \quad \text{AG} $$

(c) Back-substitute M1·A1·A1

From $R_{3}$: $\;z = \tfrac{10}{3}$.
From $R_{2}$: $-5y - z = -15 \Rightarrow -5y = -15 + \tfrac{10}{3} = -\tfrac{45}{3} + \tfrac{10}{3} = -\tfrac{35}{3} \Rightarrow y = \tfrac{7}{3}$.
From $R_{1}$: $x + 2y + z = 9 \Rightarrow x = 9 - 2 \cdot \tfrac{7}{3} - \tfrac{10}{3} = 9 - \tfrac{14}{3} - \tfrac{10}{3} = 9 - \tfrac{24}{3} = 9 - 8 = 1$.
Hence $(x, y, z) = \left(1, \tfrac{7}{3}, \tfrac{10}{3}\right)$.

(d) Verify in each original equation A1·A1

  • $x + 2y + z = 1 + \tfrac{14}{3} + \tfrac{10}{3} = 1 + \tfrac{24}{3} = 1 + 8 = 9$. $\checkmark$
  • $2x - y + z = 2 - \tfrac{7}{3} + \tfrac{10}{3} = 2 + \tfrac{3}{3} = 2 + 1 = 3$. $\checkmark$
  • $x + y - z = 1 + \tfrac{7}{3} - \tfrac{10}{3} = 1 - \tfrac{3}{3} = 1 - 1 = 0$. $\checkmark$
Reading a $3 \times 3$ system off the row-reduced matrix. A $3 \times 3$ linear system has exactly four geometric outcomes, all visible in the rref of the augmented matrix. (1) Unique point, rref is the $3 \times 3$ identity with a column of constants on the right, three non-zero pivots, no zero rows; three planes meet at a single point (this question). (2) Line of solutions, exactly one zero row $[0\,0\,0\,|\,0]$, two pivots; the three planes share a common line (one free parameter). (3) Plane of solutions, two zero rows $[0\,0\,0\,|\,0]$, one pivot; the three "planes" are actually the same plane up to scaling. (4) No solution, a row of the form $[0\,0\,0\,|\,k]$ with $k \ne 0$; some pair of planes is parallel or the three planes form a triangular prism with no common point. Row-reduce first, then classify. The classification rule is robust to which order you perform the operations in, because rref is unique. This is the bridge from BC PC12 / ON MCV4U honors work into first-year linear algebra (rank-nullity).

Q10MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §6 Break-even · HSF-LE.B.5 [8 marks]

T-shirt sale. Cost $C(n) = 90 + 6.50 n$; revenue $R(n) = 12 n$. (a) Set up. (b) Interpret slope and intercept of $C$. (c) Break-even $n^{*}$. (d) Smallest whole-shirt profit and profit value.

Answer:  (a) $C(n) = 90 + 6.50 n$, $R(n) = 12 n$  ·  (c) $n^{*} = \tfrac{180}{11} \approx 16.36$  ·  (d) $17$ shirts $\Rightarrow$ profit $\$3.50$

(a) Set up the cost and revenue functions A1·A1

Cost has a fixed setup of $\$90$ plus $\$6.50$ per shirt: $C(n) = 90 + 6.50 n$.
Revenue: each shirt sells for $\$12$, so $R(n) = 12 n$. Both have domain $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ (whole shirts).

(b) Interpret slope and intercept of $C(n)$ A1·A1

Slope $6.50$: the marginal cost, the additional cost of producing one more shirt, in US dollars per shirt.
Intercept $90$: the fixed cost, the cost when $n = 0$, i.e. the one-time setup fee owed to the printer even if zero shirts are produced. This is exactly the interpretation Common Core HSF-LE.B.5 rewards.

(c) Break-even $n^{*}$ M1·A1

Set $R(n) = C(n)$: $$ 12 n \;=\; 90 + 6.50 n \;\Longrightarrow\; 5.50 n \;=\; 90 \;\Longrightarrow\; n^{*} \;=\; \frac{90}{5.50} \;=\; \frac{180}{11} \;\approx\; 16.36. $$

(d) Smallest whole-shirt profit M1·A1

Profit requires $R(n) > C(n)$, i.e. $n > 16.36$. The smallest whole number satisfying this is $n = 17$. Profit at $n = 17$: $$ P(17) \;=\; R(17) - C(17) \;=\; 12(17) - \bigl(90 + 6.50 \cdot 17\bigr) \;=\; 204 - (90 + 110.50) \;=\; 204 - 200.50 \;=\; \$3.50. $$
"Break-even" is a continuous answer; "smallest profitable" is a discrete answer. The break-even point $n^{*} = 16.36$ is the mathematically clean threshold, but no club can sell $0.36$ of a shirt. AP-feeder graders deduct when students answer "$n^{*} = 16$" or round break-even down, at $n = 16$, revenue is $\$192$ and cost is $\$194$, so the club is still losing $\$2$. The discrete answer is always $\lceil n^{*} \rceil$, the ceiling, not the floor. Get this dichotomy locked: the fractional break-even is the modelling output; the whole-shirt threshold is the contextual answer.
Q11HARD 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §6 Mixture System · MPM2D Analytic Geometry [10 marks]

Mixture: $24$ L of $35\%$ alcohol from $20\%$ ($x$ L) and $50\%$ ($y$ L) stock. (a) Volume eq. (b) Alcohol eq. (c) Solve. (d) Verify percentage. (e) One-sentence answer.

Answer:  (a) $x + y = 24$  ·  (b) $0.20 x + 0.50 y = 8.4$  ·  (c) $(x, y) = (12, 12)$  ·  (d) mixture is $35\%$

(a) Volume constraint A1

Total litres in the mixed solution equal the sum of the litres of each stock used: $$ x + y \;=\; 24. $$

(b) Alcohol constraint M1·A1

Litres of alcohol in $20\%$ stock: $0.20\,x$. Litres of alcohol in $50\%$ stock: $0.50\,y$. The target mixture contains $0.35 \times 24 = 8.4$ litres of alcohol. So $$ 0.20\,x + 0.50\,y \;=\; 8.4. $$

(c) Solve the system M1·A1·A1·A1

From (a), $x = 24 - y$. Substitute into (b): $$ 0.20\,(24 - y) + 0.50\,y \;=\; 8.4 \;\Longrightarrow\; 4.8 - 0.20\,y + 0.50\,y \;=\; 8.4. $$ Combine $y$-terms: $0.30\,y = 8.4 - 4.8 = 3.6$, so $$ y \;=\; \frac{3.6}{0.30} \;=\; 12 \;\;\text{L}. $$ Back-substitute: $x = 24 - 12 = 12$ L. So $(x, y) = (12, 12)$.

(d) Verify the percentage M1·A1

Total alcohol $= 0.20(12) + 0.50(12) = 2.4 + 6.0 = 8.4$ L. Percentage: $$ \frac{8.4}{24} \;=\; 0.35 \;=\; 35\%. \;\;\checkmark $$

(e) Concluding sentence A1

The technician must combine $12$ litres of the $20\%$ alcohol solution with $12$ litres of the $50\%$ alcohol solution to obtain $24$ litres of $35\%$ alcohol mixture.
The "weighted-average shortcut", and why the algebra still matters. The target $35\%$ sits exactly midway between $20\%$ and $50\%$: $35 = \tfrac{20 + 50}{2}$. By the linear weighted-average principle, when the target is the midpoint of the two stocks, the answer is always a $50 / 50$ split, half of each. So one could write the answer $x = y = 12$ in a single line. But provincial markers split the marks across setting up the system, solving it, and verifying the percentage; shortcut answers without the system earn 1 mark out of 10. The shortcut is a checking tool, not an answer. The deeper takeaway: in a two-stock mixture with target $t$, the ratio of stocks is $\dfrac{t - p_{2}}{p_{1} - t}$ where $p_{1}, p_{2}$ are the stock percentages, this is the "alligation rule" and generalises directly to weighted averages and centre-of-mass.
Q12MEDIUM 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §5 / §6 Rate System · FMPC10 systems [10 marks]

Two cyclists from the same trailhead. Maya: $18$ km/h from $t = 0$. Jin: $24$ km/h from $t = 0.5$. (a) $d_{M}(t)$. (b) $d_{J}(t)$ + restriction. (c) Catch-up time, $t$ and clock. (d) Sketch.

Answer:  (a) $d_{M}(t) = 18\,t$  ·  (b) $d_{J}(t) = 24\,t - 12$ for $t \ge 0.5$  ·  (c) $t = 2$ h, i.e. $11{:}00$ a.m.

(a) Maya's distance equation A1·A1

Maya leaves the trailhead at $t = 0$ at a constant $18$ km/h. With $d$ in km and $t$ in hours from $9{:}00$ a.m.: $$ d_{M}(t) \;=\; 18\,t, \quad t \ge 0. $$

(b) Jin's distance equation and restriction M1·A1·A1

Jin starts $30$ minutes ($0.5$ h) later. He has been riding for $(t - 0.5)$ hours at $24$ km/h, so $$ d_{J}(t) \;=\; 24\,(t - 0.5) \;=\; 24\,t - 12, \quad t \ge 0.5. $$ Restriction. The equation only models Jin's position from the moment he leaves; for $t < 0.5$ he is still at the trailhead ($d = 0$), so the linear model $24t - 12$ (which gives negative distances) does not apply.

(c) Catch-up time M1·A1·A1

Catch-up means $d_{M}(t) = d_{J}(t)$: $$ 18\,t \;=\; 24\,t - 12 \;\Longrightarrow\; 12 \;=\; 6\,t \;\Longrightarrow\; t \;=\; 2 \;\;\text{hours}. $$ At $t = 2$, Maya has ridden $18 \cdot 2 = 36$ km and Jin has ridden $24 \cdot 1.5 = 36$ km. $\checkmark$
Clock time: $9{:}00$ a.m. $+ \;2$ h $= \mathbf{11{:}00}$ a.m.

(d) Sketch on $(t, d)$ axes, $0 \le t \le 3$ M1·A1

Maya's line: through $(0, 0)$ and $(3, 54)$, slope $18$, drawn for all $t \in [0, 3]$.
Jin's line: through $(0.5, 0)$ and $(3, 60)$, slope $24$, drawn only for $t \in [0.5, 3]$.
Mark the $t$-intercept of Jin's line at $(0.5, 0)$ (his start time) and the intersection point $(2, 36)$. Label each line "$d_{M}$" and "$d_{J}$". Jin's line is steeper and crosses Maya's at $t = 2$, then climbs above.
The "head-start" structural pattern. Any "B leaves later and goes faster, when does B catch A?" problem is a $2 \times 2$ system with two linear functions of the same $t$: $d_{A}(t) = v_{A}\,t$ and $d_{B}(t) = v_{B}\,(t - t_{0})$. Setting them equal gives a single linear equation in $t$ with closed form $t^{*} = \dfrac{v_{B} \, t_{0}}{v_{B} - v_{A}}$. For this question: $t^{*} = \dfrac{24 \cdot 0.5}{24 - 18} = \dfrac{12}{6} = 2$. This same template solves the train-relay, boat-current, plane-vs-jet, and the "second runner with a head start" SAT problems. The domain restriction on $d_{J}$, easy to forget, is the BC-provincial marker's favourite trap: writing $d_{J}(t) = 24t - 12$ without "$t \ge 0.5$" surrenders a clean A1.
