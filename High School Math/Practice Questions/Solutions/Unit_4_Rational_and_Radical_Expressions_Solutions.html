Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Simplify Rationals · HSA-APR.D.6 [3 marks]

Simplified form of $\dfrac{x^{2} - 25}{x^{2} - 4 x - 5}$ with restrictions.

Answer:  (B)  $\dfrac{x + 5}{x + 1}$, $x \ne -1, 5$

(a) Factor numerator and denominator M1·A1

Numerator: $x^{2} - 25 = (x - 5)(x + 5)$ (difference of squares). Denominator: $x^{2} - 4x - 5 = (x - 5)(x + 1)$ (integers with product $-5$ and sum $-4$ are $-5$ and $1$). The full restriction set comes from the original denominator zeros: $x - 5 = 0$ or $x + 1 = 0$, so $x \ne 5$ and $x \ne -1$.

(b) Cancel the common factor; reaffirm restrictions A1

$$ \frac{(x - 5)(x + 5)}{(x - 5)(x + 1)} \;=\; \frac{x + 5}{x + 1}, \qquad x \ne -1, 5. $$ Match: option (B).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) $\frac{x - 5}{x - 1}$, $x \ne 5$, denominator mis-factored as $(x-5)(x-1)$, which expands to $x^2-6x+5$, not $x^2-4x-5$. Also drops the $x \ne -1$ restriction.
  • (C) $\frac{x + 5}{x - 1}$ (no restrictions), correct form of the simplified fraction would still need restrictions; "no restrictions" violates HSA-APR.D.6, which requires that the simplified rational be equivalent to the original on the original domain.
  • (D) $\frac{x + 5}{x + 1}$, $x \ne -1$, correct simplified form but drops $x \ne 5$. After cancellation the $(x-5)$ factor is invisible, but the hole at $x = 5$ in the original function survives, restrictions must be stated before cancellation.
Restrictions are read off the original denominator, not the simplified one. Cancellation hides domain information; HSA-APR.D.6 requires that every zero of the original denominator be listed, even if the corresponding factor cancels. The diagnostic: state restrictions first, then simplify. A cancelled factor like $(x-5)$ in this question marks a point discontinuity (hole), not a vertical asymptote, the function value is undefined at $x = 5$ but the limit exists. (This vocabulary returns in Q9.) Distractor (D) is the classic trap: the simplified expression is correct but the restriction list is incomplete.
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §5 Radical Operations · HSN-RN.A.2 [3 marks]

Simplify $\sqrt{50} + \sqrt{18} - \sqrt{8}$.

Answer:  (A)  $6\sqrt{2}$

(a) Extract the largest perfect-square factor from each radicand M1·A1

$\sqrt{50} = \sqrt{25 \cdot 2} = 5\sqrt{2}$; $\sqrt{18} = \sqrt{9 \cdot 2} = 3\sqrt{2}$; $\sqrt{8} = \sqrt{4 \cdot 2} = 2\sqrt{2}$. Every term now carries the same radical $\sqrt{2}$, they are like radicals and combine just as $\sqrt{2}$ were a single variable.

(b) Combine like radicals A1

$$ 5\sqrt{2} + 3\sqrt{2} - 2\sqrt{2} \;=\; (5 + 3 - 2)\sqrt{2} \;=\; 6\sqrt{2}. $$ Match: option (A).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (B) $\sqrt{60}$, radicands cannot be added inside a single radical. $\sqrt{50}+\sqrt{18}-\sqrt{8} \ne \sqrt{50+18-8}$; the operation $\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b} = \sqrt{a+b}$ is false in general.
  • (C) $10\sqrt{2}$, arithmetic slip: $5 + 3 + 2 = 10$ would arise from adding the third term instead of subtracting.
  • (D) $2\sqrt{10}$, algebraic-looking decoy with the wrong magnitude; $2\sqrt{10} \approx 6.32$ is close to the correct $6\sqrt{2} \approx 8.49$ but not equal.
Like radicals combine like like terms, they must share both index and radicand. $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3}$ do not combine; $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt[3]{2}$ do not combine. The simplification habit: pull out the largest perfect-square factor first, then check whether the leftover radicals match. The SAT loves seeding choice (B) as the "add the radicands" trap, it tests whether the student has internalised that radicals are operators, not just labels.
Q3EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §4 Horizontal Asymptote · HSF-IF.B.4 [3 marks]

Horizontal asymptote of $f(x) = \dfrac{4 x^{2} + 3 x - 1}{2 x^{2} - 5}$.

Answer:  (B)  $y = 2$

(a) Compare degrees M1

Numerator degree $= 2$; denominator degree $= 2$. Equal degrees. The horizontal-asymptote rule for rational functions:
  • If $\deg(\text{num}) < \deg(\text{den})$: HA at $y = 0$.
  • If $\deg(\text{num}) = \deg(\text{den})$: HA at $y = \dfrac{\text{leading coeff of num}}{\text{leading coeff of den}}$.
  • If $\deg(\text{num}) = \deg(\text{den}) + 1$: no HA, but a slant asymptote (long division).
  • If $\deg(\text{num}) > \deg(\text{den}) + 1$: no HA, no slant.

(b) Apply the equal-degree shortcut A1·A1

Ratio of leading coefficients: $\dfrac{4}{2} = 2$. So $y = 2$ is the HA. Match: option (B).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) $y = 0$, applies only when the numerator's degree is strictly less than the denominator's. Here degrees are equal.
  • (C) $y = -1/5$, wrong shortcut: ratio of the constant terms $-1/-5 = 1/5$ (with a sign error). The HA shortcut uses leading coefficients, not constants, as $x \to \infty$ the leading terms dominate.
  • (D) No HA; slant asymptote instead, applies only when $\deg(\text{num}) = \deg(\text{den}) + 1$ exactly. Here degrees are equal, not off by one.
End-behaviour is a leading-coefficient race. As $|x| \to \infty$ every non-leading term becomes negligible: $\frac{4x^2 + 3x - 1}{2x^2 - 5} \approx \frac{4x^2}{2x^2} = 2$. The constant terms $-1$ and $-5$ never enter the asymptote, they affect transient behaviour for small $x$ but vanish at infinity. The four-case degree comparison above is worth memorising as a single block: AP/SAT/Provincial all test it identically and a student who reaches for long division on every problem wastes time on the easy half of the exam.
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §2 Add & Subtract Rationals · MHF4U Strand C [6 marks]

Combine into single rational in lowest terms; state restrictions. (a) $\frac{3}{x-2}+\frac{5}{x+4}$. (b) $\frac{2x}{x^2-9}-\frac{1}{x-3}$.

Answer:  (a) $\dfrac{2(4x + 1)}{(x - 2)(x + 4)}$, $x \ne 2, -4$  ·  (b) $\dfrac{1}{x + 3}$, $x \ne 3, -3$

(a) Form the LCD; combine; state restrictions M1·A1·A1

Denominators $(x-2)$ and $(x+4)$ share no factor, so LCD $= (x-2)(x+4)$. Restrictions: $x \ne 2$ and $x \ne -4$ (stated before clearing). $$ \frac{3}{x-2} + \frac{5}{x+4} \;=\; \frac{3(x+4) + 5(x-2)}{(x-2)(x+4)} \;=\; \frac{3x + 12 + 5x - 10}{(x-2)(x+4)} \;=\; \frac{8x + 2}{(x-2)(x+4)} \;=\; \frac{2(4x + 1)}{(x-2)(x+4)}. $$ The numerator $8x + 2 = 2(4x + 1)$ shares no factor with the denominator, so the form is in lowest terms.

(b) Factor the first denominator; form LCD; combine M1·A1·A1

$x^2 - 9 = (x - 3)(x + 3)$, so denominators are $(x-3)(x+3)$ and $(x-3)$. LCD $= (x-3)(x+3)$. Restrictions: $x \ne 3$ and $x \ne -3$ (stated before clearing). $$ \frac{2x}{(x-3)(x+3)} - \frac{1}{x-3} \;=\; \frac{2x - (x+3)}{(x-3)(x+3)} \;=\; \frac{2x - x - 3}{(x-3)(x+3)} \;=\; \frac{x - 3}{(x-3)(x+3)}. $$ The $(x-3)$ factor cancels, leaving $$ \frac{1}{x + 3}, \qquad x \ne 3, -3. $$ Reaffirm both restrictions: the surviving denominator $x + 3$ forces $x \ne -3$, and the cancelled $(x - 3)$ leaves a point discontinuity at $x = 3$, both are required by HSA-APR.D.6.
State restrictions before finding the LCD, never after cancellation. If you wait until the simplified form to read restrictions, the cancelled factor's zero becomes invisible and you lose marks. The professional protocol is a fixed three-step sequence: (1) factor every denominator, (2) read the restriction set off the union of zeros, (3) only then form the LCD and combine. Part (b) is the canonical trap: the answer looks like $\frac{1}{x+3}$ with restriction $x \ne -3$, but the original $\frac{2x}{x^2-9}$ also excluded $x = 3$. Provincial markers deduct an A1 for incomplete restrictions every time.
Q5MEDIUMHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §7 Rational Exponents · HSN-RN.A.1 / BC PC11 [7 marks]

$x > 0$ throughout. (a) Evaluate $27^{2/3}$ and $32^{-3/5}$. (b) Single-power form of $\frac{\sqrt{x}\cdot\sqrt[3]{x^2}}{\sqrt[6]{x^5}}$. (c) Cite HSN-RN.A.1.

Answer:  (a) $27^{2/3} = 9$, $32^{-3/5} = \dfrac{1}{8}$  ·  (b) $x^{1/3}$  ·  (c) see cited explanation

(a) Evaluate using $a^{m/n} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^{m}$ A1·A1

$27^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{27})^{2} = 3^{2} = 9$. (Take the cube root first to keep magnitudes small, then square, the inner-root-first order is computationally easier than $(27^{2})^{1/3} = 729^{1/3}$.)
$32^{-3/5} = \dfrac{1}{32^{3/5}} = \dfrac{1}{(\sqrt[5]{32})^{3}} = \dfrac{1}{2^{3}} = \dfrac{1}{8}$. (Negative exponent flips to reciprocal first; then root-then-power as above.)

(b) Convert each radical to a rational exponent; combine via LCD of fractions M1·A1·A1

$\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$, $\sqrt[3]{x^{2}} = x^{2/3}$, $\sqrt[6]{x^{5}} = x^{5/6}$. So $$ \frac{\sqrt{x}\cdot\sqrt[3]{x^{2}}}{\sqrt[6]{x^{5}}} \;=\; \frac{x^{1/2} \cdot x^{2/3}}{x^{5/6}} \;=\; x^{1/2 + 2/3 - 5/6}. $$ LCD of $\{2, 3, 6\}$ is $6$: $\frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{6}$, $\frac{2}{3} = \frac{4}{6}$, $\frac{5}{6} = \frac{5}{6}$. So the exponent is $\frac{3 + 4 - 5}{6} = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$. Final form: $\;x^{1/3}$ (equivalently $\sqrt[3]{x}$).

(c) Justify the radical–exponent identification via HSN-RN.A.1 R1·R1

HSN-RN.A.1: "Explain how the definition of the meaning of rational exponents follows from extending the properties of integer exponents to those values, allowing for a notation for radicals in terms of rational exponents." Concretely: if the exponent law $(a^{p})^{q} = a^{pq}$ must continue to hold for rational $p, q$, then $(a^{1/n})^{n} = a^{(1/n) \cdot n} = a^{1} = a$. But the unique positive number whose $n$-th power is $a$ (for $a > 0$) is by definition $\sqrt[n]{a}$. Hence $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$ is forced by the requirement of preserving the integer-exponent law, it is the only definition that makes the algebra of exponents consistent across rationals.
Rational exponent and radical are two notations for one object, pick whichever simplifies the algebra in front of you. The rational-exponent form excels at multiplicative combination (Q5b: just add fractions in the exponent). The radical form excels at numerical evaluation when the inner root is clean (Q5a: $\sqrt[3]{27} = 3$ is a one-liner). The fluent move is to convert in the direction that shortens the work: rationals-in $\to$ exponent arithmetic; nice integer roots $\to$ stay radical. The HSN-RN.A.1 justification matters for AP graders because it underlies why the law $a^{m/n} \cdot a^{p/q} = a^{m/n + p/q}$ holds in the first place, it's not a definition imposed by convention but a theorem forced by consistency.

Q6MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Add & Subtract Rationals · HSA-APR.D.6 [8 marks]

$R(x) = \frac{x+1}{x^2-4} + \frac{2}{x-2} - \frac{1}{x+2}$. (a) LCD + restrictions. (b) Combine; expand numerator. (c) Simplify; full restrictions.

Answer:  (a) LCD $= (x-2)(x+2)$, $x \ne 2, -2$  ·  (b) $R(x) = \dfrac{2x + 7}{(x-2)(x+2)}$  ·  (c) already in lowest terms; $x \ne 2, -2$

(a) Factor every denominator; state LCD and restrictions M1·A1

$x^{2} - 4 = (x - 2)(x + 2)$; the other two denominators are $(x - 2)$ and $(x + 2)$. LCD $= (x - 2)(x + 2)$. Restriction set (stated before combining): $x \ne 2$ and $x \ne -2$.

(b) Rewrite each term over the LCD; expand and collect the numerator M1·A1·A1·A1

$$ R(x) \;=\; \frac{x+1}{(x-2)(x+2)} + \frac{2(x+2)}{(x-2)(x+2)} - \frac{1(x-2)}{(x-2)(x+2)} \;=\; \frac{(x+1) + 2(x+2) - (x-2)}{(x-2)(x+2)}. $$ Expand and collect: $$ (x + 1) + (2x + 4) - (x - 2) \;=\; x + 1 + 2x + 4 - x + 2 \;=\; 2x + 7. $$ So $R(x) = \dfrac{2x + 7}{(x - 2)(x + 2)}$. The numerator $2x + 7$ is linear with root $x = -7/2$, which is neither $2$ nor $-2$, so no factor of the denominator divides it, already in lowest terms.

(c) Final form; reaffirm full restriction set A1

$$ R(x) \;=\; \frac{2x + 7}{(x - 2)(x + 2)}, \qquad x \ne 2, -2. $$ No cancellation occurred, so the restriction set from part (a) carries through unchanged. The simplified form is equivalent to the original $R(x)$ on the original domain, satisfying HSA-APR.D.6.
Always do the sign of the subtraction before reaching for the LCD. The most common mark-losing error in Q6-style problems is dropping the minus sign on $\frac{1}{x+2}$: students write $(x+1) + 2(x+2) - 1 \cdot (x-2)$ but expand the last term as $(x - 2)$ instead of $-(x - 2) = -x + 2$. The bracket discipline: write the subtraction with its bracket, "$-(x - 2)$", before distributing, never as "$- x - 2$". When the numerator does not factor against the denominator (as here, $2x+7$ has root $-7/2$), the work is done; no cancellation step is needed beyond the audit.
Q7MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §3 Rational Equations · HSA-REI.A.2 [9 marks]

Solve, state restrictions before clearing, verify each candidate. (a) $\frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{1}{x+1}=\frac{4}{x^2-1}$. (b) $\frac{x}{x-3}-\frac{2}{x}=\frac{6}{x(x-3)}$. (c) Cite HSA-REI.A.2.

Answer:  (a) $x = 2$ (verified)  ·  (b) $x = 2$; $x = 0$ extraneous  ·  (c) see cited explanation

(a) Restrictions $\to$ LCD $\to$ solve $\to$ verify M1·A1·A1·A1

$x^{2} - 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1)$. Restrictions: $x \ne 1, -1$. LCD $= (x - 1)(x + 1)$. Multiply through: $$ (x + 1) + (x - 1) \;=\; 4 \;\Longrightarrow\; 2x \;=\; 4 \;\Longrightarrow\; x \;=\; 2. $$ Verification (required for rational equations): $x = 2$ satisfies the restrictions ($2 \ne \pm 1$). LHS: $\frac{1}{1} + \frac{1}{3} = 1 + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{4}{3}$. RHS: $\frac{4}{4 - 1} = \frac{4}{3}$. LHS $=$ RHS. $\checkmark$ Solution: $x = 2$, with no extraneous candidates.

(b) Restrictions $\to$ LCD $\to$ solve $\to$ verify; flag extraneous root M1·A1·A1·A1

Restrictions: $x \ne 0$ and $x \ne 3$. LCD $= x(x - 3)$. Multiply through: $$ x \cdot x \;-\; 2(x - 3) \;=\; 6 \;\Longrightarrow\; x^{2} - 2x + 6 \;=\; 6 \;\Longrightarrow\; x^{2} - 2x \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x(x - 2) \;=\; 0. $$ Candidate roots: $x = 0$ and $x = 2$.
Verification of $x = 0$: violates the restriction $x \ne 0$ (would force division by zero in the original equation). Extraneous, reject.
Verification of $x = 2$: $2 \ne 0$ and $2 \ne 3$, so the restriction is satisfied. LHS: $\frac{2}{-1} - \frac{2}{2} = -2 - 1 = -3$. RHS: $\frac{6}{2 \cdot (-1)} = \frac{6}{-2} = -3$. LHS $=$ RHS. $\checkmark$ Solution: $x = 2$ only.

(c) Cite HSA-REI.A.2 R1

HSA-REI.A.2: "Solve simple rational and radical equations in one variable, and give examples showing how extraneous solutions may arise." Multiplying both sides of an equation by an expression containing the variable (here, the LCD $x(x - 3)$) is not a reversible operation at the values that make the multiplier zero. Those zero-of-LCD values can become "solutions" of the cleared polynomial equation but are not solutions of the original rational equation, hence the verification step is structurally mandatory in part (b) to catch $x = 0$ as extraneous. (In part (a) the cleared equation happened to have no zero-of-LCD root, so verification turned up no extraneous solutions; the step is still required, not optional.)
Extraneous roots are an artefact of multiplying by a variable expression, not of arithmetic error. Two distinct philosophies for solving rational equations: (i) Common denominator: multiply both sides by the LCD, reducing to a polynomial equation. Always works; produces extraneous roots when a candidate makes the LCD zero. (ii) Cross-multiplication: only valid when each side is a single fraction; faster on 2-term equations but error-prone when more terms are present (you must combine first). The provincial-style protocol locks in approach (i) with explicit restriction-then-verify because it scales to any number of terms. Approach (ii) is fine on $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$ but stops being a shortcut once the equation has three or more rational terms, by then you have to find the LCD anyway, so just start there.
Q8HARD 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §6 Radical Equations · BC PC11 radical equations [8 marks]

Isolate, square, verify; state radicand-non-negative condition. (a) $\sqrt{3x+1}=x-1$. (b) $\sqrt{x+4}-\sqrt{x-3}=1$. (c) Identify the extraneous-root step.

Answer:  (a) $x = 5$; $x = 0$ extraneous  ·  (b) $x = 12$  ·  (c) squaring step introduces the extraneous root

(a) Single squaring; reject any candidate that fails verification M1·A1·A1

Radicand-non-negative: $3x + 1 \ge 0 \Leftrightarrow x \ge -\tfrac{1}{3}$. RHS-non-negative (since the square root is non-negative): $x - 1 \ge 0 \Leftrightarrow x \ge 1$. Combined constraint: $x \ge 1$.
Square both sides: $$ 3x + 1 \;=\; (x - 1)^{2} \;=\; x^{2} - 2x + 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; x^{2} - 5x \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x(x - 5) \;=\; 0. $$ Candidates: $x = 0$ and $x = 5$.
Verify $x = 0$: LHS $= \sqrt{1} = 1$; RHS $= -1$. LHS $\ne$ RHS. Also fails the $x \ge 1$ constraint. Extraneous.
Verify $x = 5$: LHS $= \sqrt{16} = 4$; RHS $= 4$. $\checkmark$ Solution: $x = 5$.

(b) Two squarings; isolate one radical at a time M1·A1·A1·A1

Radicand-non-negative conditions: $x + 4 \ge 0$ and $x - 3 \ge 0$, so $x \ge 3$.
Isolate one radical: $\sqrt{x + 4} = 1 + \sqrt{x - 3}$. Square both sides: $$ x + 4 \;=\; \bigl(1 + \sqrt{x - 3}\bigr)^{2} \;=\; 1 + 2\sqrt{x - 3} + (x - 3) \;=\; x - 2 + 2\sqrt{x - 3}. $$ Isolate the remaining radical: $6 = 2\sqrt{x - 3}$, so $\sqrt{x - 3} = 3$. Square again: $x - 3 = 9 \Rightarrow x = 12$.
Verify $x = 12$: $\sqrt{16} - \sqrt{9} = 4 - 3 = 1$. $\checkmark$ Also $12 \ge 3$. Solution: $x = 12$ (no extraneous candidate emerged this time).

(c) BC PC11 Big Idea + extraneous-root source R1

BC PC11 Big Idea: "The meanings of, and connections between, operations extend to powers, radicals, and polynomials." The single step in part (b) that introduces the possibility of an extraneous root is the squaring: $\sqrt{x + 4} = 1 + \sqrt{x - 3}$ implies $x + 4 = (1 + \sqrt{x - 3})^{2}$, but the converse fails because $(\cdot)^{2}$ is not injective, both $+u$ and $-u$ square to $u^{2}$. (Each subsequent squaring is another chance for an extraneous root, which is why two-radical equations need verification at the end.)
Squaring is a non-injective map; verification is not optional. The function $u \mapsto u^{2}$ collapses $+u$ and $-u$ to the same image, so the equation after squaring may have more solutions than the original. The "candidate set $\supseteq$ true solution set" inclusion can only be checked by substitution into the original equation. Two radical-specific protocol moves: (1) state the radicand-non-negative conditions before squaring, they prune candidates before you compute; (2) when the RHS of a $\sqrt{\cdot} = (\text{expression})$ equation has a sign you can determine (e.g., RHS $= x - 1$ in (a) must be $\ge 0$), add that as a constraint too. Both habits compress the work and catch extraneous roots structurally rather than by accident.
Q9HARDHonors 🇨🇦 BC 🇨🇦 ON AP-feeder FRQ §4 Rational Function · BC PC12 / MHF4U Strand C [11 marks]

$f(x)=\frac{x^2-x-6}{x^2-4x+3}$. (a) Factor. (b) Hole. (c) VA. (d) HA, $x$-int, $y$-int. (e) Domain.

Answer:  (a) $\dfrac{(x-3)(x+2)}{(x-3)(x-1)}$, common $(x-3)$  ·  (b) hole at $\bigl(3, \tfrac{5}{2}\bigr)$  ·  (c) VA $x = 1$  ·  (d) HA $y = 1$; $x$-int $(-2, 0)$; $y$-int $(0, -2)$  ·  (e) $\{x \in \mathbb{R} \,:\, x \ne 1, 3\}$

(a) Factor numerator and denominator M1·A1

Numerator $x^{2} - x - 6$: integers with product $-6$ and sum $-1$ are $-3$ and $2$, so $(x - 3)(x + 2)$. Denominator $x^{2} - 4x + 3$: integers with product $3$ and sum $-4$ are $-3$ and $-1$, so $(x - 3)(x - 1)$. Common factor: $(x - 3)$. So $$ f(x) \;=\; \frac{(x - 3)(x + 2)}{(x - 3)(x - 1)}. $$

(b) Point discontinuity (hole) M1·A1

The cancelled factor $(x - 3)$ produces a hole at $x = 3$. After cancellation, $f^{*}(x) = \dfrac{x + 2}{x - 1}$, and $f^{*}(3) = \dfrac{5}{2}$. So the hole is at $\bigl(3, \tfrac{5}{2}\bigr)$.

(c) Vertical asymptote(s) M1·A1

Apply the rule "denominator-only zero $\Rightarrow$ VA; common-factor zero $\Rightarrow$ hole." Denominator zeros are $x = 3$ (common factor) and $x = 1$ (denominator-only). So the only VA is at $x = 1$. Sign analysis confirms: as $x \to 1^{-}$, $f^{*}(x) = \frac{x+2}{x-1} \to \frac{3}{0^{-}} = -\infty$; as $x \to 1^{+}$, $f^{*}(x) \to +\infty$. Standard VA behaviour.

(d) Horizontal asymptote; intercepts M1·A1·A1

HA by degree comparison (Q3 shortcut): both num and den have degree $2$, ratio of leading coefficients $\frac{1}{1} = 1$. So $y = 1$.
$x$-intercept: solve $f^{*}(x) = 0 \Leftrightarrow x + 2 = 0 \Leftrightarrow x = -2$. (Numerator zero, denominator non-zero: $-2 - 1 = -3 \ne 0$.) Point: $(-2, 0)$.
$y$-intercept: $f^{*}(0) = \frac{2}{-1} = -2$. (Also satisfies the original: $0$ is not in the excluded set.) Point: $(0, -2)$.

(e) Domain in set-builder notation A1·A1

The original denominator zeros are $x = 1$ and $x = 3$. Both are excluded, even though the $x = 3$ exclusion is invisible in the simplified $f^{*}$, BC PC12's "domain" elaboration explicitly requires that cancelled-factor zeros remain excluded, since the original $f$ is undefined there. Domain: $$ \{\,x \in \mathbb{R} \,:\, x \ne 1 \text{ and } x \ne 3\,\}, \qquad \text{or equivalently} \qquad \mathbb{R} \setminus \{1, 3\}. $$
Hole vs. vertical asymptote: read the factorisation, not the equation. The rule is structural and never about substitution: (denominator factor that cancels) $\Rightarrow$ hole at that $x$; (denominator factor that does not cancel) $\Rightarrow$ vertical asymptote at that $x$. Sign analysis (the $\pm \infty$ behaviour as $x$ approaches the VA from each side) is a consequence, not the test. The hole's $y$-coordinate is read off the simplified $f^{*}$ at the cancelled $x$, not off the original (which is undefined there). Domain follows the original: every original-denominator zero is excluded, full stop. BC PC12 markers test this distinction every year, the simplified function and the original function have the same VA and HA but different domains, and the gap is the cancelled-factor hole.

Q10MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §6 Radical Equation Modeling · HSA-REI.A.2 [10 marks]

Pendulum: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$, $g = 9.8$. (a) $T$ for $L = 0.994$. (b) Solve for $L(T)$. (c) $L$ when $T = 3.5$. (d) Verify; cite HSA-REI.A.2.

Answer:  (a) $T \approx 2.001$ s  ·  (b) $L = \dfrac{g T^{2}}{4\pi^{2}}$  ·  (c) $L \approx 3.04$ m  ·  (d) back-substitution gives $T \approx 3.500$ s. $\checkmark$

(a) Substitute and compute M1·A1

$$ T \;=\; 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{0.994}{9.8}} \;=\; 2\pi\sqrt{0.10143} \;=\; 2\pi \cdot 0.31848 \;\approx\; 2.001 \;\text{s}. $$ (To three decimal places. The $L = 0.994$ m specification is chosen so that the period is essentially exactly $2$ seconds, the standard grandfather-clock "seconds pendulum" calibration.)

(b) Solve $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ for $L$ M1·A1·A1

Square both sides (legal because $T > 0$ and $L > 0$, so both sides are non-negative and squaring is reversible on the situational domain): $$ T^{2} \;=\; 4\pi^{2} \cdot \dfrac{L}{g} \;\Longrightarrow\; L \;=\; \dfrac{g T^{2}}{4 \pi^{2}}, \qquad L > 0, \;\; T > 0. $$

(c) Compute $L$ for $T = 3.5$ s M1·A1·A1

$$ L \;=\; \dfrac{9.8 \cdot (3.5)^{2}}{4\pi^{2}} \;=\; \dfrac{9.8 \cdot 12.25}{4\pi^{2}} \;=\; \dfrac{120.05}{39.478} \;\approx\; 3.04 \;\text{m}. $$

(d) Verify by back-substitution; cite HSA-REI.A.2 A1·R1

Back-substitute $L = 3.04$ into the original: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{3.04/9.8} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.31020} = 2\pi \cdot 0.55696 \approx 3.500$ s. $\checkmark$
HSA-REI.A.2: "Solve simple rational and radical equations in one variable, and give examples showing how extraneous solutions may arise." Squaring is a non-injective operation ($u^{2} = v^{2}$ does not imply $u = v$, only $u = \pm v$), so the algebra in part (b) could in principle introduce extraneous solutions, explicit verification in part (d) confirms that the $L$ found really does produce the target $T$ in the original radical equation. (In this question the situational constraint $T > 0$, $L > 0$ already rules out the spurious negative branch, but the verification step is the formal closure of the radical-equation protocol.)
Squaring is reversible on the situational domain $T, L > 0$, but you must say so. Models with physical quantities (period, length, mass, time) almost always restrict to positives, and on the positive ray squaring is injective. Once you cite the restriction, the algebra is bidirectional and no extraneous root can creep in. AP markers reward students who name this, the citation of HSA-REI.A.2 plus the back-substitution check is what distinguishes a 4-mark answer from a 3-mark answer. The pendulum model is also useful as a sanity check: the period scales like $\sqrt{L}$, so quadrupling $L$ doubles $T$. Compare (a) ($L \approx 1$ m, $T \approx 2$ s) with (c) ($L \approx 3$ m, $T \approx 3.5$ s), the ratio $\sqrt{3.04/0.994} = \sqrt{3.057} \approx 1.749$ matches $3.500/2.001 \approx 1.749$. $\checkmark$
Q11MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §3 Rational Equation Modeling · MHF4U Strand C [11 marks]

Work-rate. Aanya alone $= x$ h, Ben alone $= x+3$ h, together $= 2$ h. (a) Rates. (b) Rational equation + restrictions. (c) Quadratic + formula. (d) Reject negative; state times. (e) Verify $1/x + 1/(x+3) = 1/2$.

Answer:  (a) Aanya $\frac{1}{x}$, Ben $\frac{1}{x+3}$ (jobs/h)  ·  (b) $\frac{1}{x}+\frac{1}{x+3}=\frac{1}{2}$, $x > 0$, $x \ne 0, -3$  ·  (c) $x^{2} - x - 6 = 0$, $x = 3$ or $x = -2$  ·  (d) Aanya $3.0$ h, Ben $6.0$ h  ·  (e) $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.500$. $\checkmark$

(a) Rates of work A1·A1

"Rate of work" $=$ fraction of one job completed per hour. Aanya finishes one full job in $x$ hours, so her rate is $\frac{1}{x}$ jobs per hour. Ben's rate is $\frac{1}{x + 3}$.

(b) Combined-rate equation; restrictions M1·A1

Together for $2$ hours they complete one full job, so $2 \cdot (\text{Aanya} + \text{Ben}) = 1$: $$ 2\left(\frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{x + 3}\right) \;=\; 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{x + 3} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}. $$ Algebraic restrictions: $x \ne 0$ and $x \ne -3$. Situational restriction: $x > 0$ (an alone-time of zero or negative hours is meaningless).

(c) Multiply by LCD; reduce to quadratic; quadratic formula M1·A1·A1·A1

LCD $= 2x(x + 3)$. Multiply through: $$ 2(x + 3) + 2x \;=\; x(x + 3) \;\Longrightarrow\; 4x + 6 \;=\; x^{2} + 3x \;\Longrightarrow\; x^{2} - x - 6 \;=\; 0. $$ Quadratic formula (Unit 2): $$ x \;=\; \dfrac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 24}}{2} \;=\; \dfrac{1 \pm 5}{2} \;\Longrightarrow\; x \;=\; 3 \;\text{ or }\; x \;=\; -2. $$ (The roots are exact integers; equivalently $x^{2} - x - 6 = (x - 3)(x + 2) = 0$.)

(d) Reject negative root; state alone-times A1·A1

The negative root $x = -2$ violates the situational domain $x > 0$ (a worker cannot have a negative completion time, even though the algebra produces it as a candidate). The positive root $x = 3$ is the unique physical solution. Aanya: $x = 3.0$ h. Ben: $x + 3 = 6.0$ h.
(Note: the Practice prompt asks for one-decimal rounding, but the roots here are exact integers, see the Practice inconsistency flagged at the foot of this solution. Stating $3.0$ h and $6.0$ h satisfies the rounding instruction trivially.)

(e) Verification A1

$$ \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6} \;=\; \frac{2}{6} + \frac{1}{6} \;=\; \frac{3}{6} \;=\; \frac{1}{2} \;=\; 0.500. \;\checkmark $$ The combined hourly rate is exactly $\frac{1}{2}$ jobs/h, so two hours of joint work complete exactly one job, as required.
Modeling restrictions can be stronger than algebraic ones. The algebra forbids only $x = 0$ and $x = -3$ (zeros of the LCD), but the situational context forbids the entire interval $x \le 0$, a negative alone-time is meaningless. The negative root $x = -2$ passes the algebraic restriction (it's not $0$ or $-3$) and even satisfies the original equation as a number ($\frac{1}{-2} + \frac{1}{1} = -\frac{1}{2} + 1 = \frac{1}{2}$ $\checkmark$), but it's rejected on situational grounds, not algebraic ones. The ON Provincial marker awards the A1 for the justification, not the rejection alone, saying "$x > 0$ is required because an alone-completion-time must be positive" is the load-bearing sentence.
Q12HARD 🇨🇦 BC 🇺🇸 US BC Provincial-style §2 + §4 Rational Function App · BC PC12 / HSA-CED.A.1 [11 marks]

Bottling plant. $\bar{C}(x) = (240 + 0.60x)/x$, $x > 0$. (a) Split into sum. (b) $\bar{C}(100), \bar{C}(400), \bar{C}(1200)$. (c) HA + interpretation. (d) Break-even at $0.85. (e) Situational domain.

Answer:  (a) $\bar{C}(x) = \dfrac{240}{x} + 0.60$  ·  (b) $2.50, $1.20, $0.80  ·  (c) HA $\bar{C} = 0.60$  ·  (d) $x = 960$ bottles/day  ·  (e) $x > 0$

(a) Split single rational into a sum M1·A1

$$ \bar{C}(x) \;=\; \dfrac{240 + 0.60 x}{x} \;=\; \dfrac{240}{x} + \dfrac{0.60 x}{x} \;=\; \dfrac{240}{x} + 0.60. $$ The $\frac{240}{x}$ term came from the fixed cost ($240/day) spread across $x$ bottles, it's a true rational term that shrinks as production rises. The constant $0.60$ came from the variable cost ($0.60/bottle) and is independent of $x$.

(b) Three point values A1·A1·A1

$\bar{C}(100) = \frac{240}{100} + 0.60 = 2.40 + 0.60 = 2.50$, i.e. $2.50/bottle.
$\bar{C}(400) = \frac{240}{400} + 0.60 = 0.60 + 0.60 = 1.20$, i.e. $1.20/bottle.
$\bar{C}(1200) = \frac{240}{1200} + 0.60 = 0.20 + 0.60 = 0.80$, i.e. $0.80/bottle.
As $x$ increases, the $\frac{240}{x}$ term shrinks toward $0$, so the per-bottle cost decreases, the fixed cost gets amortised over more units (economies of scale).

(c) Horizontal asymptote and interpretation M1·A1·A1

Two ways to see the HA:
Algebraic: degree of numerator $= 1$, degree of denominator $= 1$, so ratio of leading coefficients $= \frac{0.60}{1} = 0.60$.
Split form: as $x \to \infty$, $\frac{240}{x} \to 0$ and $\bar{C}(x) \to 0.60$ directly from the sum decomposition.
Either way, the HA is $\bar{C} = 0.60$ (or, in context, $0.60 per bottle). Interpretation: as the plant scales up, the average cost per bottle approaches $0.60, the variable cost, but never quite reaches it, because the fixed cost is always being amortised over some finite number of bottles. The HA is the asymptotic floor.

(d) Break-even production M1·A1

Set $\bar{C}(x) = 0.85$: $$ \dfrac{240}{x} + 0.60 \;=\; 0.85 \;\Longrightarrow\; \dfrac{240}{x} \;=\; 0.25 \;\Longrightarrow\; x \;=\; \dfrac{240}{0.25} \;=\; 960. $$ So break-even occurs at $x = 960$ bottles/day. Below this, $\bar{C}(x) > 0.85$ and the plant runs at a loss; above this, $\bar{C}(x) < 0.85$ and the plant is profitable.

(e) Situational domain; algebraic + contextual exclusion of $x = 0$ R1

$x > 0$. The exclusion of $x = 0$ is justified twice: algebraically, $x = 0$ is a denominator-only zero (no common-factor cancellation), so $\bar{C}$ has a vertical asymptote at $x = 0$, the average cost diverges as production tends to zero. Contextually, a plant producing zero bottles has no per-bottle cost defined (any fixed cost divided by zero output is undefined). BC PC12's rational functions elaboration names exactly this checklist, asymptotes, intercepts, point discontinuities, domain, end-behaviour, and Q12 hits each item: VA $x = 0$, no point discontinuities (no common factor), HA $\bar{C} = 0.60$, domain $x > 0$, end-behaviour $\bar{C} \to 0.60$ as $x \to \infty$ and $\bar{C} \to \infty$ as $x \to 0^{+}$.
Split-form $\frac{240}{x} + 0.60$ shows the asymptote, the economies of scale, and the interpretation all at once. The unified rational form $\frac{240 + 0.60x}{x}$ is correct but hides the structure. The split decomposes the rational into a "diminishing" piece ($\frac{240}{x}$, the fixed-cost-per-unit term) and a "constant" piece ($0.60$, the variable cost). The HA $y = 0.60$ then reads directly as the constant piece, with no degree-comparison shortcut needed. This is the reverse of the §4.2 "combine into single fraction" move and is the load-bearing technique for asymptotic interpretation in BC PC12. Whenever the numerator decomposes cleanly against the denominator, splitting is strictly better than degree comparison, you get the interpretation for free.
