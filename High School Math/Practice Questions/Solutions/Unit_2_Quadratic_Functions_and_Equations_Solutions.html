Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Parabola Key Features · HSF-IF.C.7a [3 marks]

Vertex of $y = (x - 3)^{2} - 4$.

Answer:  (B)  $(3, -4)$

(a) Read the vertex off vertex form M1·A1·A1

The equation is already in vertex form $y = a(x - h)^{2} + k$ with $a = 1$, $h = 3$, $k = -4$. The vertex is $(h, k) = (3, -4)$, which matches option (B).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) $(-3, -4)$ — sign-flip error: the student reads the $-3$ inside $(x - 3)$ as the $x$-coordinate of the vertex, instead of recognising that vertex form encodes $h$ as the value that zeroes the bracket.
  • (C) $(3, 4)$ — correct $x$-coordinate but copies the magnitude $4$ without the sign; vertex form's $+k$ outside the bracket carries its sign directly.
  • (D) $(-3, 4)$ — both signs flipped at once; the "double negative" error from reading $(x - h)^{2} + k$ as if every sign were reversed.
The vertex is the $(h, k)$ that makes the squared bracket zero. The fast diagnostic: ask "what value of $x$ makes $(x - 3)^{2}$ equal to zero?" The answer is $x = 3$, so $h = 3$ — the sign in the bracket flips when you solve for $h$. The $y$-coordinate $k = -4$ sits outside the bracket and is read directly. This $(h, k)$-from-bracket move generalises to every transformed-graph question (Unit 2 §2.5 and downstream Unit 5 transformations), so lock it in.
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §3 Factoring (a = 1) · HSA-SSE.B.3a [3 marks]

Factor $x^{2} - 5x - 14$.

Answer:  (A)  $(x - 7)(x + 2)$

(a) Find two integers with product $-14$ and sum $-5$ M1·A1

With $a = 1$, factoring $x^{2} + bx + c$ reduces to finding integers $p, q$ with $p \cdot q = c$ and $p + q = b$. Here $c = -14$, $b = -5$. Candidate pairs for product $-14$: $(1, -14), (-1, 14), (2, -7), (-2, 7)$. Sums: $-13, 13, -5, 5$. The pair $(2, -7)$ gives sum $-5$. $\checkmark$

(b) Write the factorisation and check A1

So $x^{2} - 5x - 14 = (x + 2)(x - 7)$, which matches option (A). Verify by expansion: $(x - 7)(x + 2) = x^{2} + 2x - 7x - 14 = x^{2} - 5x - 14$. $\checkmark$
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (B) $(x - 7)(x - 2)$ — both factors negative gives product $+14$, not $-14$. Expansion check: $x^{2} - 9x + 14$.
  • (C) $(x + 7)(x - 2)$ — correct magnitudes but sign-swapped; expansion gives $x^{2} + 5x - 14$ (wrong middle-term sign).
  • (D) $(x - 14)(x + 1)$ — uses the $1 \cdot 14$ pair instead of $2 \cdot 7$; expansion gives $x^{2} - 13x - 14$.
The sum tells you the signs; the product tells you the magnitudes. For $x^{2} + bx + c$ with $c < 0$, the two factors must have opposite signs — one positive, one negative. With $c > 0$, both factors share the sign of $b$. Lock this sign rule down before listing pairs and you eliminate half the distractors instantly. Common trap: the SAT loves seeding the answer set with the same magnitudes in every wrong combination of signs.
Q3MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §4 Discriminant · HSA-REI.B.4 [3 marks]

Number of real solutions of $2x^{2} + 3x + 5 = 0$.

Answer:  (C)  No real solutions

(a) Compute the discriminant M1·A1

With $a = 2$, $b = 3$, $c = 5$: $$ \Delta \;=\; b^{2} - 4ac \;=\; 3^{2} - 4(2)(5) \;=\; 9 - 40 \;=\; -31. $$

(b) Classify by sign of $\Delta$ A1

Since $\Delta = -31 < 0$, the equation has no real solutions (the two roots are a complex-conjugate pair). This matches option (C).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) Two distinct real solutions — requires $\Delta > 0$; this would be the answer if $b^{2}$ exceeded $4ac$, but $9 < 40$.
  • (B) One real solution (double root) — requires $\Delta = 0$ exactly; here $\Delta = -31 \ne 0$.
  • (D) Infinitely many real solutions — impossible for a non-trivial polynomial equation; a quadratic equation has at most two roots over $\mathbb{C}$ by the fundamental theorem of algebra.
$\sqrt{\text{negative}}$ is not real — do not "square-root through" a negative discriminant. A common slip is to write $x = \dfrac{-3 \pm \sqrt{-31}}{4}$ and then claim $\sqrt{-31} = \pm \sqrt{31}$ (it is not — both signs of $\sqrt{31}$ are real, neither is $\sqrt{-31}$). When $\Delta < 0$, the quadratic formula produces $\pm i\sqrt{|\Delta|}/(2a)$ — complex roots, not real ones. The SAT rewards students who answer "no real solutions" decisively after a five-second discriminant computation, rather than wading through the formula.
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §2 Three Forms · MPM2D Quadratic Relations [6 marks]

$y = x^{2} - 8x + 11$. (a) Vertex form + vertex. (b) AOS + opening. (c) $y$-intercept.

Answer:  (a) $y = (x - 4)^{2} - 5$, vertex $(4, -5)$  ·  (b) $x = 4$, opens upward  ·  (c) $(0, 11)$

(a) Complete the square M1·A1·A1

Take $x^{2} - 8x + 11$. Half of the $x$-coefficient is $-4$; its square is $16$. Add-and-subtract: $$ y \;=\; (x^{2} - 8x + 16) - 16 + 11 \;=\; (x - 4)^{2} - 5. $$ Vertex form: $y = (x - 4)^{2} - 5$, vertex $(h, k) = (4, -5)$.

(b) Axis of symmetry and direction of opening A1·A1

Axis of symmetry: the vertical line through the vertex, $x = 4$.
Direction of opening: the leading coefficient $a = 1 > 0$, so the parabola opens upward — the vertex is a minimum.

(c) $y$-intercept A1

Set $x = 0$ in the standard form: $y = 0 - 0 + 11 = 11$. The $y$-intercept is $(0, 11)$.
Read three forms, three things; do not re-derive what is already visible. Standard form $y = ax^{2} + bx + c$ gives the $y$-intercept $(0, c)$ for free. Vertex form gives the vertex, the axis of symmetry, and the max/min for free. Factored form (when it exists) gives the $x$-intercepts for free. Provincial markers split marks across conversion (the M1) and reading (the A1s); students who only ever convert to one form and re-derive the rest leave easy A1s on the table. Train the eye to ask "which form gives me this feature without computation?" before reaching for the algebra.
Q5MEDIUM 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §5 Completing the Square · BC PC11 quadratic functions [7 marks]

$f(x) = 2x^{2} + 12x + 7$. (a) Vertex form by CTS. (b) Vertex, AOS, min. (c) Domain and range in interval notation.

Answer:  (a) $f(x) = 2(x + 3)^{2} - 11$  ·  (b) vertex $(-3, -11)$, AOS $x = -3$, min $-11$  ·  (c) $D = (-\infty, \infty)$, $R = [-11, \infty)$

(a) Complete the square with $a \ne 1$ M1·A1·A1

Factor the leading coefficient out of the first two terms only: $$ f(x) \;=\; 2(x^{2} + 6x) + 7. $$ Half of $6$ is $3$; its square is $9$. Add-and-subtract $9$ inside the bracket: $$ f(x) \;=\; 2\bigl((x^{2} + 6x + 9) - 9\bigr) + 7 \;=\; 2(x + 3)^{2} - 18 + 7 \;=\; 2(x + 3)^{2} - 11. $$

(b) Vertex, axis of symmetry, minimum A1·A1

From $f(x) = 2(x - (-3))^{2} + (-11)$: vertex $(-3, -11)$, axis of symmetry $x = -3$. Since $a = 2 > 0$ the parabola opens upward, so the vertex is a minimum: $f_{\min} = -11$ attained at $x = -3$.

(c) Domain and range A1·A1

Every polynomial is defined for all real $x$: $D = (-\infty, \infty)$.
Since the minimum value is $-11$ and the parabola opens upward, the range is $R = [-11, \infty)$.
The "$- 9$ inside the bracket" is really "$- 18$ outside" — track the multiplier. The most common BC PC11 sign error: writing $f(x) = 2(x + 3)^{2} - 9 + 7$ at the end, forgetting that the $-9$ sits inside a bracket multiplied by $2$. When you pull the $-9$ out, it becomes $-2 \cdot 9 = -18$, not $-9$. The audit move: re-expand your vertex form back to standard and confirm. Here $2(x + 3)^{2} - 11 = 2(x^{2} + 6x + 9) - 11 = 2x^{2} + 12x + 18 - 11 = 2x^{2} + 12x + 7$. $\checkmark$ The minute it takes to re-expand catches every sign slip.

Q6MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Forms & Conversion · HSF-IF.C.8a [8 marks]

$g(x) = -2x^{2} + 8x + 10$. (a) Vertex form + max/min. (b) Factored form + $x$-intercepts. (c) $y$-intercept + AOS.

Answer:  (a) $g(x) = -2(x - 2)^{2} + 18$, vertex $(2, 18)$, maximum  ·  (b) $g(x) = -2(x - 5)(x + 1)$, $x$-ints $x = 5, -1$  ·  (c) $y$-int $(0, 10)$, AOS $x = 2$

(a) Vertex form by completing the square M1·A1·A1

Factor $-2$ from the first two terms only: $$ g(x) \;=\; -2(x^{2} - 4x) + 10. $$ Half of $-4$ is $-2$; its square is $4$. Add-and-subtract $4$ inside the bracket: $$ g(x) \;=\; -2\bigl((x^{2} - 4x + 4) - 4\bigr) + 10 \;=\; -2(x - 2)^{2} + 8 + 10 \;=\; -2(x - 2)^{2} + 18. $$ Vertex $(2, 18)$. Since $a = -2 < 0$, the parabola opens downward — the vertex is a maximum.

(b) Factored form by factoring out $-2$ M1·A1·A1

$$ g(x) \;=\; -2x^{2} + 8x + 10 \;=\; -2(x^{2} - 4x - 5). $$ Factor $x^{2} - 4x - 5$: integers with product $-5$ and sum $-4$ are $-5$ and $1$, so $x^{2} - 4x - 5 = (x - 5)(x + 1)$. Hence $$ g(x) \;=\; -2(x - 5)(x + 1). $$ The $x$-intercepts are the roots: $x = 5$ and $x = -1$, i.e. the points $(5, 0)$ and $(-1, 0)$.

(c) $y$-intercept and axis of symmetry A1·A1

$y$-intercept: $g(0) = -2(0) + 8(0) + 10 = 10$, so $(0, 10)$.
Axis of symmetry: the vertical line through the vertex, $x = 2$. (Cross-check using the midpoint of the $x$-intercepts: $\tfrac{5 + (-1)}{2} = 2$. $\checkmark$)
Three forms, three guaranteed cross-checks. Once you have all three forms of the same parabola, every key feature is over-determined and you can audit your work in one line: vertex form's $h$ must equal the midpoint of factored form's roots; vertex form's $k$ must equal $g(h)$ computed from standard form; standard form's constant must equal $g(0)$ from any form. Here $h = 2 = \tfrac{5 + (-1)}{2}$, $k = 18 = -2(0)^{2} + 8(0) + 10 + \ldots$ wait — $g(2) = -2(4) + 16 + 10 = -8 + 16 + 10 = 18$. $\checkmark$ AP graders explicitly look for this cross-form consistency on HSF-IF.C.8a.
Q7HARD 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §3 Factoring (a ≠ 1) · BC PC11 polynomial factoring [8 marks]

Solve by factoring. (a) $6x^{2} - 11x - 10 = 0$ (AC-method). (b) $4x^{2} - 25 = 0$ (DoS). (c) $9x^{2} - 12x + 4 = 0$ (perfect square).

Answer:  (a) $x = -\tfrac{2}{3}, \tfrac{5}{2}$  ·  (b) $x = \pm \tfrac{5}{2}$  ·  (c) $x = \tfrac{2}{3}$ (double root)

(a) AC-method on $6x^{2} - 11x - 10 = 0$ M1·A1·A1

Compute $AC = 6 \cdot (-10) = -60$. Find integers with product $-60$ and sum $-11$. Candidates: $(-15, 4)$ gives sum $-11$. $\checkmark$
Split the middle term and group: $$ 6x^{2} - 15x + 4x - 10 \;=\; 3x(2x - 5) + 2(2x - 5) \;=\; (3x + 2)(2x - 5). $$ Setting each factor to zero: $3x + 2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -\tfrac{2}{3}$; $2x - 5 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{5}{2}$. Solution set $\bigl\{-\tfrac{2}{3}, \tfrac{5}{2}\bigr\}$.

(b) Difference-of-squares pattern on $4x^{2} - 25 = 0$ M1·A1

Recognise $4x^{2} = (2x)^{2}$ and $25 = 5^{2}$, so this fits $A^{2} - B^{2} = (A - B)(A + B)$ with $A = 2x$, $B = 5$: $$ 4x^{2} - 25 \;=\; (2x - 5)(2x + 5) \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x \;=\; \pm \tfrac{5}{2}. $$

(c) Perfect-square trinomial on $9x^{2} - 12x + 4 = 0$ M1·A1·A1

Test the perfect-square pattern $A^{2} - 2AB + B^{2} = (A - B)^{2}$ with $A^{2} = 9x^{2} \Rightarrow A = 3x$ and $B^{2} = 4 \Rightarrow B = 2$. Check the cross term: $-2AB = -2(3x)(2) = -12x$. $\checkmark$ So $$ 9x^{2} - 12x + 4 \;=\; (3x - 2)^{2} \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; 3x - 2 \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x \;=\; \tfrac{2}{3}. $$ This is a double root (multiplicity 2), confirmed by the discriminant $\Delta = (-12)^{2} - 4(9)(4) = 144 - 144 = 0$.
Spot the pattern before reaching for AC. Difference of squares ($b = 0$ with $a, c$ both perfect squares of opposite sign) and perfect-square trinomial ($b^{2} = 4ac$ exactly) are the two patterns to scan for before setting up AC-method tables. Parts (b) and (c) are each one line of pattern-matching; running AC-method on them is six lines of wasted work. The structural diagnostic for a perfect square: check $b^{2} - 4ac$ — if it's zero, you have $(A \pm B)^{2}$, where $A = \sqrt{a}\,x$ and $B = \sqrt{c}$, and the sign in the bracket matches the sign of $b$.
Q8MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §4 Quadratic Formula · MCR3U A2 [9 marks]

$3x^{2} - 7x - 2 = 0$. (a) $\Delta$. (b) Classify roots. (c) Solve via formula, exact radical. (d) $f(0)$ and graphical meaning.

Answer:  (a) $\Delta = 73$  ·  (b) two distinct real roots  ·  (c) $x = \dfrac{7 \pm \sqrt{73}}{6}$  ·  (d) $f(0) = -2$, the $y$-intercept

(a) Discriminant M1·A1

With $a = 3$, $b = -7$, $c = -2$: $$ \Delta \;=\; b^{2} - 4ac \;=\; (-7)^{2} - 4(3)(-2) \;=\; 49 + 24 \;=\; 73. $$

(b) Classify the roots R1

$\Delta = 73 > 0$ and $73$ is not a perfect square, so the equation has two distinct real (irrational) roots.

(c) Solve via the quadratic formula M1·A1·A1·A1

$$ x \;=\; \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a} \;=\; \frac{-(-7) \pm \sqrt{73}}{2(3)} \;=\; \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{73}}{6}. $$ Both roots: $x_{1} = \dfrac{7 + \sqrt{73}}{6}$ and $x_{2} = \dfrac{7 - \sqrt{73}}{6}$. $\sqrt{73}$ does not simplify ($73$ is prime), so this is the final exact form.

(d) $f(0)$ and its meaning A1·A1

$f(0) = 3(0)^{2} - 7(0) - 2 = -2$. Geometrically, $f(0)$ is the value of $f$ at $x = 0$, i.e. the height where the parabola crosses the $y$-axis. So the graph of $y = f(x)$ has $y$-intercept $(0, -2)$.
Simplify the radical before stopping. Provincial markers deduct an A1 for "unsimplified" radicals — answers like $\dfrac{7 \pm \sqrt{292}}{12}$ (the result of forgetting to reduce $\sqrt{4 \cdot 73}$ to $2\sqrt{73}$ and dividing through). Habit move: once the discriminant is computed, factor it into a perfect square times a square-free part: $\Delta = k^{2} \cdot m$ where $m$ is square-free, then $\sqrt{\Delta} = k\sqrt{m}$. Here $73$ is prime so $k = 1$. Always state explicitly "$\sqrt{73}$ is in lowest terms because $73$ is prime" — markers want to see the awareness.
Q9HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §4 Complex Roots · HSN-CN.A.7 (+) / BC PC11 [11 marks]

Family $x^{2} - 4x + c = 0$. (a) $\Delta(c)$. (b) $c$ for double root + the root. (c) Ranges of $c$ for two real, two complex. (d) $c = 13$: complex solutions, conjugate check. (e) Geometric meaning of no real roots.

Answer:  (a) $\Delta(c) = 16 - 4c$  ·  (b) $c = 4$, double root $x = 2$  ·  (c) two real: $c < 4$; two complex: $c > 4$  ·  (d) $x = 2 \pm 3i$  ·  (e) graph lies entirely above $x$-axis

(a) Discriminant as a function of $c$ A1

With $a = 1$, $b = -4$, constant $c$: $$ \Delta(c) \;=\; (-4)^{2} - 4(1)(c) \;=\; 16 - 4c. $$

(b) Double-root condition M1·A1

One real solution $\Leftrightarrow \Delta(c) = 0 \Leftrightarrow 16 - 4c = 0 \Leftrightarrow c = 4$. The root is $x = \dfrac{-b}{2a} = \dfrac{4}{2} = 2$. Sanity-check: $x^{2} - 4x + 4 = (x - 2)^{2}$. $\checkmark$

(c) Discriminant ranges M1·A1·A1

Two distinct real solutions $\Leftrightarrow \Delta(c) > 0 \Leftrightarrow 16 - 4c > 0 \Leftrightarrow c < 4$.
Two non-real complex solutions (conjugate pair) $\Leftrightarrow \Delta(c) < 0 \Leftrightarrow 16 - 4c < 0 \Leftrightarrow c > 4$.
(Together with part (b), the three ranges $c < 4$, $c = 4$, $c > 4$ partition $\mathbb{R}$.)

(d) Solve when $c = 13$; verify conjugacy M1·A1·A1·R1

$\Delta(13) = 16 - 52 = -36$. Then $$ x \;=\; \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{-36}}{2} \;=\; \frac{4 \pm 6i}{2} \;=\; 2 \pm 3i. $$ So $x_{1} = 2 + 3i$ and $x_{2} = 2 - 3i$. Conjugacy check: the conjugate of $a + bi$ is $a - bi$, so $\overline{x_{1}} = \overline{2 + 3i} = 2 - 3i = x_{2}$. $\checkmark$ This is exactly the statement of HSN-CN.A.7: a real-coefficient polynomial whose roots include $a + bi$ must also have $a - bi$ as a root, because the conjugate of zero is zero.

(e) Geometric interpretation A1

No real roots means the parabola $y = x^{2} - 4x + 13$ never meets the $x$-axis. Since $a = 1 > 0$ the parabola opens upward, and its minimum value $y = 13 - 4 = 9 > 0$ confirms the graph lies entirely above the $x$-axis.
Real coefficients lock complex roots into conjugate pairs — and the dividing line is $\Delta = 0$. The discriminant is a single real number whose sign sorts the three regimes: $\Delta > 0$, $\Delta = 0$, $\Delta < 0$. As $c$ slides through $4$ in this family, the two real roots merge at $x = 2$ (double root), then split apart vertically into the complex plane as $\pm i \sqrt{(c - 4)}$ added to the real part $\tfrac{-b}{2a} = 2$. This is the prototype for honors topics later: every real-coefficient polynomial of degree $n$ has its non-real complex roots in conjugate pairs (HSN-CN.A.7), which is why $\Delta < 0$ never produces a single complex root or three of them — only pairs.

Q10MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §6 Projectile Modeling · HSF-IF.B.4 [10 marks]

Rocket: $h(t) = -4.9 t^{2} + 30 t + 2$ (m, s). (a) $h(0)$ + meaning. (b) Time + height at max. (c) Time hits ground. (d) Practical domain/range + monotone intervals.

Answer:  (a) $h(0) = 2$ m (launch platform height)  ·  (b) $t \approx 3.1$ s, $h_{\max} \approx 47.9$ m  ·  (c) $t \approx 6.2$ s  ·  (d) $D = [0, 6.2]$, $R = [0, 47.9]$; increasing on $[0, 3.1]$, decreasing on $[3.1, 6.2]$

(a) Initial height A1·A1

$h(0) = -4.9(0)^{2} + 30(0) + 2 = 2$ m. In context: the rocket is launched from a platform $2$ metres above the ground, so $h(0) = 2$ is the launch height (the constant term encodes the starting altitude).

(b) Time and height at maximum M1·A1·A1

The vertex of $h(t) = at^{2} + bt + c$ is at $t = -\dfrac{b}{2a}$. With $a = -4.9$, $b = 30$: $$ t^{*} \;=\; -\frac{30}{2(-4.9)} \;=\; \frac{30}{9.8} \;\approx\; 3.0612\ldots \;\approx\; 3.1 \;\text{s}. $$ Maximum height: $$ h(t^{*}) \;=\; -4.9(3.0612)^{2} + 30(3.0612) + 2 \;\approx\; -45.918 + 91.837 + 2 \;\approx\; 47.918 \;\approx\; 47.9 \;\text{m}. $$ (Cross-check via $k = c - \tfrac{b^{2}}{4a} = 2 - \tfrac{900}{-19.6} = 2 + 45.918 = 47.918$. $\checkmark$)

(c) Time the rocket hits the ground M1·A1·A1

Solve $h(t) = 0$ for $t > 0$: $$ -4.9 t^{2} + 30 t + 2 \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; t \;=\; \frac{-30 \pm \sqrt{900 + 39.2}}{-9.8} \;=\; \frac{-30 \pm \sqrt{939.2}}{-9.8}. $$ $\sqrt{939.2} \approx 30.646$. The two algebraic roots are $$ t \;\approx\; \frac{-30 - 30.646}{-9.8} \;\approx\; 6.188 \quad \text{and} \quad t \;\approx\; \frac{-30 + 30.646}{-9.8} \;\approx\; -0.066. $$ Reject the negative root (before launch). So $t \approx 6.2$ s.

(d) Practical domain and range; monotone intervals A1·A1

Practical domain: $t \in [0, 6.2]$ — from launch through impact.
Practical range: $h \in [0, 47.9]$ — from ground level (at impact) up to the peak. Note the platform height $2$ is not the minimum of the practical range; the rocket also reaches ground level $0$ on the way down.
Increasing on $[0, 3.1]$ (ascent); decreasing on $[3.1, 6.2]$ (descent). This matches the HSF-IF.B.4 reading: the function rises until the vertex, then falls.
Algebraic domain $\ne$ practical domain — and the negative root is the giveaway. The algebraic domain of $h$ is all of $\mathbb{R}$, but the model is only valid where the situation makes sense: $t \ge 0$ (no time-travel) and $h(t) \ge 0$ (rocket above ground). The negative algebraic root $t \approx -0.066$ is a tell that the model would have predicted "ground crossing in the past" — physically meaningless, but a useful sanity check that the algebra is right. AP graders explicitly reward students who state the practical domain in interval notation and name a reason ("rocket cannot exist before launch" or "rocket cannot pass through the ground"); writing only $t \ge 0$ misses an A1.
Q11MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §6 Revenue Modeling · MCR3U A2.5 [11 marks]

Bakery: base $200$ croissants at CA\$3.00; each CA\$0.10 hike drops sales by $5$. Let $x =$ number of \$0.10 hikes. (a) $P(x), Q(x)$. (b) $R(x)$ expanded. (c) Optimal $x$ + max revenue. (d) Optimal price + quantity. (e) Restriction on $x$.

Answer:  (a) $P(x) = 3 + 0.10 x$, $Q(x) = 200 - 5 x$  ·  (b) $R(x) = -0.5 x^{2} + 5 x + 600$  ·  (c) $x^{*} = 5$, $R_{\max} = \$612.50$  ·  (d) price $\$3.50$, quantity $175$  ·  (e) $0 \le x \le 40$, $x \in \mathbb{Z}$

(a) Linear price and quantity A1·A1

Each \$0.10 hike adds \$0.10 to the price: $P(x) = 3.00 + 0.10 x$ (in CA\$).
Each hike drops sales by $5$: $Q(x) = 200 - 5 x$ (in croissants).

(b) Revenue in expanded standard form M1·A1

$$ R(x) \;=\; P(x) \cdot Q(x) \;=\; (3 + 0.10 x)(200 - 5 x). $$ Expand: $$ R(x) \;=\; 600 - 15 x + 20 x - 0.5 x^{2} \;=\; -0.5 x^{2} + 5 x + 600. $$

(c) Maximise $R(x)$ M1·A1·A1·A1

$R$ is a downward-opening parabola ($a = -0.5 < 0$), so the maximum is at the vertex: $$ x^{*} \;=\; -\frac{b}{2a} \;=\; -\frac{5}{2(-0.5)} \;=\; \frac{5}{1} \;=\; 5. $$ Maximum revenue: $$ R(5) \;=\; -0.5(25) + 5(5) + 600 \;=\; -12.5 + 25 + 600 \;=\; \$612.50. $$

(d) Optimal price and quantity A1·A1

Price: $P(5) = 3.00 + 0.10(5) = \$3.50$ per croissant.
Quantity: $Q(5) = 200 - 5(5) = 175$ croissants/day.
(Cross-check: $\$3.50 \times 175 = \$612.50$. $\checkmark$)

(e) Restriction on $x$ A1

Two restrictions follow from the situation. First, $Q(x) \ge 0 \Leftrightarrow 200 - 5 x \ge 0 \Leftrightarrow x \le 40$: at $40$ hikes the bakery sells zero croissants, beyond that the model would predict negative sales. Second, $x \ge 0$ (number of hikes cannot be negative) and $x$ must be a non-negative integer (\$0.10 hikes are discrete). Hence $x \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, 40\}$, i.e. $0 \le x \le 40$ with $x \in \mathbb{Z}$.
Optimum-of-product-of-linears: always a parabola, always halfway between the zeros. When revenue is the product of a linear price and a linear quantity, $R(x) = (a + bx)(c + dx)$, the result is a quadratic whose roots are $x = -a/b$ and $x = -c/d$ — the price-zero and the quantity-zero. The maximum sits at the midpoint of those two roots: $x^{*} = \tfrac{1}{2}(-a/b - c/d)$. Here zeros are at $x = -30$ (price hits zero) and $x = 40$ (quantity hits zero); midpoint is $\tfrac{-30 + 40}{2} = 5$. That midpoint shortcut catches arithmetic errors faster than re-running $-b/(2a)$ — and provincial markers reward the student who explicitly cites it.

$2x^{2} - 5x - 12 < 0$. (a) Factor + roots. (b) Sign chart. (c) Solution set ($< 0$). (d) Solution set ($\ge 0$). (e) Park profit context $\Pi(x) = -2x^{2} + 5x + 12$, profitable prices $x \ge 0$.

Answer:  (a) $(2x + 3)(x - 4)$, roots $x = -\tfrac{3}{2}, 4$  ·  (c) $\bigl(-\tfrac{3}{2}, 4\bigr)$  ·  (d) $\bigl(-\infty, -\tfrac{3}{2}\bigr] \cup [4, \infty)$  ·  (e) $0 \le x < 4$ (dollars)

(a) Factor and roots M1·A1·A1

AC-method on $2x^{2} - 5x - 12$: $AC = 2 \cdot (-12) = -24$. Integers with product $-24$ and sum $-5$: $(-8, 3)$. Split and group: $$ 2x^{2} - 8x + 3x - 12 \;=\; 2x(x - 4) + 3(x - 4) \;=\; (2x + 3)(x - 4). $$ Roots of $2x^{2} - 5x - 12 = 0$: $x = -\tfrac{3}{2}$ and $x = 4$.

(b) Sign chart M1·A1·A1

The two roots partition the real line into three intervals: $\bigl(-\infty, -\tfrac{3}{2}\bigr)$, $\bigl(-\tfrac{3}{2}, 4\bigr)$, $(4, \infty)$. Test one point from each, using the factored form $f(x) = (2x + 3)(x - 4)$:
  • $x = -2$: $(2(-2) + 3)(-2 - 4) = (-1)(-6) = +6 > 0$. Sign: $+$.
  • $x = 0$: $(3)(-4) = -12 < 0$. Sign: $-$.
  • $x = 5$: $(13)(1) = +13 > 0$. Sign: $+$.
Sign chart (left to right): $\;+ \;\big|\; -\tfrac{3}{2} \;\big|\; - \;\big|\; 4 \;\big|\; +$. Since $a = 2 > 0$ the parabola opens upward, so the signs $+, -, +$ are exactly what symmetry predicts.

(c) Solution set of $2x^{2} - 5x - 12 < 0$ M1·A1

Strictly negative on $\bigl(-\tfrac{3}{2}, 4\bigr)$ from the sign chart. Solution set: $\bigl(-\tfrac{3}{2}, 4\bigr)$ (open interval — endpoints excluded because the inequality is strict).

(d) Solution set of $2x^{2} - 5x - 12 \ge 0$ A1

Non-strict $\ge 0$: take the $+$ regions plus the two roots. Solution set: $\bigl(-\infty, -\tfrac{3}{2}\bigr] \cup [4, \infty)$ (closed at the roots).

(e) Park profit context M1·A1

Notice $\Pi(x) = -2x^{2} + 5x + 12 = -(2x^{2} - 5x - 12)$, so $\Pi(x) > 0 \Leftrightarrow 2x^{2} - 5x - 12 < 0 \Leftrightarrow x \in \bigl(-\tfrac{3}{2}, 4\bigr)$ by part (c). Combined with the situational restriction $x \ge 0$ (admission price in dollars cannot be negative), the profitable price range is $\;0 \le x < 4$. The park earns strictly positive profit when daily admission is set somewhere between \$0 and \$4 (excluding \$4 itself, where profit is exactly zero).
Sign chart $\Rightarrow$ instant relative inequality, no re-work. Once the sign chart is drawn, every related inequality — $< 0$, $\le 0$, $> 0$, $\ge 0$ — reads off directly without recomputing. Strict vs. non-strict only changes whether the root endpoints are included. The "$\ge 0$" solution is the complement of the "$< 0$" solution (as a subset of $\mathbb{R}$); the union of the two answers is all of $\mathbb{R}$, with the roots counted in whichever side has $\ge$ or $\le$. The deeper move in (e): negating $\Pi(x) = -f(x)$ flips the sign chart wholesale ($+ \leftrightarrow -$), so "$\Pi > 0$" $=$ "$f < 0$" — the algebra you already did transfers directly, no second sign chart needed. BC provincial markers love this kind of structural reuse.
