Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Sample Spaces · HSS-CP.A.1 [3 marks]

Two fair dice; $P($ sum $= 8)$.

Answer:  (B)  $5/36$

(a) Sample space and event size M1·A1·A1

The sample space of two ordered dice has $|S| = 6 \cdot 6 = 36$ equally-likely outcomes. The event $A = \{(d_{1}, d_{2}) : d_{1} + d_{2} = 8\}$ is $$ A = \{(2, 6), (3, 5), (4, 4), (5, 3), (6, 2)\}, \qquad |A| = 5. $$ Hence $P(A) = |A|/|S| = 5/36$, which matches option (B).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) $4/36$, off-by-one: the student lists $(2,6), (3,5), (5,3), (6,2)$ but forgets $(4,4)$, where the two dice show the same face.
  • (C) $6/36 = 1/6$, the count for sum $= 7$ (the modal sum), not $8$. A classic "anchored on the middle" trap.
  • (D) $8/36$, the student conflates the target sum $8$ with the count of outcomes, $|A| = 8$. The target value is not the count.
Two ordered dice means $36$ outcomes, not $21$, and $(4, 4)$ is its own outcome. A common SAT pitfall: students who think of dice as unordered pairs $\{d_{1}, d_{2}\}$ get $21$ pairs (the $15$ off-diagonal pairs counted once each, plus the $6$ doubles), but those $21$ are not equally likely; off-diagonal pairs are twice as likely as doubles. The classical formula $P(A) = |A|/|S|$ requires equally-likely outcomes, so always model dice as ordered pairs. The bell-shaped sum distribution ($2$ and $12$ each rare, $7$ most common, $5/36$ for $8$ and for $6$) drops out for free.
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Complement Rule · HSS-CP.A.1 [3 marks]

$P($ at least one tail in $5$ flips $)$.

Answer:  (D)  $31/32$

(a) Apply the complement rule M1·A1

"At least one tail" is the complement of "no tails", i.e. all five flips are heads. For independent fair flips, $$ P(\text{no tails}) = P(\text{all heads}) = \bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr)^{5} = \tfrac{1}{32}. $$

(b) Subtract from $1$ A1

$$ P(\text{at least one tail}) = 1 - P(\text{no tails}) = 1 - \tfrac{1}{32} = \tfrac{31}{32}. $$ This matches option (D).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) $1/32$, the probability of "no tails" (i.e. all heads), which is the complement of the event asked for.
  • (B) $5/32$, $P(X = 1)$ for $X \sim B(5, 1/2)$, the probability of exactly one tail. "Exactly" $\ne$ "at least".
  • (C) $1/2$, the probability one specific flip lands tails. The asker is about any of the $5$ flips landing tails, not a single one.
"At least one" is the complement-rule fingerprint. Whenever you see "at least one $X$ in $n$ independent trials" for an event with probability $p$ per trial, write $$ P(\text{at least one}) = 1 - (1 - p)^{n}, $$ because the only thing easier than "at least one $X$" to compute is its complement, "no $X$ at all", which factorises by independence. The direct sum $P(X = 1) + P(X = 2) + \cdots + P(X = n)$ is always more work. The complement trick reappears verbatim in Q12(c), and on the AP Stats exam every May, so lock it in.
Q3MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §3 Independence Test · HSS-CP.A.2 [4 marks]

$P(A) = 0.6$, $P(B) = 0.5$, $P(A \cup B) = 0.8$. Decide independence.

Answer:  (B)  Independent, $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B) = 0.3$

(a) Recover $P(A \cap B)$ from the addition rule M1·A1

The addition rule gives $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$. Rearrange: $$ P(A \cap B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cup B) = 0.6 + 0.5 - 0.8 = 0.3. $$

(b) Run the independence test M1·A1

Compare $P(A \cap B)$ to $P(A) P(B)$: $$ P(A) P(B) = 0.6 \cdot 0.5 = 0.30 = P(A \cap B). $$ The two are equal, so $A$ and $B$ are independent by CCSSM HSS-CP.A.2. This matches option (B).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) Mutually exclusive, would require $P(A \cap B) = 0$, but $P(A \cap B) = 0.3 \ne 0$. Mutually exclusive and independent are different, and two events with positive probability cannot be both.
  • (C) Dependent, would require $P(A \cap B) \ne P(A) P(B)$; here they are equal, so the test fails to detect dependence.
  • (D) Insufficient information, the addition rule pins $P(A \cap B)$ down uniquely from the three given numbers, so the question is fully determined.
Independent and mutually exclusive are opposites, not synonyms. If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive (disjoint), then knowing $B$ has occurred tells you $A$ has not occurred, $P(A \mid B) = 0$, which is maximal dependence. Conversely, if $A$ and $B$ are independent, then $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$, $B$ tells you nothing about $A$. Two events with $P(A), P(B) > 0$ can be one or the other or neither, but never both. The SAT/AP loves seeding "mutually exclusive" as a distractor on independence questions because students conflate the two; the moment you see "exclusive" on an independence problem, check the intersection.
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON MDM4U-style §2 Conditional Probability · HSS-CP.B.6 [4 marks]

$7$R, $5$B in a bag; draw $2$ without replacement. (a) $P($ RR $)$. (b) $P($ same colour $)$.

Answer:  (a) $P(\text{RR}) = \tfrac{7}{22}$  ·  (b) $P(\text{same}) = \tfrac{31}{66}$

(a) Multiplication rule with conditional M1·A1

Without replacement, the second draw depends on the first. The multiplication rule reads $$ P(R_{1} \cap R_{2}) = P(R_{1}) \cdot P(R_{2} \mid R_{1}). $$ Initially $|S| = 12$ marbles ($7$R + $5$B), so $P(R_{1}) = 7/12$. Given $R_{1}$ occurred, $6$ reds remain in $11$ marbles, so $P(R_{2} \mid R_{1}) = 6/11$. Hence $$ P(\text{RR}) = \frac{7}{12} \cdot \frac{6}{11} = \frac{42}{132} = \frac{7}{22}. $$

(b) Add the two same-colour branches M1·A1

"Both same colour" is the union of disjoint events RR and BB. For BB: $P(B_{1}) = 5/12$, $P(B_{2} \mid B_{1}) = 4/11$, so $$ P(\text{BB}) = \frac{5}{12} \cdot \frac{4}{11} = \frac{20}{132} = \frac{5}{33}. $$ Add the two disjoint branches: $$ P(\text{same}) = P(\text{RR}) + P(\text{BB}) = \frac{42}{132} + \frac{20}{132} = \frac{62}{132} = \frac{31}{66}. $$
Multiply along a branch; add across branches. A tree diagram is the universal organiser for sequential-event problems. Each branch is an intersection (multiply the conditional probabilities along it), and disjoint branches at the same level get added. The without-replacement giveaway: the denominator in the second factor shrinks by $1$, and the numerator of the matching colour shrinks by $1$. If you instead wrote $\bigl(\tfrac{7}{12}\bigr)^{2}$ for $P(\text{RR})$, you assumed independence (with-replacement), and your answer would be wrong by exactly the amount of dependence introduced by the shrinking sample space. ON MDM4U markers explicitly want the conditional probability $P(R_{2} \mid R_{1}) = 6/11$ named, not just numerically computed.
Q5MEDIUM 🇨🇦 AB AB 30-2-style §6 z-Scores · Math 30-2 Stats GO 1.6 [4 marks]

$X \sim N(500, 80^{2})$. (a) $z$ at $620$. (b) $P(X > 660)$ via $68$-$95$-$99.7$.

Answer:  (a) $z = 1.5$  ·  (b) $P(X > 660) \approx 0.025$ (i.e. $2.5\%$)

(a) Apply the $z$-score formula M1·A1

The $z$-score measures the number of standard deviations a raw value sits above (or below) the mean: $$ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} = \frac{620 - 500}{80} = \frac{120}{80} = 1.5. $$ So a raw score of $620$ is $1.5$ standard deviations above the mean.

(b) Read off the tail probability via the empirical rule M1·A1

The raw score $660$ has $z = (660 - 500)/80 = 2$. Under $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^{2})$, the $68$-$95$-$99.7$ rule says $$ P(|X - \mu| \le 2 \sigma) \approx 0.95, \qquad \text{so} \qquad P(X > \mu + 2\sigma) \approx \frac{1 - 0.95}{2} = 0.025. $$ Hence $P(X > 660) \approx 0.025$, i.e. about $2.5\%$.
The empirical rule is the AB Math 30-2 / FPM go-to estimator. The $68$-$95$-$99.7$ rule gives quick tail probabilities at exactly $\pm 1\sigma$, $\pm 2\sigma$, $\pm 3\sigma$, and by symmetry, each tail outside $\pm 2\sigma$ holds half of the $5\%$ that's not in the middle $95\%$. The standard normal table $\Phi(2.00) = 0.9772$ gives the more precise $P(X > 660) = 1 - 0.9772 = 0.0228$, but $0.025$ is the curriculum-expected estimate when only the empirical rule is invoked. AB indicator 1.6 explicitly requires this kind of "use the rule to estimate, then if asked, refine with technology" two-step.

Q6HARDHonors for US A2 / AB 30-2 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Bayes' Rule · HSS-CP.A.3 / .B.6 [8 marks]

$P(D) = 0.02$; $P(+ \mid D) = 0.95$; $P(+ \mid D^{c}) = 0.04$. (a) Identify prior + likelihoods. (b) $P(+)$. (c) Posterior $P(D \mid +)$. (d) Interpret.

Answer:  (a) prior $P(D) = 0.02$; likelihoods $0.95$, $0.04$  ·  (b) $P(+) = 0.0582$  ·  (c) $P(D \mid +) \approx 0.3265$  ·  (d) base-rate fallacy — rarity of $D$ swamps high sensitivity

(a) Label the Bayesian quantities A1

Translate the prose into the three Bayesian roles:
  • Prior: $P(D) = 0.02$ (baseline allergy prevalence; what we believed before seeing the test result).
  • Likelihood under $D$: $P(+ \mid D) = 0.95$ (the test's sensitivity, true-positive rate).
  • Likelihood under $D^{c}$: $P(+ \mid D^{c}) = 0.04$ (the false-positive rate; complement of specificity).

(b) Total probability of a positive test M1·A1

The events $D$ and $D^{c}$ partition the sample space, so the law of total probability gives $$ P(+) = P(+ \mid D) P(D) + P(+ \mid D^{c}) P(D^{c}) = 0.95 \cdot 0.02 + 0.04 \cdot 0.98. $$ Compute: $0.95 \cdot 0.02 = 0.019$ and $0.04 \cdot 0.98 = 0.0392$. Hence $$ P(+) = 0.019 + 0.0392 = 0.0582. $$

(c) Bayes' rule for the posterior M1·A1·A1

Bayes' rule reads $$ P(D \mid +) = \frac{P(+ \mid D) \, P(D)}{P(+)} = \frac{0.019}{0.0582} \approx 0.3265. $$ So even after a positive screen, the posterior probability of having the allergy is about $32.65\%$, well under half.

(d) Interpret the base-rate fallacy R1·A1

Decompose the numerator and denominator of (c) into "true positives" vs. "false positives":
  • True positives: $0.95 \cdot 0.02 = 0.019$ (sensitivity times prior).
  • False positives: $0.04 \cdot 0.98 = 0.0392$ (false-positive rate times prior of not having the allergy).
Because the disease is rare ($P(D) = 0.02$), the pool of healthy people ($P(D^{c}) = 0.98$) is $49$ times larger than the pool of sick people. Even a low $4\%$ false-positive rate applied to that huge healthy pool produces $0.0392$ false positives, more than double the $0.019$ true positives. The posterior $0.019/0.0582 \approx 32.65\%$ reflects this imbalance. Base-rate fallacy: reasoning from the conditional $P(+ \mid D) = 0.95$ to the (mis)conclusion "positive test means $95\%$ chance of allergy" ignores the prior $P(D)$ entirely, that's what Bayes corrects.
Posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior; rarity wins when sensitivity is finite. The qualitative law: as $P(D)$ shrinks, $P(D \mid +)$ shrinks too even when sensitivity is high. To dominate a $4\%$ false-positive rate at $P(D) = 0.02$ you'd need sensitivity well above the practical ceiling, the math is structural, not a quirk of these numbers. AP Stats and HSS-CP.A.3 explicitly call out that conditional probabilities are not symmetric: $P(D \mid +) \ne P(+ \mid D)$, and the Bayes inversion is the lone formula that exchanges them correctly. Whenever you see a "rare disease + sensitive test" prompt, set up the $2 \times 2$ table or the Bayes ratio before reading the multiple-choice answers.
Q7HARDHonors for US A2 / AB 30-2 🇨🇦 ON ON MDM4U-style §4 Discrete RV: $E(X)$, $\mathrm{Var}(X)$ · HSS-MD.A.2–3 [9 marks]

$X$ takes values $0, 1, 2, 3, 4$ with $P = 0.10, 0.25, 0.30, 0.25, 0.10$. (a) Validity. (b) $E(X)$. (c) $E(X^{2})$. (d) $\mathrm{Var}(X)$ + $\sigma_{X}$. (e) Interpret.

Answer:  (a) valid  ·  (b) $E(X) = 2.00$  ·  (c) $E(X^{2}) = 5.30$  ·  (d) $\mathrm{Var}(X) = 1.30$, $\sigma_{X} \approx 1.1402$  ·  (e) long-run average $2$; typical deviation $\approx 1.14$

(a) Validity check A1

All five probabilities are non-negative, and $$ \sum_{x=0}^{4} P(X = x) = 0.10 + 0.25 + 0.30 + 0.25 + 0.10 = 1.00. \;\checkmark $$ So the distribution is valid (CCSSM HSS-MD.A.1).

(b) Expected value M1·A1

Apply $E(X) = \sum x \, P(X = x)$: $$ E(X) = 0(0.10) + 1(0.25) + 2(0.30) + 3(0.25) + 4(0.10) = 0 + 0.25 + 0.60 + 0.75 + 0.40 = 2.00. $$

(c) Expected value of $X^{2}$ M1·A1

$$ E(X^{2}) = 0(0.10) + 1(0.25) + 4(0.30) + 9(0.25) + 16(0.10) = 0 + 0.25 + 1.20 + 2.25 + 1.60 = 5.30. $$

(d) Variance and standard deviation M1·A1·A1

Using the computational formula: $$ \mathrm{Var}(X) = E(X^{2}) - [E(X)]^{2} = 5.30 - 2.00^{2} = 5.30 - 4.00 = 1.30. $$ $$ \sigma_{X} = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)} = \sqrt{1.30} \approx 1.1402. $$

(e) Interpret A1

$E(X) = 2$ is the long-run mean: if you observed many independent copies of $X$ and averaged them, the sample mean would tend to $2$. $\sigma_{X} \approx 1.14$ is the typical deviation from that mean: most observed values sit within roughly $\pm 1.14$ of $2$, i.e. in the rough range $[0.86, 3.14]$ which captures the bulk of the distribution.
$\mathrm{Var}(X) = E(X^{2}) - [E(X)]^{2}$ is the computational shortcut for the defining $E[(X - \mu)^{2}]$. The expanded form $E[(X - \mu)^{2}]$ is conceptually cleaner but algebraically heavier: you'd compute five deviations $(x - 2)^{2}$, multiply by the probabilities, and sum. The shortcut needs only two sums, $\sum x P$ and $\sum x^{2} P$. MDM4U markers happily award the same A1 for either path, but the computational formula is the one used in the binomial proof $\mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$ (which factors $E(X^{2})$ via $E[X(X-1)] + E(X)$, see Q8). Lock the shortcut in early; you'll use it in §5 binomial and in the standard-normal moment derivation in IB Math AA HL.
Q8MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 AB AP-feeder FRQ §5 Binomial Distribution · HSS-MD.A.3 / Math 30-1 Binomial [9 marks]

$X \sim B(10, 0.8)$. (a) PMF. (b) $P(X = 8)$. (c) $P(X \ge 9)$. (d) $E(X)$, $\mathrm{Var}(X)$, $\sigma_{X}$. (e) BINS conditions.

Answer:  (a) $P(X = k) = \dbinom{10}{k} (0.8)^{k} (0.2)^{10-k}$  ·  (b) $\approx 0.3020$  ·  (c) $\approx 0.3758$  ·  (d) $E(X) = 8$, $\mathrm{Var}(X) = 1.6$, $\sigma_{X} \approx 1.2649$  ·  (e) all four BINS conditions met

(a) Binomial PMF A1

$$ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^{k} (1-p)^{n-k} = \binom{10}{k} (0.8)^{k} (0.2)^{10-k}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, 10. $$

(b) $P(X = 8)$ M1·A1

$$ P(X = 8) = \binom{10}{8} (0.8)^{8} (0.2)^{2} = 45 \cdot 0.16777216 \cdot 0.04. $$ Compute step by step: $0.16777216 \cdot 0.04 = 0.0067108864$, and $45 \cdot 0.0067108864 \approx 0.30199$. Round to four decimals: $P(X = 8) \approx 0.3020$.

(c) $P(X \ge 9)$ M1·A1

$$ P(X = 9) = \binom{10}{9} (0.8)^{9} (0.2)^{1} = 10 \cdot 0.134217728 \cdot 0.2 \approx 0.2684. $$ $$ P(X = 10) = (0.8)^{10} \approx 0.1074. $$ $$ P(X \ge 9) = 0.2684 + 0.1074 = 0.3758. $$

(d) Binomial moments M1·A1

$E(X) = np = 10(0.8) = 8$. $\mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1-p) = 10(0.8)(0.2) = 1.6$. $\sigma_{X} = \sqrt{1.6} \approx 1.2649$.

(e) BINS conditions A1·A1

The four binomial-model assumptions:
  • Binary outcomes: each free throw is a make or a miss. $\checkmark$
  • Independent trials: the prompt asserts "each shot independently". $\checkmark$
  • Number of trials fixed in advance: $n = 10$. $\checkmark$
  • Success probability constant: $p = 0.8$ on every shot. $\checkmark$
All four hold, so the binomial model is appropriate for $X$.
$E(X) = np$ is the indicator-variable proof you should keep close. Write $X = \sum_{i=1}^{n} I_{i}$ where $I_{i} = 1$ if trial $i$ is a success and $0$ otherwise. Then $E(I_{i}) = p$ for each trial, and linearity of expectation gives $E(X) = \sum E(I_{i}) = np$, no independence needed. For the variance, $\mathrm{Var}(I_{i}) = p(1-p)$, and by independence of the trials the variances add: $\mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$. The independence hypothesis appears in the variance but not in the mean, and AP Stats explicitly distinguishes the two. The indicator trick is also how you'd prove these for sums of arbitrary independent Bernoullis with different $p_{i}$, the workhorse of next-step inferential statistics.
Q9MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §2 Two-Way Tables · HSS-CP.B.6 [9 marks]

$200$ students; bike/no-bike $\times$ walk/no-walk. (a) Marginals. (b) Intersection. (c) Both conditional probabilities. (d) Independence test.

Answer:  (a) $P(B) = 0.6$, $P(W) = 0.3$  ·  (b) $P(B \cap W) = 0.225$  ·  (c) $P(W \mid B) = 0.375$, $P(B \mid W) = 0.75$  ·  (d) not independent: $0.225 \ne 0.18$

(a) Marginal probabilities from row / column totals A1·A1

Let $B = $ "owns bike", $W = $ "walks to school". Row totals give $$ P(B) = \frac{120}{200} = 0.6, \qquad P(W) = \frac{60}{200} = 0.3. $$

(b) Joint probability from the intersection cell A1

The cell at the intersection of "owns bike" and "walks" is $45$, out of $200$: $$ P(B \cap W) = \frac{45}{200} = 0.225. $$

(c) Conditional probabilities via row / column restriction M1·A1·A1

Row restriction: condition on "owns bike" (row total $120$), then within that row ask what fraction walks (cell $45$): $$ P(W \mid B) = \frac{45}{120} = 0.375 = \frac{P(B \cap W)}{P(B)} = \frac{0.225}{0.6}. \;\checkmark $$ Column restriction: condition on "walks" (column total $60$), then within that column ask what fraction owns a bike (cell $45$): $$ P(B \mid W) = \frac{45}{60} = 0.75 = \frac{P(B \cap W)}{P(W)} = \frac{0.225}{0.3}. \;\checkmark $$ Note $P(W \mid B) \ne P(B \mid W)$, conditional probabilities are not symmetric in their arguments.

(d) Independence test M1·A1·R1

Compare $P(B \cap W) = 0.225$ to $P(B) P(W) = 0.6 \cdot 0.3 = 0.180$: $$ P(B \cap W) = 0.225 \;\;\ne\;\; 0.180 = P(B) P(W). $$ Therefore $B$ and $W$ are not independent. The discrepancy direction $0.225 > 0.180$ means bike-owners are more likely to walk than the marginal would predict , a positive association.
Conditional probability $=$ row-restriction $=$ joint $/$ marginal, all three say the same thing. CCSSM HSS-CP.B.6 explicitly names the "row / column restriction" interpretation because it grounds the abstract formula $P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B)/P(B)$ in something visual: shrink the sample space from the whole table to one slice, then renormalise. The two-way-table workflow is the AP Stats default for any "given that" prompt: (i) build the table from counts; (ii) restrict to the conditioning row or column; (iii) report the relative frequency. Same answer, no formula required. The independence test in (d) collapses to "joint cell vs. (row $\times$ column) outer product", and any $\chi^{2}$ test of independence in AP Stats is just this comparison scaled up.

Q10MEDIUM 🇨🇦 AB AB 30-2-style §6 Normal Distribution · Math 30-2 Stats GO 1 [12 marks]

$X \sim N(165, 7^{2})$ cm. (a) 1-2-3 SD intervals. (b) $P(158 \le X \le 172)$, $P(151 \le X \le 179)$. (c) $z(176)$, $z(150)$. (d) Tail probabilities. (e) $P(150 \le X \le 176)$ + interpret.

Answer:  (a) $[158, 172]$, $[151, 179]$, $[144, 186]$  ·  (b) $\approx 0.68$, $\approx 0.95$  ·  (c) $z(176) \approx 1.57$, $z(150) \approx -2.14$  ·  (d) $P(X > 176) \approx 0.0580$, $P(X < 150) \approx 0.0161$  ·  (e) $\approx 0.9259$

(a) Empirical-rule intervals A1·A1

With $\mu = 165$, $\sigma = 7$:
  • $\mu \pm 1\sigma = [165 - 7,\; 165 + 7] = [158, 172]$.
  • $\mu \pm 2\sigma = [165 - 14,\; 165 + 14] = [151, 179]$.
  • $\mu \pm 3\sigma = [165 - 21,\; 165 + 21] = [144, 186]$.

(b) Empirical-rule probabilities A1·A1

The interval $[158, 172]$ is exactly $\mu \pm 1\sigma$, so $P(158 \le X \le 172) \approx 0.68$.
The interval $[151, 179]$ is exactly $\mu \pm 2\sigma$, so $P(151 \le X \le 179) \approx 0.95$.

(c) $z$-scores M1·A1

$$ z(176) = \frac{176 - 165}{7} = \frac{11}{7} \approx 1.5714 \approx 1.57. $$ $$ z(150) = \frac{150 - 165}{7} = \frac{-15}{7} \approx -2.1429 \approx -2.14. $$

(d) Tail probabilities via $z$-table M1·A1·A1

Standard-normal CDF lookups:
  • $\Phi(1.57) \approx 0.9418$, so $P(X > 176) = 1 - \Phi(1.57) \approx 0.0582$. (Using the more precise $z = 1.5714$ gives $\approx 0.0580$.)
  • $\Phi(-2.14) \approx 0.0162$, so $P(X < 150) \approx 0.0161$. (Equivalent to $1 - \Phi(2.14)$ by symmetry.)

(e) Middle probability + interpretation M1·A1·A1

The interval $[150, 176]$ is the complement of the two tails: $$ P(150 \le X \le 176) = 1 - P(X < 150) - P(X > 176) \approx 1 - 0.0161 - 0.0580 = 0.9259. $$ Context: roughly $92.6\%$ of women in this population have heights between $150$ cm and $176$ cm. The upper tail (above $176$ cm) is much thinner than the lower tail because $176$ is $1.57\sigma$ above the mean while $150$ is $2.14\sigma$ below; the interval is not centred on $\mu = 165$.
The $z$-score reduces every normal problem to one table. The standard-normal CDF $\Phi(z) = P(Z \le z)$ is the only function you ever need to look up; everything else is arithmetic. Symmetry shortcut: $\Phi(-z) = 1 - \Phi(z)$ for any $z$, so you only need the positive half of the table. AB Math 30-2 indicators 1.7–1.9 explicitly want students to "solve contextual problems using $z$-scores and a standard normal table or technology"; the two-step "standardise $\to$ look up" workflow is the curriculum-named method. Whenever the interval is not symmetric about $\mu$, like $[150, 176]$ here, compute each tail separately and subtract from $1$; do not try to apply the empirical rule to an off-centre interval.
Q11MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON MDM4U-style §7 Descriptive Statistics · HSS-ID.A.2–3 [12 marks]

Data: $4, 6, 7, 7, 8, 8, 8, 9, 9, 9, 10$ ($n = 11$). (a) Mean / median / mode. (b) Range / $Q_{1}$ / $Q_{3}$ / IQR. (c) Sample SD. (d) $1.5 \cdot$ IQR outlier check. (e) Skew.

Answer:  (a) $\bar{x} = 85/11 \approx 7.7273$, median $= 8$, modes $8$ and $9$ (bimodal)  ·  (b) range $= 6$, $Q_{1} = 7$, $Q_{3} = 9$, IQR $= 2$  ·  (c) $s \approx 1.6788$  ·  (d) fences $[4, 12]$; no outliers  ·  (e) slightly left-skewed

(a) Centre measures M1·A1·A1

Sum: $4 + 6 + 7 + 7 + 8 + 8 + 8 + 9 + 9 + 9 + 10 = 85$. $$ \bar{x} = \frac{85}{11} \approx 7.7273. $$ With $n = 11$ (odd), the median is the $6$th value of the sorted list: median $= 8$.
Frequencies: $4(1), 6(1), 7(2), 8(3), 9(3), 10(1)$. Both $8$ and $9$ appear $3$ times: bimodal, modes $= 8$ and $9$.

(b) Spread measures M1·A1·A1

Range $= \max - \min = 10 - 4 = 6$.
Lower half (first $5$ values, excluding the median): $4, 6, 7, 7, 8$. Median of lower half $= Q_{1} = 7$.
Upper half (last $5$ values): $8, 9, 9, 9, 10$. Median of upper half $= Q_{3} = 9$.
$\mathrm{IQR} = Q_{3} - Q_{1} = 9 - 7 = 2$.

(c) Sample standard deviation M1·A1·A1

Deviations from $\bar{x} = 85/11$, squared (use common denominator $11$ for arithmetic precision): $$ \sum_{i} (x_{i} - \bar{x})^{2} = \frac{1681 + 361 + 64 + 64 + 9 + 9 + 9 + 196 + 196 + 196 + 625}{121} = \frac{3410}{121}. $$ (Numerators: $|4 - 85/11| = 41/11$ so $(41)^{2} = 1681$; $|6 - 85/11| = 19/11$ so $361$; $|7 - 85/11| = 8/11$ so $64$ (twice); $|8 - 85/11| = 3/11$ so $9$ (three times); $|9 - 85/11| = 14/11$ so $196$ (three times); $|10 - 85/11| = 25/11$ so $625$.) $$ s^{2} = \frac{1}{n - 1} \sum (x_{i} - \bar{x})^{2} = \frac{3410}{121 \cdot 10} = \frac{341}{121} \approx 2.8182. $$ $$ s = \sqrt{2.8182} \approx 1.6788. $$

(d) Outlier check M1·A1

Fences: $$ Q_{1} - 1.5 \cdot \mathrm{IQR} = 7 - 3 = 4, \qquad Q_{3} + 1.5 \cdot \mathrm{IQR} = 9 + 3 = 12. $$ A data point is an outlier if $x < 4$ or $x > 12$. The minimum is exactly $4$ (on the fence, not below) and the maximum $10$ is well inside the upper fence. No outliers.

(e) Skew A1

$\bar{x} \approx 7.7273$ sits below the median $= 8$. The mean is pulled toward the long lower tail (one value at $4$, one at $6$, while the upper tail is short and dense). So the distribution is mildly left-skewed (negatively skewed): mean $<$ median.
Centre measures answer different questions; pick the one matching the prompt. Mean is sensitive to extremes (any value of $4$ pulls $\bar{x}$ down by $4/11$ relative to a value of $8$); median is robust (a single change to the minimum from $4$ to $0$ leaves the median at $8$); mode finds the most common value (here ambiguous, two modes, the data is bimodal). HSS-ID.A.3 explicitly asks students to "interpret differences in shape, center, and spread in the context of the data sets, accounting for possible effects of extreme data points (outliers)." The mean/median gap is the standard diagnostic: $|\bar{x} - \text{median}| / s$ is a quick skew detector, here about $(8 - 7.73)/1.68 \approx 0.16$, small, so the skew is mild. Provincial markers reward the explicit comparison sentence in (e).
Q12HARDHonors for US A2 / AB 30-2 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 AB AP-feeder FRQ §5–§6 Binomial + Normal · HSS-MD.A.3 / HSS-IC.A [13 marks]

$X \sim B(20, 0.05)$. (a) State the model. (b) $P(X = 0)$, $P(X = 1)$. (c) $P(X \ge 2)$ via complement. (d) $E(X)$, $\mathrm{SD}(X)$. (e) $Y \sim B(400, 0.05)$, estimate $P(11 \le Y \le 29)$ via normal approx + empirical rule. (f) Normal-approximation rule of thumb.

Answer:  (a) $X \sim B(20, 0.05)$  ·  (b) $P(X = 0) \approx 0.3585$, $P(X = 1) \approx 0.3774$  ·  (c) $P(X \ge 2) \approx 0.2641$  ·  (d) $E(X) = 1$, $\mathrm{SD}(X) \approx 0.9747$  ·  (e) $E(Y) = 20$, $\mathrm{SD}(Y) \approx 4.359$; $P(11 \le Y \le 29) \approx 0.95$  ·  (f) use when $np \ge 10$ and $n(1-p) \ge 10$

(a) Binomial model A1

Each bulb is defective independently with probability $p = 0.05$, and the inspector samples a fixed $n = 20$. So $X \sim B(20, 0.05)$ (BINS: binary, independent, $n$ fixed, $p$ constant).

(b) PMF at $k = 0$ and $k = 1$ M1·A1

$$ P(X = 0) = \binom{20}{0} (0.05)^{0} (0.95)^{20} = (0.95)^{20} \approx 0.3585. $$ $$ P(X = 1) = \binom{20}{1} (0.05)^{1} (0.95)^{19} = 20 \cdot 0.05 \cdot (0.95)^{19} = 1 \cdot (0.95)^{19} \approx 0.3774. $$ (Numerical: $(0.95)^{19} = (0.95)^{20} / 0.95 \approx 0.3585 / 0.95 \approx 0.3774$.)

(c) Complement trick for "at least two" M1·A1·A1

$$ P(X \ge 2) = 1 - P(X = 0) - P(X = 1) \approx 1 - 0.3585 - 0.3774 = 0.2641. $$ So about $26.41\%$ of samples have $2$ or more defects, more than one in four batches will trip a "two defects" inspection threshold.

(d) Mean and SD of $X$ M1·A1

$$ E(X) = np = 20 \cdot 0.05 = 1.00, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1-p) = 20 \cdot 0.05 \cdot 0.95 = 0.95. $$ $$ \mathrm{SD}(X) = \sqrt{0.95} \approx 0.9747. $$

(e) Normal approximation for $Y$ M1·A1·A1

$E(Y) = 400 \cdot 0.05 = 20$ and $\mathrm{Var}(Y) = 400 \cdot 0.05 \cdot 0.95 = 19$, so $\mathrm{SD}(Y) = \sqrt{19} \approx 4.3589$. Use the normal approximation $Y \approx N(20, 19)$ and recognise the asked interval relative to $\mu_{Y} \pm 2 \sigma_{Y}$: $$ \mu_{Y} - 2 \sigma_{Y} \approx 20 - 8.72 = 11.28, \qquad \mu_{Y} + 2 \sigma_{Y} \approx 20 + 8.72 = 28.72. $$ The interval $[11, 29]$ is approximately $\mu_{Y} \pm 2\sigma_{Y}$ (slightly wider). By the $68$-$95$-$99.7$ rule, $$ P(11 \le Y \le 29) \;\approx\; P(\mu_{Y} - 2\sigma_{Y} \le Y \le \mu_{Y} + 2\sigma_{Y}) \;\approx\; 0.95. $$

(f) Normal approximation rule of thumb R1·A1

The normal approximation $Y \approx N(np, np(1-p))$ is appropriate when both $np \ge 10$ and $n(1-p) \ge 10$ (the "large-sample" rule of thumb taught in AP Stats and AB Math 30-2). Check for our $Y$: $np = 20 \ge 10$ and $n(1-p) = 380 \ge 10$, both satisfied, so the approximation is justified. For $X \sim B(20, 0.05)$ in parts (a)–(d), $np = 1$ fails the rule and the normal approximation would be poor, exactly why parts (b)–(c) use the exact binomial PMF.
The binomial transitions to the normal as $np$, $n(1-p)$ both grow. This is the de Moivre–Laplace special case of the central limit theorem: a sum of $n$ independent Bernoullis is approximately normal once neither tail is too thin. The $np \ge 10$, $n(1-p) \ge 10$ rule of thumb is calibrated so the binomial PMF is well-approximated by the normal density across the bulk of the distribution; for our $Y \sim B(400, 0.05)$ it holds, and the empirical-rule estimate $0.95$ for $\mu \pm 2\sigma$ matches an exact binomial computation to about $0.5\%$. For our $X \sim B(20, 0.05)$, $np = 1 \ll 10$, and the normal approximation would be terrible (the distribution is sharply right-skewed), so always check the rule before invoking the normal. AP Stats grades this as a separate point: state the rule, verify it, then apply the approximation.
