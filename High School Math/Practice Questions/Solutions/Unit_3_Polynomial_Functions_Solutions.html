Q1EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §1 Polynomial Vocabulary · HSA-SSE.A.1 [3 marks]

Degree, leading coefficient, and constant term of $p(x) = -4x^{5} + 7x^{3} - 2x^{2} + 9$.

Answer:  (A)  $5,\ -4,\ 9$

(a) Identify the three structural pieces M1·A1·A1

Write $p(x)$ in descending powers of $x$ (it already is). The degree is the highest exponent on a non-zero term: $5$. The leading coefficient is the coefficient of that highest-degree term: $-4$. The constant term is the coefficient of $x^{0}$, i.e. the term with no $x$: $9$. So the triple is $(5,\ -4,\ 9)$, matching (A).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (B) $5,\ 7,\ 9$, reads degree correctly but picks the coefficient of the second-highest term ($7x^{3}$) as the leading coefficient. Trap: students who skip past the $-4x^{5}$ because the sign is negative.
  • (C) $4,\ -4,\ -2$, off-by-one on the exponent (degree $4$ instead of $5$) and picks the $-2x^{2}$ constant by mistake; the constant term is the term with no $x$, not the smallest-power term that still has $x$ in it.
  • (D) $9,\ -4,\ 5$, swaps degree and constant term: confuses "the constant" ($9$) with "the degree" ($5$).
Standard form first; then read off the three labels in one sweep. Polynomial vocabulary questions are 100% reading comprehension after the polynomial is in descending order. The discipline: rewrite $p(x)$ with terms ordered by decreasing degree before answering anything, even if it already looks ordered , this catches polynomials given as $9 - 2x^{2} + 7x^{3} - 4x^{5}$ where the leading coefficient hides at the right end. The "constant term" is the value $p(0)$; in standard form it's literally the term with no $x$. This three-token sweep (degree, leading coefficient, constant) is the muscle memory for §3.5 end-behaviour (degree + sign of lead) and §3.4 rational root theorem (constant + lead).
Q2EASY 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §3 Remainder Theorem · HSA-SSE.A.2 [3 marks]

Remainder when $p(x) = x^{3} - 4x^{2} + 5x - 7$ is divided by $(x - 2)$.

Answer:  (A)  $-5$

(a) Apply the remainder theorem M1·A1

The remainder theorem: when $p(x)$ is divided by $(x - c)$, the remainder is $p(c)$. Here $c = 2$, so the remainder is $p(2)$.

(b) Evaluate $p(2)$ A1

$$ p(2) \;=\; 2^{3} - 4(2)^{2} + 5(2) - 7 \;=\; 8 - 16 + 10 - 7 \;=\; -5. $$ Remainder is $-5$, matching (A).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (B) $-3$, sign error inside $-4(2)^{2}$: student computes $-4 \cdot 4 = -8$ instead of $-16$ (or applies $-4 \cdot 2^{2} = (-4 \cdot 2)^{2}$, an order-of-operations slip).
  • (C) $0$, the "of course it's a factor" trap; students who memorise that easy MCQ remainders are zero. But the remainder theorem must be computed, not guessed.
  • (D) $5$, drops the overall negative sign at the end, or evaluates $p(-2)$ instead of $p(2)$ by misreading the divisor sign.
The remainder theorem turns long division into one substitution. If the divisor is linear of the form $(x - c)$, never carry out polynomial long division for the remainder , just compute $p(c)$. The sign trap is loud: divisor $(x - 2)$ means $c = +2$, but divisor $(x + 2)$ means $c = -2$ (because $x + 2 = x - (-2)$). The remainder theorem is the engine behind the factor theorem (§3.3): $p(c) = 0 \Leftrightarrow (x - c)$ is a factor. Internalise the substitution-instead-of-division move now; it pays for itself in §3.4 (rational root theorem), Q6, Q12.
Q3MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US SAT-style MCQ §5 End Behaviour · HSF-IF.B.4 [4 marks]

End behaviour of $f(x) = -2x^{4} + 6x^{3} - x + 8$ as $x \to \pm \infty$.

Answer:  (B)  $f(x) \to -\infty$ on both ends

(a) Isolate the leading term M1·A1

For large $|x|$, every lower-degree term is dominated by the leading term, so $f(x) \approx -2x^{4}$. Read off two pieces from $-2x^{4}$: the degree $n = 4$ (even) and the sign of the leading coefficient ($-2 < 0$, i.e. negative).

(b) Combine degree-parity and sign A1·A1

Even degree means both ends behave the same way (left and right go to the same infinity). Negative leading coefficient flips them down. So $f(x) \to -\infty$ as $x \to +\infty$ and as $x \to -\infty$. Matches (B).
Why the wrong choices fail.
  • (A) $f(x) \to +\infty$ on both ends, would be correct for $+2x^{4}$ (positive lead, even degree, both ends up); ignores the sign of the leading coefficient.
  • (C) opposite ends, with $+\infty$ on the left, would be correct for an odd-degree polynomial with negative lead (e.g. $-2x^{5}$); misreads the parity of the degree.
  • (D) opposite ends, with $+\infty$ on the right, would be correct for an odd-degree polynomial with positive lead (e.g. $+2x^{5}$); double error on degree-parity and sign.
Even/odd $\to$ same/opposite; sign of lead $\to$ up/down. The end-behaviour mnemonic is two binary toggles: parity of degree decides whether the two ends agree (even = same direction) or disagree (odd = opposite directions), and sign of leading coefficient decides whether the right end goes up ($+$) or down ($-$). Build the table in your head in four seconds: $(+,$ even$) \to$ up/up; $(-,$ even$) \to$ down/down; $(+,$ odd$) \to$ down/up; $(-,$ odd$) \to$ up/down. Lower-degree terms (the $+6x^{3} - x + 8$ here) are irrelevant for end behaviour , they only shift the middle of the graph, not the wings. SAT MCQ writers love seeding the answer set with all four parity/sign combinations.
Q4MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §2 Polynomial Long Division · MHF4U Polynomial & Rational Functions [6 marks]

Divide $p(x) = 2x^{3} + 5x^{2} - 4x + 3$ by $d(x) = x + 3$ using polynomial long division. (a) Tableau, $q(x)$, $r$. (b) Division identity.

Answer:  (a) $q(x) = 2x^{2} - x - 1$, $r = 6$  ·  (b) $2x^{3} + 5x^{2} - 4x + 3 = (x + 3)(2x^{2} - x - 1) + 6$

(a) Long-division tableau M1·M1·A1·A1

Divide $2x^{3} + 5x^{2} - 4x + 3$ by $x + 3$ step by step. At each step, divide the current leading term by $x$ to get the next quotient term, then multiply and subtract.
  • Step 1. $2x^{3} \div x = 2x^{2}$. Multiply: $2x^{2}(x + 3) = 2x^{3} + 6x^{2}$. Subtract from $2x^{3} + 5x^{2}$: $(2x^{3} + 5x^{2}) - (2x^{3} + 6x^{2}) = -x^{2}$. Bring down $-4x$: new partial dividend $-x^{2} - 4x$.
  • Step 2. $-x^{2} \div x = -x$. Multiply: $-x(x + 3) = -x^{2} - 3x$. Subtract: $(-x^{2} - 4x) - (-x^{2} - 3x) = -x$. Bring down $+3$: new partial dividend $-x + 3$.
  • Step 3. $-x \div x = -1$. Multiply: $-1(x + 3) = -x - 3$. Subtract: $(-x + 3) - (-x - 3) = 6$. Degree of remainder $(0) <$ degree of divisor $(1)$, so stop.
Quotient $q(x) = 2x^{2} - x - 1$. Remainder $r = 6$.

(b) Division identity M1·A1

The identity $p(x) = d(x) \cdot q(x) + r$ with values substituted: $$ 2x^{3} + 5x^{2} - 4x + 3 \;=\; (x + 3)(2x^{2} - x - 1) + 6. $$ Cross-check via remainder theorem: dividing by $(x + 3)$ should give remainder $p(-3) = 2(-27) + 5(9) - 4(-3) + 3 = -54 + 45 + 12 + 3 = 6$. $\checkmark$
"Divide-multiply-subtract-bring down", and keep place-value columns aligned. The single most common ON provincial error is a missing-term column: writing $2x^{3} + 5x^{2} - 4x + 3$ without explicitly leaving a column for any missing power slot. Here every power is present, but for $x^{3} + 1 \div (x + 1)$ you must write $x^{3} + 0 x^{2} + 0 x + 1$ before dividing , otherwise the subtractions misalign and the remainder is wrong. Sign-subtract trap (Step 2 here): subtracting $-3x$ from $-4x$ is $-4x - (-3x) = -4x + 3x = -x$, not $-7x$. The remainder-theorem cross-check at the end is free insurance , if $p(-3) \ne r$, the long division has an error somewhere.
Q5EASY 🇨🇦 BC BC Provincial-style §2 Synthetic Division · BC PC12 polynomial functions and equations [6 marks]

$p(x) = x^{3} - 6x^{2} + 11x - 6$. (a) Synthetic division by $(x - 1)$ with tableau. (b) $q(x)$ and $r$. (c) Factor theorem conclusion.

Answer:  (a) tableau below  ·  (b) $q(x) = x^{2} - 5x + 6$, $r = 0$  ·  (c) $(x - 1)$ is a factor by the factor theorem

(a) Synthetic-division tableau M1·A1·A1

The divisor is $(x - 1)$, so the synthetic-division root is $c = 1$. Write the coefficients of $p(x)$ in descending order: $1,\ -6,\ 11,\ -6$. Bring down the leading $1$; then multiply by $c = 1$ and add to the next column. Repeat.
  • Bring down $1$.
  • $1 \cdot 1 = 1$; $-6 + 1 = -5$.
  • $-5 \cdot 1 = -5$; $11 + (-5) = 6$.
  • $6 \cdot 1 = 6$; $-6 + 6 = 0$.
Bottom row: $1,\ -5,\ 6 \mid 0$. The first three numbers are the coefficients of the quotient (degree drops by 1), and the boxed $0$ is the remainder.

(b) Read off $q(x)$ and $r$ A1·A1

Quotient: $q(x) = x^{2} - 5x + 6$. Remainder: $r = 0$.

(c) Factor-theorem conclusion R1

Since the remainder $p(1) = 0$, by the factor theorem $(x - 1)$ is a factor of $p(x)$.
Synthetic division is long division with the column-bookkeeping removed. It works only when the divisor is linear $(x - c)$ , for higher-degree divisors you must use long division. Two locked-in habits: (1) write the coefficients in descending order with zeros for any missing power (e.g. $x^{3} + 1 \to$ coefficients $1, 0, 0, 1$), and (2) the sign of $c$ flips: divisor $(x - 1) \to c = +1$; divisor $(x + 1) \to c = -1$. The bottom row's last entry is the remainder; the rest are quotient coefficients of one degree lower than the dividend. Bonus: this question's $q(x) = x^{2} - 5x + 6 = (x - 2)(x - 3)$ factors further, so the full factorization is $p(x) = (x - 1)(x - 2)(x - 3)$ , zeros at $1, 2, 3$, a tidy classroom example. Reuse this pattern as a memory hook.

Q6MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §3 Factor Theorem · HSF-IF.C.8 [8 marks]

$p(x) = x^{3} + 2x^{2} - 5x - 6$. (a) $p(-1)$, $p(2)$, $p(-3)$. (b) Factor-theorem identification. (c) Complete factorisation and zeros.

Answer:  (a) $p(-1) = 0$, $p(2) = 0$, $p(-3) = 0$  ·  (b) all three are factors  ·  (c) $p(x) = (x + 1)(x - 2)(x + 3)$; zeros $-1, 2, -3$

(a) Evaluate at the three candidates M1·A1·A1

$$ p(-1) \;=\; (-1)^{3} + 2(-1)^{2} - 5(-1) - 6 \;=\; -1 + 2 + 5 - 6 \;=\; 0. $$ $$ p(2) \;=\; (2)^{3} + 2(2)^{2} - 5(2) - 6 \;=\; 8 + 8 - 10 - 6 \;=\; 0. $$ $$ p(-3) \;=\; (-3)^{3} + 2(-3)^{2} - 5(-3) - 6 \;=\; -27 + 18 + 15 - 6 \;=\; 0. $$

(b) Factor-theorem identification R1·A1

The factor theorem states: $(x - c)$ is a factor of $p(x)$ iff $p(c) = 0$. Each evaluation in (a) gave zero, so all three of $(x + 1)$, $(x - 2)$, $(x + 3)$ are factors of $p(x)$.

(c) Complete factorisation and zeros M1·A1·A1

A cubic has at most three real zeros and at most three linear factors. We have found three distinct factors, so they exhaust $p$ up to a constant. The leading coefficient of the product $(x + 1)(x - 2)(x + 3)$ expanded is $1$, matching the leading $1$ of $p(x)$, so no extra scalar is needed: $$ p(x) \;=\; (x + 1)(x - 2)(x + 3). $$ Verify by expansion. $(x + 1)(x + 3) = x^{2} + 4x + 3$, then $(x^{2} + 4x + 3)(x - 2) = x^{3} - 2x^{2} + 4x^{2} - 8x + 3x - 6 = x^{3} + 2x^{2} - 5x - 6$. $\checkmark$ The three real zeros of $p$ are therefore $x = -1$, $x = 2$, $x = -3$.
Factor theorem turns the cubic into three independent yes/no checks. Once you have a candidate root list, evaluation is the fastest test , one substitution per candidate, no division required. AP graders explicitly award the R1 for citing the factor theorem by name; writing "since $p(-1) = 0$, $(x + 1)$ is a factor" without the citation loses a mark on HSF-IF.C.8. The leading-coefficient check at the end is the audit: if the product of factors has lead $1$ but $p$ has lead $a \ne 1$, the complete factorisation is $p(x) = a \cdot \prod (x - c_{i})$ , do not omit the scalar. This three-zero cubic is the prototype for Q12's sign-chart machinery.
Q7HARD 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §4 Rational Root Theorem · MHF4U Polynomial & Rational Functions [10 marks]

$2x^{3} - 3x^{2} - 11x + 6 = 0$. (a) Candidate rational roots. (b) Find one rational root. (c) Depress and factor the quadratic. (d) All three roots and full factorisation.

Answer:  (a) $\pm \{1, 2, 3, 6, \tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{3}{2}\}$  ·  (b) $x = 3$  ·  (c) depressed quadratic $2x^{2} + 3x - 2 = (2x - 1)(x + 2)$  ·  (d) roots $3,\ \tfrac{1}{2},\ -2$; full factorisation $(x - 3)(2x - 1)(x + 2)$

(a) Candidate $\pm p/q$ list M1·A1·A1

The rational root theorem: any rational root $p/q$ in lowest terms must have $p$ dividing the constant term and $q$ dividing the leading coefficient. Here constant $= 6$, lead $= 2$.
Divisors of $6$ (the $p$-values): $1, 2, 3, 6$.
Divisors of $2$ (the $q$-values): $1, 2$.
All $\pm p/q$: $\pm \{1, 2, 3, 6\} \cup \pm \{\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{2}{2}, \tfrac{3}{2}, \tfrac{6}{2}\} = \pm \{1, 2, 3, 6, \tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{3}{2}\}$ after dropping duplicates ($\tfrac{2}{2} = 1$, $\tfrac{6}{2} = 3$). Twelve candidates in all.

(b) Test candidates by substitution M1·A1

Test $x = 1$: $2 - 3 - 11 + 6 = -6 \ne 0$. Test $x = -1$: $-2 - 3 + 11 + 6 = 12 \ne 0$. Test $x = 2$: $16 - 12 - 22 + 6 = -12 \ne 0$. Test $x = 3$: $2(27) - 3(9) - 11(3) + 6 = 54 - 27 - 33 + 6 = 0$. $\checkmark$
Rational root: $x = 3$.

(c) Depress the cubic via synthetic division M1·A1·A1

Synthetic division by $(x - 3)$ on coefficients $2, -3, -11, 6$:
  • Bring down $2$. Multiply $2 \cdot 3 = 6$; $-3 + 6 = 3$.
  • Multiply $3 \cdot 3 = 9$; $-11 + 9 = -2$.
  • Multiply $-2 \cdot 3 = -6$; $6 + (-6) = 0$.
Quotient: $2x^{2} + 3x - 2$, remainder $0$. Factor the quadratic by AC-method: $AC = 2 \cdot (-2) = -4$; integers with product $-4$ and sum $3$ are $4$ and $-1$. Split and group: $$ 2x^{2} + 4x - x - 2 \;=\; 2x(x + 2) - 1(x + 2) \;=\; (2x - 1)(x + 2). $$

(d) All three roots and full factorisation A1·A1

Roots: $x = 3$, $2x - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $x + 2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -2$.
Full factorisation: $$ 2x^{3} - 3x^{2} - 11x + 6 \;=\; (x - 3)(2x - 1)(x + 2). $$ Audit by expansion of leading and constant terms. Leading: $1 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 = 2$ $\checkmark$. Constant: $(-3)(-1)(2) = 6$ $\checkmark$.
RRT shrinks an infinite search to a finite list , but only fractions in lowest terms. The classic trap: students with leading coefficient $\ne 1$ forget to include the $\pm p/q$ fractions and only test integer candidates , missing roots like $\tfrac{1}{2}$ here. The second trap: testing every candidate when an audit shortcut exists. The sum of all roots equals $-b/a = 3/2$ (Vieta); once you find $x = 3$, the remaining two roots sum to $\tfrac{3}{2} - 3 = -\tfrac{3}{2}$, and their product equals (last coefficient of depressed quadratic) $\div$ (lead of depressed) $= -2/2 = -1$. Then $x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $x = -2$ are forced. Reading roots from coefficients via Vieta is the speed-multiplier MHF4U markers reward. Always cite the rational root theorem by name to bank the R1.
Q8MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §5 Zeros, Multiplicity & End Behaviour · HSF-IF.C.7 [9 marks]

$f(x) = -(x + 2)^{2}(x - 1)(x - 3)$. (a) Degree, sign of lead, end behaviour. (b) $x$-intercepts with multiplicities & cross/touch. (c) $y$-intercept. (d) Sketch.

Answer:  (a) degree $4$, lead $-1 < 0$, both ends $\to -\infty$  ·  (b) $x = -2$ (mult 2, touch); $x = 1$ (mult 1, cross); $x = 3$ (mult 1, cross)  ·  (c) $(0, -12)$  ·  (d) sketch description below

(a) Degree, lead sign, end behaviour M1·A1

Degree = sum of multiplicities = $2 + 1 + 1 = 4$ (even). Leading coefficient of the expanded form: multiply the $x$-coefficient of each factor and the overall $-1$: $(-1)(1)(1)(1) \cdot 1 = -1$ (negative). With even degree and negative lead, end behaviour: $f(x) \to -\infty$ as $x \to +\infty$ and as $x \to -\infty$ (both ends down).

(b) $x$-intercepts with multiplicities A1·A1·A1

Read directly from the factored form. Each factor $(x - c)^{m}$ contributes a zero $x = c$ of multiplicity $m$:
  • $x = -2$, multiplicity $2$ (even) $\to$ graph touches the $x$-axis at $(-2, 0)$ and bounces back.
  • $x = 1$, multiplicity $1$ (odd) $\to$ graph crosses through $(1, 0)$.
  • $x = 3$, multiplicity $1$ (odd) $\to$ graph crosses through $(3, 0)$.

(c) $y$-intercept A1

$$ f(0) \;=\; -(0 + 2)^{2}(0 - 1)(0 - 3) \;=\; -(4)(-1)(-3) \;=\; -12. $$ So the $y$-intercept is $(0, -12)$.

(d) Sketch and verification of sign on the four intervals M1·A1

Zeros at $-2, 1, 3$ split the $x$-axis into four intervals; combined with end behaviour and multiplicity, the sign of $f$ on each is forced. Check $x = 0$: $f(0) = -12 < 0$ on $(-2, 1)$. By the multiplicity rules (sign flips at odd-mult zeros, stays at even-mult zeros), the sign pattern from left to right is:
$(-\infty, -2)$: $\;-\;$; bounce at $x = -2$ (no sign change) $\to$ $(-2, 1)$: $\;-\;$; cross at $x = 1$ $\to$ $(1, 3)$: $\;+\;$; cross at $x = 3$ $\to$ $(3, \infty)$: $\;-\;$.
Sketch: starts at $-\infty$ on the left, rises toward $x = -2$, just touches the axis there and falls back, deep negative dip through $(0, -12)$, crosses up through $(1, 0)$, peaks in $(1, 3)$, crosses back down through $(3, 0)$, and falls to $-\infty$ on the right.
Multiplicity is the sign-change toggle. At a zero of odd multiplicity, $f$ changes sign (graph crosses the $x$-axis , multiplicity $1$ = straight crossing, multiplicity $3$ = inflection-shaped crossing). At a zero of even multiplicity, $f$ keeps its sign (graph touches the axis and bounces back , multiplicity $2$ = parabolic kiss, multiplicity $4$ = flatter kiss). Once one interval's sign is known (use $f(0)$ , the $y$-intercept is free), the rest are forced by walking left/right and flipping signs only at odd-multiplicity zeros. AP graders mark all three sub-rubrics: degree+sign $\to$ end behaviour (the M1), multiplicity $\to$ cross/touch (the A1s), and the actual sketch must show the correct turning behaviour at each zero, not just hit the points. A "touch and bounce" at $x = -2$ that looks like a "cross" loses an A1.
Q9HARDHonors 🇺🇸 US 🇨🇦 BC AP-feeder FRQ §6 Complex Conjugate Roots · HSN-CN.A.7 / BC PC12 [9 marks]

Real cubic, lead $1$, two known roots $x = 3$ and $x = 1 + 2i$. (a) Third root. (b) Conjugate pair $\to$ real quadratic factor $x^{2} - 2x + 5$. (c) Standard form. (d) Geometric meaning.

Answer:  (a) third root $x = 1 - 2i$  ·  (b) $(x - (1 + 2i))(x - (1 - 2i)) = x^{2} - 2x + 5$  ·  (c) $p(x) = x^{3} - 5x^{2} + 11x - 15$  ·  (d) one real $x$-intercept at $x = 3$; irreducible quadratic has no real zeros

(a) Third root by complex conjugate root theorem R1·A1

The complex conjugate root theorem (HSN-CN.A.7 in US, equivalent under BC PC12 polynomial-equations content) states: if a polynomial has real coefficients and a non-real complex root $a + bi$, then its conjugate $a - bi$ is also a root. Here $p$ has integer (hence real) coefficients and $1 + 2i$ is a root, so $\overline{1 + 2i} = 1 - 2i$ must also be a root. The three roots of the cubic are therefore $3,\ 1 + 2i,\ 1 - 2i$, i.e. third root $x = 1 - 2i$.

(b) Conjugate pair multiplies to a real quadratic M1·A1·A1

Use the difference-of-squares pattern $(A - B)(A + B) = A^{2} - B^{2}$ with $A = x - 1$ and $B = 2i$: $$ (x - (1 + 2i))(x - (1 - 2i)) \;=\; ((x - 1) - 2i)((x - 1) + 2i) \;=\; (x - 1)^{2} - (2i)^{2}. $$ Compute: $(x - 1)^{2} = x^{2} - 2x + 1$ and $(2i)^{2} = 4i^{2} = -4$. Therefore $$ (x - 1)^{2} - (2i)^{2} \;=\; (x^{2} - 2x + 1) - (-4) \;=\; x^{2} - 2x + 5. $$ The coefficients are real (in fact integer) as the theorem promises. $\checkmark$

(c) Multiply by the real linear factor and expand M1·A1·A1

Since the third root is $x = 3$, the cubic with lead $1$ is $$ p(x) \;=\; (x - 3)(x^{2} - 2x + 5). $$ Expand step by step: $$ \begin{aligned} p(x) &= x \cdot (x^{2} - 2x + 5) - 3 \cdot (x^{2} - 2x + 5) \\ &= (x^{3} - 2x^{2} + 5x) - (3x^{2} - 6x + 15) \\ &= x^{3} - 2x^{2} + 5x - 3x^{2} + 6x - 15 \\ &= x^{3} - 5x^{2} + 11x - 15. \end{aligned} $$ So $a = -5$, $b = 11$, $c = -15$, all integers as required.
Sanity-check $p(3) = 0$: $27 - 5(9) + 11(3) - 15 = 27 - 45 + 33 - 15 = 0$. $\checkmark$

(d) Geometric interpretation A1

The graph of $y = p(x)$ has exactly one real $x$-intercept, at $x = 3$. The irreducible quadratic factor $x^{2} - 2x + 5$ has discriminant $(-2)^{2} - 4(5) = -16 < 0$, so it has no real zeros , its parabola lies entirely above the $x$-axis (lead $+1 > 0$, no crossings), which means $p$ does not vanish anywhere else on the real line.
Real coefficients $\Rightarrow$ non-real roots in conjugate pairs $\Rightarrow$ odd-degree polynomials have at least one real root. The complex conjugate root theorem has three downstream consequences worth locking in: (1) a real-coefficient polynomial of odd degree must have an odd number of real roots (because non-real roots come in pairs), so cubics always have at least one real $x$-intercept , here, the $x = 3$. (2) Every real-coefficient polynomial factors over $\mathbb{R}$ as a product of real linear factors and real irreducible quadratics (discriminant $< 0$) , never higher-degree irreducibles. (3) The conjugate-pair product $(x - (a + bi))(x - (a - bi)) = x^{2} - 2ax + (a^{2} + b^{2})$ gives a real quadratic with discriminant $-(2b)^{2} < 0$, automatically irreducible. The "$(A - B)(A + B)$" trick with $A = x - a$, $B = bi$ is the speed move , never multiply the brackets term by term.

Q10MEDIUM 🇺🇸 US AP-feeder FRQ §4 Polynomial Modeling · HSA-CED.A.1 [10 marks]

Open-top box: $V(x) = x(20 - 2x)(16 - 2x)$, $x$ in cm. (a) Expand + degree. (b) Practical domain. (c) Solve $V(x) = 384$. (d) Box dimensions for one solution.

Answer:  (a) $V(x) = 4x^{3} - 72x^{2} + 320x$, degree $3$  ·  (b) $x \in (0, 8)$  ·  (c) $x = 2$ and $x = 4$ (the third algebraic root $x = 12$ is outside the domain)  ·  (d) e.g. $x = 2$: $L \times W \times H = 16 \times 12 \times 2$ cm

(a) Expand $V(x)$ into standard form M1·A1

Multiply the two binomials first: $(20 - 2x)(16 - 2x) = 320 - 40x - 32x + 4x^{2} = 4x^{2} - 72x + 320$. Then $$ V(x) \;=\; x(4x^{2} - 72x + 320) \;=\; 4x^{3} - 72x^{2} + 320x. $$ Degree $= 3$ (cubic).

(b) Practical domain A1·A1

Physical constraints: $x > 0$ (must cut a square of positive side), $20 - 2x > 0 \Rightarrow x < 10$ (long side stays positive), and $16 - 2x > 0 \Rightarrow x < 8$ (short side stays positive). The binding constraint is the smaller upper bound, $x < 8$. So $x \in (0, 8)$. Endpoints excluded: at $x = 0$ no box is cut (no height); at $x = 8$ the short side collapses to width $0$ , degenerate box.

(c) Solve $V(x) = 384$ M1·M1·A1·A1

Set $4x^{3} - 72x^{2} + 320x = 384$ and divide through by $4$: $$ x^{3} - 18x^{2} + 80x - 96 \;=\; 0. $$ Rational root theorem. Lead $1$, constant $-96$; candidates are divisors of $96$ with both signs: $\pm \{1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 96\}$.
Test $x = 2$: $8 - 72 + 160 - 96 = 0$. $\checkmark$
Synthetic-divide by $(x - 2)$ on $1, -18, 80, -96$ with $c = 2$: bring down $1$; $1 \cdot 2 = 2$, $-18 + 2 = -16$; $-16 \cdot 2 = -32$, $80 - 32 = 48$; $48 \cdot 2 = 96$, $-96 + 96 = 0$.
Depressed quadratic: $x^{2} - 16x + 48 = 0$. Solve by quadratic formula: $$ x \;=\; \frac{16 \pm \sqrt{256 - 192}}{2} \;=\; \frac{16 \pm \sqrt{64}}{2} \;=\; \frac{16 \pm 8}{2} \;=\; 12 \;\text{or}\; 4. $$ Three algebraic roots: $x = 2, 4, 12$. Keep only those in $(0, 8)$: $x = 2$ and $x = 4$. (Reject $x = 12$.) Both are rational, so no rounding is needed.

(d) Box dimensions for one solution A1·A1

Take $x = 2$. Length $= 20 - 2(2) = 16$ cm; width $= 16 - 2(2) = 12$ cm; height $= x = 2$ cm. So the box is $16 \times 12 \times 2$ cm. Volume check: $16 \cdot 12 \cdot 2 = 384$ cm$^{3}$. $\checkmark$ (The other valid solution $x = 4$ gives a $12 \times 8 \times 4$ box with the same volume.)
Always factor before expanding , factored form gives the practical domain for free. The temptation is to expand $V(x) = x(20 - 2x)(16 - 2x)$ first, then hunt for the domain. Backwards: the factored form is exactly the form where each binding inequality is one factor > 0, so the domain $\cap$ of the three positivity conditions falls out in two lines. The "rational root, then synthetic-divide, then quadratic formula" workflow is the universal cubic-equation pipeline for HSA-CED.A.1: it requires no advanced tools and is what AP graders mark step by step. The third root $x = 12$ is mathematically valid (substitute and check: $12^{3} - 18(144) + 80(12) - 96 = 1728 - 2592 + 960 - 96 = 0$ $\checkmark$) but lies outside the practical domain , a box whose corner cut exceeds half the short side is geometrically impossible. State the rejection reason explicitly to bank the A1.
Q11MEDIUM 🇨🇦 ON ON Provincial-style §5 Building a Polynomial from its Zeros · MHF4U Polynomial & Rational Functions [11 marks]

Cubic with simple zero $x = -2$, double zero $x = 1$, and $f(0) = 8$. (a) Factored form. (b) Find $a$. (c) Standard form. (d) End behaviour, multiplicity behaviour, sketch.

Answer:  (a) $f(x) = a(x + 2)(x - 1)^{2}$  ·  (b) $a = 4$  ·  (c) $f(x) = 4x^{3} - 12x + 8$  ·  (d) ends $\to \pm \infty$ matching lead $+$; cross at $-2$, touch+bounce at $1$; sketch below

(a) Factored form from zeros and multiplicities M1·A1

A simple zero at $r = -2$ contributes the factor $(x - (-2)) = (x + 2)$ with multiplicity $1$; the double zero at $r = 1$ contributes $(x - 1)^{2}$. With unknown leading coefficient $a$: $$ f(x) \;=\; a(x + 2)(x - 1)^{2}. $$ Total degree $= 1 + 2 = 3$, matching "cubic" as stated. $\checkmark$

(b) Determine $a$ from $f(0) = 8$ M1·A1

$$ f(0) \;=\; a(0 + 2)(0 - 1)^{2} \;=\; a \cdot 2 \cdot 1 \;=\; 2a. $$ Set $2a = 8 \Rightarrow a = 4$.

(c) Expand to standard form M1·A1·A1

$(x - 1)^{2} = x^{2} - 2x + 1$, then $(x + 2)(x^{2} - 2x + 1) = x^{3} - 2x^{2} + x + 2x^{2} - 4x + 2 = x^{3} - 3x + 2$. Multiply by $a = 4$: $$ f(x) \;=\; 4(x^{3} - 3x + 2) \;=\; 4x^{3} - 12x + 8. $$ Note the $x^{2}$-coefficient is $0$ , the linear and cubic terms expand cleanly with no quadratic term. Audit: $f(0) = 8$ $\checkmark$; $f(1) = 4 - 12 + 8 = 0$ $\checkmark$; $f(-2) = -32 + 24 + 8 = 0$ $\checkmark$.

(d) End behaviour, multiplicity behaviour, sketch A1·A1·A1·A1

End behaviour. Degree $3$ (odd), lead $+4 > 0$. So $f(x) \to -\infty$ as $x \to -\infty$ and $f(x) \to +\infty$ as $x \to +\infty$ (down-on-left, up-on-right).
Multiplicity behaviour at each zero. At $x = -2$ (mult $1$, odd): graph crosses the $x$-axis. At $x = 1$ (mult $2$, even): graph touches the axis and bounces back (no sign change).
Sketch. Coming from $-\infty$ on the far left, $f$ rises, crosses the $x$-axis at $(-2, 0)$ into the upper half-plane, peaks somewhere in $(-2, 1)$ (passing through the $y$-intercept $(0, 8)$ , in fact this is near the local max), descends, touches the $x$-axis at $(1, 0)$ without crossing, and continues climbing to $+\infty$ on the right. The graph stays $\ge 0$ on $[-2, \infty)$ , the bounce at $x = 1$ keeps $f$ on the same (positive) side.
Build factored form from zeros first, then pin down $a$ with the remaining condition. Reverse-engineering a polynomial from its graph data is a two-step recipe: (1) every zero $r$ with multiplicity $m$ contributes $(x - r)^{m}$; multiply them all and prefix an unknown leading coefficient $a$; (2) use the one "extra" datum (here $f(0) = 8$) to solve for $a$. The order matters: if you try to find $a$ before writing all the factors, you have no equation to solve. Sign trap: signed zero $x = -2$ becomes factor $(x + 2)$, not $(x - 2)$ , the bracket sign flips relative to the root. MHF4U markers reward students who explicitly link odd/even multiplicity to cross/touch and end behaviour; writing "the graph crosses at $-2$ because the factor $(x + 2)$ has multiplicity $1$" earns an A1 that "the graph touches at $1$" alone does not.
Q12HARDHonors 🇨🇦 BC 🇺🇸 US BC Provincial-style §7 Polynomial Inequality (Sign Analysis) · BC PC12 / HSA-REI.D.11 [11 marks]

$x^{3} - 4x^{2} + x + 6 \le 0$. (a) Verify $x = -1$ is a root. (b) Full factor + zeros. (c) Sign chart. (d) Solution set. (e) Reservoir context: $r(t) = -p(t) \ge 0$ on $[0, 4]$.

Answer:  (a) $p(-1) = 0 \Rightarrow (x + 1)$ is a factor  ·  (b) $p(x) = (x + 1)(x - 2)(x - 3)$; zeros $-1, 2, 3$  ·  (c) $-, +, -, +$ on $(-\infty, -1), (-1, 2), (2, 3), (3, \infty)$  ·  (d) $(-\infty, -1] \cup [2, 3]$  ·  (e) $t \in [2, 3]$ (hours after midnight)

(a) Verify $x = -1$ is a root M1·A1

$$ p(-1) \;=\; (-1)^{3} - 4(-1)^{2} + (-1) + 6 \;=\; -1 - 4 - 1 + 6 \;=\; 0. $$ Since $p(-1) = 0$, by the factor theorem $(x - (-1)) = (x + 1)$ is a factor of $p(x)$.

(b) Full factorisation and zeros M1·A1·A1

Synthetic-divide $1, -4, 1, 6$ by $c = -1$: bring down $1$; $1 \cdot (-1) = -1$, $-4 + (-1) = -5$; $-5 \cdot (-1) = 5$, $1 + 5 = 6$; $6 \cdot (-1) = -6$, $6 + (-6) = 0$.
Depressed quadratic: $x^{2} - 5x + 6 = (x - 2)(x - 3)$. So $$ p(x) \;=\; (x + 1)(x - 2)(x - 3). $$ Three real zeros: $x = -1$, $x = 2$, $x = 3$.

(c) Sign chart M1·A1·A1

The three zeros partition the real line into four intervals. Test one point per interval in $p(x) = (x + 1)(x - 2)(x - 3)$:
  • $x = -2$: $(-2 + 1)(-2 - 2)(-2 - 3) = (-1)(-4)(-5) = -20$. Sign: $-$.
  • $x = 0$: $(1)(-2)(-3) = 6$. Sign: $+$.
  • $x = 2.5$: $(3.5)(0.5)(-0.5) = -0.875$. Sign: $-$.
  • $x = 4$: $(5)(2)(1) = 10$. Sign: $+$.
Sign chart (left to right): $\;-\;\big|\;-1\;\big|\;+\;\big|\;2\;\big|\;-\;\big|\;3\;\big|\;+\;$. Each odd-multiplicity zero flips the sign; the alternating $-, +, -, +$ pattern is exactly what symmetry predicts for a cubic with positive lead and three simple real zeros (the graph rises from $-\infty$, crosses up at $-1$, crosses down at $2$, crosses up at $3$, climbs to $+\infty$).

(d) Solution set of $p(x) \le 0$ A1·A1

Non-strict $\le 0$: take the $-$ intervals and the boundary zeros (where $p = 0$). From (c) the $-$ intervals are $(-\infty, -1)$ and $(2, 3)$; adjoin the zeros $-1, 2, 3$: $$ \{x : p(x) \le 0\} \;=\; (-\infty, -1] \cup [2, 3]. $$

(e) Reservoir context A1

$r(t) = -p(t)$, so $r(t) \ge 0 \Leftrightarrow p(t) \le 0$. On the situational domain $t \in [0, 4]$, intersect with the solution from (d): $\bigl((-\infty, -1] \cup [2, 3]\bigr) \cap [0, 4] = [2, 3]$. Water flows into the reservoir on the subinterval $\boxed{t \in [2, 3]}$ , from 2 a.m. to 3 a.m. (the rest of the four-hour window the net flow is out of the reservoir).
Sign chart $=$ one diagram, all four inequalities for free. Once $p$ is fully factored and the sign chart is drawn, every related inequality reads off without re-work: $p < 0$ takes the open $-$ intervals; $p \le 0$ takes the closed $-$ intervals (including the zeros); $p > 0$ takes the open $+$ intervals; $p \ge 0$ takes the closed $+$ intervals. The $\le$ vs $<$ distinction only changes whether the zeros are bracketed. Two structural moves to lock in: (1) cubic with positive lead and three distinct real zeros always alternates $-, +, -, +$ , you can read the sign chart off the leading-coefficient sign alone, then audit with one test point; (2) negating $p$ flips the sign chart wholesale ($+ \leftrightarrow -$), so part (e)'s "$r = -p \ge 0$" is identical to "$p \le 0$" , no second sign chart needed. BC PC12 markers reward this kind of structural reuse with an R1; a re-drawn sign chart for $r$ wastes time. Caution at boundary: open vs closed brackets are an A1 trap , lose a mark by writing $(2, 3)$ instead of $[2, 3]$.
