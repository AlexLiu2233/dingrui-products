Unit 4: Rational and Radical Expressions

Rational expressions extend fractions from numbers to polynomials, and radicals are the inverse of exponentiation. This unit covers the arithmetic of rational expressions, rational equations and their extraneous solutions, the graph of a rational function (vertical and horizontal asymptotes, removable holes), the operations of radicals (simplify, combine, rationalise denominators), radical equations and extraneous roots, and the bridge between radicals and rational exponents. The unit is BC Pre-Calc 11/12 territory by curriculum design and feeds into IB Math HL B3 (functions with asymptotes), IB Math HL E3 (partial-fraction integration), and AP Calculus (limits at infinity).

If you are cramming the night before

Five non-negotiables. (1) Factor first, then cancel: never cancel across a $+$ or $-$. (2) For rational equations: state the domain restriction before you clear denominators, and re-check every candidate root against it. (3) For radical equations: state $\text{radicand} \ge 0$ and the principal-root sign convention, then square once and check. (4) Asymptote shortcut for $p(x)/q(x)$: vertical asymptotes at roots of $q$ that aren't roots of $p$; roots of both give holes; horizontal asymptote is read off the leading coefficients via degree comparison ($<$, $=$, $>$). (5) Rational exponents convert in one move: $a^{m/n} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^{m} = \sqrt[n]{a^{m}}$.

Section 4.1 · Simplify, Multiply, Divide

Rational Expressions: Simplifying, Multiplying, Dividing 🇨🇦 BC PC11 · 🇺🇸 US Alg 2+

What is a rational expression? Any expression of the form $p(x) / q(x)$ where $p$ and $q$ are polynomials and $q(x) \ne 0$. The set of values of $x$ where $q(x) = 0$ is called the restriction (or excluded values).

Simplify ($p(x) / q(x)$).
  1. Factor $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ completely.
  2. State restrictions: write the values of $x$ for which $q(x) = 0$. These are excluded from the domain.
  3. Cancel common factors (never terms across a $+$ or $-$).
Multiply. $\dfrac{a}{b} \cdot \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{a c}{b d}$. Factor first, then cancel any common factor between any numerator and any denominator; finally multiply across.

Divide. $\dfrac{a}{b} \div \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{a}{b} \cdot \dfrac{d}{c}$ (multiply by the reciprocal). Restrictions: $b \ne 0$, $c \ne 0$, $d \ne 0$.
Worked Example 4.1 · Simplify and multiply

Simplify $\dfrac{x^{2} - 9}{x^{2} - 6 x + 9} \cdot \dfrac{x - 3}{x + 3}$ and state restrictions.

Factor every polynomial. $\dfrac{x^{2} - 9}{x^{2} - 6 x + 9} = \dfrac{(x - 3)(x + 3)}{(x - 3)^{2}}$.

Restrictions. From the denominators: $x \ne 3$ and $x \ne -3$.

Multiply and cancel.

$$ \frac{(x - 3)(x + 3)}{(x - 3)^{2}} \cdot \frac{x - 3}{x + 3} \;=\; \frac{(x - 3)(x + 3)(x - 3)}{(x - 3)^{2}(x + 3)} \;=\; 1. $$

Evaluate. The product simplifies to the constant $1$, valid for $x \ne 3$ and $x \ne -3$. State the restrictions even when they vanish from the visible expression; the original product is undefined at those values.

Going deeper · Why "factor before you cancel" is non-negotiable

The cancellation law $\dfrac{a c}{b c} = \dfrac{a}{b}$ (for $c \ne 0$) applies to factors joined by multiplication, not terms joined by $+$ or $-$. The classic illegal move is $\dfrac{x + 3}{x} \;\not\to\; 1 + 3 = 4$: the numerator is a sum, not a product. Compare with the legal $\dfrac{x (x + 3)}{x} = x + 3$ (for $x \ne 0$), where $x$ is a true factor. The factor-first rule of §4.1's first step exists to expose true factors so the cancellation law applies cleanly; forgetting it produces the single most common error in this entire unit.

Section 4.2 · Add and Subtract

Rational Expressions: Adding and Subtracting

Same denominator. Just add (or subtract) the numerators: $\dfrac{a}{c} \pm \dfrac{b}{c} = \dfrac{a \pm b}{c}$. Keep the common denominator; never add the denominators.

Different denominators (the general algorithm).
  1. Factor every denominator completely.
  2. Build the LCD (least common denominator): for each distinct factor, take its highest power appearing in any single denominator. Multiply those together.
  3. Rewrite each fraction over the LCD by multiplying its numerator and denominator by the missing factor(s).
  4. Add numerators over the common LCD; simplify the resulting numerator.
  5. Factor and cancel the result if possible. State restrictions from every denominator that ever appeared.
Subtraction warning. Distribute the minus across every term in the second numerator. $\dfrac{a}{c} - \dfrac{b + d}{c} = \dfrac{a - b - d}{c}$, not $\dfrac{a - b + d}{c}$.
Worked Example 4.2 · Add two rationals with different denominators

Combine $\dfrac{2}{x - 1} + \dfrac{3}{x + 2}$ into a single rational expression and state restrictions.

Factor and build the LCD. Each denominator is already a single distinct linear factor, so $\text{LCD} = (x - 1)(x + 2)$.

Rewrite over the LCD and add numerators.

$$ \frac{2 (x + 2) + 3 (x - 1)}{(x - 1)(x + 2)} \;=\; \frac{2 x + 4 + 3 x - 3}{(x - 1)(x + 2)} \;=\; \frac{5 x + 1}{(x - 1)(x + 2)}. $$

Restrictions. $x \ne 1$ and $x \ne -2$.

Evaluate. The numerator $5 x + 1$ does not share a factor with $(x - 1)(x + 2)$ (root $x = -1/5$ is in neither denominator), so the expression is already in simplest form.

Going deeper · The LCD as a polynomial-version of LCM

For integers, $\operatorname{lcm}(12, 18) = \operatorname{lcm}(2^{2} \cdot 3, \;2 \cdot 3^{2}) = 2^{2} \cdot 3^{2} = 36$ — take the highest power of each prime. The polynomial LCD is the exact same recipe with irreducible polynomials playing the role of primes. If denominators factor as $(x - 1)(x + 2)^{2}$ and $(x - 1)^{2}(x - 3)$, the LCD is $(x - 1)^{2}(x + 2)^{2}(x - 3)$. This is the smallest polynomial that contains both original denominators as factors, which is exactly the property that lets you rewrite each fraction over a single denominator without introducing junk factors.

Section 4.3 · Rational Equations

Rational Equations and Extraneous Solutions 🇺🇸 CCSSM HSA-REI.A.2 · 🇨🇦 BC PC11

Syllabus note. US Common Core HSA-REI.A.2 reads "solve simple rational and radical equations in one variable, and give examples showing how extraneous solutions may arise." The "give examples" wording is unusual — it tells you explicitly that the curriculum expects the student to explain the extraneous-solution mechanism, not just to compute it. BC PC11 lists rational expressions and equations as a single Content topic, with the curriculum's sample inquiry question "How do the strategies for solving linear equations extend to solving quadratic, radical, or rational equations?" framing the procedural-fluency expectation.
The algorithm for $\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \dfrac{r(x)}{s(x)}$ (or any rational equation).
  1. State the domain restriction first: $q(x) \ne 0$, $s(x) \ne 0$, and so on for every denominator.
  2. Multiply both sides by the LCD of every denominator.
  3. Solve the resulting polynomial equation by any method from Unit 2-3.
  4. Discard any candidate root that lands inside the restriction set. Such a root is called extraneous: it solves the multiplied-out equation but not the original.
Why extraneous solutions arise. Multiplying both sides by an expression containing $x$ can introduce solutions where that expression is zero — precisely the values that were excluded by the original denominators. The check against the restriction set is mandatory.

Shortcut for "cross-multiply" form $\dfrac{a}{b} = \dfrac{c}{d}$. Equivalent to $a d = b c$ provided $b \ne 0$ and $d \ne 0$. Same extraneous-solution check applies.
Worked Example 4.3 · A rational equation with an extraneous root

Solve $\dfrac{x}{x - 2} = \dfrac{2}{x - 2} + 1$ and identify any extraneous solutions.

State restrictions. $x - 2 \ne 0$, so $x \ne 2$.

Multiply both sides by the LCD $(x - 2)$ and simplify.

$$ x \;=\; 2 + (x - 2) \;=\; x. $$

The result is the identity $x = x$ — it holds for every real $x$, but only every $x \ne 2$ satisfies the original equation. So the solution set is $\{x \in \mathbb{R} : x \ne 2\}$; $x = 2$ would be extraneous (it solves the cleared equation trivially but is excluded by the original denominator).

Contrast with a concrete-root case. Solve $\dfrac{3}{x - 1} = \dfrac{x + 1}{x - 1}$. Restriction $x \ne 1$. Multiply by $(x - 1)$: $3 = x + 1$, so $x = 2$ (valid since $2 \ne 1$). If the cleared equation had given $x = 1$, that would be extraneous and the equation would have no solution.

Going deeper · The mechanism behind extraneous solutions

Multiplying an equation by an expression $E(x)$ produces a new equation whose solution set is the union of (a) solutions of the original and (b) zeros of $E(x)$. If the original has $E(x)$ as a denominator, then zeros of $E(x)$ were excluded; the multiplied equation re-includes them, producing extraneous solutions. Formally: if $A = B$ on a domain $D$, then $A E = B E$ on $D$ trivially — but the polynomial equation $A E = B E$ holds on all of $\mathbb{R}$ wherever $E = 0$ (both sides are $0$), regardless of whether the original was true there. The extra solutions live at $\{x : E(x) = 0\}$, exactly the restriction set. The discard step in §4.3's algorithm filters them out.

Section 4.4 · Rational Functions

Graphing Rational Functions: Asymptotes, Intercepts, Holes 🇨🇦 BC PC12 · ON MHF4U · 🇺🇸 US Pre-Calc

Syllabus note. BC PC12's rational functions Content elaboration names asymptotes, intercepts, point discontinuities (holes), domain, and end-behaviour as the elaboration list. Ontario MHF4U's Strand C is Polynomial and Rational Functions; the course description names rational-function graphing as in-scope. US Common Core covers asymptote / end behavior loosely under HSF-IF.B.4 and Algebra 2; deep rational-function graphing is generally Pre-Calc. The five-step algorithm below is the BC PC12 / MHF4U / Pre-Calc capstone for this unit.
Five-step algorithm for $f(x) = \dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ in lowest terms (after cancellation).
  1. Factor $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ completely. Identify common factors first; each common factor $(x - a)$ becomes a hole (point discontinuity) at $x = a$ in the simplified graph.
  2. $x$-intercepts: roots of $p(x)$ that are not roots of $q(x)$. Equivalently, zeros of $f$ after cancellation.
  3. Vertical asymptotes (VAs): roots of $q(x)$ that are not roots of $p(x)$ (after cancellation). At each VA, $f(x) \to \pm \infty$.
  4. Horizontal asymptote (HA) by degree comparison. Let $\deg p = n$, $\deg q = m$, with leading coefficients $a_{n}, b_{m}$. If $n < m$: HA is $y = 0$. If $n = m$: HA is $y = a_{n}/b_{m}$. If $n > m$: no HA — instead a slant (oblique) asymptote if $n = m + 1$, via polynomial long division.
  5. $y$-intercept: $f(0)$, when $0$ is in the domain.
Domain. $\{x \in \mathbb{R} : q(x) \ne 0\}$. Even cancelled holes are excluded.
Worked Example 4.4 · A rational function with hole, VA, and HA

Sketch $f(x) = \dfrac{x^{2} - 4}{x^{2} - x - 6}$. Identify intercepts, holes, vertical asymptotes, horizontal asymptote, and domain.

Factor. $f(x) = \dfrac{(x - 2)(x + 2)}{(x - 3)(x + 2)}$.

Hole. Common factor $(x + 2)$ cancels → hole at $x = -2$. $y$-value from simplified $\dfrac{x - 2}{x - 3}$ at $-2$: $\dfrac{-4}{-5} = 0.8$, so hole at $(-2, 0.8)$.

Simplified $f(x) = \dfrac{x - 2}{x - 3}$. Use this for the rest. $x$-intercept: $x = 2$. Vertical asymptote: $x = 3$. Horizontal asymptote: equal degrees with leading coefficients $1$ and $1$, so $y = 1$. $y$-intercept: $f(0) = 2/3$.

Domain. $\{x \in \mathbb{R} : x \ne -2, \;x \ne 3\}$. Both excluded: one is a hole, one is a vertical asymptote.

Going deeper · Why "common factor = hole" and "denominator-only zero = VA"

If $p(x) = (x - a) \tilde p(x)$ and $q(x) = (x - a) \tilde q(x)$ share the factor $(x - a)$ with $\tilde q(a) \ne 0$, then for $x \ne a$ we cancel: $f(x) = \tilde p(x) / \tilde q(x)$. The simplified expression has a well-defined value $\tilde p(a) / \tilde q(a)$ at $x = a$, but $f$ itself is undefined there because $q(a) = 0$. The graph has a "hole" at $(a, \tilde p(a) / \tilde q(a))$: the surrounding curve approaches the point, but the point itself is missing.

By contrast, if $q(b) = 0$ but $p(b) \ne 0$, no cancellation removes the $(x - b)$ factor. As $x \to b$, the numerator approaches a nonzero constant and the denominator approaches $0$; $|f(x)| \to \infty$. That is a vertical asymptote. This vocabulary is the bridge to IB Math HL B3 (Functions with Asymptotes) and to AP Calc Unit 1, where the same factor-and-cancel move is the standard limits-at-a-point computation for removable discontinuities.

Section 4.5 · Radicals

Radicals: Simplifying, Operations, Rationalising 🇨🇦 BC PC11 · 🇺🇸 US Alg 1+

Vocabulary. $\sqrt[n]{a} = a^{1/n}$ is the principal $n$-th root: the unique non-negative real $n$-th root when $a \ge 0$ and $n$ is a positive integer; the unique real cube root when $n$ is odd. The expression under the radical is the radicand.

Simplify $\sqrt{N}$ (with $N$ a positive integer). Find the largest perfect-square factor of $N$; factor it out: $\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \cdot 2} = 6 \sqrt{2}$. Same for $\sqrt[3]{}$ and perfect-cube factors.

Operations. The fundamental rules (for $a, b \ge 0$ where required):
  • Product: $\sqrt{a} \cdot \sqrt{b} = \sqrt{a b}$.
  • Quotient: $\sqrt{a} / \sqrt{b} = \sqrt{a / b}$ for $b > 0$.
  • Like radicals add: $3 \sqrt{2} + 5 \sqrt{2} = 8 \sqrt{2}$. Unlike radicals don't.
Rationalising a denominator.
  • Single radical: multiply top and bottom by the same radical: $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{a}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{a}$.
  • Binomial: multiply by the conjugate. Conjugate of $\sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b}$ is $\sqrt{a} - \sqrt{b}$; their product is $a - b$ (a difference of squares, no radicals).
Worked Example 4.5 · Simplify and rationalise

(a) Simplify $\sqrt{48} + \sqrt{27} - \sqrt{12}$. (b) Rationalise the denominator of $\dfrac{2}{3 - \sqrt{5}}$.

(a) Pull out perfect-square factors: $\sqrt{48} = 4 \sqrt{3}$, $\sqrt{27} = 3 \sqrt{3}$, $\sqrt{12} = 2 \sqrt{3}$. Combine like radicals: $(4 + 3 - 2) \sqrt{3} = 5 \sqrt{3}$.

(b) Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate $3 + \sqrt{5}$:

$$ \frac{2}{3 - \sqrt{5}} \cdot \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{3 + \sqrt{5}} \;=\; \frac{2 (3 + \sqrt{5})}{9 - 5} \;=\; \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}. $$

Evaluate. No radical remains in the denominator; the expression is in standard radical form. This conjugate trick is the same difference-of-squares move from §2.3 applied to surds.

Going deeper · Why $\sqrt{a^{2}} = |a|$, not $a$

The principal square root is the unique non-negative $y$ with $y^{2} = x$. If $a$ is real, $a^{2}$ is non-negative, but $a$ itself might be negative. So $\sqrt{a^{2}} = |a|$ regardless of the sign of $a$: equals $a$ when $a \ge 0$, equals $-a$ when $a < 0$. This is why writing $\sqrt{(x - 3)^{2}} = x - 3$ is illegal in general — it's only true when $x \ge 3$. The unconditional identity is $\sqrt{(x - 3)^{2}} = |x - 3|$. For odd indices, the issue does not arise: $\sqrt[3]{a^{3}} = a$ for every real $a$ because the cube root preserves sign. The absolute-value subtlety is an even-index-specific phenomenon.

Section 4.6 · Radical Equations

Radical Equations and Extraneous Roots 🇺🇸 CCSSM HSA-REI.A.2 · 🇨🇦 BC PC11

Syllabus note. Radical equations and rational equations share the same Common Core standard HSA-REI.A.2, and the same "give examples showing how extraneous solutions may arise" wording. BC PC11 lists radical operations and equations as a single Content topic alongside rational expressions and equations. Both are anchored in PC11's algebra Big Idea: "the meanings of, and connections between, operations extend to powers, radicals, and polynomials," with the inquiry question "How do the strategies for solving linear equations extend to solving quadratic, radical, or rational equations?" naming radicals explicitly.
The algorithm for $\sqrt{f(x)} = g(x)$ (one radical).
  1. State the domain. The radicand must be non-negative: $f(x) \ge 0$. If $g(x) = \text{number}$, that number must also be $\ge 0$ for any solution to exist.
  2. Isolate the radical on one side.
  3. Square both sides to remove the radical: $f(x) = g(x)^{2}$.
  4. Solve the resulting polynomial equation.
  5. Check every candidate root in the original equation. Squaring can introduce extraneous roots because $A = B$ and $A = -B$ both square to $A^{2} = B^{2}$.
Two radicals. Isolate one, square, simplify; isolate the remaining radical (if any), square again. The second squaring still requires a final check.

Sign of the principal square root. $\sqrt{f(x)}$ always means the non-negative square root. Any candidate that makes the radical equal a negative number on the left side is extraneous.
Worked Example 4.6 · A radical equation with an extraneous root

Solve $\sqrt{x + 6} = x$.

State the domain. Radicand $x + 6 \ge 0$, so $x \ge -6$. Also $\sqrt{x + 6} \ge 0$ (principal root), so the right side $x$ must satisfy $x \ge 0$ for any solution.

Square both sides and solve. $x + 6 = x^{2}$, i.e. $(x - 3)(x + 2) = 0$, giving candidates $x = 3$ or $x = -2$.

Check each candidate in the original. $x = 3$: $\sqrt{9} = 3$, matches the right side — valid. $x = -2$: $\sqrt{4} = 2$, but the right side is $-2$; $2 \ne -2$ — extraneous.

Solution. $x = 3$ only. The root $x = -2$ satisfied the squared equation but not the original, because squaring $A = -B$ produces the same equation as $A = B$.

Going deeper · Why squaring introduces extraneous roots

$A = B \Rightarrow A^{2} = B^{2}$ is true; the converse is false: $A^{2} = B^{2}$ only tells us $A = \pm B$. Squaring an equation can introduce solutions of $A = -B$ in addition to those of $A = B$ — the new ones are extraneous because the original didn't include them. For $\sqrt{x + 6} = x$ the principal root is $\ge 0$, so the squared equation $x + 6 = x^{2}$ is equivalent to $\sqrt{x + 6} = x$ or $\sqrt{x + 6} = -x$; the candidate $x = -2$ satisfies the second branch ($\sqrt{4} = 2 = -(-2)$) but not the first. This is the same mechanism as in §4.3 (multiplying by a denominator): any non-injective operation applied to both sides can re-include excluded values and requires a post-hoc check.

Section 4.7 · Rational Exponents

Rational Exponents and Connections to Radicals Honors (BC PC11 / US Alg 2+) 🇺🇸 CCSSM HSN-RN.A.1, A.2

Syllabus note. CCSSM HSN-RN.A.1 reads "explain how the definition of the meaning of rational exponents follows from extending the properties of integer exponents to those values, allowing for a notation for radicals in terms of rational exponents" — the exact bridge between §4.5-4.6 and this section. HSN-RN.A.2 covers rewriting expressions involving radicals and rational exponents using the properties of exponents. BC PC11 lists powers with rational exponents as a Content topic in the same course as radical operations and rational expressions, treating them as one coordinated package.
The definition. For $a > 0$ and integers $m, n$ with $n > 0$, $$ a^{m/n} \;=\; \sqrt[n]{a^{m}} \;=\; \left( \sqrt[n]{a} \right)^{m}. $$ For $a < 0$ the definition extends only when $n$ is odd. For $a = 0$, $0^{p} = 0$ when $p > 0$.

Exponent laws (extend from integer to rational exponents). $a^{p} a^{q} = a^{p + q}$; $a^{p}/a^{q} = a^{p - q}$; $(a^{p})^{q} = a^{p q}$; $(a b)^{p} = a^{p} b^{p}$; $a^{-p} = 1/a^{p}$; $a^{0} = 1$ (for $a \ne 0$). The conversion table. $\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$; $\sqrt[3]{x} = x^{1/3}$; $\sqrt[3]{x^{2}} = x^{2/3}$; $1/\sqrt{x} = x^{-1/2}$; $\sqrt{x} \cdot \sqrt[3]{x} = x^{1/2 + 1/3} = x^{5/6}$. Use whichever form makes the algebra cleaner. Combining radicals with different indices almost always goes faster through rational exponents.
Worked Example 4.7 · Use rational exponents to combine radicals

Simplify $\dfrac{\sqrt[3]{x^{2}} \cdot \sqrt{x}}{\sqrt[6]{x}}$ for $x > 0$.

Convert each radical to a rational exponent. $\sqrt[3]{x^{2}} = x^{2/3}$, $\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$, $\sqrt[6]{x} = x^{1/6}$.

Apply exponent laws and combine over LCD $6$.

$$ \frac{x^{2/3} \cdot x^{1/2}}{x^{1/6}} \;=\; x^{2/3 + 1/2 - 1/6} \;=\; x^{4/6 + 3/6 - 1/6} \;=\; x^{6/6} \;=\; x. $$

Result. $x^{1} = x$. The same computation done as radicals would require a common index of $6$ and an order of magnitude more arithmetic; rational exponents make it a one-line LCD addition.

Going deeper · Why $a^{m/n}$ is forced by the exponent laws

To extend exponentiation from integer exponents to rational ones while preserving $(a^{p})^{q} = a^{p q}$: $a^{1/n}$ must satisfy $(a^{1/n})^{n} = a^{1} = a$, so $a^{1/n}$ is an $n$-th root of $a$; we pick the principal one, giving $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$. Then $a^{m/n} = (a^{1/n})^{m} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^{m}$, or equivalently $(a^{m})^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a^{m}}$. Both forms agree because both are forced by the exponent laws — CCSSM's HSN-RN.A.1 calls this "extending the properties of integer exponents." The same logic forces $a^{-p} = 1/a^{p}$ (from $a^{p} \cdot a^{-p} = a^{0} = 1$) and $a^{0} = 1$ (from $a^{p}/a^{p} = a^{0}$).

Exam Tactics

Exam Strategy and Common Pitfalls

Rational expression arithmetic
  • Factor first, always. Cancelling without factoring is the source of more wrong answers in this unit than every other mistake combined.
  • Never cancel across $+$ or $-$. $\dfrac{x + 3}{x} \ne 4$. The shortcut here looks attractive and is fatal.
  • State restrictions every time. Even when they vanish from the simplified expression, the original was undefined at those values; examiners give credit for stating them and dock for omitting.
Rational and radical equations
  • Check candidates against the original equation. Extraneous roots are not a rare exception; they arise from the standard solving algorithm.
  • Squaring step: the principal square root is non-negative, so any candidate that makes the right-hand side negative is extraneous.
  • Multiplying out a denominator can re-introduce values that were excluded; check candidates against the restriction set.
  • Cross-multiplying $a/b = c/d$ as $a d = b c$ is fine, but the restrictions $b \ne 0$ and $d \ne 0$ are still required.
Rational function graphing
  • Factor before classifying. A common factor is a hole, not a vertical asymptote. The distinction matters and is graded.
  • Horizontal asymptote rule: compare degrees. Lower-on-top gives $y = 0$; equal gives leading-coefficient ratio; higher-on-top gives no HA (slant if degree difference is $1$).
  • End behaviour follows from the HA / slant analysis — not from substituting large values.
Radicals and rational exponents
  • $\sqrt{a^{2}} = |a|$, not $a$. This trips up nearly everyone the first time they see it on a test.
  • Rationalising: single radical → multiply by itself; binomial → multiply by the conjugate. Both rely on the difference-of-squares identity.
  • Switch to rational exponents whenever you need to combine radicals of different indices — far cleaner than computing a common index by hand.
Domain hygiene
  • Rational expression: denominator $\ne 0$.
  • Even-index radical: radicand $\ge 0$.
  • Both at once (e.g. $\sqrt{p(x)} / q(x)$): intersection of both conditions.
  • Cancelled holes remain excluded; cancellation simplifies the expression's formula but does not enlarge its domain.
