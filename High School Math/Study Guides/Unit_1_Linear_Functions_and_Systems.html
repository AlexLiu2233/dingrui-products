Section 1.1 · Slope

Slope and Rate of Change

Slope formula. For two points $(x_{1}, y_{1})$ and $(x_{2}, y_{2})$ on a line, $$ m \;=\; \frac{y_{2} - y_{1}}{x_{2} - x_{1}} \qquad (x_{1} \ne x_{2}). $$ The four cases.
  • Positive slope. Line rises from left to right.
  • Negative slope. Line falls from left to right.
  • Zero slope. Horizontal line $y = b$.
  • Undefined slope. Vertical line $x = a$; the formula divides by zero.
Parallel and perpendicular. Lines with slopes $m_{1}$ and $m_{2}$ are
  • parallel if and only if $m_{1} = m_{2}$ (and they are not the same line),
  • perpendicular if and only if $m_{1} m_{2} = -1$ (and both slopes are defined).
Slope as rate of change. In a modeling context, slope carries units: dollars per hour, metres per second, degrees per minute. Always state the units when interpreting a slope.
Worked Example 1.1 · Slope from two points and a unit interpretation

A rental scooter charges according to the linear model $C = 2.50 + 0.15 t$, where $C$ is the cost in dollars and $t$ is time in minutes. (a) Identify the slope and interpret it with units. (b) Find the slope of the segment between $(t, C) = (10, 4.00)$ and $(30, 7.00)$ and confirm it matches.

Identify. The model is in slope-intercept form: $C = mt + b$ with $m = 0.15$ and $b = 2.50$.

Interpret. The slope $m = 0.15$ has units of dollars per minute. Each additional minute of rental adds $\$0.15$ to the cost. The intercept $b = 2.50$ is the unlock fee (cost at $t = 0$).

Confirm from two points.

$$ m \;=\; \frac{7.00 - 4.00}{30 - 10} \;=\; \frac{3.00}{20} \;=\; 0.15 \text{ dollars per minute}. $$

Evaluate. The numerical slope matches the model coefficient, confirming the linear fit.

Going deeper · Why slope is the same between any two points on a line

Suppose $L$ is a non-vertical line in the plane, and pick any four points $P_{1} = (x_{1}, y_{1})$, $P_{2} = (x_{2}, y_{2})$, $P_{3} = (x_{3}, y_{3})$, $P_{4} = (x_{4}, y_{4})$ on $L$ with $x_{1} \ne x_{2}$ and $x_{3} \ne x_{4}$. Drop perpendiculars to the $x$-axis from $P_{1}, P_{2}$ and from $P_{3}, P_{4}$. The two resulting right triangles share the angle that $L$ makes with the horizontal, so they are similar by AA. Hence corresponding sides are proportional:

$$ \frac{y_{2} - y_{1}}{x_{2} - x_{1}} \;=\; \frac{y_{4} - y_{3}}{x_{4} - x_{3}}. $$

That is, the slope formula returns the same value whichever pair of points we pick. This is why $m$ is well-defined as a property of the line, not of the chosen points. The argument also extends to negative differences and to the four sign cases; vertical lines fail because the limiting triangle has zero base and the ratio is undefined.

Section 1.2 · Three Forms

Forms of a Linear Equation

The three forms.
  • Slope-intercept: $y = m x + b$. Use when you can read or want to report the $y$-intercept.
  • Point-slope: $y - y_{1} = m (x - x_{1})$. Use when you have a point and a slope.
  • Standard (general): $A x + B y = C$ (or $A x + B y + C = 0$). Use when you want integer coefficients, or when the line is vertical ($B = 0$).
Conversions. All three describe the same line; you should be able to move between them in two algebra steps.
  • Slope-intercept to standard: clear the $b$ to the right; collect $x$ and $y$ on the left.
  • Standard to slope-intercept: solve for $y$.
  • Point-slope to slope-intercept: distribute the $m$ and add $y_{1}$.
Vertical and horizontal lines. $x = a$ is vertical (slope undefined). $y = b$ is horizontal (slope zero).
Worked Example 1.2 · Move between all three forms

The line $L$ passes through $(2, -1)$ with slope $-\tfrac{3}{4}$. Write its equation in (a) point-slope form, (b) slope-intercept form, (c) standard form with integer coefficients.

(a) Point-slope. Substitute directly:

$$ y - (-1) \;=\; -\tfrac{3}{4} (x - 2), \qquad \text{i.e.} \qquad y + 1 \;=\; -\tfrac{3}{4}(x - 2). $$

(b) Slope-intercept. Distribute and isolate $y$:

$$ y \;=\; -\tfrac{3}{4} x + \tfrac{3}{2} - 1 \;=\; -\tfrac{3}{4} x + \tfrac{1}{2}. $$

(c) Standard form. Multiply by $4$ to clear fractions, then move $x$ to the left:

$$ 4y \;=\; -3x + 2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 3x + 4y \;=\; 2. $$

Evaluate. Check that the point $(2, -1)$ satisfies the standard form: $3(2) + 4(-1) = 6 - 4 = 2$. Confirmed.

Going deeper · Why standard form $A x + B y = C$ handles vertical lines

Slope-intercept form $y = mx + b$ cannot represent a vertical line because a vertical line has undefined slope. Standard form $A x + B y = C$ avoids the problem by parametrising the line through its direction vector $(B, -A)$ rather than through a slope: any non-zero choice of $(A, B)$ picks out a unique line direction. When $B = 0$ and $A \ne 0$, we recover the vertical line $x = C/A$; when $A = 0$ and $B \ne 0$, the horizontal line $y = C/B$. This unified treatment is why textbooks introduce standard form even though slope-intercept is computationally lighter.

Write $5x - 2y = 8$ in slope-intercept form.
1.2 · Q1
$y = \tfrac{5}{2} x - 4$
$y = 5x - 4$
$y = -\tfrac{5}{2} x + 4$
$y = \tfrac{2}{5} x - \tfrac{8}{5}$
Solve for $y$: $-2y = -5x + 8$, so $y = \tfrac{5}{2} x - 4$.
Subtract $5x$ from both sides, then divide by $-2$. The signs flip twice and the slope is $5/2$, not $5$.

Section 1.3 · Graphing

Graphing Linear Functions

Two reliable techniques.
  • Intercepts method. Find the $y$-intercept by setting $x = 0$ and the $x$-intercept by setting $y = 0$. Plot both and draw the line. Fails on lines through the origin and on lines parallel to an axis.
  • Slope-intercept method. Plot the $y$-intercept $(0, b)$. From there, use the slope as a "rise over run" to step to a second point. For $m = 3/4$, go $4$ right and $3$ up. For $m = -2 = -2/1$, go $1$ right and $2$ down.
Reading $m$ and $b$ from a graph. The $y$-intercept is the $y$-coordinate where the line crosses the vertical axis. The slope is rise-over-run between any two clearly-marked lattice points.

Gradient triangle. Drawing a right-triangle "$\Delta$" beneath any segment of the line, with horizontal leg $\Delta x$ and vertical leg $\Delta y$, makes the slope visible: $m = \Delta y / \Delta x$.
Worked Example 1.3 · Graph from an equation, then read it back

Graph $y = -\tfrac{2}{3} x + 4$, then state two lattice points on the line that confirm the slope.

Identify. Slope $m = -\tfrac{2}{3}$, $y$-intercept $b = 4$.

Set up. Plot $(0, 4)$. From there, slope $-2/3$ means "down $2$ for every $3$ right." Step to $(3, 2)$, then to $(6, 0)$ (the $x$-intercept), then to $(9, -2)$.

Execute. Connect the points with a straight line. Two clean lattice points on the line are $(0, 4)$ and $(6, 0)$.

Evaluate. Confirm the slope from the two lattice points:

$$ m \;=\; \frac{0 - 4}{6 - 0} \;=\; -\tfrac{2}{3}. \quad \checkmark $$
Going deeper · Why the slope-intercept and intercepts methods agree

Suppose a line has slope $m \ne 0$ and $y$-intercept $b \ne 0$. The $y$-intercept is $(0, b)$ by definition. To find the $x$-intercept, set $y = 0$ in $y = mx + b$: $0 = mx + b$, so $x = -b/m$. The slope between $(0, b)$ and $(-b/m, 0)$ is

$$ \frac{0 - b}{-b/m - 0} \;=\; \frac{-b}{-b/m} \;=\; m, $$

which is the same $m$ the slope-intercept form encodes. The two graphing methods produce the same line because the intercepts are derivable from $(m, b)$ and vice versa.

A line crosses the $x$-axis at $(-3, 0)$ and the $y$-axis at $(0, 6)$. Find its slope-intercept equation.
1.3 · Q1
$y = -2x + 6$
$y = 2x + 6$
$y = \tfrac{1}{2} x + 6$
$y = 6x - 3$
$m = (6 - 0)/(0 - (-3)) = 6/3 = 2$ and $b = 6$, so $y = 2x + 6$.
Slope is rise over run between the two intercept points: $(6 - 0)/(0 - (-3)) = 2$. Then read the $y$-intercept directly.
Section 1.4 · Writing equations

Writing Equations from Descriptions

Decision tree.
  1. Two points given. Compute slope, then use point-slope with either point.
  2. Point and slope given. Use point-slope directly.
  3. Parallel to a given line through a point. Read the slope of the given line; use the same slope in point-slope.
  4. Perpendicular to a given line through a point. Read the slope; take its negative reciprocal; use point-slope.
  5. From a table. Use two rows to compute slope; substitute one row into point-slope.
  6. From a word problem. Identify the rate (slope) and a starting value or anchor point (intercept or point). Write in the form that matches what you found.
Convert to standard form only if the question asks for it; the work is the same line either way.
Worked Example 1.4 · Line perpendicular to a given line through a point

Find the equation of the line through $(4, 1)$ perpendicular to $2x + 3y = 12$.

Identify. Solve the given line for $y$: $3y = -2x + 12$, so $y = -\tfrac{2}{3} x + 4$. The given slope is $m_{1} = -\tfrac{2}{3}$.

Set up. The perpendicular slope is the negative reciprocal: $m_{2} = -1/m_{1} = \tfrac{3}{2}$.

Execute. Apply point-slope through $(4, 1)$:

$$ y - 1 \;=\; \tfrac{3}{2}(x - 4) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad y \;=\; \tfrac{3}{2} x - 6 + 1 \;=\; \tfrac{3}{2} x - 5. $$

Evaluate. Verify the perpendicularity: $m_{1} m_{2} = (-\tfrac{2}{3})(\tfrac{3}{2}) = -1$. Confirmed.

Going deeper · Why perpendicular slopes multiply to $-1$

Let $L_{1}$ and $L_{2}$ be two non-vertical lines making angles $\theta_{1}$ and $\theta_{2}$ with the positive $x$-axis, so $m_{i} = \tan \theta_{i}$. The lines are perpendicular if and only if $\theta_{2} = \theta_{1} + \pi/2$. Then

$$ m_{2} \;=\; \tan\!\left(\theta_{1} + \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right) \;=\; -\cot \theta_{1} \;=\; -\frac{1}{\tan \theta_{1}} \;=\; -\frac{1}{m_{1}}, $$

so $m_{1} m_{2} = -1$. The argument breaks if either line is vertical (one slope is undefined), in which case the perpendicular partner is horizontal and the rule is replaced with "horizontal and vertical lines are always perpendicular."

Section 1.5 · Systems

Linear Systems of Two Equations

Three methods, one goal: find every $(x, y)$ that solves both equations.
  • Substitution. Solve one equation for one variable, substitute into the other, solve the resulting one-variable equation, back-substitute. Best when one equation is already solved for a variable.
  • Elimination (addition). Scale one or both equations so that a chosen variable has opposite coefficients, add the equations to eliminate that variable, solve, back-substitute. Best when both equations are in standard form.
  • Graphical. Graph both lines; the solution is the intersection point. Useful for intuition and for the "how many solutions" question.
The three outcomes.
  • One solution. Lines cross once. Slopes differ.
  • No solution. Lines are parallel (same slope, different intercept). Algebra produces a contradiction like $0 = 5$.
  • Infinitely many solutions. Lines are identical. Algebra produces an identity like $0 = 0$.
Syllabus note. In US Common Core, HSA-REI.C.5 requires that you can prove elimination preserves the solution set, not just apply it; HSA-REI.C.6 covers the full method. In Ontario MPM2D the focus is "choosing an appropriate algebraic or graphical method"; the proof is not required. In BC FMPC 10 the elaboration lists graphical, inspection, substitution, elimination as four methods of equal weight, so a BC provincial-style question may ask which method you would choose and why.
Worked Example 1.5 · Solve a 2-by-2 system three ways

Solve the system $\begin{cases} 2x + 3y = 13 \\ x - y = 1 \end{cases}$ by (a) substitution, (b) elimination, and (c) state the geometric meaning.

(a) Substitution. Solve the second equation for $x$: $x = y + 1$. Substitute into the first:

$$ 2(y + 1) + 3y \;=\; 13 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 5y + 2 \;=\; 13 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad y \;=\; \tfrac{11}{5}. $$

Back-substitute into $x = y + 1$ to obtain $x = 11/5 + 1 = 16/5$.

(b) Elimination. Multiply the second equation by $2$: $2x - 2y = 2$. Subtract from the first:

$$ (2x + 3y) - (2x - 2y) \;=\; 13 - 2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 5y \;=\; 11 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad y \;=\; \tfrac{11}{5}. $$

Back-substitute into $x - y = 1$ to obtain $x = 1 + 11/5 = 16/5$. The two methods agree.

(c) Geometric meaning. The two lines have slopes $-2/3$ and $1$, which differ, so the lines intersect at exactly one point. That point is $(16/5, 11/5)$.

Going deeper · Why elimination preserves the solution set

Let the system be $E_{1}: a_{1} x + b_{1} y = c_{1}$ and $E_{2}: a_{2} x + b_{2} y = c_{2}$. Replace $E_{2}$ by $E_{2}' = E_{2} + k E_{1}$ for any constant $k$. We claim $\{E_{1}, E_{2}\}$ and $\{E_{1}, E_{2}'\}$ have identical solution sets.

Forward direction: if $(x, y)$ satisfies $E_{1}$ and $E_{2}$, then it satisfies $k E_{1}$ (since both sides are scaled by $k$) and so it satisfies $E_{2}' = E_{2} + k E_{1}$.

Reverse direction: if $(x, y)$ satisfies $E_{1}$ and $E_{2}'$, then it satisfies $E_{2}' - k E_{1} = E_{2}$.

The replacement is reversible (the same $k$ undoes it with the opposite sign), so the two systems have exactly the same solutions. This justifies HSA-REI.C.5: every elimination step is just a controlled replacement that preserves the solution set.

Section 1.6 · Modeling

Linear Systems: Applications and Modeling

Word-problem playbook.
  1. Name two variables. Write down what each one means, with units.
  2. Translate two sentences into two equations. One usually controls a total ($x + y = $ something); the other controls a rate, a value, or a comparison.
  3. Solve the system. Substitution if one equation is already isolated; elimination otherwise.
  4. Answer in a sentence with units. Numeric answers without context lose marks in modeling rubrics.
Three canonical problem types.
  • Mixture. Combine two amounts at different concentrations to hit a target.
  • Rate. Distance / cost / work accumulates linearly over time at a fixed rate.
  • Break-even. Cost and revenue are both linear; find the production quantity where they meet.
Worked Example 1.6 · Break-even analysis

A small printing business has fixed monthly costs of $\$1{,}200$ and a variable cost of $\$3$ per booklet. It sells booklets at $\$8$ each. How many booklets must it sell per month to break even?

Identify. Let $n$ be the number of booklets sold per month. Define

  • Total cost: $C(n) = 1200 + 3n$ (fixed plus variable).
  • Total revenue: $R(n) = 8n$.

Set up. Break-even occurs where $C(n) = R(n)$:

$$ 1200 + 3n \;=\; 8n. $$

Execute. Subtract $3n$ from both sides:

$$ 1200 \;=\; 5n \quad\Longrightarrow\quad n \;=\; 240. $$

Evaluate. The business breaks even at $240$ booklets per month. Below that the loss is $5n - 1200$ short of cost; above that the profit grows at $\$5$ per booklet.

Going deeper · A mixture problem solved as a system

A chemist needs $10$ litres of a $40\%$ acid solution. She has $20\%$ acid and $70\%$ acid stocks on hand. How many litres of each should she combine?

Let $x$ be litres of the $20\%$ stock and $y$ litres of the $70\%$ stock. The two equations:

$$ x + y \;=\; 10 \quad\text{(total volume)}, \qquad 0.20 x + 0.70 y \;=\; 0.40 \cdot 10 \;=\; 4 \quad\text{(total acid)}. $$

From the first equation, $x = 10 - y$. Substitute:

$$ 0.20 (10 - y) + 0.70 y \;=\; 4 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 2 + 0.50 y \;=\; 4 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad y \;=\; 4. $$

Then $x = 6$. The chemist mixes $6$ litres of $20\%$ stock with $4$ litres of $70\%$ stock. A quick sanity check: $0.20 \cdot 6 + 0.70 \cdot 4 = 1.2 + 2.8 = 4.0$ litres of pure acid, which is $40\%$ of $10$ litres.

Matrix Row-Reduction Honors 🇨🇦 BC PC12 / ON MCV4U 🇺🇸 CCSSM (+)

Syllabus note. US Common Core lists matrix methods as the STEM-track extension HSA-REI.C.8 (+) and HSA-REI.C.9 (+), so they appear in honors and AP-feeder classrooms but not in the regular CCSSM Algebra 2 syllabus. In BC, matrix row-reduction is standard in Pre-Calculus 12. In Ontario, it sits in MCV4U (Calculus and Vectors). Skip this section if you are a regular-track student aiming for a state test or a provincial exam at the standard level.
The augmented matrix. Write the system $\begin{cases} a_{1} x + b_{1} y = c_{1} \\ a_{2} x + b_{2} y = c_{2} \end{cases}$ as $$ \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} a_{1} & b_{1} & c_{1} \\ a_{2} & b_{2} & c_{2} \end{array}\right]. $$ Three elementary row operations.
  1. Swap two rows.
  2. Multiply a row by a non-zero constant.
  3. Add a multiple of one row to another.
Each operation preserves the solution set (it is the matrix version of the elimination argument in 1.5).

Goal: row-echelon form. Use the operations until the matrix has $1$s on the main diagonal (where possible) and $0$s below them. Back-substitute to read off $(x, y)$.
Worked Example 1.7 · Solve a 3-by-3 system by row-reduction

Solve the system $\begin{cases} x + y + z = 6 \\ 2x - y + z = 3 \\ x + 2y - z = 2 \end{cases}$ by row-reducing the augmented matrix.

Set up the augmented matrix.

$$ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 & 2 \end{array}\right]. $$

Clear column 1 below the pivot. Apply $R_{2} \to R_{2} - 2 R_{1}$ and $R_{3} \to R_{3} - R_{1}$:

$$ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \end{array}\right]. $$

Swap $R_{2}$ and $R_{3}$ for a friendlier pivot:

$$ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \end{array}\right]. $$

Clear column 2 below the new pivot. $R_{3} \to R_{3} + 3 R_{2}$:

$$ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \\ 0 & 0 & -7 & -21 \end{array}\right]. $$

Solve by back-substitution. From row 3: $-7 z = -21$, so $z = 3$. From row 2: $y - 2(3) = -4$, so $y = 2$. From row 1: $x + 2 + 3 = 6$, so $x = 1$.

Evaluate. The unique solution is $(x, y, z) = (1, 2, 3)$. Check: $1 + 2 + 3 = 6$, $2 - 2 + 3 = 3$, $1 + 4 - 3 = 2$. Confirmed.

Going deeper · What row-echelon form tells you about solution count

After row-reduction, an augmented matrix falls into one of three shapes that mirror the three outcomes of 1.5:

  • A pivot in every variable column. The system has exactly one solution.
  • A row of the form $[0, 0, \ldots, 0 \,|\, c]$ with $c \ne 0$. The system has no solution; this row reads $0 = c$.
  • A free variable (no pivot in some variable column) and no contradiction row. The system has infinitely many solutions parametrised by the free variable.

For $2 \times 2$ systems this matches "one intersection / parallel lines / coincident lines"; for $3 \times 3$ systems it matches "single point / parallel planes / line or plane of solutions." The same trichotomy generalises to every dimension.

Exam Tactics

Exam Strategy and Common Pitfalls

Slope and units
  • Always state units in a modeling problem. "The slope is $-12$" loses a mark in a rubric that expects "$-12$ litres per hour."
  • Check sign before computing. If the dependent quantity is decreasing, the slope is negative. A positive answer to a "loss" question is almost always wrong.
Forms of a line
  • Convert to slope-intercept before reading the slope. $5x - 2y = 8$ does not have slope $5$.
  • Standard form is for vertical lines and integer-coefficient answers. Use it deliberately, not as a default.
  • Perpendicular slope flips sign and reciprocal both. The negative-reciprocal rule is one of the two most-tested facts in this unit (the other is the slope formula).
Systems
  • Pick the method that minimises arithmetic. Substitution is usually faster when one equation is already in slope-intercept form. Elimination is usually faster when both equations are in standard form.
  • Read "no solution" and "infinitely many" off the algebra. $0 = 5$ means no solution; $0 = 0$ means infinitely many. Do not invent answers to "consistent" or "dependent" systems.
  • Write down units in modeling answers. "Sells $240$ booklets" beats "$240$" alone.
Honors row-reduction
  • Record each elementary row operation. Examiners give partial credit for the work even when arithmetic slips. Write "$R_{2} \to R_{2} - 2 R_{1}$" beside the step.
  • Back-substitute carefully. Once you reach row-echelon form, work from the bottom row up. Errors in the back-substitution stage are the most common loss of marks in this technique.
