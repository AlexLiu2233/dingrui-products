Unit 5: Exponential and
Logarithmic Functions

Exponentials describe what grows by equal factors over equal intervals — populations, compound interest, radioactive decay, the cooling of a cup of coffee. Their inverses, logarithms, turn multiplication into addition and let you actually solve equations where the unknown is in the exponent. This unit covers the exponential parent function and its horizontal asymptote, growth vs decay, the laws of exponents lifted to laws of logarithms (product, quotient, power, change-of-base), the natural logarithm and base $e$, continuous compounding, half-life and population modelling, and the inverse-function relationship $y = b^{x} \iff x = \log_{b} y$. The unit feeds into IB Math AA HL A2 (Exponents and Logarithms), AP Calculus Unit 2 (derivative of $e^{x}$ and $\ln x$), and IB Math HL E2 (techniques of differential calculus, including the chain rule on exponentials and logarithms).

Section 5.1 · Exponential functions

Exponential Functions: Parent, Asymptote, Growth vs Decay

The exponential parent $f(x) = b^{x}$ with $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$.
  • Domain: all real $x$. Range: $(0, \infty)$ — always positive.
  • Horizontal asymptote: $y = 0$. Approached but never crossed.
  • $y$-intercept: $(0, 1)$ since $b^{0} = 1$. No $x$-intercepts.
  • Growth vs decay: $b > 1$ is increasing (growth); $0 < b < 1$ is decreasing (decay).
General form. $f(x) = a \cdot b^{x}$ scales vertically by $a$ ($y$-intercept becomes $(0, a)$; asymptote stays $y = 0$).

CCSSM characterisation. Exponentials grow by equal factors over equal intervals; linears by equal differences (HSF-LE.A.1a).
Worked Example 5.1 · Read features off $f(x) = 3 \cdot 2^{x}$

For the exponential $f(x) = 3 \cdot 2^{x}$, find (a) the $y$-intercept, (b) the horizontal asymptote, (c) the value of $f(4)$, and (d) the growth-vs-decay classification.

Identify. Base $b = 2$, scale factor $a = 3$.

(a) $y$-intercept. $f(0) = 3 \cdot 2^{0} = 3 \cdot 1 = 3$, so the intercept is $(0, 3)$.

(b) Horizontal asymptote. As $x \to -\infty$, $2^{x} \to 0$, so $f(x) \to 0$. The asymptote is $y = 0$.

(c) Evaluate.

$$ f(4) \;=\; 3 \cdot 2^{4} \;=\; 3 \cdot 16 \;=\; 48. $$

(d) Growth or decay. Base $b = 2 > 1$, so $f$ is increasing: exponential growth.

Evaluate. Sanity-check the equal-factors property: $f(0) = 3$, $f(1) = 6$, $f(2) = 12$, $f(3) = 24$ — each value doubles. That doubling is the equal-factor signature of an exponential with base $2$.

Section 5.2 · Exponential equations

Exponential Equations 🇨🇦 BC PC12 · 🇨🇦 ON MHF4U · 🇺🇸 US Alg 2

Two solving techniques.
  • Same-base: rewrite both sides with the same base, then equate exponents: $b^{u} = b^{v} \iff u = v$ (for $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$).
  • Different-base: take $\log$ of both sides (any base; usually $\ln$) and apply the power law $\log(b^{u}) = u \log b$ to drop the exponent into a linear equation.
Laws of exponents (review). $b^{m} \cdot b^{n} = b^{m + n}$, $\;b^{m} / b^{n} = b^{m - n}$, $\;(b^{m})^{n} = b^{m n}$, $\;b^{-n} = 1/b^{n}$, $\;b^{0} = 1$. Each law has a logarithmic mirror (§5.4).
Syllabus note. BC PC12 lists "solving equations with same base and with different bases, including base $e$" verbatim under exponential functions and equations. Ontario MHF4U covers the same content in its Exponential and Logarithmic Functions strand. US CCSSM treats different-base solving in HSF-LE.A.4: "for exponential models, express as a logarithm the solution to $a b^{c t} = d$…" — the linear-focused Unit 1 extract notes this code as out of scope for Unit 1 (in scope here).
Worked Example 5.2a · Same-base technique

Solve $4^{x + 1} = 32$ exactly, without a calculator.

Rewrite to a common base. Both $4$ and $32$ are powers of $2$: $4 = 2^{2}$ and $32 = 2^{5}$.

$$ (2^{2})^{x + 1} \;=\; 2^{5} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 2^{2 (x + 1)} \;=\; 2^{5}. $$

Set exponents equal.

$$ 2 (x + 1) \;=\; 5 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x \;=\; \tfrac{3}{2}. $$

Evaluate. Check: $4^{1 + 3/2} = 4^{5/2} = (4^{1/2})^{5} = 2^{5} = 32$. Confirmed.

Worked Example 5.2b · Different-base technique

Solve $5 \cdot 3^{x} = 80$ to three decimal places.

Isolate the exponential.

$$ 3^{x} \;=\; 16. $$

Take the natural log of both sides.

$$ \ln(3^{x}) \;=\; \ln 16 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x \ln 3 \;=\; \ln 16. $$

Solve for $x$.

$$ x \;=\; \frac{\ln 16}{\ln 3} \;\approx\; \frac{2.7726}{1.0986} \;\approx\; 2.524. $$

Evaluate. Sanity check: $3^{2} = 9$ and $3^{3} = 27$, so $x$ between $2$ and $3$ is plausible. We can also write $x = \log_{3} 16$ — the same answer in base-$3$ logarithm notation.

Section 5.3 · Logarithms

Logarithms as the Inverse of Exponentials

Definition. For $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$, the logarithm $\log_{b} x$ is the inverse of $b^{x}$: $$ y \;=\; \log_{b} x \;\iff\; x \;=\; b^{y} \qquad (x > 0). $$ In English: "$\log_{b} x$ is the exponent on $b$ that produces $x$."

Two named logarithms. Common log $\log x = \log_{10} x$ (calculators, pH); natural log $\ln x = \log_{e} x$ (calculus, continuous compounding — §5.6).

Graph of $y = \log_{b} x$ (with $b > 1$). Domain $(0, \infty)$, range all real $y$; vertical asymptote $x = 0$; $x$-intercept $(1, 0)$ since $\log_{b} 1 = 0$; slowly increasing, concave down. The graph is the reflection of $b^{x}$ across $y = x$.
Syllabus note. BC PC12 lists "exploring inverse of exponential" verbatim as a Content elaboration under logarithms, with sample inquiry questions like "How are exponential and logarithmic functions related?" and "How are the laws of exponents connected to the laws of logarithms?" The CCSSM Unit 1 extract notes HSF-BF.B.4 as the inverse-functions standard and flags it as relevant when a unit covers inverses; the exp/log pair is the canonical Algebra 2 / Pre-Calc instance.
Worked Example 5.3 · Translate between exponential and logarithmic form

Rewrite each equation in the other form: (a) $2^{5} = 32$; (b) $\log_{3} 81 = 4$; (c) $e^{0} = 1$.

(a) Exponential to log. $2^{5} = 32$ says "$5$ is the exponent that takes $2$ to $32$." So $\log_{2} 32 = 5$.

(b) Log to exponential. $\log_{3} 81 = 4$ says "the exponent on $3$ that gives $81$ is $4$." So $3^{4} = 81$.

(c) Exponential to log. $e^{0} = 1$, so $\ln 1 = 0$. More generally, $\log_{b} 1 = 0$ for any base, since $b^{0} = 1$.

Evaluate. The translation has a fixed shape: "base on the same side, exponent and result swap." Memorise the pattern as a single move rather than three rules.

Section 5.4 · Laws of logarithms

Laws of Logarithms 🇨🇦 BC PC12 · 🇨🇦 ON MHF4U

The four core laws (for $x, y > 0$, $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$, $p \in \mathbb{R}$). $$ \log_{b}(x y) = \log_{b} x + \log_{b} y, \qquad \log_{b}(x / y) = \log_{b} x - \log_{b} y, $$ $$ \log_{b}(x^{p}) = p \log_{b} x, \qquad \log_{b} x = \frac{\log_{a} x}{\log_{a} b} \;\;\text{(change-of-base)}. $$ Special values. $\log_{b} 1 = 0$, $\;\log_{b} b = 1$, $\;\log_{b}(b^{p}) = p$, $\;b^{\log_{b} x} = x$.

What the laws are doing. They turn multiplication into addition, division into subtraction, and exponents into multipliers — exactly the laws of exponents read through the inverse pair $b^{x} \leftrightarrow \log_{b} x$. BC PC12 names this connection in its inquiry question "How are the laws of exponents connected to the laws of logarithms?"
Worked Example 5.4 · Expand and contract

(a) Expand $\log_{2}\!\left( \dfrac{x^{3} \sqrt{y}}{z^{5}} \right)$ into a sum and difference of simpler logs. (b) Contract $3 \ln a + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln b - 4 \ln c$ into a single logarithm.

(a) Expand. Apply the quotient law first, then product on the numerator, then power on each piece:

$$ \log_{2}\!\left( \frac{x^{3} \sqrt{y}}{z^{5}} \right) = \log_{2}(x^{3} \sqrt{y}) - \log_{2}(z^{5}) = \log_{2}(x^{3}) + \log_{2}(\sqrt{y}) - \log_{2}(z^{5}) $$ $$ = 3 \log_{2} x + \tfrac{1}{2} \log_{2} y - 5 \log_{2} z. $$

(b) Contract. Pull each coefficient inside as an exponent, then combine via product / quotient:

$$ 3 \ln a + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln b - 4 \ln c \;=\; \ln(a^{3}) + \ln(b^{1/2}) - \ln(c^{4}) \;=\; \ln\!\left( \frac{a^{3} \sqrt{b}}{c^{4}} \right). $$

Evaluate. Expansion and contraction are perfect inverses: a clean expansion should re-contract back to the original expression. Use that as a built-in sanity check.

Section 5.5 · Solving log and exp equations

Logarithmic Equations and Solving Exp Equations with Logs

Three common shapes.
  • $\log_{b} u = c$: rewrite as $u = b^{c}$ and solve.
  • $\log_{b} u = \log_{b} v$: log is one-to-one, so $u = v$. Then check arguments are positive.
  • $a \cdot b^{u} = c$: isolate, take $\log$ of both sides, bring $u$ down via the power law.
Extraneous solutions. Logarithms require positive arguments. Discard any candidate that makes any log argument $\le 0$. For exponential or log inequalities, sign analysis (Unit 2 §2.7) returns: find boundary values, test signs in each interval.
Worked Example 5.5a · A log equation with two log terms

Solve $\log_{2}(x + 3) + \log_{2}(x - 1) = 5$.

Combine the logs. Product law:

$$ \log_{2}\!\big( (x + 3)(x - 1) \big) \;=\; 5. $$

Convert to exponential form.

$$ (x + 3)(x - 1) \;=\; 2^{5} \;=\; 32. $$

Expand and solve.

$$ x^{2} + 2 x - 3 \;=\; 32 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x^{2} + 2 x - 35 \;=\; 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad (x + 7)(x - 5) \;=\; 0. $$

Candidates: $x = -7$ or $x = 5$.

Check arguments. $x = -7$ makes $\log_{2}(x + 3) = \log_{2}(-4)$ undefined — extraneous. $x = 5$ gives $\log_{2} 8 + \log_{2} 4 = 3 + 2 = 5$ — valid.

Evaluate. Only $x = 5$ is a solution.

Worked Example 5.5b · A different-base exponential equation

Solve $2 \cdot 3^{2 x - 1} = 54$ exactly.

Isolate the exponential.

$$ 3^{2 x - 1} \;=\; 27. $$

Recognise same base. $27 = 3^{3}$, so:

$$ 3^{2 x - 1} \;=\; 3^{3} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 2 x - 1 \;=\; 3 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x \;=\; 2. $$

Evaluate. Check: $2 \cdot 3^{3} = 2 \cdot 27 = 54$. Confirmed. (If the right-hand side had not been a clean power of $3$, we would have taken $\log$ of both sides instead.)

Section 5.6 · Base $e$ and the natural log

Base $e$, the Natural Logarithm, and Continuous Compounding Honors (US Alg 2) 🇨🇦 BC PC12 core · 🇨🇦 ON MHF4U core

Syllabus note. BC PC12 lists "including base $e$" verbatim under exponential equations and "using common and natural logarithms" verbatim under logarithms. Ontario MHF4U covers base $e$ and the natural logarithm in its Exponential and Logarithmic Functions strand. US Common Core typically introduces $e$ in Pre-Calculus or honors Algebra 2; on a standard Algebra 2 track this section is the honors block.
The number $e$. $e \approx 2.71828$. It is the unique base for which $\frac{d}{dx} e^{x} = e^{x}$ (a calculus fact; for now, treat $e$ as a special irrational like $\pi$). The natural log is $\ln x = \log_{e} x$, with inverse identities $e^{\ln x} = x$ ($x > 0$) and $\ln(e^{x}) = x$.

Compound interest. $A(t) = P (1 + r/n)^{n t}$ for discrete compounding $n$ times per year. Letting $n \to \infty$: $$ A(t) \;=\; P e^{r t} \qquad (\text{continuous compounding}). $$ The bridge $\lim_{n \to \infty} (1 + r/n)^{n t} = e^{r t}$ follows from the limit definition $e = \lim_{m \to \infty} (1 + 1/m)^{m}$.
Worked Example 5.6a · Continuous vs annual compounding

You invest $\$1000$ at a nominal annual rate of $5\%$. Compare the account value after $10$ years under (a) annual compounding and (b) continuous compounding.

(a) Annual compounding ($n = 1$).

$$ A \;=\; 1000 \left( 1 + 0.05 \right)^{10} \;=\; 1000 \cdot (1.05)^{10} \;\approx\; 1000 \cdot 1.6289 \;\approx\; \$1628.89. $$

(b) Continuous compounding.

$$ A \;=\; 1000 \cdot e^{0.05 \cdot 10} \;=\; 1000 \cdot e^{0.5} \;\approx\; 1000 \cdot 1.6487 \;\approx\; \$1648.72. $$

Evaluate. The continuous-compounding value is higher by about $\$19.83$ (about $1.2\%$). At higher rates and longer times the gap widens; at $r = 0.05$ and $t = 10$ years the two formulas are very close because the rate is small. Continuous compounding is an upper bound on what any compounding frequency can deliver at a given nominal rate.

Worked Example 5.6b · Solving for time with $\ln$

How long does it take an investment to double under continuous compounding at $r = 6\%$?

Set up. Doubling means $A = 2 P$:

$$ 2 P \;=\; P e^{0.06 t} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 2 \;=\; e^{0.06 t}. $$

Take $\ln$ of both sides.

$$ \ln 2 \;=\; 0.06 t \quad\Longrightarrow\quad t \;=\; \frac{\ln 2}{0.06} \;\approx\; \frac{0.6931}{0.06} \;\approx\; 11.55 \text{ years}. $$

Evaluate. The "rule of 72" approximation says doubling time $\approx 72 / (100 r) = 72 / 6 = 12$ years — close to the exact $11.55$. The rule of 72 is itself a Taylor-series approximation of the exact $\ln 2 / r$ formula, and works best for rates in the $4$ – $10$ percent range.

Section 5.7 · Modelling

Exponential Modelling: Population, Decay, Half-Life, Finance

Three canonical models. Percentage: $N(t) = N_{0} (1 + r)^{t}$ ($r < 0$ is decay; compound interest is the canonical instance). Continuous: $N(t) = N_{0} e^{k t}$, related to percentage via $1 + r = e^{k}$. Half-life: $N(t) = N_{0} (1/2)^{t / T}$ where $T$ is the half-life.

Exp-beats-polynomial. CCSSM HSF-LE.A.3: an exponential eventually exceeds any polynomial.

Workflow. Name variables with units → write the model → solve (take $\log$ if the unknown is in the exponent) → answer in a sentence with units.
Worked Example 5.7a · Population growth

A bacterial culture starts at $500$ cells and doubles every $3$ hours. (a) Write a model $N(t)$ with $t$ in hours. (b) How many cells after $24$ hours? (c) When does the culture first exceed $100000$ cells?

(a) Model. Doubling every $3$ hours means a base of $2$ on a time-scale $t/3$:

$$ N(t) \;=\; 500 \cdot 2^{t / 3}. $$

(b) After $24$ hours.

$$ N(24) \;=\; 500 \cdot 2^{24/3} \;=\; 500 \cdot 2^{8} \;=\; 500 \cdot 256 \;=\; 128000 \text{ cells}. $$

(c) Time to exceed $100000$. Solve $500 \cdot 2^{t/3} = 100000$:

$$ 2^{t/3} \;=\; 200 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad (t/3) \ln 2 \;=\; \ln 200 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad t \;=\; \frac{3 \ln 200}{\ln 2} \;\approx\; \frac{3 \cdot 5.298}{0.693} \;\approx\; 22.93 \text{ hours}. $$

Evaluate. So the culture first exceeds $100000$ cells just before the $23$-hour mark, consistent with the $24$-hour value of $128000$ cells from part (b).

Worked Example 5.7b · Radioactive decay and half-life

Carbon-14 has a half-life of $T = 5730$ years. A fossil contains $20\%$ of its original carbon-14. How old is it?

Model.

$$ N(t) \;=\; N_{0} \cdot \left( \tfrac{1}{2} \right)^{t / 5730}. $$

Set up. $N(t) / N_{0} = 0.20$, so:

$$ \left( \tfrac{1}{2} \right)^{t / 5730} \;=\; 0.20. $$

Take $\ln$ of both sides.

$$ \frac{t}{5730} \cdot \ln \tfrac{1}{2} \;=\; \ln 0.20 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad t \;=\; 5730 \cdot \frac{\ln 0.20}{\ln (1/2)}. $$

Evaluate. $\ln 0.20 \approx -1.6094$ and $\ln(1/2) \approx -0.6931$, so $t \approx 5730 \cdot 2.3219 \approx 13305$ years. The fossil is about $13.3$ thousand years old, somewhat older than two half-lives (which would be $25\%$ remaining at $11460$ years) but less than three (which would be $12.5\%$ at $17190$ years).

Exam Tactics

Exam Strategy and Common Pitfalls

Exponentials
  • Always read the base first. $b > 1$ is growth; $0 < b < 1$ is decay. Mistaking $(1/2)^{x}$ for growth costs the question.
  • The asymptote is $y = 0$ (horizontal), never $x = 0$. A common slip is to confuse the exponential's asymptote with the logarithm's asymptote (which is $x = 0$).
  • $y$-intercept of $a b^{x}$ is $(0, a)$. Read the leading coefficient directly.
Solving exp equations
  • Try same base first. If both sides can be rewritten as powers of $2$, $3$, $5$, or $10$, equate exponents and you're done in two lines.
  • Different bases? Take $\log$. Use $\ln$ if the problem mentions $e$ anywhere, $\log_{10}$ otherwise. Either works.
  • Isolate the exponential before taking the log. "Take log of both sides of $5 \cdot 3^{x} = 80$" produces $\ln 5 + x \ln 3 = \ln 80$ — correct, but messier than isolating $3^{x} = 16$ first.
Logarithms
  • $\log_{b} 1 = 0$ and $\log_{b} b = 1$. These two values are constantly tested and constantly forgotten.
  • The argument of a log must be positive. When solving log equations, always check that every log argument is $> 0$ at the candidate solution. Extraneous solutions are the most common error in this unit.
  • The power law brings exponents down. $\log_{b}(x^{p}) = p \log_{b} x$ is the workhorse identity; it converts an exponential equation into a linear one.
Base $e$ and modelling
  • Continuous compounding always uses $e^{r t}$. Discrete compounding uses $(1 + r/n)^{n t}$. Mixing the two forms is a common slip.
  • Rule of 72. Doubling time under continuous compounding at rate $r$ is approximately $72 / (100 r)$. Exact form: $\ln 2 / r$. Use the rule of 72 to spot-check a calculator answer.
  • Half-life: write as $(1/2)^{t/T}$. The model is the same shape as doubling time; the base is $1/2$ instead of $2$.
  • Sign analysis (Unit 2 §2.7) returns when you solve exponential inequalities like $a b^{x} \le c$. Find the boundary value with the equation, then test signs in each interval.
