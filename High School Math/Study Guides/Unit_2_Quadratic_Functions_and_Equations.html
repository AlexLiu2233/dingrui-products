Section 1 · The parabola

The Parabola and Its Key Features

Anatomy of $y = a x^{2} + b x + c$.
  • Opens up if $a > 0$; opens down if $a < 0$. The sign of $a$ controls whether the vertex is a minimum or maximum.
  • Axis of symmetry: the vertical line $x = -b/(2 a)$. The parabola is symmetric about this line.
  • Vertex: the point on the parabola where the axis of symmetry meets it. Its $x$-coordinate is $-b/(2 a)$; its $y$-coordinate is found by substituting back into the equation.
  • $y$-intercept: always $(0, c)$ , just plug in $x = 0$.
  • $x$-intercepts (roots): where $y = 0$. There are $0$, $1$, or $2$ of them depending on the discriminant.
Maximum vs. minimum. If $a > 0$, the vertex is the minimum point of the function (the parabola opens up). If $a < 0$, the vertex is the maximum point. There is no other extremum: a parabola has exactly one turning point.
Worked Example 1 · Key features from standard form

For the quadratic $f(x) = -2 x^{2} + 8 x - 3$, find (a) the axis of symmetry, (b) the vertex, (c) the $y$-intercept, and (d) the direction of opening.

Identify. Standard form $a = -2$, $b = 8$, $c = -3$.

(a) Axis of symmetry.

$$ x \;=\; -\frac{b}{2 a} \;=\; -\frac{8}{2 \cdot (-2)} \;=\; 2. $$

(b) Vertex. Substitute $x = 2$ into $f$:

$$ f(2) \;=\; -2 \cdot 4 + 8 \cdot 2 - 3 \;=\; -8 + 16 - 3 \;=\; 5. $$

So the vertex is $(2, 5)$.

(c) $y$-intercept. Set $x = 0$: $f(0) = -3$, so the $y$-intercept is $(0, -3)$.

(d) Direction. Because $a = -2 < 0$, the parabola opens downward and the vertex $(2, 5)$ is the maximum point.

Evaluate. The maximum value of $f$ is $5$, achieved at $x = 2$. The parabola crosses the $y$-axis at $(0, -3)$ on its way up to the vertex.

Going deeper · Why the axis of symmetry is $x = -b / (2 a)$

Start from $f(x) = a x^{2} + b x + c$ and apply the algebraic identity for completing the square:

$$ f(x) \;=\; a \left( x^{2} + \tfrac{b}{a} x \right) + c \;=\; a \left( x + \tfrac{b}{2 a} \right)^{2} - a \cdot \tfrac{b^{2}}{4 a^{2}} + c. $$

The first term $a (x + b/(2 a))^{2}$ is non-negative when $a > 0$ and equals zero only at $x = -b/(2 a)$. The remaining constant $c - b^{2}/(4 a)$ is the value of $f$ at that point. Symmetry of the squared term about $x = -b/(2 a)$ is what makes that vertical line the axis of symmetry of the whole parabola: $f(-b/(2 a) + h) = f(-b/(2 a) - h)$ for every $h$. The argument runs in reverse for $a < 0$ with "non-negative" replaced by "non-positive," and the same line is the axis of symmetry.

Section 2 · Three Forms

Three Forms of a Quadratic Function

The three forms.
  • Standard form: $y = a x^{2} + b x + c$. Best for reading the $y$-intercept ($c$) and computing the axis ($x = -b/(2 a)$).
  • Vertex form: $y = a (x - h)^{2} + k$. The vertex is $(h, k)$ , read directly. Best for transformations and for stating the max/min value.
  • Factored form: $y = a (x - r_{1}) (x - r_{2})$. The roots are $r_{1}$ and $r_{2}$ , read directly. Best for graphing from roots and for solving the equation.
Conversions. All three describe the same parabola; learn the moves between them cold.
  • Standard to vertex: complete the square (see §5) or use $h = -b/(2 a)$, $k = f(h)$.
  • Standard to factored: factor the quadratic (§3) or use the roots from the formula (§4).
  • Vertex to standard: expand $a (x - h)^{2} + k$.
  • Factored to standard: expand $a (x - r_{1})(x - r_{2})$. The constant term is $a r_{1} r_{2}$; the middle coefficient is $-a (r_{1} + r_{2})$.
When is the factored form unavailable? When the parabola has no real roots ($\Delta < 0$) you cannot factor over the real numbers. Vertex and standard forms always exist.
Worked Example 2 · Move between all three forms

Take $f(x) = 2 x^{2} - 12 x + 10$ and write it in (a) vertex form and (b) factored form.

(a) Vertex form. First find the vertex via $h = -b/(2 a) = 12/4 = 3$. Then

$$ k \;=\; f(3) \;=\; 2 \cdot 9 - 12 \cdot 3 + 10 \;=\; 18 - 36 + 10 \;=\; -8. $$

So $f(x) = 2 (x - 3)^{2} - 8$.

Sanity check. Expand: $2 (x - 3)^{2} - 8 = 2 (x^{2} - 6 x + 9) - 8 = 2 x^{2} - 12 x + 10$. Matches.

(b) Factored form. Factor a $2$ out: $f(x) = 2 (x^{2} - 6 x + 5)$. Then $x^{2} - 6 x + 5 = (x - 1)(x - 5)$, so

$$ f(x) \;=\; 2 (x - 1)(x - 5). $$

Evaluate. The roots are $x = 1$ and $x = 5$; the vertex is $(3, -8)$; the $y$-intercept is $(0, 10)$. The three forms encode the same parabola, each surfacing a different feature.

Going deeper · Why expanding factored form gives the right coefficients

Expand $a (x - r_{1})(x - r_{2})$ step by step:

$$ a (x - r_{1})(x - r_{2}) \;=\; a \left( x^{2} - (r_{1} + r_{2}) x + r_{1} r_{2} \right) \;=\; a x^{2} - a (r_{1} + r_{2}) x + a r_{1} r_{2}. $$

Compare with standard form $a x^{2} + b x + c$. Matching coefficients gives

$$ b \;=\; -a (r_{1} + r_{2}), \qquad c \;=\; a r_{1} r_{2}. $$

These are Vieta's formulas for a quadratic. They are a fast sanity check when factoring: if you propose roots $r_{1}, r_{2}$, the sum should equal $-b/a$ and the product should equal $c/a$. If either fails, the proposed factoring is wrong.

Section 3 · Factoring

Solving by Factoring

The zero-product property. If $A \cdot B = 0$, then $A = 0$ or $B = 0$ (or both). This is the only reason factoring solves equations.

Factoring playbook for $a x^{2} + b x + c = 0$.
  1. Factor out the GCF first if any coefficient shares a factor with all others.
  2. Trinomial with $a = 1$: find two numbers whose product is $c$ and whose sum is $b$. They become the roots' negatives: $x^{2} + b x + c = (x + p)(x + q)$ where $p + q = b$ and $p q = c$.
  3. Trinomial with $a \ne 1$: the AC method / decomposition. Multiply $a \cdot c$. Find two numbers that multiply to $a c$ and add to $b$. Split $b x$ into those two terms, then factor by grouping.
  4. Difference of squares: $a^{2} - b^{2} = (a - b)(a + b)$.
  5. Perfect-square trinomial: $a^{2} \pm 2 a b + b^{2} = (a \pm b)^{2}$.
Solve. Set each factor to zero and read the roots. If the quadratic does not factor over the integers, switch to the quadratic formula (§4) or completing the square (§5).
Worked Example 3 · Trinomial with $a \ne 1$ via the AC method

Solve $6 x^{2} - 7 x - 3 = 0$ by factoring.

Identify. $a = 6$, $b = -7$, $c = -3$. Compute $a c = 6 \cdot (-3) = -18$.

Set up. Look for two integers whose product is $-18$ and whose sum is $-7$: the pair $(-9, 2)$ works because $-9 \cdot 2 = -18$ and $-9 + 2 = -7$.

Execute. Split the middle term:

$$ 6 x^{2} - 7 x - 3 \;=\; 6 x^{2} - 9 x + 2 x - 3. $$

Factor by grouping:

$$ 6 x^{2} - 9 x + 2 x - 3 \;=\; 3 x (2 x - 3) + 1 \cdot (2 x - 3) \;=\; (3 x + 1)(2 x - 3). $$

Apply the zero-product property:

$$ 3 x + 1 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; x = -\tfrac{1}{3}, \qquad 2 x - 3 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \tfrac{3}{2}. $$

Evaluate. Check by Vieta's: sum should be $-b/a = 7/6$; sum of $-1/3 + 3/2 = -2/6 + 9/6 = 7/6$. Confirmed.

Going deeper · Why the AC method works

Assume $a x^{2} + b x + c$ factors over the integers as $(p x + r)(q x + s)$ with $p q = a$, $r s = c$, and $p s + q r = b$. Multiply out:

$$ (p x + r)(q x + s) \;=\; p q x^{2} + (p s + q r) x + r s. $$

Set $u = p s$ and $v = q r$. Then $u + v = b$ and

$$ u v \;=\; (p s)(q r) \;=\; (p q)(r s) \;=\; a c. $$

So we just need to find two integers $u, v$ whose product is $a c$ and whose sum is $b$ , that is the AC method's search step. Once you have $u, v$, splitting $b x = u x + v x$ and grouping recovers the factorisation. If no such integer pair exists, the quadratic does not factor over the integers and you should switch to the quadratic formula or completing the square.

Section 4 · Formula and discriminant

The Quadratic Formula and the Discriminant 🇨🇦 ON · 🇨🇦 BC PC11 · 🇺🇸 US Alg 2+

The quadratic formula. For $a x^{2} + b x + c = 0$ with $a \ne 0$, $$ x \;=\; \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^{2} - 4 a c}}{2 a}. $$ Works on every quadratic equation, factorable or not.

The discriminant. Define $$ \Delta \;=\; b^{2} - 4 a c. $$ The sign of $\Delta$ classifies the roots:
  • $\Delta > 0$: two distinct real roots; the parabola crosses the $x$-axis twice.
  • $\Delta = 0$: one repeated real root ($x = -b/(2 a)$); the parabola is tangent to the $x$-axis at the vertex.
  • $\Delta < 0$: no real roots; two complex conjugate roots; the parabola does not cross the $x$-axis.
Syllabus note. The $\Delta < 0$ case requires complex-number arithmetic. US Common Core treats this through HSN-CN (Number and Quantity , Complex Numbers) in Algebra 2; in Ontario MCR3U it shows up as "no real solutions" without complex follow-up; in BC Pre-Calc 11 the quadratic-equation Content elaboration covers all four solution paths (factoring, quadratic formula, completing the square, graphing, square root method) but stops at the real-solution count. Treat $\Delta < 0$ as a "no real roots, sketch shows no $x$-intercepts" outcome unless your course explicitly covers complex roots.
Worked Example 4 · The formula and the discriminant in one pass

Solve $2 x^{2} + 3 x - 5 = 0$ using the quadratic formula, and classify the roots by computing $\Delta$ first.

Identify. $a = 2$, $b = 3$, $c = -5$.

Discriminant first.

$$ \Delta \;=\; b^{2} - 4 a c \;=\; 9 - 4 \cdot 2 \cdot (-5) \;=\; 9 + 40 \;=\; 49. $$

$\Delta = 49 > 0$ and is a perfect square, so we expect two distinct rational roots.

Apply the formula.

$$ x \;=\; \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{49}}{2 \cdot 2} \;=\; \frac{-3 \pm 7}{4}. $$

So $x = 4/4 = 1$ or $x = -10/4 = -5/2$.

Evaluate. Check by Vieta's: sum $= -b/a = -3/2$; sum of $1 + (-5/2) = -3/2$. Product $= c/a = -5/2$; product of $1 \cdot (-5/2) = -5/2$. Both confirmed.

Going deeper · Deriving the quadratic formula from completing the square

Start from $a x^{2} + b x + c = 0$ with $a \ne 0$. Divide by $a$:

$$ x^{2} + \tfrac{b}{a} x + \tfrac{c}{a} \;=\; 0. $$

Move the constant and complete the square (see §5):

$$ x^{2} + \tfrac{b}{a} x \;=\; -\tfrac{c}{a} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \left( x + \tfrac{b}{2 a} \right)^{2} \;=\; -\tfrac{c}{a} + \tfrac{b^{2}}{4 a^{2}} \;=\; \frac{b^{2} - 4 a c}{4 a^{2}}. $$

Take square roots and isolate $x$:

$$ x + \tfrac{b}{2 a} \;=\; \pm \frac{\sqrt{b^{2} - 4 a c}}{2 a} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x \;=\; \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^{2} - 4 a c}}{2 a}. $$

The radicand $b^{2} - 4 a c$ is the discriminant. If it is positive, $\sqrt{\Delta}$ is a positive real number and the $\pm$ gives two distinct roots. If it is zero, the $\pm$ collapses and we get one repeated root. If it is negative, $\sqrt{\Delta}$ is not real and the roots are complex conjugates.

Section 5 · Completing the square

Completing the Square

Syllabus note. Completing the square is typically introduced in Algebra 2 (US), Grade 10 MPM2D (Ontario), and Pre-Calc 11 (BC). US Algebra 1 students often skip it on a first pass and learn the quadratic formula by recipe instead. If you're in US Algebra 1 and your row above defers §5, you can still memorise the formula in §4 , but completing the square is what the formula is, and you will need it for parabola transformations and (later) for circles, ellipses, and the rotation-to-standard-form trick in conic sections (Conic Sections).
The procedure for $x^{2} + b x + c = 0$ (with $a = 1$).
  1. Move the constant to the right: $x^{2} + b x = -c$.
  2. Halve $b$ and square it: $(b/2)^{2}$.
  3. Add $(b/2)^{2}$ to both sides: $x^{2} + b x + (b/2)^{2} = -c + (b/2)^{2}$.
  4. Factor the left side as a perfect square: $(x + b/2)^{2} = (b/2)^{2} - c$.
  5. Take square roots: $x + b/2 = \pm \sqrt{(b/2)^{2} - c}$.
  6. Isolate $x$.
When $a \ne 1$. Factor $a$ out of the $x^{2}$ and $x$ terms first: $a (x^{2} + (b/a) x) + c = 0$. Complete the square inside the bracket using $b/a$ as the new linear coefficient, then expand.

The geometric picture. Adding $(b/2)^{2}$ to $x^{2} + b x$ literally completes a square of side $x + b/2$. The visualisation is where the name comes from.
Worked Example 5 · Complete the square to find the vertex

Rewrite $f(x) = 2 x^{2} + 8 x + 5$ in vertex form by completing the square.

Factor $2$ out of the $x^{2}$ and $x$ terms.

$$ f(x) \;=\; 2 \left( x^{2} + 4 x \right) + 5. $$

Halve the linear coefficient inside the bracket. $b = 4$, so $(b/2)^{2} = 4$. Add and subtract $4$ inside the bracket (so the net change is zero):

$$ f(x) \;=\; 2 \left( x^{2} + 4 x + 4 - 4 \right) + 5 \;=\; 2 \left( (x + 2)^{2} - 4 \right) + 5. $$

Distribute and combine constants.

$$ f(x) \;=\; 2 (x + 2)^{2} - 8 + 5 \;=\; 2 (x + 2)^{2} - 3. $$

Evaluate. Read off the vertex from the form $a (x - h)^{2} + k$: $h = -2$, $k = -3$, so the vertex is $(-2, -3)$. Since $a = 2 > 0$, the parabola opens up and the vertex is the minimum.

Going deeper · The completing-the-square move, geometrically

Picture $x^{2} + b x$ as a literal square of side $x$ plus a rectangle of dimensions $b \times x$. Split the rectangle into two strips of dimensions $(b/2) \times x$ and attach one strip to each of two adjacent sides of the square. You now have an L-shape with a missing corner. The missing corner is a $(b/2) \times (b/2) = (b/2)^{2}$ square. Adding it fills out the L into a perfect $(x + b/2) \times (x + b/2)$ square , hence "completing the square."

Algebraically, the same move says: $x^{2} + b x + (b/2)^{2} = (x + b/2)^{2}$. Subtracting $(b/2)^{2}$ to undo the addition gives $x^{2} + b x = (x + b/2)^{2} - (b/2)^{2}$, which is the substitution that turns standard form into vertex form.

Halve the linear coefficient and square: $(b/2)^{2} = (-5)^{2} = 25$. Add and subtract $25$ to form a perfect square.
Section 6 · Modeling

Quadratic Modeling

Three canonical model types.
  • Projectile motion. In US customary units (feet, seconds): $h(t) = -16 t^{2} + v_{0} t + h_{0}$. In metric (metres, seconds): $h(t) = -4.9 t^{2} + v_{0} t + h_{0}$. The $-16$ and $-4.9$ are $\tfrac{1}{2} g$ for gravitational acceleration $g$. The vertex gives the maximum height; the positive root gives the landing time.
  • Revenue and profit. Demand is often modelled linearly: price $p = a - b x$ for quantity sold $x$. Revenue $R(x) = x p = x (a - b x) = a x - b x^{2}$ is quadratic with a maximum. Profit $P(x) = R(x) - C(x)$ (subtract a linear cost) is still quadratic.
  • Area-perimeter optimisation. A fixed perimeter gives a constraint $2 L + 2 W = P$; substitute into area $A = L W$ to get a quadratic in one variable. The vertex gives the maximum area (or area-constrained minimum perimeter, depending on the question).
Workflow. (1) Name your variables with units. (2) Write the quadratic. (3) Find the vertex (max or min). (4) Find the roots if the question asks "when does it hit zero?" (5) Answer in a sentence with units.
Worked Example 6 · Projectile motion

A ball is thrown upward from a $5$-metre platform at $20$ m/s. Its height is $h(t) = -4.9 t^{2} + 20 t + 5$, with $h$ in metres and $t$ in seconds. (a) When does the ball reach its maximum height? (b) What is the maximum height? (c) When does the ball hit the ground?

Identify. $a = -4.9$, $b = 20$, $c = 5$. Parabola opens downward (since $a < 0$), so the vertex is a maximum.

(a) Time of maximum height.

$$ t \;=\; -\frac{b}{2 a} \;=\; -\frac{20}{2 \cdot (-4.9)} \;=\; \frac{20}{9.8} \;\approx\; 2.04 \text{ seconds}. $$

(b) Maximum height. Substitute $t = 20/9.8$ back:

$$ h_{\max} \;=\; -4.9 \cdot (20/9.8)^{2} + 20 \cdot (20/9.8) + 5 \;=\; -\tfrac{(20)^{2}}{2 \cdot 9.8} + \tfrac{(20)^{2}}{9.8} + 5 \;=\; \tfrac{200}{9.8} + 5 \;\approx\; 25.41 \text{ metres}. $$

(c) Landing time. Set $h(t) = 0$ and use the quadratic formula:

$$ t \;=\; \frac{-20 \pm \sqrt{400 + 4 \cdot 4.9 \cdot 5}}{2 \cdot (-4.9)} \;=\; \frac{-20 \pm \sqrt{498}}{-9.8}. $$

Take the positive root (negative time is unphysical): $\sqrt{498} \approx 22.32$, so

$$ t \;=\; \frac{-20 - 22.32}{-9.8} \;\approx\; \frac{-42.32}{-9.8} \;\approx\; 4.32 \text{ seconds}. $$

Evaluate. The ball rises for $\approx 2.04$ seconds to a peak of $\approx 25.4$ metres, then falls back, landing at $\approx 4.32$ seconds. The total flight time is slightly more than twice the rise time because it falls farther (from $25.4$ m to $0$ m) than it rose (from $5$ m to $25.4$ m).

Going deeper · A revenue-optimisation worked example

A streaming service models monthly demand as $x = 1000 - 50 p$ subscribers when the price is $p$ dollars per month. Find the price that maximises revenue, and the resulting revenue.

Revenue is $R(p) = p \cdot x = p (1000 - 50 p) = 1000 p - 50 p^{2}$, which is a downward-opening parabola in $p$. The maximum is at the vertex:

$$ p^{*} \;=\; -\frac{1000}{2 \cdot (-50)} \;=\; \frac{1000}{100} \;=\; 10 \text{ dollars}. $$

At $p^{*} = 10$, demand is $x^{*} = 1000 - 50 \cdot 10 = 500$ subscribers, and revenue is

$$ R(10) \;=\; 10 \cdot 500 \;=\; 5000 \text{ dollars per month}. $$

The same calculation appears in AP Macroeconomics (monopoly revenue maximisation) and in IB Math HL E4 (problem-solving using calculus), where the calculus version uses $\frac{dR}{dp} = 0$ instead of the vertex formula. Both methods give the same answer; the calculus approach generalises beyond quadratics.

Section 7 · Inequalities

Quadratic Inequalities Honors (US Alg 1) 🇨🇦 BC PC11 core

Syllabus note. BC Pre-Calc 11 lists linear and quadratic inequalities as a dedicated Content topic with sign analysis as the named technique , that is the curriculum's own wording. US Common Core covers linear inequalities under HSA-REI.B.3 and graphical inequality solving under HSA-REI.D.12, but quadratic inequalities are usually deferred to Algebra 2 or Pre-Calc. In Ontario MCR3U the technique surfaces alongside the function-domain work in A1. The sign-analysis method below works across all three curricula.
Sign analysis for $a x^{2} + b x + c \;\square\; 0$ (where $\square \in \{ <, \le, >, \ge \}$).
  1. Find the roots. Solve $a x^{2} + b x + c = 0$ by factoring, the formula, or completing the square. Call them $r_{1} \le r_{2}$ if they exist.
  2. Draw the parabola. Use the sign of $a$ to decide whether it opens up or down. Mark the roots on the $x$-axis.
  3. Read intervals. Between two roots, the parabola is on the opposite side of the $x$-axis from its tails. Use the sketch to read which intervals make the inequality true.
  4. Express in interval notation. $(-\infty, r_{1}) \cup (r_{2}, \infty)$ for "outside the roots"; $(r_{1}, r_{2})$ for "between the roots." Include the endpoints with square brackets when the inequality is $\le$ or $\ge$.
Edge cases. If $\Delta < 0$, the parabola has no real roots: it is either everywhere positive ($a > 0$) or everywhere negative ($a < 0$). Then the inequality is true on all of $\mathbb{R}$ or on no real numbers depending on direction.
Worked Example 7 · Sign analysis for a strict inequality

Solve $x^{2} - x - 6 > 0$ by sign analysis.

Find the roots. Factor: $x^{2} - x - 6 = (x - 3)(x + 2)$. Roots are $x = -2$ and $x = 3$.

Read the parabola. $a = 1 > 0$, so the parabola opens upward. It crosses the $x$-axis at $-2$ and $3$. Between the roots the parabola is below the $x$-axis (negative); outside the roots it is above (positive).

Pick intervals. The inequality asks where $x^{2} - x - 6 > 0$, that is, where the parabola is positive. That is outside the roots:

$$ x \in (-\infty, -2) \cup (3, \infty). $$

Evaluate. Strict inequality means we exclude the roots (round brackets). Spot-check with $x = 0$ (between the roots): $0 - 0 - 6 = -6 < 0$, correctly outside the solution set. Spot-check with $x = 5$: $25 - 5 - 6 = 14 > 0$, correctly inside.

Going deeper · A quadratic inequality with $\Delta < 0$

Solve $x^{2} + 2 x + 5 \le 0$.

Compute the discriminant: $\Delta = 4 - 20 = -16 < 0$. The parabola has no real roots and never crosses the $x$-axis. Since $a = 1 > 0$, the parabola opens upward and lies entirely above the $x$-axis , the expression $x^{2} + 2 x + 5$ is always strictly positive.

The inequality $\le 0$ asks where the expression is non-positive. It never is. So the solution set is empty: no real $x$ satisfies it.

Compare with the opposite direction: $x^{2} + 2 x + 5 > 0$ is true for all real $x$. The contrast is a useful sanity check whenever $\Delta < 0$: depending on the direction of the inequality and the sign of $a$, the solution set is either everything or nothing.

Exam Tactics

Exam Strategy and Common Pitfalls

Parabola anatomy
  • Always read the sign of $a$ first. It tells you opening direction and whether the vertex is a max or a min. Forgetting this is the most common single-mark slip on parabola questions.
  • Vertex from standard form: $x = -b/(2 a)$, then substitute back. Don't try to read the vertex off standard form by inspection.
  • The $y$-intercept is always $c$. Plug $x = 0$ in to confirm if you're rushing.
Solving methods
  • Try factoring first. If integer coefficients factor cleanly, you save time over the formula.
  • Fall back to the formula for messy coefficients or when factoring fails. Always state $\Delta$ on the page before you solve , it earns marks even when the arithmetic slips.
  • Use completing the square for vertex form or when the question wants exact form (e.g. SAT, IB-style). The formula gives numeric roots; completing the square gives structure.
  • Graph for "how many solutions" questions. The vertex's relation to the $x$-axis answers it in one move.
Discriminant
  • $\Delta = b^{2} - 4 a c$ is a single number you compute first. Many exam questions only ask for the classification, not the roots themselves , in which case computing $\Delta$ is the whole answer.
  • When $\Delta = 0$, the repeated root is $x = -b/(2 a)$. It is also the $x$-coordinate of the vertex. The parabola is tangent to the $x$-axis at that point.
  • When $\Delta < 0$, the parabola does not cross the $x$-axis. Phrase your answer in those terms unless your course covers complex roots, in which case the roots are $(-b \pm i \sqrt{-\Delta})/(2 a)$.
Modeling
  • Always name the variable and its units. "Let $W$ be the side perpendicular to the river, in metres" beats "let $W$ be a number."
  • Vertex = optimum. Maximum height, maximum revenue, maximum area for a fixed perimeter , all are read off the vertex of a quadratic, not by trial.
  • Check that the answer is physical. Negative time, negative length, negative revenue: discard.
Inequalities (BC PC11 / honors track)
  • Sketch the parabola first. Sign analysis is faster on a sketch than on a symbol table.
  • Strict vs. inclusive. $<$ and $>$ exclude the roots (round brackets); $\le$ and $\ge$ include them (square brackets). Mark this on the page before writing the final answer.
  • Multiplying by a negative flips the inequality. If you flip the sign of $a$ to make the parabola open up, flip the inequality direction too.

What This Feeds Into

Quadratics are the second function family and the gateway to every later one. Polynomials are the natural higher-degree generalisation; rational and exponential functions both reuse the discriminant-and-roots vocabulary. The cross-references below point at units already shipped in this repo.
