Polynomial
Functions

Polynomials are the natural generalisation of the parabola you mastered in the Quadratic Functions guide. This unit names the parts (degree, leading coefficient, constant term), does the arithmetic (addition, multiplication, long and synthetic division), and then unlocks roots through the remainder theorem, the factor theorem, and the rational root theorem. From there we graph by zeros, multiplicity, and end behaviour, count turning points against the degree, and finish with complex conjugate roots and polynomial inequalities via sign analysis. The unit feeds into Rational and Radical Expressions, where polynomial factoring rebuilds the numerator and denominator, AP Calc Unit 2 (derivative of a polynomial), and IB Math AA HL B2 (polynomial functions with the full factor / remainder / sum-and-product machinery).

If you are cramming the night before

Memorise four facts. (1) The factor theorem: $p(a) = 0$ if and only if $(x - a)$ is a factor of $p(x)$. (2) The remainder theorem: the remainder when $p(x)$ is divided by $(x - a)$ is $p(a)$. (4) A polynomial of degree $n$ has at most $n - 1$ turning points and exactly $n$ roots counted with multiplicity (in $\mathbb{C}$). Skim the cram-cheat boxes at the top of each section in your row.

Section 1 · Vocabulary

Polynomial Vocabulary 🇺🇸 CCSSM · 🇨🇦 ON MHF4U · 🇨🇦 BC PC12

A polynomial in $x$. Finite sum of $a_{k} x^{k}$ with $k$ a non-negative integer: $$ p(x) \;=\; a_{n} x^{n} + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \cdots + a_{1} x + a_{0}, \qquad a_{n} \ne 0. $$
  • Degree $n$ , largest exponent with non-zero coefficient (linear $1$, quadratic $2$, cubic $3$, quartic $4$, quintic $5$).
  • Leading coefficient $a_{n}$ , controls end behaviour.
  • Constant term $a_{0} = p(0)$ , the $y$-intercept.
  • Monic , leading coefficient $1$. Terms vs. factors: terms are added, factors are multiplied.
End behaviour. Only $a_{n} x^{n}$ matters as $x \to \pm \infty$. Four cases by parity of $n$ and sign of $a_{n}$: even-positive both ends to $+\infty$ (like $x^{2}$); even-negative both to $-\infty$; odd-positive left $\to -\infty$, right $\to +\infty$ (like $x^{3}$); odd-negative the reverse.
Syllabus note. BC Pre-Calc 12 enumerates the named graph characteristics , degree, extrema, zeros, end-behaviour , verbatim in its polynomial-functions Content elaboration. That four-word list is the right vocabulary checklist for any BC PC12 polynomial question; the same vocabulary surfaces in MHF4U (Ontario) and in US Pre-Calc / AP Pre-Calc, often word-for-word.
Worked Example 1 · Identify the parts

For $p(x) = -3 x^{4} + 5 x^{2} - 2 x + 7$, state the degree, leading coefficient, constant term, and end behaviour.

Identify. $p(x) = -3 x^{4} + 0 \cdot x^{3} + 5 x^{2} - 2 x + 7$ (note the missing $x^{3}$ term).

Degree $= 4$. Leading coefficient $a_{4} = -3$. Constant term $a_{0} = 7 = p(0)$.

End behaviour. $n = 4$ even and $a_{n} = -3 < 0$: both ends fall, $p(x) \to -\infty$ as $x \to \pm \infty$.

Evaluate. Quartic, opens "down on both ends," $y$-intercept $(0, 7)$, at most $3$ turning points (general: degree $n \Rightarrow$ at most $n - 1$ turning points). The next sections find where it crosses zero.

Going deeper · Why only the leading term decides end behaviour

Factor $a_{n} x^{n}$ out: $p(x) = a_{n} x^{n} \left( 1 + \frac{a_{n-1}}{a_{n} x} + \cdots + \frac{a_{0}}{a_{n} x^{n}} \right)$. As $|x| \to \infty$, every term in the bracket except the constant $1$ heads to $0$, so the bracket approaches $1$ and $p(x)$ behaves like $a_{n} x^{n}$ for large $|x|$. That is the whole "only the leading term matters" rule: lower-order terms are eventually negligible. The same argument explains why polynomials of different degree must diverge from each other , the algebraic content behind HSF-LE.A.3's "exponential eventually beats polynomial" claim, applied within the polynomial family itself.

Section 2 · Arithmetic and division

Polynomial Arithmetic and Division

Addition and multiplication. Add by combining like terms (same exponent). Multiply by the distributive law term-by-term; the degree of a product is the sum of the degrees.

Long division. To divide $p(x)$ by $d(x)$:
  1. Arrange both in descending order, including a $0$ coefficient for any missing power.
  2. Divide the leading term of $p$ by the leading term of $d$ to get the next term of the quotient.
  3. Multiply $d$ by that term and subtract from $p$.
  4. Repeat with the new "remainder so far" until its degree is less than $\deg d$.
The result is $p(x) = d(x) \cdot q(x) + r(x)$ with $\deg r < \deg d$.

Synthetic division. A streamlined shortcut for dividing by a linear factor $(x - a)$:
  1. Write the coefficients of $p$ in descending order (use $0$ for missing powers).
  2. Put $a$ on the left.
  3. Bring down the first coefficient. Multiply by $a$ and add to the next coefficient. Repeat.
  4. The final number is $p(a)$ , the remainder. The other numbers, in order, are the coefficients of the quotient (degree one less than $p$).
Synthetic division is exactly long division by $(x - a)$, rewritten as a number-crunching procedure.
Worked Example 2 · Long division of $p(x) = 2 x^{3} - 5 x^{2} + 3 x - 7$ by $d(x) = x - 2$

Find the quotient $q(x)$ and the remainder $r$.

Set up. Coefficients of $p$: $2, -5, 3, -7$.

Step 1. $2 x^{3} \div x = 2 x^{2}$; multiply by $d$: $2 x^{3} - 4 x^{2}$; subtract: $-x^{2}$. Bring down: dividend $-x^{2} + 3 x - 7$.

Step 2. $-x^{2} \div x = -x$; multiply: $-x^{2} + 2 x$; subtract: $x$. Bring down: dividend $x - 7$.

Step 3. $x \div x = 1$; multiply: $x - 2$; subtract: $-5$. Degree $0 < \deg d$, stop.

Read off. $q(x) = 2 x^{2} - x + 1$, $r = -5$, so $2 x^{3} - 5 x^{2} + 3 x - 7 = (x - 2)(2 x^{2} - x + 1) - 5$. Sanity check at $x = 2$: left $= 16 - 20 + 6 - 7 = -5$, matching $r$ , previewing the remainder theorem in §3.

Going deeper · Synthetic division on the same problem

Coefficients $2, -5, 3, -7$. Divisor $(x - 2)$, so $a = 2$. Bring down $2$; multiply by $a = 2$ and add: $-5 + 4 = -1$. Continue: $3 + (-2) = 1$; $-7 + 2 = -5$. Read off the quotient coefficients $2, -1, 1$ (one degree lower than $p$) and the remainder $-5$, matching long division exactly. Synthetic division is faster but only works for divisors of the form $(x - a)$; for higher-degree divisors, fall back to long division.

Section 3 · Remainder and Factor Theorems

The Remainder and Factor Theorems 🇨🇦 ON MHF4U · 🇨🇦 BC PC12 core

Remainder theorem. When $p(x)$ is divided by $(x - a)$, the remainder equals $p(a)$. $$ p(x) \;=\; (x - a) \cdot q(x) + p(a). $$ Factor theorem. $(x - a)$ is a factor of $p(x)$ if and only if $p(a) = 0$. (Special case of the remainder theorem with $p(a) = 0$.)

How to use them.
  • Check whether $a$ is a root without doing long division: compute $p(a)$ and see if it is zero.
  • Factor a polynomial once you have spotted a root $a$: divide by $(x - a)$ (synthetic division is fastest) to get the quotient $q(x)$, then continue factoring $q(x)$.
  • Find an unknown coefficient when you know a root: substitute and solve.

In US Common Core they sit one click above the linear-scoped extract under HSA-APR (Arithmetic with Polynomials and Rational Expressions); the cluster name and category exist, but the granular bullets are not in ccssm_hs_math_extract.md. Worked Example 3 · Factor a cubic by spotting a root

Fully factor $p(x) = x^{3} - 6 x^{2} + 11 x - 6$ over the reals.

Hunt for a root. Try $x = 1$: $p(1) = 1 - 6 + 11 - 6 = 0$. By the factor theorem, $(x - 1)$ is a factor.

Divide. Synthetic division with $a = 1$ on $1, -6, 11, -6$ gives quotient $x^{2} - 5 x + 6$ and remainder $0$. Factor the quotient: $(x - 2)(x - 3)$ (sum $5$, product $6$).

Assemble. $p(x) = (x - 1)(x - 2)(x - 3)$. Roots $1, 2, 3$, all simple. Vieta check on the cubic: sum $1 + 2 + 3 = 6 = -a_{n-1}/a_{n}$; product $1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 = 6 = (-1)^{n} a_{0}/a_{n} = -(-6)$. Confirmed.

Going deeper · Why the remainder theorem is true

Long division of $p(x)$ by $(x - a)$ leaves a remainder $r(x)$ with $\deg r < \deg(x - a) = 1$, so $r$ is a constant: $p(x) = (x - a) q(x) + r$. Substitute $x = a$: $p(a) = 0 \cdot q(a) + r = r$. So the remainder equals $p(a)$ , the remainder theorem in one substitution. The factor theorem is the special case $p(a) = 0$: then $p(x) = (x - a) q(x)$, exhibiting $(x - a)$ as a factor; the converse is immediate.

Section 4 · Finding roots

Finding Roots: Rational Root Theorem and Factoring Strategies

Rational root theorem. Let $p(x) = a_{n} x^{n} + \cdots + a_{1} x + a_{0}$ have integer coefficients with $a_{n} \ne 0$ and $a_{0} \ne 0$. If $p$ has a rational root $\frac{p}{q}$ in lowest terms (so $\gcd(p, q) = 1$), then
  • $p$ divides $a_{0}$ (the constant term), and
  • $q$ divides $a_{n}$ (the leading coefficient).
The candidate list is finite: every $\pm (\text{factor of } a_{0}) / (\text{factor of } a_{n})$ in lowest terms.

Strategy for solving $p(x) = 0$.
  1. Pull a GCF first if one is staring at you (factors out a power of $x$ to expose any $x = 0$ roots).
  2. Try special factorings: difference of squares $a^{2} - b^{2} = (a - b)(a + b)$; sum/difference of cubes $a^{3} \pm b^{3} = (a \pm b)(a^{2} \mp a b + b^{2})$; grouping for four-term expressions.
  3. List rational candidates with the rational root theorem. Test each with synthetic division (which delivers $p(a)$ and the quotient simultaneously).
  4. Recurse. Each rational root drops the degree by $1$; eventually you reach a quadratic, which you solve by §2.3-§2.5 techniques.
  5. Irrational roots come from the residual quadratic via the quadratic formula. Complex roots come in conjugate pairs (§6).
Syllabus note. The rational root theorem is standard in US Pre-Calc / Algebra 2 Honors; it is the canonical tool for hand-factoring a cubic or quartic with integer coefficients. BC Pre-Calc 12 lists "solving equations algebraically and graphically" in the polynomial Content elaboration without naming the theorem , the theorem is the standard solving technique that any PC12 candidate-list question expects. Ontario MHF4U's polynomial strand uses the same hunt-then-divide workflow.
Worked Example 4 · Solve a cubic with the rational root theorem

Solve $2 x^{3} - 3 x^{2} - 11 x + 6 = 0$ completely over the reals.

Candidate list. $a_{n} = 2$, $a_{0} = 6$. Lowest-terms $p/q$ with $p \mid 6$ and $q \mid 2$: $\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 6, \pm \tfrac{1}{2}, \pm \tfrac{3}{2}$.

Test. Try $x = 3$: $p(3) = 54 - 27 - 33 + 6 = 0$. Root found.

Divide. Synthetic division with $a = 3$ on $2, -3, -11, 6$ gives quotient coefficients $2, 3, -2$ with remainder $0$, so the quotient is $2 x^{2} + 3 x - 2$.

Solve the quotient. Factor $2 x^{2} + 3 x - 2$ by AC method: $a c = -4$, pair $(4, -1)$ sums to $3$, so $2 x^{2} + 3 x - 2 = (2 x - 1)(x + 2)$ giving $x = 1/2$ or $x = -2$.

Assemble. All roots: $x = 3, \tfrac{1}{2}, -2$. Fully factored: $2 x^{3} - 3 x^{2} - 11 x + 6 = (x - 3)(2 x - 1)(x + 2)$.

Going deeper · Why the rational root theorem is true

If $p(x) = a_{n} x^{n} + \cdots + a_{0}$ has integer coefficients and rational root $p/q$ in lowest terms, then $p(p/q) = 0$. Multiply through by $q^{n}$ to clear denominators:

$$ a_{n} p^{n} + a_{n-1} p^{n-1} q + \cdots + a_{1} p q^{n-1} + a_{0} q^{n} \;=\; 0. $$

Every term except the last is divisible by $p$, so $p \mid a_{0} q^{n}$; since $\gcd(p, q) = 1$, this forces $p \mid a_{0}$. Symmetrically, every term except the first is divisible by $q$, so $q \mid a_{n} p^{n}$ and $q \mid a_{n}$. That is the theorem. Without integer coefficients the result fails; if the polynomial has rational coefficients, clear denominators first.

Section 5 · Graphing

Graphing Polynomial Functions

Five-step polynomial sketch.
  1. Degree and leading coefficient set end behaviour (four cases of §1).
  2. $y$-intercept is $p(0) = a_{0}$.
  3. Zeros and multiplicity. Factor $p(x)$; each factor $(x - r)^{m}$ contributes a zero at $r$ with multiplicity $m$. Odd $m$: graph crosses. Even $m$: graph touches without crossing. $m \ge 2$: graph is flatter near $r$.
  4. Turning points: at most $n - 1$; exact locations need calculus.
  5. Sketch: plot intercepts, apply end behaviour, use multiplicities at each zero.
Sign sketch shortcut. Mark zeros. Set the rightmost interval's sign from end behaviour. Walk leftward; sign flips at odd-multiplicity zeros, stays the same at even-multiplicity zeros.

Going deeper · Why even multiplicity gives a "touch" and odd a "cross"

Factor the local behaviour at a zero $r$: $p(x) = (x - r)^{m} \cdot g(x)$ with $g(r) \ne 0$. Near $x = r$, $g(x) \approx g(r)$ is a non-zero constant, so $p(x) \approx g(r) \cdot (x - r)^{m}$ locally; the sign of $p$ near $r$ is the sign of $g(r)$ times the sign of $(x - r)^{m}$. Odd $m$: $(x - r)^{m}$ flips sign as $x$ crosses $r$, so $p$ flips , graph crosses. Even $m$: $(x - r)^{m} \ge 0$ everywhere with equality only at $r$, so $p$ keeps its sign , graph touches and returns. Larger $m$ also makes the graph flatter near $r$ because $(x - r)^{m}$ is closer to $0$ , the "flat-zero" appearance.

Section 6 · Complex roots

Complex Roots and Conjugate Pairs Honors (US Alg 2+ / BC PC12)

Syllabus note. Complex numbers sit in US Common Core under HSN-CN (Number and Quantity , Complex Numbers), which is one cluster above the linear-scoped CCSSM extract. The polynomial consequence , that non-real roots of a real polynomial come in conjugate pairs , is standard US Algebra 2 / Pre-Calc content. BC Pre-Calc 12 names "solving equations algebraically and graphically" in the polynomial Content elaboration; the PC12 extract does not single out complex roots, but the framework supports them. Ontario MHF4U's polynomial strand uses the same conjugate-pair principle when factoring quartics.
The complex conjugate root theorem. If $p(x)$ has real coefficients and $z = a + b i$ (with $b \ne 0$) is a root, then $\bar{z} = a - b i$ is also a root. Non-real roots of a real polynomial come in conjugate pairs.

Consequences.
  • Real factorisation always exists into linear and irreducible quadratic factors. Pair each non-real root with its conjugate; the product $(x - z)(x - \bar{z}) = x^{2} - 2 a x + (a^{2} + b^{2})$ is an irreducible real quadratic.
  • An odd-degree real polynomial has at least one real root. Complex roots come in pairs (even count), so degree $n$ odd forces an odd number of real roots, which is at least $1$.
  • The fundamental theorem of algebra: every polynomial of degree $n \ge 1$ with complex coefficients has exactly $n$ roots in $\mathbb{C}$, counted with multiplicity.
How to use. If a problem hands you one complex root, the conjugate is the second root for free.
Worked Example 6 · Build a polynomial from a complex root

Find a polynomial with real coefficients of degree $3$ that has roots $x = 2$ and $x = 1 + i$.

Identify. Given real root $x = 2$ and complex root $z = 1 + i$. Real coefficients force $\bar{z} = 1 - i$ as the third root. Three roots in hand, matching degree $3$.

Multiply the conjugate pair. $(x - (1 + i))(x - (1 - i)) = ((x - 1) - i)((x - 1) + i) = (x - 1)^{2} + 1 = x^{2} - 2 x + 2$ (irreducible: $\Delta = 4 - 8 < 0$).

Assemble. $p(x) = (x - 2)(x^{2} - 2 x + 2) = x^{3} - 4 x^{2} + 6 x - 4$.

Evaluate. Check $p(2) = 8 - 16 + 12 - 4 = 0$. For $p(1 + i)$, use $(1 + i)^{2} = 2 i$ and $(1 + i)^{3} = -2 + 2 i$; substituting gives $p(1 + i) = (-2 + 2 i) - 4 (2 i) + 6 (1 + i) - 4 = 0$. Both roots confirmed.

Going deeper · Why the conjugate root theorem is true

Complex conjugation satisfies $\overline{z + w} = \bar{z} + \bar{w}$ and $\overline{z w} = \bar{z} \cdot \bar{w}$. If $p(x) = a_{n} x^{n} + \cdots + a_{0}$ has real coefficients (so $\bar{a}_{k} = a_{k}$) and $p(z) = 0$, apply conjugation to both sides: $\overline{p(z)} = a_{n} \bar{z}^{n} + \cdots + a_{0} = p(\bar{z})$, while the left side is $\overline{0} = 0$. So $p(\bar{z}) = 0$, i.e. $\bar{z}$ is also a root. The proof fails for non-real coefficients (then $\bar{a}_{k} \ne a_{k}$) , the conjugate-pair statement is specifically about real polynomials.

Section 7 · Inequalities

Polynomial Inequalities Honors (US Alg 2) 🇨🇦 BC PC11 sign-analysis

Sign analysis for $p(x) \;\square\; 0$ (where $\square \in \{ <, \le, >, \ge \}$).
  1. Factor $p(x)$ completely over the reals into linear factors $(x - r)^{m}$ and irreducible quadratics ($\Delta < 0$, fixed sign throughout).
  2. List zeros in increasing order.
  3. Rightmost-interval sign from end behaviour (sign of $a_{n}$ as $x \to +\infty$).
  4. Walk leftward. Flip at odd-multiplicity zeros; keep at even-multiplicity zeros.
  5. Read off intervals and write in interval notation. Square brackets at zeros for $\le, \ge$; round for strict.
Strict vs. non-strict. Zeros satisfy $p = 0$, so they belong to $\le, \ge$ solutions but not to $<, >$.

Generalising from quadratic to higher-degree polynomial is a direct extension: the same workflow, more factors. Worked Example 7 · Solve $(x + 2)(x - 1)^{2}(x - 4) > 0$

State the solution in interval notation.

Factor. Already factored. Zeros: $x = -2$ (mult $1$, odd), $x = 1$ (mult $2$, even), $x = 4$ (mult $1$, odd). Total multiplicity $1 + 2 + 1 = 4$; expanded leading term is $+x^{4}$ (degree $4$, leading coefficient $+1$).

End behaviour and walk. Even degree, positive leading coefficient $\Rightarrow (4, \infty): +$. Cross at $4$: flip to $-$ on $(1, 4)$. Touch at $1$: no flip, $-$ on $(-2, 1)$. Cross at $-2$: flip to $+$ on $(-\infty, -2)$.

Read off. Solution (strict $> 0$, excluding zeros): $x \in (-\infty, -2) \cup (4, \infty)$. Sanity check: $x = 0$ gives $(2)(1)(-4) = -8 < 0$ (correctly outside); $x = 5$ gives $(7)(16)(1) = 112 > 0$ (correctly inside). Note $x = 1$ is excluded even though $p(1) = 0$, since the inequality is strict.

Going deeper · A polynomial inequality with an irreducible quadratic factor

Solve $(x - 3)(x^{2} + 4) \le 0$. The factor $(x^{2} + 4)$ has $\Delta = -16 < 0$ and positive leading coefficient, so $x^{2} + 4 > 0$ for every real $x$. The product therefore has the same sign as $(x - 3)$, which is $\le 0$ iff $x \le 3$. Answer: $x \in (-\infty, 3]$ (square bracket because $\le$ is non-strict). Irreducible-quadratic factors of a real polynomial contribute a fixed sign and never cause sign changes , pull them out and ignore them once their sign is known.

Exam Tactics

Exam Strategy and Common Pitfalls

Vocabulary and end behaviour
  • Always state degree and leading coefficient first. End behaviour follows immediately from those two and is worth marks even when the rest of the question goes sideways.
  • Insert $0$ coefficients for missing powers before any long or synthetic division. Skipping this step is the single most common arithmetic slip.
  • $y$-intercept is always $a_{0}$. The constant term is $p(0)$; no calculation needed.
Theorems by name
  • Quote the factor theorem and remainder theorem by name. "By the factor theorem, $(x - a)$ is a factor iff $p(a) = 0$." Examiners look for this phrasing in MHF4U and PC12.
  • The remainder theorem is the fastest way to compute a remainder. Don't do long division if the divisor is $(x - a)$ , just compute $p(a)$.
  • The rational root theorem narrows but does not guarantee. Every rational root is on the candidate list, but most candidates are not roots. Test by substitution or synthetic division.
Graphing
  • Read multiplicity off each factor. Write "$(x - r)^{m}$, mult $m$" beside each zero so you don't forget when sketching.
  • Even multiplicity = touch; odd multiplicity = cross. The bounce vs. cross distinction is a common single-mark question on its own.
  • Sum of multiplicities equals the degree, counting complex roots. A consistency check: if your multiplicities don't sum to $n$, you missed a factor.
Complex roots (honors)
  • Real polynomial $+$ one complex root means the conjugate is free. Always state this explicitly when building a polynomial from roots.
  • Pair complex conjugates into a real irreducible quadratic: $(x - z)(x - \bar{z}) = x^{2} - 2 \operatorname{Re}(z) x + |z|^{2}$. This is a clean way to keep arithmetic real.
  • Odd-degree real polynomials always have at least one real root. Useful as a sanity check on any cubic-roots question.
Inequalities
  • Always factor first, then sign-analyse. Trying to sign-analyse an unfactored polynomial wastes time.
  • Flip sign only at odd-multiplicity zeros. Forgetting that even multiplicity is a "touch" is the second-most-common slip.
  • Strict $<, >$ exclude zeros (round brackets); $\le, \ge$ include (square brackets). Decide this before writing the final answer.
