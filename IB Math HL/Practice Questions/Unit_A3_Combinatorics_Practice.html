Q1MEDIUM Paper 1A 1.9 Binomial — Term Hunt [6 marks]

Consider the expansion of $\displaystyle\left(2x^{2} - \dfrac{1}{x}\right)^{9}$ in descending powers of $x$.

(a) Write down the general term $T_{r+1}$ in this expansion. Simplify so it is a single power of $x$ multiplied by a numerical coefficient. [2]
(b) Hence find the coefficient of $x^{6}$. [2]
(c) Find the term in the expansion that is independent of $x$. [2]
Q2MEDIUM Paper 1A 1.10 Multiset Perms + Gap Method [7 marks]

Consider the letters of the word MISSISSIPPI.

(a) Show that the number of distinguishable arrangements of all eleven letters is $\dfrac{11!}{4!\,4!\,2!}$, and evaluate this number. [2]
(b) Find the number of distinguishable arrangements in which no two I's are adjacent. Justify your method clearly. [5]
Q3HARD Paper 1A 1.10 Consecutive Coefficient Ratios [7 marks]

Three consecutive binomial coefficients $\dbinom{n}{r}$, $\dbinom{n}{r+1}$, $\dbinom{n}{r+2}$ are in the ratio

$$ \binom{n}{r} \,:\, \binom{n}{r+1} \,:\, \binom{n}{r+2} \;=\; 6 \,:\, 14 \,:\, 21, $$

where $n, r \in \mathbb{Z}^{+}$ and $r + 2 \le n$.

(a) Show that $\dbinom{n}{r+1}/\dbinom{n}{r} = (n-r)/(r+1)$. [2]
(b) Hence set up two equations relating $n$ and $r$, and solve them to find $n$ and $r$. [5]
Q4HARD Paper 1A 1.9 Identity via Differentiation [8 marks]

Let $n \in \mathbb{Z}^{+}$ with $n \ge 2$. The binomial theorem gives

$$ (1+x)^{n} \;=\; \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r}\, x^{r}. $$
(a) By differentiating both sides with respect to $x$ and substituting a suitable value of $x$, prove that [3] $$ \sum_{r=1}^{n} r\,\binom{n}{r} \;=\; n\,2^{\,n-1}. $$
(b) By differentiating twice and substituting a suitable value of $x$, prove that [3] $$ \sum_{r=2}^{n} r(r-1)\,\binom{n}{r} \;=\; n(n-1)\,2^{\,n-2}. $$
(c) Hence, or otherwise, show that for $n \ge 2$ [2] $$ \sum_{r=0}^{n} r^{2}\,\binom{n}{r} \;=\; n(n+1)\,2^{\,n-2}. $$

Q5HARD Paper 1B 1.9 Binomial — System & Largest Coefficient [12 marks]

Consider the expansion of $(1 + ax)^{n}$ in ascending powers of $x$, where $a$ is a non-zero real constant and $n$ is a positive integer with $n \ge 3$.

(a) Write down the general term $T_{r+1}$ in terms of $n$, $a$, $r$. [1]
(b) Show that the coefficient of $x^{k}$ in this expansion is $\dbinom{n}{k}\,a^{k}$. [2]
(c) The coefficient of $x$ in this expansion is $24$ and the coefficient of $x^{2}$ is $252$.
(i) Use the result of (b) to write down two equations in $n$ and $a$. [2]
(ii) Show that $n = 8$ and find the value of $a$. [3]
(d) Hence find the coefficient of $x^{3}$. [1]
(e) Using the ratio $T_{r+2}/T_{r+1}$, determine the value of $r$ for which the term $T_{r+1}$ in the expansion of $(1+ax)^{8}$ (with the value of $a$ from (c)) is largest, and state the largest coefficient. [3]
Q6HARD Paper 1B 1.10 Hockey-Stick Identity [13 marks]

For integers $n \ge r \ge 0$ the hockey-stick identity states that

$$ \binom{r}{r} + \binom{r+1}{r} + \binom{r+2}{r} + \cdots + \binom{n}{r} \;=\; \binom{n+1}{r+1}. $$
(a) Verify the identity for $r = 2$ and $n = 5$ by evaluating both sides as integers. [3]
(b) Prove the identity by counting the $(r+1)$-element subsets of $\{1, 2, 3, \ldots, n+1\}$ in two different ways. Begin by classifying each such subset by its largest element. [5]
(c) Use the identity to evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{k=3}^{20}\binom{k}{3}$, expressing your answer as a single binomial coefficient and as an integer. [2]
(d) Noting that $k = \dbinom{k}{1}$, use the identity to prove that [3] $$ 1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + n \;=\; \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}. $$

Q7MEDIUM Paper 2 1.10 Combinations + Conditional Probability [7 marks]

A committee of $4$ is selected at random from a group of $12$ students, consisting of $5$ IB Diploma students and $7$ AP students. Each of the $\dbinom{12}{4}$ possible committees is equally likely.

(a) Find the probability that the committee contains exactly $2$ IB students. Give your answer as an exact fraction in lowest terms. [2]
(b) Find the probability that the committee contains at least $2$ IB students. [3]
(c) Given that the committee contains at least one IB student, find the probability that it contains exactly $2$ IB students. [2]
Q8MEDIUM Paper 2 1.10 Linear Perms — Gap Method [7 marks]

A class of $6$ boys and $4$ girls is to be seated in a row of $10$ chairs. Treat all students as distinguishable.

(a) Find the total number of seating arrangements. [1]
(b) Find the number of arrangements in which no two girls sit next to each other. [3]
(c) Hence find the probability that, in a random arrangement, no two girls are adjacent. [1]
(d) Find the number of arrangements in which a particular girl $G$ sits between two particular boys $B_{1}$ and $B_{2}$ (so the three of them form a consecutive block $B_{1} \,G\, B_{2}$ or $B_{2}\, G \, B_{1}$, somewhere in the row). [2]
Q9HARD Paper 2 1.10 Circular Perms — Cases & Gaps [7 marks]

Ten people, including three sisters Alice, Beth, and Cara, are to be seated around a round table with $10$ chairs. Two seatings are considered identical when one can be rotated to the other.

(a) Find the total number of distinguishable seating arrangements. [1]
(b) Find the number of arrangements in which the three sisters all sit together (as a consecutive block of three). [2]
(c) Find the number of arrangements in which no two of the three sisters sit next to each other. [4]
Q10HARD Paper 2 1.11 Extended Binomial — Scaling Trick [7 marks]

This question uses the extended (rational-exponent) binomial theorem to approximate $\sqrt{2}$. The series in (a) is the workhorse; parts (b)–(d) apply a scaling trick because the naive substitution $x=1$ lies outside the radius of convergence.

(a) Use the extended binomial theorem to find the first four non-zero terms of the expansion of $\sqrt{1+x}$ in ascending powers of $x$, and state the values of $x$ for which the expansion is valid. [3]
(b) Explain in one sentence why the value $x = 1$ cannot be substituted directly into your expansion to approximate $\sqrt{2}$. [1]
(c) Show that $\sqrt{2}$ can be written as $\dfrac{7}{5}\,\sqrt{1+r}$ where $r = \dfrac{1}{49}$. [1]
(d) Hence, using the first four terms of your expansion from (a) with $x = \dfrac{1}{49}$, approximate $\sqrt{2}$ to $5$ decimal places. Compare with the GDC value of $\sqrt{2}$ to $5$ decimal places and state the magnitude of the error. [2]
