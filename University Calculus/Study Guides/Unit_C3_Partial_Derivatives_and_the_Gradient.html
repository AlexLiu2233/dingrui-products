Unit C3: Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

Differentiation in several variables, from partial derivatives and the multivariable chain rule to the gradient vector that drives all of multivariable optimization and geometry.

Calculus III Multivariable Vector Calculus MIT 18.02 / GT 2551 / Princeton MAT 201
Read me first. This unit extends differentiation to functions of several variables. We begin with functions of two and three variables, their domains and level sets, then confront the new subtlety of multivariable limits, where a single function must agree along every approach path. From there we build partial derivatives, the higher and mixed partials governed by Clairaut's theorem, and the multivariable chain rule. The payoff is the gradient vector, which encodes the direction of steepest increase and sits perpendicular to every level set. Work the proofs and the worked examples; the gap between existence of partials and true differentiability is the conceptual heart of the unit.

Section 1

Functions of Several Variables

Key idea. A function of several variables assigns a single real number to each point of a region in $\mathbb{R}^n$. For $n=2$ we write $z = f(x,y)$; the domain is a subset of the plane and the graph is a surface sitting in three-space. The tools of single-variable calculus generalize, but now there are infinitely many directions in which the input can move.

Definition (function of two variables). A function $f$ of two variables is a rule that assigns to each ordered pair $(x,y)$ in a set $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ a unique real number $f(x,y)$. The set $D$ is the domain and the set of output values is the range. The graph of $f$ is the surface $\{(x,y,z) : z = f(x,y),\ (x,y)\in D\}$.

Level curves and level surfaces
$$ \text{level curve: } f(x,y)=k, \qquad \text{level surface: } f(x,y,z)=k. $$

A level curve of $f(x,y)$ is the set of points where $f$ takes a fixed value $k$. The collection of level curves for several values of $k$ is a contour map: closely spaced curves indicate a steep surface, widely spaced curves a gentle one. For three variables the analogous objects are level surfaces $f(x,y,z)=k$.

Worked Example 1.1: domain and level curves of a logarithmic-radical function

Find the domain and describe the level curves of $f(x,y) = \sqrt{9 - x^2 - y^2}$.

The radicand must be nonnegative, so the domain is the closed disk

$$ 9 - x^2 - y^2 \ge 0 \iff x^2 + y^2 \le 9, $$

a disk of radius $3$ centered at the origin. A level curve sets $f(x,y)=k$ with $0 \le k \le 3$:

$$ \sqrt{9 - x^2 - y^2} = k \implies x^2 + y^2 = 9 - k^2, $$

a circle of radius $\sqrt{9-k^2}$. The graph is the upper hemisphere of radius $3$; its contour map is a family of concentric circles that shrink to a point as $k \to 3$.

Correct. The logarithm requires $x+y>0$ and the denominator requires $x-y\ne 0$, that is $x\ne y$.
The logarithm needs a strictly positive argument ($x+y>0$, not $\ge 0$) and the denominator must be nonzero ($x\ne y$).
Section 2

Limits and Continuity

Key idea. For a limit of two variables to exist, $f(x,y)$ must approach the same value along every path into the point. A single pair of paths giving different limits proves the limit does not exist. This is the central new subtlety: the plane offers infinitely many approach directions, not just left and right.
Limit (informal definition)
$$ \lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y) = L $$

means $f(x,y)$ can be made arbitrarily close to $L$ by taking $(x,y)$ sufficiently close to $(a,b)$, for every approach path.

Definition (epsilon-delta). $\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y)=L$ if for every $\varepsilon>0$ there is a $\delta>0$ such that $0 < \sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2} < \delta$ implies $|f(x,y)-L| < \varepsilon$.

Definition (continuity). $f$ is continuous at $(a,b)$ if $\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y) = f(a,b)$. Polynomials are continuous everywhere; rational functions are continuous wherever the denominator is nonzero; sums, products, quotients, and compositions of continuous functions are continuous on their domains.

Worked Example 2.1: a nonexistent limit by the two-path test

Show that $\displaystyle \lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{xy}{x^2+y^2}$ does not exist.

Approach along the $x$-axis ($y=0$): the function is $\frac{0}{x^2}=0$, so the limit along this path is $0$.

Approach along the line $y=x$:

$$ \frac{x\cdot x}{x^2+x^2} = \frac{x^2}{2x^2} = \frac{1}{2}. $$

Two paths give two different values ($0$ and $\tfrac12$), so the limit does not exist.

Going deeper: paths are not enough, but polar coordinates can close the case

Agreement along every straight line does not guarantee a limit; a path may be curved. Consider $g(x,y)=\dfrac{x^2 y}{x^4 + y^2}$. Along any line $y=mx$ the limit is $0$, yet along the parabola $y=x^2$ we get $\dfrac{x^4}{x^4+x^4}=\tfrac12$, so the limit fails.

To prove a limit exists, bound it. For $h(x,y)=\dfrac{x^3}{x^2+y^2}$ switch to polar coordinates $x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta$:

$$ |h| = \left| \frac{r^3\cos^3\theta}{r^2} \right| = r\,|\cos^3\theta| \le r \to 0 \quad \text{as } r\to 0. $$

The bound is independent of $\theta$, so the limit is $0$ regardless of direction. The squeeze on $r$ is what makes the argument airtight.

Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{x^2-y^2}{x^2+y^2}$ along $y=0$ and along $x=0$. What do you conclude?
2.1

Both give $1$; the limit is $1$
The limit is $\tfrac12$

Correct. Along $y=0$ the value is $\frac{x^2}{x^2}=1$; along $x=0$ it is $\frac{-y^2}{y^2}=-1$. Different path limits mean the limit does not exist.
Substitute each path: $y=0$ gives $\frac{x^2}{x^2}=1$, while $x=0$ gives $\frac{-y^2}{y^2}=-1$. Two values, so no limit.
Section 3

Partial Derivatives

Key idea. A partial derivative measures the rate of change of $f$ as one variable moves and the others are held fixed. Geometrically, $f_x(a,b)$ is the slope of the curve cut from the surface by the plane $y=b$. Computationally, you differentiate in one variable and treat the rest as constants.
Partial derivatives as limits
$$ f_x(a,b) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a,b)}{h}, \qquad f_y(a,b) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(a,b+h)-f(a,b)}{h}. $$

Notation. The partial derivative of $z=f(x,y)$ with respect to $x$ is written $f_x$, $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, or $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}$. The symbol $\partial$ distinguishes a partial derivative from the ordinary derivative $d/dx$ of a single-variable function. Higher dimensions are identical: for $f(x,y,z)$ there are three first-order partials $f_x, f_y, f_z$.

Worked Example 3.1: computing both first partials

Let $f(x,y) = x^3 y^2 + \sin(xy)$. Find $f_x$ and $f_y$.

For $f_x$, treat $y$ as a constant and differentiate in $x$:

$$ f_x = 3x^2 y^2 + y\cos(xy). $$

For $f_y$, treat $x$ as a constant and differentiate in $y$:

$$ f_y = 2x^3 y + x\cos(xy). $$

The chain rule supplies the inner factor ($y$ in $f_x$, $x$ in $f_y$) on the $\sin(xy)$ term.

If $f(x,y) = e^{xy}$, what is $f_y$?
3.1
$e^{xy}$
$y\,e^{xy}$
$x\,e^{xy}$
$xy\,e^{xy}$
Correct. Holding $x$ fixed, $\frac{\partial}{\partial y} e^{xy} = e^{xy}\cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xy) = x\,e^{xy}$.
Treat $x$ as constant: the inner derivative $\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xy)=x$, so $f_y = x\,e^{xy}$.
Section 4

Higher Partial Derivatives

Key idea. Differentiating a partial derivative again produces second-order partials. The two mixed partials $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are equal whenever they are continuous, a fact known as Clairaut's theorem. This symmetry is the reason gradients of nice functions behave so cleanly.
Four second-order partials of $f(x,y)$
$$ f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}, \quad f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}, \quad f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\,\partial x}, \quad f_{yx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\,\partial y}. $$

Theorem (Clairaut, equality of mixed partials). If $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are both continuous on an open disk containing $(a,b)$, then $f_{xy}(a,b) = f_{yx}(a,b)$. The notation $f_{xy}$ means differentiate first with respect to $x$, then with respect to $y$.

Worked Example 4.1: verifying Clairaut's theorem

Let $f(x,y) = x^2 y^3 + e^{x} y$. Compute $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ and confirm they agree.

First partials:

$$ f_x = 2xy^3 + e^x y, \qquad f_y = 3x^2 y^2 + e^x. $$

Now the mixed partials:

$$ f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\big(2xy^3 + e^x y\big) = 6xy^2 + e^x, $$ $$ f_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\big(3x^2 y^2 + e^x\big) = 6xy^2 + e^x. $$

They match, as Clairaut's theorem guarantees for this smooth function.

Going deeper: why continuity is required for Clairaut's theorem

The hypothesis of continuity is not cosmetic. The classic counterexample is

$$ f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy(x^2-y^2)}{x^2+y^2}, & (x,y)\ne(0,0) \\[4pt] 0, & (x,y)=(0,0). \end{cases} $$

A direct computation from the limit definitions gives $f_{xy}(0,0) = -1$ but $f_{yx}(0,0) = +1$. The mixed partials disagree at the origin precisely because they fail to be continuous there. Away from the origin the function is smooth and the two mixed partials coincide as usual.

The takeaway: order of differentiation can be swapped freely for the smooth functions you meet in practice, but the underlying reason is the continuity hypothesis, not a universal law.

$-\sin(x^2+y)$

Correct. $f_x = 2x\cos(x^2+y)$. Differentiating again in $x$ with the product and chain rules: $f_{xx} = 2\cos(x^2+y) + 2x\cdot(-\sin(x^2+y))(2x) = 2\cos(x^2+y) - 4x^2\sin(x^2+y)$.

Section 5

The Multivariable Chain Rule

Key idea. When the inputs of $f$ are themselves functions of other variables, the rate of change accumulates one contribution per intermediate variable. Each path from the final variable up to $f$ contributes the product of the partials along it, and you sum over all paths. A tree diagram makes the bookkeeping mechanical.
Chain rule, one independent variable
$$ \text{If } z=f(x,y),\ x=x(t),\ y=y(t): \qquad \frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}. $$
Chain rule, two independent variables
$$ \text{If } z=f(x,y),\ x=x(s,t),\ y=y(s,t): \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}, $$

and similarly for $\partial z/\partial s$.

Worked Example 5.1: chain rule along a parametrized path

Let $z = x^2 y$ with $x = \cos t$ and $y = \sin t$. Find $dz/dt$.

The partials and the parameter derivatives are

$$ \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 2xy, \quad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = x^2, \quad \frac{dx}{dt} = -\sin t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = \cos t. $$

Assemble:

$$ \frac{dz}{dt} = 2xy(-\sin t) + x^2(\cos t) = -2\cos t\sin^2 t + \cos^3 t. $$

Substituting $x=\cos t,\ y=\sin t$ gives the answer entirely in $t$.

Going deeper: deriving the chain rule from the increment formula

Suppose $f$ is differentiable at $(x,y)$. Differentiability means the increment satisfies

$$ \Delta z = f_x\,\Delta x + f_y\,\Delta y + \varepsilon_1\,\Delta x + \varepsilon_2\,\Delta y, $$

where $\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2 \to 0$ as $(\Delta x,\Delta y)\to(0,0)$. Divide by $\Delta t$ and let $\Delta t \to 0$:

$$ \frac{dz}{dt} = f_x\,\frac{dx}{dt} + f_y\,\frac{dy}{dt} + \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\left(\varepsilon_1\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} + \varepsilon_2\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta t}\right). $$

Because $\Delta x/\Delta t \to dx/dt$ and $\Delta y/\Delta t \to dy/dt$ are finite while $\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2\to 0$, the trailing limit vanishes. What remains is exactly the chain-rule formula. The hypothesis of differentiability (not merely the existence of partials) is what makes the linear increment approximation valid.

Correct. $w_x = 1,\ w_y = 2,\ \partial x/\partial s = t,\ \partial y/\partial s = 1$, so $\partial w/\partial s = 1\cdot t + 2\cdot 1 = t + 2$.

Section 6

The Gradient Vector

Key idea. The gradient $\nabla f$ packages all first partials into a single vector. It points in the direction of steepest increase of $f$, its length is the maximum rate of increase, and it is perpendicular to the level curves and level surfaces of $f$. Almost every multivariable optimization and geometry result flows from these three facts.
Gradient of a scalar field
$$ \nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle \quad (\text{two variables}), \qquad \nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y, f_z \rangle \quad (\text{three variables}). $$

Properties of the gradient. At a point where $\nabla f \ne \mathbf{0}$: (1) $\nabla f$ points in the direction of greatest rate of increase of $f$; (2) the maximum rate of increase equals $|\nabla f|$; (3) $\nabla f$ is orthogonal to the level curve (in 2D) or level surface (in 3D) through that point. The directional derivative in a unit direction $\mathbf{u}$ is the dot product $\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$, which Unit C4 develops in full.

Worked Example 6.1: gradient and steepest-ascent direction

For $f(x,y) = x^2 + 3y^2$, find $\nabla f$ at $(1,1)$, the direction of steepest increase, and the maximum rate of increase.

The partials are $f_x = 2x$ and $f_y = 6y$, so

$$ \nabla f = \langle 2x, 6y\rangle, \qquad \nabla f(1,1) = \langle 2, 6\rangle. $$

The direction of steepest increase is that of $\langle 2,6\rangle$; as a unit vector,

$$ \mathbf{u} = \frac{\langle 2,6\rangle}{\sqrt{4+36}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{40}}\langle 2,6\rangle. $$

The maximum rate of increase is $|\nabla f(1,1)| = \sqrt{2^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{40} = 2\sqrt{10}$.

Going deeper: why the gradient is orthogonal to level curves

Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t)\rangle$ be a curve lying inside a single level curve, so $f(x(t),y(t)) = k$ is constant. Differentiate both sides with respect to $t$ using the chain rule:

$$ \frac{d}{dt} f(x(t),y(t)) = f_x\,x'(t) + f_y\,y'(t) = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = 0. $$

The right side is $0$ because $k$ is constant. Thus $\nabla f$ is orthogonal to the tangent vector $\mathbf{r}'(t)$ of every curve through the point that stays on the level set. Since $\mathbf{r}'(t)$ spans the tangent direction of the level curve, $\nabla f$ is normal to the level curve. The same argument in three variables shows $\nabla f$ is normal to the level surface, which is exactly what gives tangent planes their normal vectors in Unit C4.

Section 7

Going Deeper

Key idea. Existence of all partial derivatives does not imply differentiability or even continuity in several variables. The honest definition of differentiability is the existence of a good linear approximation, the tangent plane, near the point. Understanding this gap is what separates mechanical computation from genuine multivariable analysis.
Differentiability: the linear approximation criterion
$$ f(a+\Delta x, b+\Delta y) = f(a,b) + f_x(a,b)\,\Delta x + f_y(a,b)\,\Delta y + \varepsilon_1\Delta x + \varepsilon_2\Delta y, $$

with $\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2 \to 0$ as $(\Delta x,\Delta y)\to(0,0)$.

Theorem (sufficient condition for differentiability). If $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist and are continuous on an open disk around $(a,b)$, then $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$, and therefore continuous there. Continuous partials are the practical guarantee you almost always rely on.

Worked Example 7.1: partials exist but the function is discontinuous

Consider

$$ f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2}, & (x,y)\ne(0,0) \\[4pt] 0, & (x,y)=(0,0). \end{cases} $$

From the limit definition, $f_x(0,0) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(h,0)-0}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{0}{h} = 0$, and likewise $f_y(0,0)=0$. Both partials exist at the origin.

Yet $f$ is not continuous at the origin: along $y=x$ the value is $\frac{x^2}{2x^2}=\tfrac12 \ne 0 = f(0,0)$. So a function can have all first partials at a point and still fail to be continuous there. Differentiability is strictly stronger than the existence of partials.

Which condition is sufficient to guarantee that $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$?
7.1
$f_x(a,b)$ and $f_y(a,b)$ merely exist
$f$ is continuous at $(a,b)$
The limit of $f$ exists at $(a,b)$
$f_x$ and $f_y$ exist and are continuous near $(a,b)$
Correct. Continuity of the first partials on an open disk around the point is the standard sufficient condition for differentiability.
Mere existence of partials, or continuity of $f$ alone, is not enough. The sufficient condition is that $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist and are continuous near the point.
