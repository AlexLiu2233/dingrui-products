Unit B8: Parametric Equations and Polar Coordinates

Two new ways to describe a curve: as a pair of functions of a parameter, and by radius and angle from the origin. Calculus then carries over to slopes, areas, and arc lengths in both settings.

Section 1

Parametric Curves

Key idea. A parametric curve describes position as a pair of functions of an independent parameter, usually written $t$. Instead of $y$ as a function of $x$, we give $x=f(t)$ and $y=g(t)$. As $t$ runs over an interval, the point $(x,y)$ traces a curve in the plane. This frees us from the vertical-line test, so loops, self-intersections, and full circles are all expressible.

Let $f$ and $g$ be continuous on an interval $I$. The set of points $\bigl(f(t),g(t)\bigr)$ for $t\in I$ is a parametric curve, and the equations $x=f(t)$, $y=g(t)$ are parametric equations. The parameter $t$ often represents time, in which case the curve carries an orientation: the direction of increasing $t$.

Parametric equations
$$x=f(t),\qquad y=g(t),\qquad t\in[a,b].$$

To recover a Cartesian relation we eliminate the parameter: solve one equation for $t$ and substitute, or exploit an identity. For trigonometric parametrizations the Pythagorean identity is the usual tool.

Standard circle and ellipse
$$\text{circle: } x=r\cos t,\; y=r\sin t;\qquad \text{ellipse: } x=a\cos t,\; y=b\sin t.$$

Remark. A curve has many parametrizations. The same unit circle is traced by $(\cos t,\sin t)$ counterclockwise once on $[0,2\pi)$ and by $(\cos 2t,\sin 2t)$ twice as fast on the same interval. The geometric image is identical, but the orientation, speed, and starting point differ, and these matter for arc length and motion.

Worked Example 1.1: eliminating the parameter

Identify the curve $x=t^2-1$, $y=t+2$ for $t\in\mathbb{R}$.

Solve the second equation for the parameter: $t=y-2$. Substitute into the first:

$$x=(y-2)^2-1.$$

This is a parabola opening in the positive $x$ direction with vertex at $(-1,2)$. As $t$ increases, $y=t+2$ increases steadily, so the curve is traced upward along the parabola.

Worked Example 1.2: a trigonometric parametrization

Eliminate the parameter from $x=3\cos t$, $y=2\sin t$, $0\le t\le 2\pi$.

Isolate the trigonometric functions: $\cos t=x/3$ and $\sin t=y/2$. Apply $\cos^2 t+\sin^2 t=1$:

$$\frac{x^2}{9}+\frac{y^2}{4}=1.$$

This is an ellipse with semi-axes $3$ and $2$, traced once counterclockwise starting at $(3,0)$.

Section 2

Calculus with Parametric Curves

Key idea. The slope of a parametric curve is the ratio of the two velocities, $dy/dt$ divided by $dx/dt$, by the chain rule. This gives tangent lines without first eliminating the parameter. Repeating the process on the slope (again dividing by $dx/dt$) yields the second derivative and hence concavity.

If $x=f(t)$ and $y=g(t)$ are differentiable and $dx/dt\neq 0$, the chain rule $dy/dt=(dy/dx)(dx/dt)$ rearranges to give the slope of the tangent line.

First derivative
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt},\qquad \frac{dx}{dt}\neq 0.$$

The curve has a horizontal tangent where $dy/dt=0$ (and $dx/dt\neq 0$) and a vertical tangent where $dx/dt=0$ (and $dy/dt\neq 0$). For the second derivative, differentiate the slope $dy/dx$ with respect to $t$ and divide again by $dx/dt$.

Second derivative
$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{\dfrac{d}{dt}\!\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)}{dx/dt}.$$

Remark. A common error is to take $d^2y/dx^2$ as $(d^2y/dt^2)/(d^2x/dt^2)$. That is wrong. The second derivative is the $t$-derivative of the slope, divided once more by $dx/dt$.

Worked Example 2.1: tangent to a cycloid arch

For the cycloid $x=t-\sin t$, $y=1-\cos t$, find the slope at $t=\pi/2$.

Differentiate: $dx/dt=1-\cos t$ and $dy/dt=\sin t$. Hence

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{\sin t}{1-\cos t}.$$

At $t=\pi/2$, $\sin t=1$ and $1-\cos t=1-0=1$, so the slope is $1$. The point is $(\pi/2-1,\,1)$.

Worked Example 2.2: concavity from the second derivative

For $x=t^2$, $y=t^3-3t$, find $d^2y/dx^2$ and determine where the curve is concave up.

First $dx/dt=2t$ and $dy/dt=3t^2-3$, so

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{3t^2-3}{2t}=\frac{3}{2}\left(t-\frac{1}{t}\right).$$

Differentiate the slope in $t$: $\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)=\dfrac{3}{2}\left(1+\dfrac{1}{t^2}\right)$. Divide by $dx/dt=2t$:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{\tfrac{3}{2}\bigl(1+t^{-2}\bigr)}{2t}=\frac{3(t^2+1)}{4t^3}.$$

Since $t^2+1>0$, the sign matches that of $t^3$, so the curve is concave up for $t>0$ and concave down for $t<0$.

Section 3

Arc Length with Parametric Curves

Key idea. The arc length element is the same in every setting: $ds=\sqrt{(dx)^2+(dy)^2}$. For a parametric curve we factor out $dt$ instead of $dx$, which gives the speed $\sqrt{(dx/dt)^2+(dy/dt)^2}$ as the integrand. Integrating speed over the parameter interval gives the distance traveled, which is the arc length when the curve is traced once.

Let $x=f(t)$, $y=g(t)$ have continuous derivatives on $[\alpha,\beta]$, and suppose the curve is traced exactly once with no backtracking. The length of a short chord is $\sqrt{(\Delta x)^2+(\Delta y)^2}$; factoring out $\Delta t$ and passing to the limit replaces the difference quotients by derivatives.

Parametric arc length
$$L=\int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt.$$

If $y=F(x)$ is a plain function, parametrize by $x=t$, $y=F(t)$; then $dx/dt=1$ and $dy/dt=F'(t)$, and the formula collapses to the familiar $\int\sqrt{1+(F')^2}\,dx$. The parametric form is strictly more general.

Remark. The integrand is a speed and is therefore nonnegative. If the parametrization retraces part of the curve, the integral counts the distance traveled rather than the geometric length, so always check that the interval traces the curve once.

Worked Example 3.1: circumference of a circle

Find the length of $x=r\cos t$, $y=r\sin t$ for $0\le t\le 2\pi$.

Differentiate: $dx/dt=-r\sin t$ and $dy/dt=r\cos t$. The speed is

$$\sqrt{r^2\sin^2 t+r^2\cos^2 t}=\sqrt{r^2}=r.$$

Therefore $L=\int_0^{2\pi} r\,dt=2\pi r$, the circumference of a circle of radius $r$.

Going deeper: deriving the parametric arc length formula

Partition $[\alpha,\beta]$ as $\alpha=t_0 $$L_n=\sum_{i=1}^n\sqrt{(\Delta x_i)^2+(\Delta y_i)^2}=\sum_{i=1}^n\sqrt{\left(\frac{\Delta x_i}{\Delta t_i}\right)^2+\left(\frac{\Delta y_i}{\Delta t_i}\right)^2}\,\Delta t_i.$$

By the Mean Value Theorem applied to $f$ and $g$ on each subinterval, there are points $t_i^\ast$ and $t_i^{\ast\ast}$ with $\Delta x_i/\Delta t_i=f'(t_i^\ast)$ and $\Delta y_i/\Delta t_i=g'(t_i^{\ast\ast})$. As the mesh tends to zero these sample points coalesce, and continuity of $f'$ and $g'$ makes the sum a Riemann sum for $\sqrt{(f')^2+(g')^2}$. Hence $L_n\to\int_\alpha^\beta\sqrt{(f')^2+(g')^2}\,dt$.

Section 4

Polar Coordinates

Key idea. Polar coordinates locate a point by its distance $r$ from the origin and the angle $\theta$ its ray makes with the positive $x$-axis. Curves that are awkward in $x$ and $y$, such as circles through the origin, spirals, and roses, become simple equations $r=f(\theta)$. The conversion to and from Cartesian coordinates rests on basic right-triangle trigonometry.

A point with polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$ sits at distance $r$ along the ray of angle $\theta$. The conversions follow from the definitions of sine and cosine.

Polar to Cartesian
$$x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta.$$
Cartesian to polar
$$r^2=x^2+y^2,\qquad \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}.$$

Polar coordinates are not unique: $(r,\theta)$ and $(r,\theta+2\pi)$ name the same point, and a negative radius reflects through the origin, so $(-r,\theta)=(r,\theta+\pi)$. The origin is $r=0$ for any $\theta$.

Remark. When solving $\tan\theta=y/x$ you must choose $\theta$ in the correct quadrant by inspecting the signs of $x$ and $y$; the arctangent alone returns only values in $(-\pi/2,\pi/2)$.

Worked Example 4.1: converting a polar equation to Cartesian

Show that $r=2\cos\theta$ is a circle and find its center and radius.

Multiply both sides by $r$ so that the conversions apply: $r^2=2r\cos\theta$. Replace $r^2=x^2+y^2$ and $r\cos\theta=x$:

$$x^2+y^2=2x\quad\Longrightarrow\quad x^2-2x+y^2=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad (x-1)^2+y^2=1.$$

This is a circle of radius $1$ centered at $(1,0)$, passing through the origin.

Worked Example 4.2: a point in polar form

Write the Cartesian point $(-1,1)$ in polar coordinates with $r>0$ and $0\le\theta<2\pi$.

The radius is $r=\sqrt{(-1)^2+1^2}=\sqrt2$. The point lies in the second quadrant ($x<0$, $y>0$), where $\tan\theta=y/x=-1$ gives $\theta=3\pi/4$. Hence the polar coordinates are

$$\left(\sqrt2,\ \tfrac{3\pi}{4}\right).$$

Section 5

Area in Polar Coordinates

Key idea. In polar coordinates the natural area element is a thin circular sector, not a rectangle. A sector of radius $r$ and small central angle $d\theta$ has area $\tfrac12 r^2\,d\theta$. Summing these wedges from $\theta=\alpha$ to $\theta=\beta$ gives the area swept out by the polar curve.

A circular sector of radius $r$ subtending angle $d\theta$ is a fraction $d\theta/(2\pi)$ of the full disk of area $\pi r^2$, hence has area $\tfrac12 r^2\,d\theta$. Letting $r=f(\theta)$ vary gives the polar area integral.

Area enclosed by a polar curve
$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \bigl(f(\theta)\bigr)^2\,d\theta=\frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,d\theta.$$

For the area between two polar curves $r=R(\theta)$ (outer) and $r=\rho(\theta)$ (inner) on $[\alpha,\beta]$, subtract the inner sectors from the outer.

Area between two polar curves
$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \bigl(R(\theta)^2-\rho(\theta)^2\bigr)\,d\theta.$$

Remark. Choosing the limits $\alpha$ and $\beta$ is the crux. Find where the curve passes through the origin ($r=0$) to delimit a single loop, and find intersections by solving the two polar equations simultaneously. Sketching one period prevents double-counting overlapping regions.

Worked Example 5.1: area of one petal of a rose

Find the area of one petal of $r=\cos 2\theta$.

A petal begins and ends where $r=0$, that is $\cos 2\theta=0$. The first petal runs from $2\theta=-\pi/2$ to $2\theta=\pi/2$, so $\theta$ goes from $-\pi/4$ to $\pi/4$. Then

$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos^2 2\theta\,d\theta=\frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{1+\cos 4\theta}{2}\,d\theta.$$ $$=\frac{1}{4}\left[\theta+\frac{\sin 4\theta}{4}\right]_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}=\frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{\pi}{2}+0\right)=\frac{\pi}{8}.$$
Worked Example 5.2: inside one circle and outside another

Find the area inside the circle $r=2\sin\theta$ and outside the circle $r=1$.

The curves meet where $2\sin\theta=1$, that is $\sin\theta=1/2$, giving $\theta=\pi/6$ and $\theta=5\pi/6$. On that interval $2\sin\theta\ge 1$, so $r=2\sin\theta$ is the outer curve and $r=1$ the inner. Then

$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\bigl((2\sin\theta)^2-1^2\bigr)\,d\theta=\frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\bigl(4\sin^2\theta-1\bigr)\,d\theta.$$

Use $4\sin^2\theta=2(1-\cos 2\theta)=2-2\cos 2\theta$, so the integrand is $1-2\cos 2\theta$:

$$A=\frac{1}{2}\Bigl[\theta-\sin 2\theta\Bigr]_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}-\bigl(\sin\tfrac{5\pi}{3}-\sin\tfrac{\pi}{3}\bigr)\right)=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}+\sqrt3\right)=\frac{\pi}{3}+\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.$$

Section 6

Arc Length in Polar Coordinates

Key idea. A polar curve $r=f(\theta)$ is just a parametric curve with parameter $\theta$: $x=f(\theta)\cos\theta$ and $y=f(\theta)\sin\theta$. Feeding these into the parametric arc length integral and simplifying with the product rule collapses everything to a tidy formula involving $r$ and $dr/d\theta$.

Write $x=r\cos\theta$ and $y=r\sin\theta$ with $r=f(\theta)$. Differentiating by the product rule,

Velocity components
$$\frac{dx}{d\theta}=\frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta-r\sin\theta,\qquad \frac{dy}{d\theta}=\frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta+r\cos\theta.$$

Squaring and adding, the cross terms cancel and the Pythagorean identity simplifies the rest, leaving $(dx/d\theta)^2+(dy/d\theta)^2=r^2+(dr/d\theta)^2$. Hence the polar arc length is the following.

Polar arc length
$$L=\int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2+\left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2}\,d\theta.$$

Remark. As a sanity check, the circle $r=a$ has $dr/d\theta=0$, so $L=\int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{a^2}\,d\theta=2\pi a$, the correct circumference.

Worked Example 6.1: length of a spiral

Find the length of the spiral $r=e^{\theta}$ from $\theta=0$ to $\theta=2\pi$.

Here $dr/d\theta=e^{\theta}=r$, so $r^2+(dr/d\theta)^2=e^{2\theta}+e^{2\theta}=2e^{2\theta}$. Then

$$L=\int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{2e^{2\theta}}\,d\theta=\sqrt2\int_0^{2\pi}e^{\theta}\,d\theta=\sqrt2\,\bigl(e^{2\pi}-1\bigr).$$
Going deeper: where $r^2+(dr/d\theta)^2$ comes from

Start from the velocity components above and form the sum of their squares. Abbreviate $r'=dr/d\theta$:

$$\left(\frac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2=r'^2\cos^2\theta-2rr'\cos\theta\sin\theta+r^2\sin^2\theta,$$ $$\left(\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2=r'^2\sin^2\theta+2rr'\sin\theta\cos\theta+r^2\cos^2\theta.$$

Adding, the middle terms $\mp 2rr'\sin\theta\cos\theta$ cancel. Grouping the rest and applying $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ twice,

$$\left(\frac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2=r'^2+r^2.$$

Substituting this into the parametric arc length integral $\int\sqrt{(dx/d\theta)^2+(dy/d\theta)^2}\,d\theta$ gives the polar formula.

Section 7

Conic Sections

Key idea. Parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas are the curves cut from a cone by a plane, and each has a clean focus-directrix description. In Cartesian form they are second-degree equations; in polar form, taken with a focus at the origin, all three share a single equation governed by the eccentricity $e$. This unifies the planetary-orbit applications that motivate the whole subject.

The standard Cartesian forms (centered or with vertex at the origin) are as follows.

Standard conics
$$\text{parabola: } y^2=4px;\qquad \text{ellipse: } \frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1;\qquad \text{hyperbola: } \frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1.$$

The eccentricity $e$ classifies a conic: $e=0$ is a circle, $01$ a hyperbola. With a focus at the origin and directrix at distance $d$, all conics share one polar equation.

Polar equation of a conic (focus at origin)
$$r=\frac{ed}{1+e\cos\theta}.$$

Remark. Replacing $\cos\theta$ by $\sin\theta$, or flipping the sign in the denominator, simply rotates the conic or moves the directrix to a different side. The shape is fixed by $e$; the trigonometric function and sign fix the orientation.
