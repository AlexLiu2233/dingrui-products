Q1EASY5.1 Rotational KinematicsNo Calculator

A wheel rotates at $60~\mathrm{rev/min}$. Its angular speed in $\mathrm{rad/s}$ is

  • (A) $\pi/2$
  • (B) $\pi$
  • (C) $2\pi$
  • (D) $4\pi$
Answer: (C)
$$\omega = 60\,\frac{\text{rev}}{\text{min}} \cdot \frac{2\pi~\text{rad}}{1~\text{rev}} \cdot \frac{1~\text{min}}{60~\text{s}} = 2\pi~\mathrm{rad/s}$$
One revolution per second corresponds to $2\pi~\mathrm{rad/s}$.
Q2EASY5.2 Linear & RotationalNo Calculator

A point on the rim of a disk of radius $0.50~\mathrm{m}$ moves with the disk at angular speed $\omega = 4.0~\mathrm{rad/s}$. The linear speed of that point is

  • (A) $0.50~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $2.0~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $4.0~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $8.0~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (B)
$$v = \omega r = 4.0(0.50) = 2.0~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Q3EASY5.3 TorqueNo Calculator

A force of $20~\mathrm{N}$ is applied perpendicular to the handle of a wrench at a distance $0.30~\mathrm{m}$ from the axis of rotation. The magnitude of the torque about the axis is

  • (A) $0.6~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (B) $6.0~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (C) $60~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (D) $66~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
Answer: (B)
$$\tau = r F \sin\theta = 0.30(20)(1) = 6.0~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$$
Perpendicular force gives the maximum torque ($\sin 90^\circ = 1$).
Q4EASY5.4 Rotational InertiaNo Calculator

The moment of inertia of a thin uniform rod of mass $M$ and length $L$ about an axis through its center, perpendicular to the rod, is

  • (A) $\dfrac{1}{3}ML^2$
  • (B) $\dfrac{1}{12}ML^2$
  • (C) $ML^2$
  • (D) $\dfrac{1}{2}ML^2$
Answer: (B)
Standard table result for a rod about its center. Trap (A) is the rod about its end (also a common reference value, but distinct from the centroidal axis here).
Q5MEDIUM5.1 Constant Angular AccelerationNo Calculator

A wheel initially rotating at $\omega_0 = 2.0~\mathrm{rad/s}$ undergoes a constant angular acceleration $\alpha = 4.0~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$ for $3.0~\mathrm{s}$. Its final angular speed is

  • (A) $6~\mathrm{rad/s}$
  • (B) $8~\mathrm{rad/s}$
  • (C) $12~\mathrm{rad/s}$
  • (D) $14~\mathrm{rad/s}$
Answer: (D)
$$\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t = 2.0 + 4.0(3.0) = 14~\mathrm{rad/s}$$
Trap (C) forgets the initial speed.
Q6MEDIUM5.2 Centripetal AccelerationNo Calculator

A disk of radius $0.20~\mathrm{m}$ rotates about its central axis at constant angular speed $\omega = 10~\mathrm{rad/s}$. The centripetal acceleration of a point on the rim is

  • (A) $2.0~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (B) $5.0~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (C) $20~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (D) $100~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
Answer: (C)
$$a_c = \omega^2 r = (10)^2(0.20) = 20~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
Trap (A) is $\omega r$ (linear speed, not acceleration); (D) drops the radius.
Q7MEDIUM5.3 Torque at an AngleNo Calculator

A force of $30~\mathrm{N}$ is applied at the end of a wrench of length $0.40~\mathrm{m}$, but at an angle of $30^\circ$ to the wrench handle. The magnitude of the torque about the axis is

  • (A) $3.0~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (B) $6.0~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (C) $10.4~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (D) $12~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
Answer: (B)
$$\tau = r F \sin\theta = 0.40(30)\sin 30^\circ = 12(0.5) = 6.0~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$$
Only the component perpendicular to the handle delivers torque. Trap (C) uses $\cos 30^\circ$; (D) drops the angle.
Q8MEDIUM5.4 Parallel-Axis TheoremNo Calculator

A solid uniform disk of mass $M$ and radius $R$ rotates about an axis perpendicular to the disk through a point on its edge. Its moment of inertia about that axis is

  • (A) $\dfrac{1}{2}MR^2$
  • (B) $MR^2$
  • (C) $\dfrac{3}{2}MR^2$
  • (D) $2MR^2$
Answer: (C)
Parallel-axis theorem with $d = R$:
$$I_\text{edge} = I_\text{cm} + M d^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2 + MR^2 = \tfrac{3}{2}MR^2$$
Q9MEDIUM5.5 Static EquilibriumCalculator

A uniform horizontal beam of mass $10~\mathrm{kg}$ and length $4.0~\mathrm{m}$ is hinged at the wall at one end. The far end is supported by a vertical cable. The tension in the cable is

  • (A) $25~\mathrm{N}$
  • (B) $49~\mathrm{N}$
  • (C) $98~\mathrm{N}$
  • (D) $196~\mathrm{N}$
Answer: (B)
Take torques about the hinge; weight acts at the center (lever arm $L/2$):
$$T \cdot L = M g \cdot \frac{L}{2} \;\Longrightarrow\; T = \frac{M g}{2} = \frac{10(9.8)}{2} = 49~\mathrm{N}$$
Trap (C) reports $Mg$ (the cable carrying the full weight — would be true only if the beam were massless and pivoted at the load).
Q10MEDIUM5.6 Newton's 2nd Law (Rotation)No Calculator

A net torque of $6.0~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$ acts on a wheel with moment of inertia $I = 2.0~\mathrm{kg \cdot m^2}$. The wheel's angular acceleration is

  • (A) $1.5~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$
  • (B) $3.0~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$
  • (C) $6.0~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$
  • (D) $12~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$
Answer: (B)
$$\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{6.0}{2.0} = 3.0~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$$
Q11MEDIUM5.1 Angular DisplacementNo Calculator

A wheel starts from rest and undergoes constant angular acceleration $\alpha = 2.0~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$. Its angular displacement after $5.0~\mathrm{s}$ is

  • (A) $10~\mathrm{rad}$
  • (B) $12.5~\mathrm{rad}$
  • (C) $25~\mathrm{rad}$
  • (D) $50~\mathrm{rad}$
Answer: (C)
$$\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)(25) = 25~\mathrm{rad}$$
Trap (A) is $\alpha t$ (final $\omega$, not $\theta$); (D) drops the $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
Q12MEDIUM5.4 Comparing Moments of InertiaNo Calculator

A thin hoop, a solid disk, and a solid sphere all have the same mass $M$ and the same radius $R$, and rotate about axes through their centers. Ranked from greatest to least moment of inertia, they are

  • (A) hoop $>$ disk $>$ sphere
  • (B) sphere $>$ disk $>$ hoop
  • (C) disk $>$ sphere $>$ hoop
  • (D) all three are equal
Answer: (A)
$$I_\text{hoop} = MR^2,\quad I_\text{disk} = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2,\quad I_\text{sphere} = \tfrac{2}{5}MR^2$$
The more concentrated the mass near the axis, the smaller $I$. The hoop has all its mass at radius $R$; the sphere distributes mass throughout the volume, much of it near the axis.
Q13MEDIUM5.6 Disk Angular AccelerationNo Calculator

A net torque $\tau$ acts on a uniform solid disk of mass $M$ and radius $R$ about its central axis. The angular acceleration of the disk is

  • (A) $\dfrac{\tau}{MR^2}$
  • (B) $\dfrac{2\tau}{MR^2}$
  • (C) $\dfrac{\tau}{2MR^2}$
  • (D) $\dfrac{\tau R}{M}$
Answer: (B)
$$\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{\tau}{\tfrac{1}{2}MR^2} = \frac{2\tau}{MR^2}$$
Trap (A) uses $I = MR^2$ (hoop); (C) doubles the half-factor in the wrong place.
Q14HARD5.6 Atwood with Massive PulleyCalculator

Two masses, $m_1 = 4.0~\mathrm{kg}$ and $m_2 = 2.0~\mathrm{kg}$, are connected by a massless string passing over a uniform-disk pulley of mass $M = 4.0~\mathrm{kg}$ and radius $R$. The string does not slip on the pulley. Take $g = 10~\mathrm{m/s^2}$. The acceleration of the masses is closest to

  • (A) $2.0~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (B) $2.5~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (C) $3.3~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (D) $5.0~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
Answer: (B)
No-slip $\Rightarrow a = R\alpha$. The pulley adds an effective "mass" $I/R^2 = M/2$ to the system inertia:
$$a = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)\,g}{m_1 + m_2 + M/2} = \frac{(4 - 2)(10)}{4 + 2 + 2} = \frac{20}{8} = 2.5~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
Trap (C) ignores the pulley's rotational inertia (which would give $20/6 \approx 3.33$).
Q15HARD5.5 Ladder EquilibriumNo Calculator

A uniform ladder of mass $M$ leans at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal against a frictionless vertical wall. The coefficient of static friction between the ladder and the floor is $\mu$. The minimum value of $\theta$ for which the ladder does not slip is given by

  • (A) $\tan\theta = \mu$
  • (B) $\tan\theta = 2\mu$
  • (C) $\tan\theta = \dfrac{1}{2\mu}$
  • (D) $\tan\theta = \dfrac{1}{\mu}$
Answer: (C)
Wall is frictionless, so $N_w$ is horizontal. Floor: $N_f = Mg$, friction $f \le \mu N_f$ (horizontal). Torques about the floor contact, length $L$, weight at $L/2$:
$$N_w L\sin\theta = M g\,\tfrac{L}{2}\cos\theta \;\Longrightarrow\; N_w = \frac{Mg}{2\tan\theta}$$
Horizontal balance: $f = N_w$. At the slip threshold $f = \mu N_f = \mu M g$:
$$\mu M g = \frac{M g}{2\tan\theta} \;\Longrightarrow\; \tan\theta = \frac{1}{2\mu}$$
Steeper than $\tan\theta = \mu$ (which would apply for a simple block on an incline).
Q16HARD5.4 Composite Rigid BodyNo Calculator

A point mass $m$ is rigidly attached to one end of a uniform rod of mass $M$ and length $L$. The system rotates about an axis through the rod's free end (i.e. the end opposite the point mass), perpendicular to the rod. Its moment of inertia about that axis is

  • (A) $L^2\!\left(\dfrac{M}{3} + m\right)$
  • (B) $L^2\!\left(\dfrac{M}{12} + m\right)$
  • (C) $L^2\!\left(\dfrac{M}{3} + \dfrac{m}{2}\right)$
  • (D) $\dfrac{1}{2}ML^2 + mL^2$
Answer: (A)
Rod about an end (parallel-axis from center): $I_\text{rod, end} = \tfrac{1}{3}ML^2$. Point mass at distance $L$: $mL^2$.
$$I = \tfrac{1}{3}ML^2 + mL^2 = L^2\!\left(\tfrac{M}{3} + m\right)$$
Trap (B) uses the centroidal $\tfrac{1}{12}ML^2$ (wrong axis); (C) halves the point-mass distance.
Q17HARD5.3 Vector TorqueNo Calculator

A force $\vec F = 3\,\hat\jmath~\mathrm{N}$ is applied at the position $\vec r = 2\,\hat\imath~\mathrm{m}$ relative to a fixed pivot. The torque about that pivot is

  • (A) $6\,\hat\imath~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (B) $6\,\hat\jmath~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (C) $6\,\hat k~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
  • (D) $-6\,\hat k~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$
Answer: (C)
$$\vec\tau = \vec r \times \vec F = (2\,\hat\imath) \times (3\,\hat\jmath) = 6\,(\hat\imath \times \hat\jmath) = 6\,\hat k~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$$
Right-hand rule: fingers from $+\hat\imath$ to $+\hat\jmath$ curl in the $+\hat k$ sense. Trap (D) reverses the cross-product order.
Q18HARD5.6 Yo-Yo on Fixed StringNo Calculator

A uniform solid disk of mass $M$ and radius $R$ has a light cord wrapped around it; the upper end of the cord is fixed. The disk is released from rest and falls vertically as the cord unwinds. The downward acceleration of the disk's center is

  • (A) $\dfrac{g}{2}$
  • (B) $\dfrac{2g}{3}$
  • (C) $\dfrac{g}{3}$
  • (D) $g$
Answer: (B)
Translation: $Mg - T = M a$. Rotation about the disk's center: $TR = I\alpha = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2 \alpha$ with $a = R\alpha$ (no slip), so $T = \tfrac{1}{2}M a$. Substitute:
$$M g - \tfrac{1}{2}M a = M a \;\Longrightarrow\; a = \tfrac{2g}{3}$$
The cord-tension term steals $\tfrac{1}{3}$ of $g$ to spin up the disk.

FRQ 1MEDIUM5.6 Massive Pulley + Hanging BlockCalculator

$M = 2.0~\mathrm{kg}$, $R = 0.10~\mathrm{m}$ pulley; $m = 1.0~\mathrm{kg}$ block hanging; cord no-slip; released from rest.

(a) Free-body diagrams.
Block: weight $m g$ down, tension $T$ up.
Pulley: tension $T$ at rim (tangential, producing torque about the axle), axle reaction at the center, gravity $M g$ at center (no torque).
(b) Newton's 2nd law.
$$m g - T = m a \qquad T R = I \alpha = \tfrac{1}{2} M R^2 \alpha$$
No-slip $a = R\alpha$ collapses the rotational equation to $T = \tfrac{1}{2} M a$. Substitute:
$$m g - \tfrac{1}{2} M a = m a \;\Longrightarrow\; a = \frac{m g}{m + M/2} = \frac{1.0(9.8)}{1.0 + 1.0} = 4.9~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
$$T = \tfrac{1}{2} M a = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)(4.9) = 4.9~\mathrm{N}$$
(c) After the block falls $h = 1.0~\mathrm{m}$:
$$v^2 = 2 a h = 2(4.9)(1.0) = 9.8 \;\Longrightarrow\; v \approx 3.13~\mathrm{m/s}$$
$$\omega = \frac{v}{R} = \frac{3.13}{0.10} \approx 31.3~\mathrm{rad/s}$$
(d) Energy check. $m g h$ becomes block KE plus pulley KE with $\omega = v/R$:
$$m g h = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}MR^2\bigr)\!\left(\tfrac{v}{R}\right)^2 = v^2\!\left(\tfrac{m}{2} + \tfrac{M}{4}\right)$$
$$9.8 = v^2 (0.50 + 0.50) = v^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; v = \sqrt{9.8} \approx 3.13~\mathrm{m/s}~\checkmark$$
FRQ 2MEDIUM5.5 Beam-and-Cable EquilibriumCalculator

Horizontal beam $M = 20~\mathrm{kg}$, $L = 4.0~\mathrm{m}$, hinged at wall; cable from far end at $30^\circ$ above horizontal to wall; load $m = 10~\mathrm{kg}$ at far end.

(a) FBD. Beam weight $M g$ at center, load $m g$ at far end (down), cable tension $T$ at far end at $30^\circ$ above horizontal, hinge reaction components $H_x$ (horizontal) and $H_y$ (vertical) at the wall end.
(b) Torque about the hinge. Only the vertical component of $T$ (i.e. $T\sin 30^\circ$) does work against gravity:
$$T \sin 30^\circ \cdot L = M g \cdot \tfrac{L}{2} + m g \cdot L$$
$$T \cdot 0.5 \cdot 4.0 = 20(9.8)(2.0) + 10(9.8)(4.0) \;\Longrightarrow\; 2.0 T = 392 + 392$$
$$T = 392~\mathrm{N}$$
(c) Force balance.
$$\sum F_x = 0\!: \;\; H_x = T \cos 30^\circ = 392(0.866) \approx 339~\mathrm{N}$$
$$\sum F_y = 0\!: \;\; H_y = M g + m g - T \sin 30^\circ = 196 + 98 - 196 = 98~\mathrm{N}$$
(d) Resultant hinge force:
$$|\vec H| = \sqrt{339^2 + 98^2} \approx 353~\mathrm{N},\quad \theta_H = \arctan\!\left(\tfrac{98}{339}\right) \approx 16^\circ~\text{above horizontal}$$
FRQ 3HARD5.4 / 5.6 Pivoted Composite RodCalculator

Rod $M = 0.40~\mathrm{kg}$, $L = 1.20~\mathrm{m}$ with point mass $m = 0.10~\mathrm{kg}$ at one end; pivot at the opposite (light) end. Released from horizontal.

(a) Moment of inertia about the pivot. Rod about end: $\tfrac{1}{3}ML^2$. Point mass at distance $L$: $mL^2$.
$$I = \tfrac{1}{3}(0.40)(1.20)^2 + (0.10)(1.20)^2 = 0.192 + 0.144 = 0.336~\mathrm{kg \cdot m^2}$$
(b) Center of mass from the pivot (rod CM at $L/2$, point mass at $L$):
$$x_\text{cm} = \frac{M(L/2) + m L}{M + m} = \frac{0.40(0.60) + 0.10(1.20)}{0.50} = \frac{0.36}{0.50} = 0.72~\mathrm{m}$$
(c) Initial angular acceleration. At horizontal release, gravity torque about the pivot equals $(M+m) g\,x_\text{cm}$:
$$\tau_0 = (M+m)\,g\,x_\text{cm} = 0.50(9.8)(0.72) \approx 3.53~\mathrm{N \cdot m}$$
$$\alpha_0 = \frac{\tau_0}{I} = \frac{3.53}{0.336} \approx 10.5~\mathrm{rad/s^2}$$
(d) Angular speed when rod has rotated to vertical (point mass straight down). CM falls by $x_\text{cm}$:
$$\tfrac{1}{2} I \omega^2 = (M+m) g\,x_\text{cm} = 3.53~\mathrm{J}$$
$$\omega^2 = \frac{2(3.53)}{0.336} \approx 21 \;\Longrightarrow\; \omega \approx 4.58~\mathrm{rad/s}$$
FRQ 4HARD5.6 Rolling Cylinder on InclineCalculator

Uniform solid cylinder ($M$, $R$) rolls without slipping down an incline of angle $\theta$.

(a) FBD. Weight $Mg$ vertical (components $Mg\sin\theta$ down-slope and $Mg\cos\theta$ into-slope), normal $N$ out of the slope, static friction $f_s$ up the slope (opposes the contact point's tendency to slip backward as the cylinder accelerates down).
(b) Newton's laws.
$$M g\sin\theta - f_s = M a \qquad f_s R = I_\text{cm}\,\alpha = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2 \alpha$$
No-slip $a = R\alpha$ reduces the rotation equation to $f_s = \tfrac{1}{2}M a$.
(c) Solve. Substituting:
$$M g\sin\theta - \tfrac{1}{2}M a = M a \;\Longrightarrow\; a = \tfrac{2}{3}\,g\sin\theta$$
$$f_s = \tfrac{1}{2}M a = \tfrac{1}{3}M g\sin\theta$$
For comparison, a frictionless slide has $a = g\sin\theta$ — rolling costs $\tfrac{1}{3}$ of that, used to spin up the cylinder.
(d) Minimum $\mu_s$ for rolling without slipping. The static-friction constraint $f_s \le \mu_s N$ becomes
$$\tfrac{1}{3} M g\sin\theta \le \mu_s\,(M g\cos\theta) \;\Longrightarrow\; \mu_s \ge \tfrac{1}{3}\tan\theta$$
Steep inclines or low-$\mu_s$ surfaces eventually drive the cylinder to slip.
