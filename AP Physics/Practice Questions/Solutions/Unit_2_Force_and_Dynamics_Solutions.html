Q1EASY 2.1 Newton's First LawNo Calculator

A puck slides on a frictionless horizontal surface with constant velocity. Which of the following must be true?

  • (A) A net force acts on the puck in the direction of motion.
  • (B) The puck experiences no forces at all.
  • (C) The vector sum of all forces on the puck is zero.
  • (D) The puck must be accelerating.
Answer: (C)
Constant velocity means $\vec a = 0$, so by Newton's second law $\sum \vec F = 0$. (A) is the common Aristotelian misconception that "motion requires a force." (B) is wrong — gravity and the normal force still act; they just cancel. (D) contradicts the given constant velocity.
Q2EASY 2.2 Newton's Second LawNo Calculator

A net force of $12~\mathrm{N}$ acts on a $3~\mathrm{kg}$ block. The block's acceleration is

  • (A) $0.25~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (B) $4~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (C) $9~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (D) $36~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
Answer: (B)
$$a = \frac{F_\text{net}}{m} = \frac{12}{3} = 4~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
Trap (A) inverts the formula; (D) multiplies; (C) subtracts.
Q3EASY 2.3 Newton's Third LawNo Calculator

A car pushes a heavier truck along a road, accelerating both. By Newton's third law, the truck pushes back on the car with a force that is

  • (A) larger than the car's force on the truck (because the truck is heavier).
  • (B) smaller than the car's force on the truck (because the car is moving the system).
  • (C) equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the car's force on the truck.
  • (D) equal only when the system has zero acceleration.
Answer: (C)
Third-law pairs are always equal in magnitude and opposite in direction — independent of mass, motion, or acceleration. The fact that both vehicles accelerate is consistent: the unequal accelerations come from different forces on different bodies (car–road traction vs. truck–car contact), not from unequal third-law pairs.
Q4EASY 2.4 Static FrictionNo Calculator

A $5.0~\mathrm{kg}$ block sits at rest on a horizontal surface with $\mu_s = 0.40$. A horizontal force of $12~\mathrm{N}$ is applied. Take $g = 10~\mathrm{m/s^2}$. Which statement best describes the situation?

  • (A) The block accelerates at $2.4~\mathrm{m/s^2}$.
  • (B) The block accelerates at $0.4~\mathrm{m/s^2}$.
  • (C) The block does not move; static friction is $12~\mathrm{N}$ opposing the applied force.
  • (D) The block does not move; static friction is exactly $20~\mathrm{N}$.
Answer: (C)
Maximum static friction is
$$f_{s,\max} = \mu_s\,m g = 0.40(5.0)(10) = 20~\mathrm{N}$$
Since the applied $12~\mathrm{N}$ is below this threshold, the block stays at rest and static friction adjusts to exactly balance the applied force at $12~\mathrm{N}$. Trap (D) reports the maximum value as if it were always active; (A)/(B) compute kinetic motion that doesn't occur.
Q5MEDIUM 2.2 Connected BlocksNo Calculator

Two blocks of masses $m_1 = 2~\mathrm{kg}$ and $m_2 = 3~\mathrm{kg}$ sit in contact on a frictionless horizontal surface. A horizontal force $F = 10~\mathrm{N}$ is applied to $m_1$, pushing both blocks. The contact force between them is

  • (A) $4~\mathrm{N}$
  • (B) $6~\mathrm{N}$
  • (C) $10~\mathrm{N}$
  • (D) $8.3~\mathrm{N}$
Answer: (B)
Treat the pair as one system to get the common acceleration:
$$a = \frac{F}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{10}{5} = 2~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
The contact force is the only horizontal force on $m_2$, so $F_\text{contact} = m_2\,a = 3(2) = 6~\mathrm{N}$. Trap (C) reports the applied force itself; (A) uses $m_1\,a$ on the wrong block.
Q6MEDIUM 2.6 Inclined PlaneNo Calculator

A block slides down a frictionless $30^\circ$ incline. Take $g = 10~\mathrm{m/s^2}$. Its acceleration along the incline is

  • (A) $5.0~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (B) $8.66~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (C) $10~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (D) $9.8~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
Answer: (A)
$$a = g\sin\theta = 10\sin 30^\circ = 5.0~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
Mass-independent. Trap (B) is $g\cos\theta$ (the normal component, not the slope-parallel one). (C) and (D) ignore the geometry entirely.
Q7MEDIUM 2.4 Static vs. Kinetic FrictionCalculator

A $4.0~\mathrm{kg}$ block sits on a horizontal surface with $\mu_s = 0.30$ and $\mu_k = 0.20$. A horizontal force of $F = 16~\mathrm{N}$ is applied. The block's acceleration is closest to

  • (A) $0$ (the block does not move)
  • (B) $2.04~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (C) $4.0~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
  • (D) $1.04~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
Answer: (B)
First check whether the block moves:
$$f_{s,\max} = \mu_s\,m g = 0.30(4.0)(9.8) = 11.76~\mathrm{N} < F = 16~\mathrm{N}$$
It does. Once moving, friction is kinetic:
$$a = \frac{F - \mu_k\,m g}{m} = \frac{16 - 0.20(4.0)(9.8)}{4.0} = \frac{16 - 7.84}{4} \approx 2.04~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
Trap (C) ignores friction altogether; (D) uses $\mu_s$ in the kinetic equation.
Q8MEDIUM 2.5 Drag & Terminal VelocityNo Calculator

An object falls through air under gravity and a velocity-dependent drag force opposing motion. At terminal velocity, the object's

  • (A) velocity is zero.
  • (B) acceleration is zero.
  • (C) acceleration equals $g$.
  • (D) acceleration equals the drag force divided by its mass.
Answer: (B)
"Terminal velocity" means a steady (terminal) speed; drag has grown until it balances gravity, so $\sum F = 0$ and $a = 0$. Trap (A) confuses zero acceleration with zero velocity. Trap (D) drops the gravity contribution.
Q9MEDIUM 2.7 Spring CombinationsNo Calculator

Two identical springs, each with constant $k$, are connected end-to-end (in series). The equivalent spring constant of the combination is

  • (A) $2k$
  • (B) $k$
  • (C) $k/2$
  • (D) $k^2/2$
Answer: (C)
$$\frac{1}{k_\text{eq}} = \frac{1}{k} + \frac{1}{k} = \frac{2}{k} \;\Longrightarrow\; k_\text{eq} = \frac{k}{2}$$
Series-combined springs are softer than either alone. Trap (A) is the parallel result; (D) has the wrong units.
Q10MEDIUM 2.8 Universal GravitationNo Calculator

Two point masses separated by a distance $r$ feel a gravitational attraction of magnitude $F$. If the separation is doubled to $2r$ (and the masses are unchanged), the new gravitational force is

  • (A) $2F$
  • (B) $F/2$
  • (C) $F/4$
  • (D) $F/\sqrt{2}$
Answer: (C)
$F \propto 1/r^2$, so doubling $r$ divides $F$ by $2^2 = 4$:
$$F_\text{new} = \frac{F}{(2)^2} = \frac{F}{4}$$
Trap (B) treats the law as $1/r$; (D) inverts the wrong power.
Q11MEDIUM 2.9 Circular Motion (Flat Curve)Calculator

A $1200~\mathrm{kg}$ car rounds a flat (unbanked) curve of radius $r = 50~\mathrm{m}$. The static-friction coefficient between tires and road is $\mu_s = 0.50$. The maximum speed without slipping is closest to

  • (A) $9.0~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $15.7~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $22.0~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $49.0~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (B)
At the slip threshold, static friction provides exactly the required centripetal force:
$$\mu_s\,m g = \frac{m\,v_\max^2}{r} \;\Longrightarrow\; v_\max = \sqrt{\mu_s\,g\,r}$$
$$v_\max = \sqrt{0.50 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 50} = \sqrt{245} \approx 15.7~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Mass-independent — trap (C) is $v_\max^2$ (correct quantity but missing the square root).
Q12MEDIUM 2.6 Slip Threshold on InclineNo Calculator

A block sits at rest on an incline that is slowly tilted upward. The static-friction coefficient between block and incline is $\mu_s$. The block first begins to slide when the incline angle satisfies

  • (A) $\tan\theta = \mu_s$
  • (B) $\sin\theta = \mu_s$
  • (C) $\cos\theta = \mu_s$
  • (D) $\theta = 45^\circ$ regardless of $\mu_s$
Answer: (A)
At the slip threshold, the slope-parallel gravity component equals the maximum static friction:
$$m g\sin\theta = \mu_s\,m g\cos\theta \;\Longrightarrow\; \tan\theta = \mu_s$$
Mass cancels. Trap (D) confuses the universal frictionless answer (always slides) with a specific surface.
Q13MEDIUM 2.10 Kepler's Third LawNo Calculator

A satellite orbits Earth at radius $R$ with period $T$. A second satellite at radius $4R$ has period

  • (A) $T$
  • (B) $2T$
  • (C) $4T$
  • (D) $8T$
Answer: (D)
Kepler's third law: $T^2 \propto R^3$, so $T_2/T_1 = (R_2/R_1)^{3/2}$.
$$T_2 = T\,(4)^{3/2} = T\cdot 8 = 8T$$
Trap (C) takes the ratio linearly; (B) takes the square root.
Q14HARD 2.2 Atwood MachineNo Calculator

Two masses with $m_1 < m_2$ hang from opposite ends of a light inextensible string passing over a frictionless, massless pulley. The downward acceleration of $m_2$ is

  • (A) $\dfrac{(m_2 - m_1)\,g}{m_1 + m_2}$
  • (B) $\dfrac{(m_2 - m_1)\,g}{m_2}$
  • (C) $\dfrac{m_2\, g}{m_1 + m_2}$
  • (D) $g$
Answer: (A)
Newton's second law on each mass (with $T$ the common tension, $a$ the common acceleration magnitude):
$$m_2 g - T = m_2 a, \qquad T - m_1 g = m_1 a$$
Adding eliminates $T$:
$$(m_2 - m_1)\,g = (m_1 + m_2)\,a \;\Longrightarrow\; a = \frac{(m_2 - m_1)\,g}{m_1 + m_2}$$
Sanity check: $m_1 = m_2$ gives $a = 0$; $m_1 \to 0$ gives $a \to g$ (free fall of $m_2$). Trap (B) divides by only one mass; (D) ignores the pulley constraint.
Q15HARD 2.5 Linear Drag — Time ConstantCalculator

A particle of mass $m$ falls from rest under gravity with linear drag $F_\mathrm{drag} = -bv$. Its velocity satisfies $v(t) = v_t\bigl(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\bigr)$ with terminal velocity $v_t = mg/b$ and time constant $\tau = m/b$. The time at which the speed reaches $0.9\,v_t$ is closest to

  • (A) $0.5\,\tau$
  • (B) $1.5\,\tau$
  • (C) $2.3\,\tau$
  • (D) $9\,\tau$
Answer: (C)
Set $v(t) = 0.9\,v_t$:
$$0.9 = 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \;\Longrightarrow\; e^{-t/\tau} = 0.1 \;\Longrightarrow\; t = \tau\ln 10 \approx 2.30\,\tau$$
Useful landmark: $0.63 \approx 1\,\tau$, $0.86 \approx 2\,\tau$, $0.95 \approx 3\,\tau$.
Q16HARD 2.9 Conical PendulumNo Calculator

A bob of mass $m$ swings in a horizontal circle at the end of a string of length $\ell$ that makes a fixed angle $\theta$ with the vertical (a conical pendulum). The angular speed satisfies

  • (A) $\omega^2 = g\,\ell\,\sin\theta$
  • (B) $\omega^2 = g/\ell$
  • (C) $\omega^2 = \dfrac{g}{\ell\,\cos\theta}$
  • (D) $\omega^2 = \dfrac{g\,\cos\theta}{\ell}$
Answer: (C)
Let $T$ be the string tension and $r = \ell\sin\theta$ the radius of the horizontal circle. Vertical and radial Newton's laws:
$$T\cos\theta = m g, \qquad T\sin\theta = m\,\omega^2 r = m\,\omega^2 \ell\sin\theta$$
The radial equation gives $T = m\omega^2\ell$. Substitute into the vertical:
$$m\omega^2\ell\cos\theta = m g \;\Longrightarrow\; \omega^2 = \frac{g}{\ell\cos\theta}$$
Sanity check: $\theta \to 0$ recovers the small-angle pendulum frequency $\sqrt{g/\ell}$; $\theta \to 90^\circ$ pushes $\omega \to \infty$ as the string approaches horizontal.
Q17HARD 2.6 Incline + Applied ForceCalculator

A $5.0~\mathrm{kg}$ block rests on a frictionless incline of angle $\theta = 25^\circ$. A force $F = 30~\mathrm{N}$ is applied parallel to the slope, directed up the slope. The block's acceleration along the incline is closest to

  • (A) $1.86~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ up the slope
  • (B) $1.86~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ down the slope
  • (C) $4.14~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ up the slope
  • (D) $0$ (block remains at rest)
Answer: (A)
Take up the slope positive. Slope-parallel gravity component is $mg\sin\theta$ down-slope; no friction.
$$a = \frac{F - m g\sin\theta}{m} = \frac{30 - 5(9.8)\sin 25^\circ}{5} = \frac{30 - 20.71}{5} \approx 1.86~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
Positive, so up the slope. Trap (C) accidentally uses $\cos\theta$.
Q18HARD 2.8 Escape vs. Orbital SpeedCalculator

The escape speed from a planet's surface is $v_e = \sqrt{2GM/R}$. Compared with the circular orbital speed $v_o = \sqrt{GM/R}$ at radius $R$, the escape speed satisfies

  • (A) $v_e = v_o$
  • (B) $v_e = \sqrt{2}\,v_o$
  • (C) $v_e = 2\,v_o$
  • (D) $v_e = v_o^2$
Answer: (B)
$$\frac{v_e}{v_o} = \frac{\sqrt{2GM/R}}{\sqrt{GM/R}} = \sqrt{2}$$
So $v_e \approx 1.41\,v_o$ — escape is only ~41% faster than orbit, not double. (D) has the wrong units.

FRQ 1MEDIUM 2.2 / 2.4 Atwood with Table FrictionCalculator

$m_1 = 2.0~\mathrm{kg}$ on a horizontal table with $\mu_k = 0.30$; string over a frictionless pulley to hanging $m_2 = 4.0~\mathrm{kg}$. Released from rest.

(a) Free-body diagrams and Newton's second law.
$m_1$ (on the table): weight $m_1 g$ down, normal $N$ up, tension $T$ toward the pulley, kinetic friction $f_k = \mu_k N$ opposite the motion.
$m_2$ (hanging): weight $m_2 g$ down, tension $T$ up.
$$T - \mu_k m_1 g = m_1 a \qquad m_2 g - T = m_2 a$$
Vertical equation for $m_1$ gives $N = m_1 g$.
(b) System acceleration. Add the two equations:
$$m_2 g - \mu_k m_1 g = (m_1 + m_2)\,a$$
$$a = \frac{(m_2 - \mu_k m_1)\,g}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{(4.0 - 0.30 \cdot 2.0)(9.8)}{6.0} = \frac{3.4 \cdot 9.8}{6.0} \approx 5.55~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
(c) Tension. From the hanging-mass equation:
$$T = m_2(g - a) = 4.0(9.8 - 5.55) \approx 17.0~\mathrm{N}$$
Cross-check via the table equation: $T = m_1(a + \mu_k g) = 2.0(5.55 + 2.94) \approx 17.0~\mathrm{N}$. ✓
(d) Kinetic energy of $m_1$ after $1.5~\mathrm{s}$. From rest with constant $a$,
$$v = a t = 5.55(1.5) \approx 8.32~\mathrm{m/s}$$
$$K_1 = \tfrac{1}{2} m_1 v^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)(8.32)^2 \approx 69.2~\mathrm{J}$$
FRQ 2MEDIUM 2.6 / 2.4 Incline with Friction & Applied ForceCalculator

$m = 4.0~\mathrm{kg}$ on an incline at $\theta = 30^\circ$, $\mu_k = 0.25$. Applied force $F = 25~\mathrm{N}$ up the slope.

(a) Free-body diagram, slope-aligned axes.
Forces on block: weight $m g$ vertical (components $m g\sin\theta$ down-slope, $m g\cos\theta$ into-slope), normal $N$ out of slope, applied $F$ up-slope, kinetic friction $f_k = \mu_k N$ opposing motion.
(b) Normal force. Perpendicular balance:
$$N = m g\cos\theta = 4.0(9.8)\cos 30^\circ \approx 33.9~\mathrm{N}$$
(c) Acceleration assuming up-slope motion.
$$a = \frac{F - m g\sin\theta - \mu_k N}{m} = \frac{25 - 4.0(9.8)(0.5) - 0.25(33.9)}{4.0}$$
$$a = \frac{25 - 19.6 - 8.49}{4.0} = \frac{-3.09}{4.0} \approx -0.77~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
Negative means the assumed direction of motion (up-slope) is inconsistent — the applied force cannot overcome gravity-along-slope plus the friction that would oppose up-slope motion. Conversely, the slope-parallel gravity component ($19.6~\mathrm{N}$) is also less than $F = 25~\mathrm{N}$, so the block doesn't slide down either. With $\mu_s \ge \mu_k$ the block remains static and $a = 0$.
(d) $F$ for constant up-slope velocity. With $a = 0$ and motion up-slope, friction is fully kinetic and opposes motion:
$$F = m g\sin\theta + \mu_k N = 19.6 + 8.49 \approx 28.1~\mathrm{N}$$
Just $3~\mathrm{N}$ more than the given value — explaining why part (c)'s assumed motion didn't take off.
FRQ 3HARD 2.5 Linear Drag — DerivationNo Calculator

Particle of mass $m$ falls from rest through a fluid with $\vec F_\mathrm{drag} = -b\vec v$. Downward positive.

(a) Newton's second law with downward as $+$:
$$m\,\frac{dv}{dt} = m g - b v \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \frac{dv}{dt} = g - \frac{b}{m}\,v$$
(b) Terminal velocity. At $v = v_t$, $dv/dt = 0$:
$$0 = g - \frac{b}{m}\,v_t \;\Longrightarrow\; v_t = \frac{m g}{b}$$
(c) Solve the ODE. Define $\tau = m/b$; then $dv/dt = (v_t - v)/\tau$. Separate:
$$\frac{dv}{v_t - v} = \frac{dt}{\tau} \;\Longrightarrow\; -\ln(v_t - v) = \frac{t}{\tau} + C$$
Use $v(0) = 0$: $C = -\ln v_t$. Then
$$\ln\!\left(\frac{v_t - v}{v_t}\right) = -\frac{t}{\tau} \;\Longrightarrow\; v(t) = v_t\bigl(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\bigr), \quad \tau = \frac{m}{b}$$
(d) Sketch.
$v(t)$ starts at $0$ with initial slope $g$, curves over, and approaches $v_t$ asymptotically as $t \to \infty$. The point $(\tau,\,0.63\,v_t)$ sits on the curve; the asymptote at $v = v_t$ is a horizontal dashed line above. By $4\tau$ the curve is within $\approx 2\%$ of $v_t$.
FRQ 4HARD 2.9 Vertical Loop — Centripetal & EnergyCalculator

Frictionless track with a vertical loop of radius $R$; block enters the bottom moving horizontally with speed $v_0$.

(a) Minimum entry speed. At the very top of the loop, gravity points toward the center; the normal force vanishes at the threshold $N = 0$, leaving gravity to supply all the centripetal force:
$$m g = \frac{m\,v_\text{top,min}^2}{R} \;\Longrightarrow\; v_\text{top,min}^2 = g R$$
Energy conservation from entry (height $0$) to top (height $2R$) for a frictionless track:
$$\tfrac{1}{2} m v_{0,\min}^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} m v_\text{top,min}^2 + m g (2R)$$
$$v_{0,\min}^2 = g R + 4 g R = 5 g R \;\Longrightarrow\; v_{0,\min} = \sqrt{5 g R}$$
(b) Speed at the top for $v_0 = 2\,v_{0,\min}$. Then $v_0^2 = 4(5 g R) = 20\,g R$. Energy conservation:
$$\tfrac{1}{2} v_0^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} v_\text{top}^2 + g(2R)$$
$$v_\text{top}^2 = 20 g R - 4 g R = 16 g R \;\Longrightarrow\; v_\text{top} = 4\sqrt{g R}$$
(c) Normal force at the top. Both $N$ and gravity point toward the center (downward), so
$$N + m g = \frac{m\,v_\text{top}^2}{R} = \frac{m (16 g R)}{R} = 16\,m g$$
$$N = 16 m g - m g = 15\,m g$$
(d) Bottom vs. top.
The normal force at the bottom is larger than at the top. Two effects compound: (i) at the bottom, the normal force must both support gravity and supply the centripetal acceleration — they point opposite ways, so $N_\text{bot} - m g = m v_\text{bot}^2/R$, i.e. $N = m g + m v_\text{bot}^2/R$ (gravity now adds to $N$ rather than helping); and (ii) the block is moving faster at the bottom by energy conservation, so $v_\text{bot}^2/R$ itself is larger than $v_\text{top}^2/R$.
