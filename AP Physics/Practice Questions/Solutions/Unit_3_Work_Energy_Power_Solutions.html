Q1EASY 3.1 Kinetic EnergyNo Calculator

A $2.0~\mathrm{kg}$ block moves at $6.0~\mathrm{m/s}$. Its kinetic energy is

  • (A) $6~\mathrm{J}$
  • (B) $12~\mathrm{J}$
  • (C) $24~\mathrm{J}$
  • (D) $36~\mathrm{J}$
Answer: (D)
$$K = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)(6.0)^2 = 36~\mathrm{J}$$
Trap (B) forgets to square $v$; (C) uses $mv$ (momentum-like, wrong factor of $\tfrac{1}{2}$).
Q2EASY 3.2 Work at an AngleNo Calculator

A constant force of magnitude $F = 50~\mathrm{N}$ pulls a block through a displacement of $d = 4.0~\mathrm{m}$. The angle between the force and the displacement is $60^\circ$. The work done by the force is

  • (A) $50~\mathrm{J}$
  • (B) $100~\mathrm{J}$
  • (C) $173~\mathrm{J}$
  • (D) $200~\mathrm{J}$
Answer: (B)
$$W = F\,d\cos\theta = 50(4.0)\cos 60^\circ = 200(0.5) = 100~\mathrm{J}$$
Trap (D) drops the cosine; (C) uses $\cos 30^\circ$ (i.e., the complement of the given angle).
Q3EASY 3.3 Average PowerNo Calculator

A motor performs $600~\mathrm{J}$ of work over $30~\mathrm{s}$. Its average power output is

  • (A) $0.05~\mathrm{W}$
  • (B) $20~\mathrm{W}$
  • (C) $600~\mathrm{W}$
  • (D) $18{,}000~\mathrm{W}$
Answer: (B)
$$P_\text{avg} = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{600}{30} = 20~\mathrm{W}$$
Trap (D) multiplies by $t$; (A) inverts the ratio.
Q4EASY 3.4 Spring Potential EnergyNo Calculator

A spring with $k = 200~\mathrm{N/m}$ is compressed $0.10~\mathrm{m}$ from its natural length. The elastic potential energy stored in the spring is

  • (A) $0.5~\mathrm{J}$
  • (B) $1.0~\mathrm{J}$
  • (C) $2.0~\mathrm{J}$
  • (D) $10~\mathrm{J}$
Answer: (B)
$$U_s = \tfrac{1}{2} k (\Delta x)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(200)(0.10)^2 = 1.0~\mathrm{J}$$
Trap (C) drops the factor of $\tfrac{1}{2}$; (D) forgets to square $\Delta x$.
Q5MEDIUM 3.2 Work–Energy TheoremNo Calculator

A $5.0~\mathrm{kg}$ block moving at $10~\mathrm{m/s}$ along a horizontal surface is brought to rest by friction alone. The total work done on the block by friction is

  • (A) $+250~\mathrm{J}$
  • (B) $-250~\mathrm{J}$
  • (C) $+500~\mathrm{J}$
  • (D) $-500~\mathrm{J}$
Answer: (B)
Work–energy theorem: $W_\text{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i$. Friction is the only horizontal force doing work, so
$$W_f = 0 - \tfrac{1}{2}(5.0)(10)^2 = -250~\mathrm{J}$$
The minus sign reflects that friction opposes the motion (removes KE). Trap (A) gets the magnitude right but the sign wrong; (D) drops the factor of $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
Q6MEDIUM 3.4 Force from Potential EnergyNo Calculator

A particle moves along the $x$-axis under a conservative potential $U(x) = 3x^2 - 6x + 2$ (J, with $x$ in m). The force on the particle at $x = 1~\mathrm{m}$ is

  • (A) $0$
  • (B) $-1~\mathrm{N}$
  • (C) $+1~\mathrm{N}$
  • (D) $+6~\mathrm{N}$
Answer: (A)
$$F(x) = -\frac{dU}{dx} = -(6x - 6) = 6 - 6x$$
$$F(1) = 6 - 6(1) = 0$$
$x = 1$ is the minimum of $U$ (stable equilibrium), so the force vanishes there. Trap (D) reports $|dU/dx|$ at the wrong point; the sign trap returns $\pm 1$ if $\theta$ is shifted by one unit.
Q7MEDIUM 3.5 Frictionless RampNo Calculator

A $1.0~\mathrm{kg}$ block is released from rest on a frictionless ramp at height $h = 5.0~\mathrm{m}$ above the bottom. Take $g = 10~\mathrm{m/s^2}$. Its speed at the bottom of the ramp is

  • (A) $5~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $10~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $25~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $50~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (B)
Energy conservation: $m g h = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2 g h}$.
$$v = \sqrt{2(10)(5)} = \sqrt{100} = 10~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Mass-independent. Trap (C) reports $v^2$; (D) reports $2gh$.
Q8MEDIUM 3.2 Variable Force IntegralCalculator

A force $F(x) = 3x^2 + 2x$ (in N, with $x$ in m) acts on a particle as it moves along the $x$-axis. The work done by this force as the particle moves from $x = 0$ to $x = 2~\mathrm{m}$ is

  • (A) $4~\mathrm{J}$
  • (B) $8~\mathrm{J}$
  • (C) $12~\mathrm{J}$
  • (D) $16~\mathrm{J}$
Answer: (C)
$$W = \int_0^2 (3x^2 + 2x)\,dx = \bigl[x^3 + x^2\bigr]_0^2 = 8 + 4 = 12~\mathrm{J}$$
Trap (D) evaluates $F(2) \cdot 2$ (treating $F$ as constant at its endpoint value).
Q9MEDIUM 3.4 Equilibrium StabilityNo Calculator

A particle moves under the potential $U(x) = x^3 - 3x$ (J, with $x$ in m). Which statement about its equilibria is correct?

  • (A) $x = +1$ is a stable equilibrium; $x = -1$ is unstable.
  • (B) $x = -1$ is a stable equilibrium; $x = +1$ is unstable.
  • (C) Both $x = \pm 1$ are stable.
  • (D) Both $x = \pm 1$ are unstable.
Answer: (A)
Equilibria from $dU/dx = 3x^2 - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$. Classify with the second derivative $d^2 U/dx^2 = 6x$:
$$x = +1\!: \;\; \tfrac{d^2 U}{dx^2} = +6 > 0~(\text{stable})$$
$$x = -1\!: \;\; \tfrac{d^2 U}{dx^2} = -6 < 0~(\text{unstable})$$
Stable equilibria sit at local minima of $U$; unstable at local maxima. Trap (B) flips the sign convention.
Q10MEDIUM 3.5 Spring LauncherNo Calculator

A spring with $k = 200~\mathrm{N/m}$ is compressed by $0.30~\mathrm{m}$ and used to launch a $0.50~\mathrm{kg}$ block on a frictionless surface. The block's speed after release is

  • (A) $3~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $6~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $9~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $12~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (B)
Energy conservation: $\tfrac{1}{2} k (\Delta x)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2$.
$$v = \Delta x\sqrt{k/m} = 0.30\sqrt{200/0.50} = 0.30(20) = 6.0~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Trap (D) misses the factor $\sqrt{m}$ in the denominator.
Q11MEDIUM 3.3 Instantaneous Power on InclineCalculator

A $1500~\mathrm{kg}$ car climbs a $5^\circ$ incline at constant velocity $v = 20~\mathrm{m/s}$. Drag is negligible. The instantaneous mechanical power delivered by the engine is closest to

  • (A) $26~\mathrm{kW}$
  • (B) $76~\mathrm{kW}$
  • (C) $147~\mathrm{kW}$
  • (D) $293~\mathrm{kW}$
Answer: (A)
Constant velocity means engine force balances slope-parallel gravity: $F = m g \sin\theta$. Then
$$P = F v = m g \sin\theta\,v = 1500(9.8)\sin 5^\circ\,(20)$$
$$P \approx 1500(9.8)(0.0872)(20) \approx 25.6~\mathrm{kW}$$
Trap (C) uses $m g v$ (no $\sin\theta$, i.e. treats road as vertical).
Q12MEDIUM 3.5 Conservative vs. Non-ConservativeNo Calculator

Which of the following forces is not conservative?

  • (A) Gravity (near Earth's surface)
  • (B) Ideal spring force
  • (C) Universal gravitation
  • (D) Kinetic friction
Answer: (D)
Conservative forces derive from a potential and have path-independent work. Kinetic friction depends on path length (the longer the path, the more energy dissipated), so it cannot be derived from a $U(x)$ — non-conservative. The other three each have a standard potential ($U = mgh$, $U = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2$, $U = -GMm/r$).
Q13MEDIUM 3.1 KE ScalingNo Calculator

A car's speed is doubled. Its kinetic energy

  • (A) halves
  • (B) stays the same
  • (C) doubles
  • (D) quadruples
Answer: (D)
$K = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2$, so $K$ scales as $v^2$. Doubling $v$ multiplies $K$ by $4$. Important consequence for safety: braking distance also scales as $v^2$ (constant friction force absorbs $\Delta K$ over the stopping distance).
Q14HARD 3.2 Line Integral of $\vec F \cdot d\vec r$No Calculator

A particle moves along the straight line from $(0, 0)$ to $(2, 3)$ (in metres) under the force $\vec F = 2y\,\hat\imath + 3x\,\hat\jmath$ (in N). The work done by $\vec F$ along this path is

  • (A) $6~\mathrm{J}$
  • (B) $12~\mathrm{J}$
  • (C) $15~\mathrm{J}$
  • (D) $30~\mathrm{J}$
Answer: (C)
Parameterize the line: $x(t) = 2t$, $y(t) = 3t$, $t \in [0, 1]$. Then $dx = 2\,dt$, $dy = 3\,dt$, and
$$\vec F \cdot d\vec r = (2y)\,dx + (3x)\,dy = (6t)(2)\,dt + (6t)(3)\,dt = 30\,t\,dt$$
$$W = \int_0^1 30 t\,dt = 30 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} = 15~\mathrm{J}$$
Trap (D) drops the factor of $\tfrac{1}{2}$ from the integral.
Q15HARD 3.4 Stable EquilibriumNo Calculator

A particle moves along the $x$-axis under the potential $U(x) = x^3 - 9x$ (J, with $x$ in m). The locations of the particle's stable equilibrium are

  • (A) $x = -\sqrt{3}~\mathrm{m}$ only
  • (B) $x = +\sqrt{3}~\mathrm{m}$ only
  • (C) $x = \pm\sqrt{3}~\mathrm{m}$
  • (D) $x = 0$
Answer: (B)
$dU/dx = 3x^2 - 9 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm\sqrt{3}$. Classify via $d^2 U/dx^2 = 6x$:
$$x = +\sqrt{3}\!: \;\; 6\sqrt{3} > 0~(\text{stable})$$
$$x = -\sqrt{3}\!: \;\; -6\sqrt{3} < 0~(\text{unstable})$$
Trap (C) lumps both equilibria as the same type.
Q16HARD 3.3 Constant-Power AccelerationNo Calculator

A car of mass $m$ starts from rest on a flat road and is driven by an engine that delivers constant mechanical power $P$. Drag is neglected. The car's speed at time $t$ is

  • (A) $v(t) = \sqrt{Pt/m}$
  • (B) $v(t) = \sqrt{2Pt/m}$
  • (C) $v(t) = 2Pt/m$
  • (D) $v(t) = \bigl(Pt/m\bigr)^{2}$
Answer: (B)
Constant $P$ means $dK/dt = P$, so $K(t) = Pt$ from rest. Then $\tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = P t$, giving $v = \sqrt{2Pt/m}$. Trap (A) drops the factor of 2; (C) inverts the square root, returning a quantity with units of length per time per time.
Q17HARD 3.5 Loop-the-LoopCalculator

A small block is released from rest at height $h$ on a frictionless track that leads into a vertical loop of radius $R$. The minimum value of $h$ such that the block maintains contact with the track at the top of the loop is

  • (A) $h = R$
  • (B) $h = 2R$
  • (C) $h = \dfrac{5R}{2}$
  • (D) $h = 3R$
Answer: (C)
Centripetal condition at the top with $N = 0$: $m g = m v_\text{top}^2/R$, so $v_\text{top}^2 = g R$. Energy conservation from height $h$ to the top (height $2R$):
$$m g h = m g (2R) + \tfrac{1}{2} m v_\text{top}^2 = 2 m g R + \tfrac{1}{2} m g R$$
$$h = 2R + \tfrac{R}{2} = \frac{5R}{2}$$
Trap (B) forgets the kinetic-energy term at the top.
Q18HARD 3.3 Pump PowerCalculator

A pump lifts water from a well of depth $h = 30~\mathrm{m}$ and delivers it to ground level at a steady rate of $5.0~\mathrm{kg/s}$. Assuming the water is delivered with negligible exit speed, the minimum mechanical power the pump must supply is closest to

  • (A) $150~\mathrm{W}$
  • (B) $250~\mathrm{W}$
  • (C) $1{,}470~\mathrm{W}$
  • (D) $6{,}000~\mathrm{W}$
Answer: (C)
Energy raised per second equals power. With $dm/dt$ kg lifted height $h$ per second:
$$P = \frac{dm}{dt}\,g\,h = 5.0(9.8)(30) = 1470~\mathrm{W}$$
Negligible exit KE means all the input work becomes gravitational PE. Trap (A) drops a factor of $g$; (D) treats $dm/dt$ as mass and uses $mgh$ once.

FRQ 1MEDIUM 3.5 Energy BookkeepingCalculator

$m = 0.50~\mathrm{kg}$ released from rest at $h = 1.2~\mathrm{m}$ on a frictionless curved ramp; floor then frictionless to a spring ($k = 600~\mathrm{N/m}$); on return trip floor has $\mu_k = 0.20$.

(a) Speed at the bottom of the ramp. Energy conservation $m g h = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2$:
$$v = \sqrt{2 g h} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(1.2)} = \sqrt{23.52} \approx 4.85~\mathrm{m/s}$$
(b) Maximum spring compression. All KE → spring PE: $\tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} k x_\max^2$, equivalently $m g h = \tfrac{1}{2} k x_\max^2$:
$$x_\max = \sqrt{\frac{2 m g h}{k}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(0.50)(9.8)(1.2)}{600}} = \sqrt{0.0196} \approx 0.140~\mathrm{m}$$
(c) Distance on the return trip before friction stops the block. The spring returns all $5.88~\mathrm{J}$ of stored PE to KE, so the block leaves the spring with the same speed $4.85~\mathrm{m/s}$. On the new (frictioned) floor, work–energy theorem with $W_f = -\mu_k m g\,d$:
$$\tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \mu_k m g\,d \;\Longrightarrow\; d = \frac{v^2}{2\mu_k g} = \frac{23.52}{2(0.20)(9.8)} = \frac{23.52}{3.92} = 6.0~\mathrm{m}$$
(d) Fraction of original mechanical energy dissipated. The block ends at rest on the floor (gravitational PE = 0 relative to its end state), so all of the original $m g h = 5.88~\mathrm{J}$ is gone to friction — fraction $= 1.00$ (100%). The spring/return-trip path simply specifies where the dissipation happens, not how much.
FRQ 2MEDIUM 3.4 Potential Energy DiagramCalculator

$m = 2.0~\mathrm{kg}$ on the $x$-axis under $U(x) = \tfrac{1}{4} x^4 - 2 x^2 + 3$ (J, $x$ in m).

(a) Force.
$$F(x) = -\frac{dU}{dx} = -(x^3 - 4x) = 4x - x^3$$
(b) Equilibria and classification. Set $dU/dx = x^3 - 4x = x(x^2 - 4) = 0$, giving $x = 0,\,\pm 2$. Then $d^2 U/dx^2 = 3 x^2 - 4$:
$$x = 0\!: \;\; -4~(\text{unstable, local max})$$
$$x = \pm 2\!: \;\; 8~(\text{stable, local min})$$
The double-well $U(x)$ has $U(\pm 2) = 4 - 8 + 3 = -1~\mathrm{J}$ and $U(0) = 3~\mathrm{J}$.
(c) Released from rest at $x = 0$. The energy is $E = U(0) = 3~\mathrm{J}$. Solving $U(x) = E$: $\tfrac{1}{4}x^4 - 2x^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x^2(x^2 - 8) = 0$, so the classical turning points are $x = 0$ and $x = \pm 2\sqrt{2}$.
Strictly, the particle sits at an unstable equilibrium and remains at rest there for all time (zero force, zero velocity). With any infinitesimal perturbation, it slides toward one of the wells; from energy conservation the motion is then bounded between $x = 0$ and $x = +2\sqrt{2}$ (or its mirror), with the orbit through $x = 0$ taking infinite time.
(d) Released from rest at $x = 3$. Now $E = U(3) = \tfrac{81}{4} - 18 + 3 = 5.25~\mathrm{J}$. Speed at $x = 0$ from $E = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 + U(0)$:
$$\tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)\,v^2 = 5.25 - 3 = 2.25 \;\Longrightarrow\; v = \sqrt{2.25} = 1.5~\mathrm{m/s}$$
FRQ 3HARD 3.2 Variable Friction CoefficientCalculator

$m = 4.0~\mathrm{kg}$ slides along a horizontal surface with $\mu_k(x) = 0.10 + 0.05 x$ (x in m); enters $x = 0$ at $v_0 = 5.0~\mathrm{m/s}$.

(a) Friction force magnitude.
$$f(x) = \mu_k(x)\,m g = (0.10 + 0.05 x)(4.0)(9.8) = 3.92 + 1.96\,x~~(\mathrm{N})$$
(b) Work by friction (opposes motion):
$$W_f = -\int_0^d f(x)\,dx = -\int_0^d (3.92 + 1.96\,x)\,dx$$
(c) Evaluate at $d = 4.0~\mathrm{m}$:
$$W_f = -\bigl[3.92 x + 0.98 x^2\bigr]_0^{4} = -\bigl(15.68 + 15.68\bigr) = -31.36~\mathrm{J}$$
(d) Speed at $x = 4~\mathrm{m}$. Initial KE: $\tfrac{1}{2}(4.0)(5.0)^2 = 50~\mathrm{J} > |W_f|$, so the block reaches $x = 4$ still moving. Work–energy theorem:
$$\tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = K_0 + W_f = 50 - 31.36 = 18.64~\mathrm{J}$$
$$v = \sqrt{2(18.64)/4.0} = \sqrt{9.32} \approx 3.05~\mathrm{m/s}$$
FRQ 4HARD 3.3 Constant-Power CarCalculator

$m = 1200~\mathrm{kg}$, constant engine power $P = 60~\mathrm{kW}$ from $t = 0$ from rest; no drag.

(a) ODE. Engine force is $F = P/v$ (since $P = Fv$), so
$$m\,\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{P}{v}$$
(b) Separate variables: $m\,v\,dv = P\,dt$. Integrate from $v(0) = 0$:
$$\tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = P t \;\Longrightarrow\; v(t) = \sqrt{2 P t / m}$$
(Same identity as the constant-power result $K(t) = P t$ from rest, dressed in calculus.)
(c) Time to reach $v = 25~\mathrm{m/s}$:
$$25 = \sqrt{\frac{2(60000)\,t}{1200}} = \sqrt{100\,t} \;\Longrightarrow\; t = \frac{625}{100} = 6.25~\mathrm{s}$$
(d) Position by integrating $v(t)$:
$$x(t) = \int_0^t \sqrt{2 P \tau / m}\,d\tau = \sqrt{2 P / m}\;\tfrac{2}{3}\,t^{3/2} = \tfrac{2}{3}\sqrt{2 P / m}\;t^{3/2}$$
At $t = 10~\mathrm{s}$ with $\sqrt{2 P / m} = \sqrt{120000/1200} = 10~\mathrm{m^{1/2}/s^{1/2}}$:
$$x(10) = \tfrac{2}{3}(10)(10)^{3/2} = \tfrac{20}{3}\,\bigl(10\sqrt{10}\bigr) \approx 210.8~\mathrm{m}$$
