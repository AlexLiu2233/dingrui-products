Q1EASY 1.1 Scalars and VectorsNo Calculator

Which pair lists one scalar and one vector, in that order?

  • (A) velocity, speed
  • (B) distance, displacement
  • (C) displacement, distance
  • (D) acceleration, force
Answer: (B)
Distance is a path length (scalar, sign-free); displacement is a vector with magnitude and direction. (A) reverses the order. (C) flips them. (D) lists two vectors.
Q2EASY 1.2 Position, Velocity, AccelerationNo Calculator

A particle's position is $x(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 5$ (SI units). Its velocity at $t=2~\mathrm{s}$ is

  • (A) $-12~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $0~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $6~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $12~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (B)
$$v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 12$$
$$v(2) = 6(2) - 12 = 0~\mathrm{m/s}$$
The particle is momentarily at rest at $t=2~\mathrm{s}$ (a turning point of $x(t)$). Choice (A) is $v(0)$; (D) is $v(4)$.
Q3EASY 1.2 Position, Velocity, AccelerationNo Calculator

For the same particle as Q2, its acceleration is

  • (A) zero everywhere
  • (B) $6~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ constant
  • (C) $-12~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ constant
  • (D) $6t~\mathrm{m/s^2}$
Answer: (B)
$$a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(6t - 12) = 6~\mathrm{m/s^2}$$
$a$ is the second derivative of the position polynomial; for a quadratic in $t$, this is a constant.
Q4MEDIUM 1.3 Representing MotionNo Calculator

A velocity vs. time graph is described in Q4 of the practice set: $v$ is constant on $[0,2]~\mathrm{s}$, slopes down on $[2,4]~\mathrm{s}$, and is constant again on $[4,6]~\mathrm{s}$. During which interval is the particle's acceleration negative and nonzero?

  • (A) $0 < t < 2~\mathrm{s}$
  • (B) $2 < t < 4~\mathrm{s}$
  • (C) $4 < t < 6~\mathrm{s}$
  • (D) nowhere on the graph
Answer: (B)
Acceleration is the slope of the $v$–$t$ graph. The flat segments (A) and (C) have $a = 0$. Only the descending segment on $2 < t < 4$ has $a < 0$ and nonzero.
Q5MEDIUM 1.3 Kinematic EquationsNo Calculator

A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly to $20~\mathrm{m/s}$ over $5~\mathrm{s}$. The distance it travels in this interval is

  • (A) $25~\mathrm{m}$
  • (B) $50~\mathrm{m}$
  • (C) $75~\mathrm{m}$
  • (D) $100~\mathrm{m}$
Answer: (B)
Use the average-velocity shortcut for uniform acceleration:
$$d = \bar v\,t = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\,t = \frac{0 + 20}{2}\,(5) = 50~\mathrm{m}$$
Equivalently, $a = \tfrac{20}{5} = 4~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ and $d = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(4)(25) = 50~\mathrm{m}$.
Q6MEDIUM 1.3 Free FallNo Calculator

A ball is dropped from rest from a height $H$ above level ground. Air resistance is negligible. Its speed just before impact is

  • (A) $\sqrt{gH}$
  • (B) $\sqrt{2gH}$
  • (C) $2gH$
  • (D) $gH$
Answer: (B)
Apply $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2g\,\Delta y$ with $v_0 = 0$ and $\Delta y = H$:
$$v^2 = 2gH \quad\Longrightarrow\quad v = \sqrt{2gH}$$
Choices (C) and (D) have wrong units (m²/s² and m²/s², not m/s).
Q7MEDIUM 1.2 Non-Constant AccelerationNo Calculator

A particle has acceleration $a(t) = 6t~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ and starts from rest at $x=0$. Its position at $t=2~\mathrm{s}$ is

  • (A) $4~\mathrm{m}$
  • (B) $8~\mathrm{m}$
  • (C) $12~\mathrm{m}$
  • (D) $24~\mathrm{m}$
Answer: (B)
Integrate twice from rest at the origin:
$$v(t) = \int_0^t 6\tau\,d\tau = 3t^2$$
$$x(t) = \int_0^t 3\tau^2\,d\tau = t^3$$
$$x(2) = 2^3 = 8~\mathrm{m}$$
Trap (D) is $a(2) \cdot t$, treating $a$ as constant.
Q8MEDIUM 1.4 Relative MotionNo Calculator

A boat heads due north across a river at $4~\mathrm{m/s}$ relative to the water. The river flows due east at $3~\mathrm{m/s}$. The boat's speed relative to the bank is

  • (A) $1~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $5~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $7~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $\sqrt{7}~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (B)
Velocities add as vectors. The components are perpendicular (north and east), so the resultant speed is
$$|\vec v_\text{bank}| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Trap (C) adds magnitudes directly (only valid for parallel vectors); (A) subtracts them (only for anti-parallel).
Q9MEDIUM 1.5 Projectile MotionCalculator

A ball is launched from ground level with initial speed $25~\mathrm{m/s}$ at $40^\circ$ above the horizontal. Take $g=9.8~\mathrm{m/s^2}$. Its time of flight (return to launch height) is closest to

  • (A) $1.6~\mathrm{s}$
  • (B) $2.6~\mathrm{s}$
  • (C) $3.3~\mathrm{s}$
  • (D) $5.1~\mathrm{s}$
Answer: (C)
Return to launch height: $T = 2 v_0 \sin\theta / g$.
$$T = \frac{2(25)\sin 40^\circ}{9.8} = \frac{50 \cdot 0.643}{9.8} \approx 3.28~\mathrm{s}$$
Trap (A) uses $v_0\sin\theta/g$ (time to apex, not full flight).
Q10MEDIUM 1.5 Projectile MotionNo Calculator

A projectile is launched from ground level with speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$. Neglect air resistance. Its horizontal range is maximized when $\theta$ equals

  • (A) $30^\circ$
  • (B) $45^\circ$
  • (C) $60^\circ$
  • (D) $90^\circ$
Answer: (B)
Range $R = (v_0^2/g)\sin(2\theta)$ is maximized when $\sin(2\theta)=1$, i.e. $2\theta = 90^\circ$, so $\theta = 45^\circ$. The $30^\circ$ and $60^\circ$ pair give equal ranges below the maximum.
Q11HARD 1.2 Average vs. InstantaneousNo Calculator

A particle's position is $x(t)=t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$ (SI). On the interval $0 \le t \le 4~\mathrm{s}$, the particle's average velocity equals its instantaneous velocity at $t=$

  • (A) $1~\mathrm{s}$ only
  • (B) $2~\mathrm{s}$ only
  • (C) $2 \pm \dfrac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}~\mathrm{s}$
  • (D) $2 \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}~\mathrm{s}$
Answer: (C)
MVT: solve $v(t) = \bar v$ on $[0,4]$.
$$\bar v = \frac{x(4) - x(0)}{4} = \frac{(64 - 96 + 36) - 0}{4} = 1~\mathrm{m/s}$$
$$v(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; 3t^2 - 12t + 8 = 0$$
$$t = \frac{12 \pm \sqrt{144 - 96}}{6} = \frac{12 \pm 4\sqrt{3}}{6} = 2 \pm \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}~\mathrm{s}$$
Both roots lie in $[0,4]$. Trap (D) drops the factor of 2 from the discriminant.
Q12HARD 1.3 Free Fall + ReactionCalculator

A stone is thrown straight up from the edge of a $40~\mathrm{m}$ cliff with initial speed $15~\mathrm{m/s}$. Take $g=9.8~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ and ignore air resistance. The stone's speed when it strikes the ground at the cliff's base is closest to

  • (A) $19~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $23~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $32~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $43~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (C)
The path detail (up then down) is irrelevant: $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2g\,|\Delta y|$ with $|\Delta y| = 40~\mathrm{m}$ downward overall.
$$v^2 = (15)^2 + 2(9.8)(40) = 225 + 784 = 1009$$
$$v = \sqrt{1009} \approx 31.8~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Trap (D) is $v_0 + \sqrt{2g\cdot 40}$, which double-counts the initial speed.
Q13HARD 1.5 2-D MotionNo Calculator

A particle moves in the $xy$-plane with $\vec r(t) = (3t)\,\hat\imath + (4t - t^2)\,\hat\jmath$ (SI). Its speed at $t=1~\mathrm{s}$ is

  • (A) $2~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (B) $\sqrt{13}~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (C) $5~\mathrm{m/s}$
  • (D) $\sqrt{18}~\mathrm{m/s}$
Answer: (B)
$$\vec v(t) = \frac{d\vec r}{dt} = 3\,\hat\imath + (4 - 2t)\,\hat\jmath$$
$$\vec v(1) = 3\,\hat\imath + 2\,\hat\jmath \quad\Longrightarrow\quad |\vec v(1)| = \sqrt{9 + 4} = \sqrt{13}~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Trap (D) is $|\vec r(1)| = \sqrt{9 + 9}$ — the magnitude of position, not velocity. Trap (A) keeps only the $y$-component.
Q14HARD 1.3 Graph InterpretationNo Calculator

A particle's $a$ vs. $t$ graph is a triangle: $a$ rises linearly from $0$ at $t=0$ to $a_0$ at $t=T/2$, then falls linearly back to $0$ at $t=T$. If the particle starts from rest, its speed at $t=T$ is

  • (A) $\dfrac{a_0 T}{4}$
  • (B) $\dfrac{a_0 T}{2}$
  • (C) $a_0 T$
  • (D) $\dfrac{a_0 T^2}{2}$
Answer: (B)
The change in velocity from rest equals the signed area under $a(t)$. For the triangle:
$$\Delta v = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\text{base}\,\cdot\,\text{height} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,T\,a_0 = \frac{a_0 T}{2}$$
Trap (D) has units of position (area-of-$v$-curve), not speed.
Q15EASY 1.4 Relative MotionNo Calculator

A train moves due east at $20~\mathrm{m/s}$ relative to the ground. A passenger walks toward the front of the train at $1.5~\mathrm{m/s}$ relative to the train. The passenger's velocity relative to the ground is

  • (A) $18.5~\mathrm{m/s}$ east
  • (B) $20~\mathrm{m/s}$ east
  • (C) $21.5~\mathrm{m/s}$ east
  • (D) $\sqrt{20^2 + 1.5^2}~\mathrm{m/s}$ northeast
Answer: (C)
One-dimensional vector addition (both vectors east):
$$v_\text{P/G} = v_\text{P/T} + v_\text{T/G} = 1.5 + 20 = 21.5~\mathrm{m/s}~\text{east}$$
Trap (D) treats the vectors as perpendicular — but the passenger walks along the train, not across it.
Q16MEDIUM 1.2 Sign AnalysisNo Calculator

A particle moves along the $x$-axis with velocity $v(t) > 0$ that is decreasing in time. Which statement best describes the motion?

  • (A) The particle is moving in the $+x$ direction and speeding up.
  • (B) The particle is moving in the $+x$ direction and slowing down.
  • (C) The particle is moving in the $-x$ direction and speeding up.
  • (D) The particle is at rest.
Answer: (B)
$v > 0$ fixes the direction ($+x$). "Decreasing in time" means $|v|$ is decreasing — the particle is slowing down. Speeding up requires $v$ and $a$ to share a sign; here they have opposite signs.
Q17MEDIUM 1.5 Projectile MotionNo Calculator

From the same height above level ground, ball A is dropped from rest at the same instant ball B is launched horizontally. Air resistance is negligible. Which ball lands first?

  • (A) Ball A (dropped)
  • (B) Ball B (horizontal)
  • (C) They land simultaneously.
  • (D) Depends on ball B's horizontal speed.
Answer: (C)
Horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Both balls have the same initial height, the same $v_{y0}=0$, and the same vertical acceleration $g$; so $y(t) = y_0 - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$ is identical for both. They share a landing time regardless of ball B's horizontal speed.
Q18HARD 1.5 Projectile + EnergyCalculator

A projectile is launched horizontally from a cliff of height $h$ with initial speed $v_0$. Air resistance is negligible. Its speed at the moment of impact is

  • (A) $v_0$
  • (B) $\sqrt{v_0^2 + gh}$
  • (C) $\sqrt{v_0^2 + 2gh}$
  • (D) $v_0 + \sqrt{2gh}$
Answer: (C)
Components at impact: $v_x = v_0$ (unchanged), $v_y = \sqrt{2gh}$ (from free-fall over height $h$).
$$|\vec v| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} = \sqrt{v_0^2 + 2gh}$$
Or, by energy conservation: $\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_0^2 + mgh$ gives the same result. Trap (D) adds magnitudes of perpendicular vectors.

FRQ 1MEDIUM 1.2 / 1.3 Calculus of MotionNo Calculator

A particle moves along a straight line with acceleration $a(t) = 6 - 2t~\mathrm{(m/s^2)}$. At $t=0$, the particle is at $x=0$ with velocity $v_0 = 0$.

(a) Derive $v(t)$ and $x(t)$.
$$v(t) = v_0 + \int_0^t (6 - 2\tau)\,d\tau = 6t - t^2$$
$$x(t) = \int_0^t (6\tau - \tau^2)\,d\tau = 3t^2 - \tfrac{1}{3}t^3$$
(b) Times where $v(t)=0$; direction change.
$$v(t) = t(6 - t) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; t = 0,\,6~\mathrm{s}$$
At $t = 0$ this is the initial condition. At $t = 6~\mathrm{s}$ check the sign of $a(6) = 6 - 12 = -6 < 0$: $v$ is decreasing through zero, so the particle reverses direction at $t = 6~\mathrm{s}$.
(c) Maximum speed on $0 \le t \le 5~\mathrm{s}$. Set $a(t) = 0$: $t = 3~\mathrm{s}$ (interior critical point in the interval).
$$v(3) = 6(3) - 3^2 = 9~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Endpoints: $v(0) = 0$, $v(5) = 30 - 25 = 5~\mathrm{m/s}$. The maximum is $\boxed{9~\mathrm{m/s}~\text{at}~t = 3~\mathrm{s}}$.
(d) Total distance on $0 \le t \le 5~\mathrm{s}$. $v(t) = t(6-t) \ge 0$ on $[0, 5]$ (the next zero is at $t = 6$), so no sign change inside the interval. Total distance equals $|x(5) - x(0)|$:
$$x(5) = 3(25) - \tfrac{125}{3} = 75 - \tfrac{125}{3} = \tfrac{100}{3} \approx 33.3~\mathrm{m}$$
Total distance $= \tfrac{100}{3}~\mathrm{m}$.
FRQ 2MEDIUM 1.5 Projectile MotionCalculator

Launched from the edge of a cliff of height $h = 30~\mathrm{m}$ with $v_0 = 22~\mathrm{m/s}$ at $\theta = 35^\circ$ above horizontal. Air resistance negligible.

(a) Components and kinematic equations (origin at launch, $+y$ up).
$$v_{x0} = 22\cos 35^\circ \approx 18.0~\mathrm{m/s}, \quad v_{y0} = 22\sin 35^\circ \approx 12.6~\mathrm{m/s}$$
$$x(t) = 18.0\,t, \qquad y(t) = 12.6\,t - 4.9\,t^2$$
(b) Time to apex and maximum height above launch. Set $v_y(t) = v_{y0} - gt = 0$:
$$t_\text{apex} = \frac{v_{y0}}{g} = \frac{12.6}{9.8} \approx 1.29~\mathrm{s}$$
$$y_\text{max} = y(1.29) = 12.6(1.29) - 4.9(1.29)^2 \approx 8.1~\mathrm{m}$$
(c) Time to land at the base ($y = -h = -30$):
$$-30 = 12.6\,t - 4.9\,t^2 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; 4.9\,t^2 - 12.6\,t - 30 = 0$$
$$t = \frac{12.6 + \sqrt{12.6^2 + 4(4.9)(30)}}{2(4.9)} = \frac{12.6 + \sqrt{746.8}}{9.8} \approx 4.07~\mathrm{s}$$
Take the positive root.
(d) Range and impact speed.
$$R = x(4.07) = 18.0(4.07) \approx 73.3~\mathrm{m}$$
$$v_y(4.07) = 12.6 - 9.8(4.07) \approx -27.3~\mathrm{m/s}$$
$$|\vec v|_\text{impact} = \sqrt{18.0^2 + 27.3^2} \approx 32.7~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Check via energy: $\tfrac{1}{2}v_0^2 + gh = \tfrac{1}{2}(22)^2 + 9.8(30) = 242 + 294 = 536$. So $v = \sqrt{2 \cdot 536} \approx 32.7~\mathrm{m/s}$. ✓
FRQ 3HARD 1.4 Relative MotionCalculator

River flows east at $u = 2.0~\mathrm{m/s}$; width $L = 80~\mathrm{m}$. Swimmer's speed relative to water is $v = 1.5~\mathrm{m/s}$. Swimmer enters at the south bank.

(a) Aiming due north. The north (across-stream) velocity is the full $v = 1.5~\mathrm{m/s}$; the east drift is the current $u = 2.0~\mathrm{m/s}$.
$$t_\text{cross} = \frac{L}{v} = \frac{80}{1.5} \approx 53.3~\mathrm{s}$$
$$x_\text{drift} = u\,t_\text{cross} = 2.0 \cdot 53.3 \approx 106.7~\mathrm{m}~\text{east}$$
(b) Can the swimmer reach a point directly across? To cancel the current the swimmer would need an upstream velocity component $v\sin\theta = u$, i.e. $\sin\theta = u/v = 2/1.5 = 1.33$. Impossible — $\sin\theta \le 1$. Minimum-drift heading. Let $\theta$ be the angle west of north. Ground velocity: east component $u - v\sin\theta$; north component $v\cos\theta$. Time across is $L/(v\cos\theta)$, so
$$D(\theta) = \frac{(u - v\sin\theta)\,L}{v\cos\theta}$$
Set $D'(\theta) = 0$. After simplification,
$$\sin\theta = \frac{v}{u} = \frac{1.5}{2.0} = 0.75 \;\Longrightarrow\; \theta \approx 48.6^\circ~\text{west of north}$$
(c) Drift and crossing time at the optimal heading. $\cos\theta = \sqrt{1 - 0.75^2} \approx 0.661$.
$$t_\text{cross} = \frac{L}{v\cos\theta} = \frac{80}{1.5(0.661)} \approx 80.7~\mathrm{s}$$
$$D_\text{min} = (u - v\sin\theta)\,t_\text{cross} = (2 - 1.125)(80.7) \approx 70.6~\mathrm{m}~\text{east}$$
Trade-off: the minimum-drift heading costs ~27 s more crossing time than aiming due north (53 s) but cuts drift by ~36 m.
FRQ 4HARD 1.2 / 1.3 Data + CalculusCalculator

A cart on a track is released; position recorded with a motion sensor:

$t$ (s)0.00.20.40.60.81.0
$x$ (m)0.000.040.160.360.641.00
(a) Consistency with constant $a$; numeric value. For constant $a$ from rest at the origin, $x = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$, so $x$ should scale as $t^2$. The table's $x$ values equal $t^2$ exactly at every sample:
$$\frac{x}{t^2} = 1.00~\mathrm{m/s^2}~\text{at every point}$$
Hence $\tfrac{1}{2}a = 1$ and $a = 2~\mathrm{m/s^2}$ (constant).
(b) Centered-difference velocity at $t = 0.5~\mathrm{s}$.
$$v(0.5) \approx \frac{x(0.6) - x(0.4)}{0.6 - 0.4} = \frac{0.36 - 0.16}{0.2} = 1.0~\mathrm{m/s}$$
Prediction from (a): $v(t) = at = (2)(0.5) = 1.0~\mathrm{m/s}$. The centered difference agrees exactly (no surprise — for a polynomial of degree 2 the centered difference is the analytic derivative).
(c) "Doubling the tilt doubles $a$" — kinematics or dynamics? Verdict: dynamics is required, and even then the claim is only approximate. Constant-$a$ kinematics ($x = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$, $v = at$) tells you only that $a$ is constant — it doesn't predict how $a$ varies with the experimental setup. To connect $a$ to the tilt angle you need Newton's second law: on a frictionless incline at angle $\alpha$,
$$a = g\sin\alpha$$
Doubling $\alpha$ doubles $\sin\alpha$ only in the small-angle limit ($\sin\alpha \approx \alpha$). For larger tilts, $\sin(2\alpha) \ne 2\sin\alpha$, so the claim is generally false even after invoking dynamics.
